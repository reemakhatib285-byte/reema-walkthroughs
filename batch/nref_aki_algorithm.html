⚠️ Board traps 13
  1. Acute kidney injury — WRONG answer is diagnosing AKI off a single high creatinine without a baseline. A stable elevated Cr is CKD, not AKI. Discriminator: AKI requires an acute CHANGE — Cr rise ≥0.3 in 48h or ≥1.5× baseline in 7 days; small kidneys on ultrasound point to chronicity instead.
  2. Prerenal — WRONG answer is giving aggressive IV fluids to every prerenal picture. In hepatorenal syndrome and cardiorenal syndrome the 'effective' circulating volume is low but the patient is total-body fluid-OVERLOADED. Discriminator: in HRS treat with albumin + vasoconstrictors (terlipressin/midodrine-octreotide); in cardiorenal AKI diurese/improve cardiac output — fluids worsen both.
  3. Intrinsic — WRONG answer is lumping all intrinsic AKI as ATN and just supporting it. Missing a rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RBC casts, rising Cr) wastes the window for immunosuppression. Discriminator: RBC casts + hematuria + proteinuria mandate urgent nephrology referral, serologies (ANCA, anti-GBM, complement), and often biopsy — not watchful waiting.
  4. Postrenal — WRONG answer is ruling out obstruction because the ultrasound shows no hydronephrosis. Early or encased (retroperitoneal fibrosis, tumor) obstruction can lack hydronephrosis. Discriminator: high clinical suspicion (anuria, distended bladder, pelvic cancer) warrants CT or retrograde studies even with a 'normal' renal US.
  5. Hypovolemia / ↓CO — WRONG answer is treating a cirrhotic's prerenal azotemia as simple dehydration with crystalloid. Discriminator: hepatorenal syndrome shows FENa <1%, no improvement after 2 days of albumin volume expansion, and no other cause — it needs vasoconstrictors and albumin, not ongoing saline that worsens ascites.
  6. Impaired autoregulation — WRONG answer is permanently stopping the ACE-I/ARB after any creatinine bump. A rise of up to ~30% in Cr after starting an ACE-I is expected and acceptable — it reflects reduced intraglomerular pressure and confers long-term renoprotection. Discriminator: stop only if Cr rises >30%, hyperkalemia develops, or bilateral renal artery stenosis is suspected (large jump signals it).
  7. Glomerular — WRONG answer is calling glomerular hematuria a 'UTI' or stone. Discriminator: RBC CASTS and DYSMORPHIC RBCs are pathognomonic of glomerular bleeding — isomorphic RBCs with clots suggest urologic/lower-tract source. Casts mean send serologies and consider biopsy, not antibiotics.
  8. Tubules & interstitium — WRONG answer is treating ATN as if fluids will fix it. Once tubular necrosis has occurred, a fluid bolus does NOT rapidly reverse it — recovery takes days to weeks of supportive care. Discriminator: prerenal improves promptly with fluids and has FENa <1% with bland urine; established ATN has FENa >2% and muddy-brown casts and will not bounce back with a bolus.
  9. Vascular — WRONG answer is giving platelet transfusions in TTP for the low platelets. This can worsen microthrombi. Discriminator: TTP (ADAMTS13 deficiency) is treated with urgent plasma exchange ± steroids/caplacizumab — platelet transfusion is contraindicated except for life-threatening bleeding or before a procedure.
  10. Ischemia / Sepsis — WRONG answer is assuming early sepsis-associated AKI must have a high FENa like other ATN. In the first hours, sepsis and ischemia can present with a LOW FENa (<1%) before frank tubular necrosis sets in — a low-FENa exception. Discriminator: don't exclude evolving ATN just because FENa is low early; follow the sediment (muddy-brown casts) and the clinical trajectory.
  11. Nephrotoxins — exogenous — WRONG answer is reflexively giving N-acetylcysteine and bicarbonate to 'prevent' contrast nephropathy. Large trials (PRESERVE) showed no benefit over isotonic saline. Discriminator: the evidence-based prophylaxis is IV isotonic saline volume expansion and minimizing contrast dose — NAC/bicarbonate are not recommended.
  12. Nephrotoxins — endogenous — WRONG answer is trusting a 'positive blood' dipstick with no RBCs on microscopy as hematuria. Discriminator: heme-positive dipstick + absent RBCs = myoglobinuria (rhabdo) or hemoglobinuria — check CK and a peripheral smear/haptoglobin; treat rhabdo with aggressive IV fluids targeting brisk urine output.
  13. Bladder outlet / pelvic obstruction — Unilateral ureteral obstruction with a normal contralateral kidney causes AKI — this is the WRONG answer. A single normal kidney maintains overall GFR, so unilateral obstruction does NOT raise creatinine (though it may cause pain/hydronephrosis). Discriminator: postrenal AKI requires bilateral obstruction or obstruction of a solitary functioning kidney.
📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Acute kidney injury
Root node. AKI = abrupt (hours-days) decline in GFR. KDIGO defines it as a rise in serum Cr ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48h, OR ≥1.5× baseline within the prior 7 days, OR urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h for >6h. The algorithm splits AKI into three mechanistic buckets — prerenal (perfusion), intrinsic (parenchymal), and postrenal (obstruction) — because the workup and treatment differ by category.
⚠️ WRONG answer is diagnosing AKI off a single high creatinine without a baseline. A stable elevated Cr is CKD, not AKI. Discriminator: AKI requires an acute CHANGE — Cr rise ≥0.3 in 48h or ≥1.5× baseline in 7 days; small kidneys on ultrasound point to chronicity instead.
2. Prerenal
Hypoperfusion of a structurally normal kidney — the single most common cause of AKI (~40-70%). The nephron is intact and conserving: tubules avidly reabsorb Na and water, giving FENa <1%, urine Na <20, urine osm >500, BUN:Cr >20, and bland sediment (hyaline casts only). Fully reversible if perfusion is restored before it progresses to ischemic ATN.
⚠️ WRONG answer is giving aggressive IV fluids to every prerenal picture. In hepatorenal syndrome and cardiorenal syndrome the 'effective' circulating volume is low but the patient is total-body fluid-OVERLOADED. Discriminator: in HRS treat with albumin + vasoconstrictors (terlipressin/midodrine-octreotide); in cardiorenal AKI diurese/improve cardiac output — fluids worsen both.
3. Intrinsic
Parenchymal injury, subdivided by compartment: glomerular (GN), tubular (ATN), interstitial (AIN), and vascular. ATN is the most common intrinsic cause. Localize by urine sediment: muddy-brown granular casts = ATN; RBC casts/dysmorphic RBCs = GN; WBC casts/eosinophiluria = AIN; bland with systemic vascular signs = vascular.
⚠️ WRONG answer is lumping all intrinsic AKI as ATN and just supporting it. Missing a rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RBC casts, rising Cr) wastes the window for immunosuppression. Discriminator: RBC casts + hematuria + proteinuria mandate urgent nephrology referral, serologies (ANCA, anti-GBM, complement), and often biopsy — not watchful waiting.
4. Postrenal
Obstruction to urine flow downstream of the kidney — BPH/prostate cancer, bilateral stones, pelvic/retroperitoneal malignancy, neurogenic bladder. Diagnosed by post-void bladder scan and renal ultrasound (hydronephrosis). Relief (Foley, nephrostomy, stent) is often curative; watch for post-obstructive diuresis afterward.
⚠️ WRONG answer is ruling out obstruction because the ultrasound shows no hydronephrosis. Early or encased (retroperitoneal fibrosis, tumor) obstruction can lack hydronephrosis. Discriminator: high clinical suspicion (anuria, distended bladder, pelvic cancer) warrants CT or retrograde studies even with a 'normal' renal US.
5. Hypovolemia / ↓CO
Two routes to prerenal: TRUE volume loss (hemorrhage, GI losses, diuretics, burns) and reduced EFFECTIVE arterial volume despite fluid overload (CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, sepsis-induced vasodilation). Both trigger RAAS and ADH, driving Na/water retention — hence FENa <1% and BUN:Cr >20.
⚠️ WRONG answer is treating a cirrhotic's prerenal azotemia as simple dehydration with crystalloid. Discriminator: hepatorenal syndrome shows FENa <1%, no improvement after 2 days of albumin volume expansion, and no other cause — it needs vasoconstrictors and albumin, not ongoing saline that worsens ascites.
6. Impaired autoregulation
Glomerular pressure is autoregulated by afferent (in) and efferent (out) arteriolar tone. NSAIDs block prostaglandin-mediated afferent VASODILATION → afferent constricts → ↓GFR. ACE-I/ARBs block angiotensin-II-mediated efferent CONSTRICTION → efferent dilates → ↓glomerular pressure → ↓GFR. Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus) cause afferent vasoconstriction. The triple whammy (NSAID + ACE/ARB + diuretic) is a classic precipitant.
⚠️ WRONG answer is permanently stopping the ACE-I/ARB after any creatinine bump. A rise of up to ~30% in Cr after starting an ACE-I is expected and acceptable — it reflects reduced intraglomerular pressure and confers long-term renoprotection. Discriminator: stop only if Cr rises >30%, hyperkalemia develops, or bilateral renal artery stenosis is suspected (large jump signals it).
7. Glomerular
Glomerular inflammation → nephritic syndrome: hematuria with dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, subnephrotic proteinuria, HTN, edema. Work up with complement levels and serologies — low complement: post-strep GN, lupus, MPGN, cryoglobulinemia; normal complement (pauci-immune/anti-GBM): ANCA vasculitis (GPA/MPA), anti-GBM/Goodpasture, IgA nephropathy. RPGN = crescents on biopsy; treat urgently with steroids ± cyclophosphamide/rituximab/plasmapheresis.
⚠️ WRONG answer is calling glomerular hematuria a 'UTI' or stone. Discriminator: RBC CASTS and DYSMORPHIC RBCs are pathognomonic of glomerular bleeding — isomorphic RBCs with clots suggest urologic/lower-tract source. Casts mean send serologies and consider biopsy, not antibiotics.
8. Tubules & interstitium
Two entities: ATN (tubular cell death from ischemia, sepsis, or nephrotoxins → muddy-brown granular casts, FENa >2%, isosthenuric urine osm <350) and AIN (immune/allergic interstitial inflammation → WBC casts, sterile pyuria, eosinophiluria, often after a drug). ATN has classic phases: initiation, maintenance (oliguria), and recovery (polyuria).
⚠️ WRONG answer is treating ATN as if fluids will fix it. Once tubular necrosis has occurred, a fluid bolus does NOT rapidly reverse it — recovery takes days to weeks of supportive care. Discriminator: prerenal improves promptly with fluids and has FENa <1% with bland urine; established ATN has FENa >2% and muddy-brown casts and will not bounce back with a bolus.
9. Vascular
Large-vessel (renal artery thromboembolism/dissection, renal vein thrombosis) and small-vessel disease (thrombotic microangiopathy: TTP-HUS, malignant HTN, scleroderma renal crisis, atheroembolic disease). TMA = MAHA (schistocytes, ↓haptoglobin, ↑LDH) + thrombocytopenia + AKI. Scleroderma renal crisis → treat with ACE inhibitors (the one place an ACE-I is first-line in AKI).
⚠️ WRONG answer is giving platelet transfusions in TTP for the low platelets. This can worsen microthrombi. Discriminator: TTP (ADAMTS13 deficiency) is treated with urgent plasma exchange ± steroids/caplacizumab — platelet transfusion is contraindicated except for life-threatening bleeding or before a procedure.
10. Ischemia / Sepsis
Ischemia (prolonged hypotension, shock, post-surgical) and sepsis are the leading triggers of ATN — prerenal hypoperfusion that crosses the threshold into tubular necrosis. The proximal tubule and outer-medulla thick ascending limb are most vulnerable (high metabolic demand, watershed O2 supply). Sepsis adds inflammatory and microvascular injury.
⚠️ WRONG answer is assuming early sepsis-associated AKI must have a high FENa like other ATN. In the first hours, sepsis and ischemia can present with a LOW FENa (<1%) before frank tubular necrosis sets in — a low-FENa exception. Discriminator: don't exclude evolving ATN just because FENa is low early; follow the sediment (muddy-brown casts) and the clinical trajectory.
11. Nephrotoxins — exogenous
Classic exogenous tubular/interstitial toxins. ATN pattern: aminoglycosides (nonoliguric, ~day 5-7, dose/duration related), cisplatin (Mg wasting), amphotericin B (distal RTA, K/Mg wasting), iodinated contrast (Cr rises 24-48h, peaks 3-5d, recovers by 7d, low FENa). AIN pattern: PPIs, NSAIDs (also cause membranous/minimal change), checkpoint inhibitors, beta-lactams.
⚠️ WRONG answer is reflexively giving N-acetylcysteine and bicarbonate to 'prevent' contrast nephropathy. Large trials (PRESERVE) showed no benefit over isotonic saline. Discriminator: the evidence-based prophylaxis is IV isotonic saline volume expansion and minimizing contrast dose — NAC/bicarbonate are not recommended.
12. Nephrotoxins — endogenous
Endogenous pigments/proteins/crystals: rhabdomyolysis (myoglobin → ↑CK >5000, heme-positive dipstick with NO RBCs, hyperK, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia), hemolysis (hemoglobin), myeloma cast nephropathy (light chains; suspect with high anion-gap, anemia, hypercalcemia, age >60), and crystal nephropathies (tumor lysis uric acid, acyclovir, methotrexate, ethylene glycol oxalate). Pigment nephropathies are a low-FENa exception.
⚠️ WRONG answer is trusting a 'positive blood' dipstick with no RBCs on microscopy as hematuria. Discriminator: heme-positive dipstick + absent RBCs = myoglobinuria (rhabdo) or hemoglobinuria — check CK and a peripheral smear/haptoglobin; treat rhabdo with aggressive IV fluids targeting brisk urine output.
13. Bladder outlet / pelvic obstruction
For obstruction to cause AKI you need BILATERAL involvement (or obstruction of a single functioning kidney) because one healthy kidney can maintain GFR. Most common is bladder outlet obstruction (BPH, prostate cancer, neurogenic bladder); upper-tract causes are bilateral stones, pelvic/retroperitoneal tumor, or retroperitoneal fibrosis. Relief can trigger post-obstructive diuresis — monitor volume and electrolytes.
⚠️ Unilateral ureteral obstruction with a normal contralateral kidney causes AKI — this is the WRONG answer. A single normal kidney maintains overall GFR, so unilateral obstruction does NOT raise creatinine (though it may cause pain/hydronephrosis). Discriminator: postrenal AKI requires bilateral obstruction or obstruction of a solitary functioning kidney.
