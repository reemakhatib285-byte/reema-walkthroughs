📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. PBP anvil (blacksmith)
S. pneumoniae penicillin resistance is altered penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) — the transpeptidases that build the cell wall. Mosaic pbp genes acquired by transformation lower beta-lactam affinity, so more drug is needed. This is TARGET modification, fundamentally different from enzymatic (beta-lactamase) drug destruction.
2. Broken hammer / wrong tool
Because pneumococcal resistance is PBP-affinity-based (not enzymatic), adding a beta-lactamase inhibitor like clavulanate does NOTHING for drug-resistant S. pneumoniae. The correct fix is to RAISE the dose. Non-meningeal breakpoints were raised (parenteral susceptible <=2 ug/mL) to reflect dose-surmountable resistance.
⚠️ "add clavulanate to beat resistant pneumococcus" -> wrong; resistance is PBP target change, not an enzyme — raise the dose instead.
3. 1g high-dose gauge
Because resistance is dose-surmountable, most non-meningeal penicillin-'resistant' pneumococci are still treatable with high-dose amoxicillin 1 g PO TID or high-dose IV penicillin/ceftriaxone. Meningeal breakpoints stay strict (<=0.06) due to poor CNS penetration.
⚠️ "non-meningeal breakpoint applies to meningitis" -> wrong; use the strict MENINGEAL breakpoint for CNS disease — empiric pneumococcal meningitis is vancomycin + 3rd-gen cephalosporin, not amoxicillin alone.
4. ermB barrel (X'd)
ermB methylates 23S rRNA (A2058) of the 50S subunit — the macrolide target — giving HIGH-level resistance (MIC >=64) and the MLSB phenotype (cross-resistance to macrolides, lincosamides, streptogramin B). Accounts for ~40-60% of resistant pneumococci in some series.
⚠️ "raise the macrolide dose" for ermB -> wrong; high-level target-site resistance is not dose-surmountable — switch class (beta-lactam, doxycycline, respiratory FQ).
5. mefA barrel (efflux)
mef(A/E) is a membrane efflux pump that exports 14- and 15-membered macrolides — LOW-level resistance (MIC <16, the M phenotype). Unlike ermB it does NOT affect clindamycin. It is the more common macrolide-resistance mechanism in some regions.
⚠️ "low MIC mef strain is safe for azithromycin monotherapy" -> wrong; even low-level efflux resistance causes clinical failures — use combination therapy or a non-macrolide where resistance is prevalent.
6. 25% resistance scale (X'd)
When local pneumococcal macrolide resistance exceeds 25% (US rates ~30-40%), do NOT use a macrolide as monotherapy for CAP. A macrolide is acceptable only as the atypical partner of a beta-lactam, or use a respiratory FQ instead. The 25% threshold is the key testable number.
⚠️ "azithromycin monotherapy for CAP" in the US -> wrong; resistance exceeds 25% almost everywhere — add a beta-lactam or use a respiratory FQ. Monotherapy is OK only for a healthy outpatient with documented low local resistance.
7. <2% FQ sword on shelf
Pneumococcal resistance to respiratory FQs (levofloxacin, moxifloxacin) stays low (<2%), so they are reliable single-agent empiric options covering both DRSP and atypicals. FQs inhibit DNA gyrase (gyrA, topo II) and topoisomerase IV (parC); resistance is stepwise gyrA/parC point mutations.
⚠️ "empiric respiratory FQ when TB is plausible" -> wrong (the skull warns); an FQ partially treats and MASKS TB, selecting FQ-resistant TB and delaying diagnosis — avoid with upper-lobe cavitary disease/risk factors. Ciprofloxacin is NOT a respiratory FQ.
8. 3 MONTHS calendar
Antibiotic exposure within the prior 90 days (3 months) is among the strongest risk factors for drug-resistant S. pneumoniae and resistant gram-negatives, and an MDR risk factor for HAP/VAP. The recent drug pressures flora toward resistance to THAT class. This single factor often upgrades to a combination/respiratory-FQ regimen.
⚠️ "re-use the recently given class" -> wrong; rotate to a DIFFERENT class — macrolide recently -> beta-lactam/doxy or FQ; beta-lactam recently -> macrolide or FQ.
9. 5-day hourglass / IV drip
Uncomplicated CAP needs a MINIMUM 5 days AND clinical stability (afebrile 48-72 h, stable vitals/oxygenation, eating) before stopping. Longer only for complications: 7+ days MRSA/Pseudomonas; weeks for abscess/empyema/meningitis. Switch IV->PO once hemodynamically stable, improving, able to ingest, with normal GI absorption (FQs, doxy, linezolid, metronidazole are ~100% oral bioavailable).
⚠️ "longer course is better" for stabilized uncomplicated CAP -> wrong; prolonging risks C. difficile/resistance — but don't stop at 5 days if still unstable or complicated.
10. Top shelf — mace (healthy outpatient)
Healthy outpatient, NO comorbidities, no resistance risk: high-dose amoxicillin 1 g PO TID preferred (high dose beats PBP resistance). Doxycycline is an alternative; a macrolide is acceptable only where local resistance <25%. Low mortality; no mandatory atypical combination.
⚠️ "macrolide monotherapy" in a high-resistance region -> wrong even for this lowest-risk group; and don't over-treat a healthy outpatient with a respiratory FQ.
11. 2nd shelf — beta-lactam shield (comorbid)
Outpatient WITH comorbidities (heart/lung/liver/renal disease, diabetes, alcoholism, malignancy, asplenia) or recent antibiotics: beta-lactam (amox-clavulanate or cephalosporin) PLUS a macrolide or doxycycline, OR a respiratory FQ as monotherapy. Comorbidity raises DRSP risk, so atypical coverage becomes mandatory.
⚠️ "plain amoxicillin monotherapy" with comorbidities -> wrong; no atypical coverage and higher DRSP risk — use beta-lactam + macrolide/doxy or a respiratory FQ alone.
12. 3rd/4th shelf — patients in beds (inpatient)
Inpatient non-severe (ward) without MRSA/Pseudomonas risk: beta-lactam (ceftriaxone/cefotaxime/amp-sulbactam) PLUS a macrolide, OR a respiratory FQ alone. Severe/ICU CAP (>=1 major or >=3 minor IDSA/ATS criteria): beta-lactam PLUS a macrolide, OR beta-lactam PLUS a respiratory FQ — always a beta-lactam-containing COMBINATION.
⚠️ "respiratory FQ monotherapy for severe/ICU CAP" -> wrong; severe disease needs a beta-lactam-containing combination — FQ alone is inadequate.
13. X'd bottles — no reflexive broad coverage
Add empiric anti-MRSA (vancomycin/linezolid) or antipseudomonal beta-lactam ONLY with specific risk factors (prior MRSA/Pseudomonas isolation, recent IV antibiotics + local validation), then de-escalate by cultures. The abandoned HCAP category drove overtreatment without better outcomes.
⚠️ "treat as HCAP with vanc + pip-tazo" for a stable patient with healthcare contact -> wrong; risk-stratify individually rather than reflexively broadening.
