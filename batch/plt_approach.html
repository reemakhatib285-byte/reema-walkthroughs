📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Threshold 150K
Thrombocytopenia is defined as a platelet count <150,000/µL (normal 150–450K). The number alone does not predict bleeding linearly — spontaneous mucocutaneous bleeding is uncommon until <10–20K, and most invasive procedures/surgery can proceed safely above 50K. Counts of 100–150K are often incidental and benign (e.g., gestational), so always anchor the workup to the trend and the smear, not a single value.
⚠️ Don't transfuse platelets just because the count is 'low' (e.g., 80K) in a stable, non-bleeding patient — the threshold for prophylactic transfusion is generally <10K (or <20K with fever/sepsis, <50K for surgery/bleeding). Reflexively transfusing wastes product and is actively harmful in TTP/HIT (fuels thrombosis).
2. Three mechanisms
Every thrombocytopenia sorts into one of three buckets: decreased PRODUCTION (marrow problem), splenic SEQUESTRATION (redistribution), or increased DESTRUCTION/consumption (peripheral). A fourth, dilutional, occurs after massive transfusion/fluids. The fastest way to separate them is the smear plus an MPV/reticulated platelet check — destruction and sequestration give young, large platelets, while production failure gives few small platelets often with other cytopenias.
⚠️ Don't assume isolated thrombocytopenia means destruction — pancytopenia or a leukoerythroblastic smear points to a marrow (production) process and mandates marrow exam, not a course of steroids for presumed ITP.
3. Decreased production
Production failure comes from marrow suppression (chemo, radiation, alcohol, viral — HIV/HCV/EBV/CMV/parvovirus), marrow infiltration/replacement (leukemia, lymphoma, myelofibrosis, metastatic cancer → leukoerythroblastic smear with teardrops and nucleated RBCs), or nutritional deficiency (B12/folate causing ineffective megaloblastic hematopoiesis, hypersegmented neutrophils). Typically OTHER cell lines are also low. Reticulated/immature platelet fraction is LOW because the marrow is not producing.
⚠️ Don't call low platelets + macro-ovalocytes + hypersegmented neutrophils 'ITP' — that pattern is B12/folate deficiency; treat the vitamin, not with immunosuppression.
4. Sequestration
A normal spleen pools ~1/3 of platelets; with splenomegaly (cirrhosis/portal hypertension, lymphoma, Gaucher, infiltration) up to 90% are sequestered. The hallmark is a moderate floor — sequestration alone rarely drops counts below ~40,000/µL, and total platelet mass/lifespan is normal, so bleeding is mild. Look for the cause of splenomegaly (chronic liver disease is #1 in adults) rather than treating the count.
⚠️ Don't attribute a platelet count of 8K purely to hypersplenism — sequestration floors around 40K; a count that low means a concurrent destruction or production process is present.
5. Destruction
Peripheral destruction/consumption is the 'fire' bucket: immune (ITP, drug-induced including HIT) and non-immune microangiopathic (TTP, HUS, DIC). Smear is the discriminator — schistocytes signal MAHA (TTP/HUS/DIC) and demand urgent action, while a clean smear with only large platelets suggests ITP. The reticulated platelet fraction is HIGH because the marrow is compensating for rapid turnover.
⚠️ Don't lump all destructive thrombocytopenias together — platelet transfusion helps in ITP bleeding but is contraindicated in TTP and HIT, where it adds substrate for thrombosis. Always check a smear and coags before transfusing.
6. Pseudothrombocytopenia
Pseudothrombocytopenia is an in-vitro artifact: EDTA in the purple-top tube triggers naturally occurring antibodies to clump platelets, so the automated analyzer counts clumps as single cells and reports a falsely low number. The patient is asymptomatic with no bleeding. Confirm by examining the smear (visible clumps) and redrawing in a citrate (blue-top) or heparin tube — the count normalizes.
⚠️ Don't work up or treat a clumping artifact — repeat in citrate first. Starting steroids, IVIG, or transfusion for a falsely low EDTA count is a classic harm. The tip-off: 'asymptomatic, isolated severe thrombocytopenia, no bleeding' on a routine CBC.
7. Smear review first
The peripheral smear is the essential, cheap first step in any thrombocytopenia. It rules in/out pseudothrombocytopenia (clumps), MAHA (schistocytes), acute leukemia (blasts), TTP/DIC, and inherited macrothrombocytopenia (giant platelets). It also lets you eyeball the count (≈ platelets per oil field × 15,000) and check the other two cell lines. No diagnostic algorithm should advance until the smear is seen.
⚠️ Don't order ADAMTS13, marrow biopsy, or immune workups before looking at the smear — skipping the smear is the classic way to miss schistocytes (TTP, a medical emergency) or platelet clumps (a non-disease).
8. Schistocytes → MAHA
Schistocytes (fragmented, helmet-shaped RBCs) on the smear define microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA): RBCs sheared as they pass platelet/fibrin microthrombi. MAHA + thrombocytopenia is a thrombotic microangiopathy until proven otherwise — TTP, HUS, or DIC. Confirm hemolysis with high LDH, high indirect bilirubin, low haptoglobin, and negative direct Coombs (non-immune). Send coags and ADAMTS13 immediately; if TTP is plausible, start plasma exchange empirically.
⚠️ Don't wait for the ADAMTS13 result before treating suspected TTP — mortality is ~90% untreated. Empiric plasma exchange (plus steroids ± caplacizumab) is started on the clinical triad of MAHA + thrombocytopenia without another explanation.
9. DIC pattern
DIC is consumptive coagulopathy from a systemic trigger (sepsis, malignancy especially APL, trauma, obstetric catastrophe). The lab signature is PROLONGED PT and aPTT, ELEVATED D-dimer (and FDPs), and LOW fibrinogen, with falling platelets and schistocytes — this coagulation derangement is exactly what distinguishes DIC from TTP/HUS, where PT/aPTT and fibrinogen are normal. Treat the underlying cause; replace with FFP/cryoprecipitate/platelets only if bleeding.
⚠️ Normal coags rule out DIC — reconsider TTP/HUS. Conversely, treating DIC with anticoagulation or factor replacement without addressing the trigger (e.g., source control in sepsis, ATRA in APL) fails — DIC is a symptom of an underlying catastrophe, not a primary diagnosis.
10. Always ask drugs/OTC
Drug history is mandatory and frequently missed — include prescription, OTC, herbals, supplements, quinine (tonic water!), and especially heparin (any form, including flushes). Drug-induced immune thrombocytopenia typically appears ~1–2 weeks after a new drug (faster on re-exposure) and resolves 7–10 days after stopping. Classic culprits: heparin, quinine/quinidine, sulfa drugs, vancomycin, GPIIb/IIIa inhibitors, valproate, linezolid.
⚠️ Don't anchor on ITP and skip the medication list — a missed quinine (tonic water) or heparin exposure means the real fix (stop the drug) is delayed and immunosuppression is started unnecessarily.
11. Marrow exam if unexplained
Bone marrow biopsy/aspirate is reserved for thrombocytopenia with features suggesting a production problem: additional cytopenias, abnormal cells (blasts) on smear, organomegaly/lymphadenopathy, age >60 with new isolated thrombocytopenia, or failure to respond to ITP therapy. Megakaryocytes are increased in destruction (ITP) and decreased/abnormal in production failure (aplasia, MDS, infiltration).
⚠️ Don't biopsy the marrow for classic isolated ITP in a young patient — it's unnecessary; but DO biopsy before committing an older patient with 'ITP' to chronic immunosuppression, since MDS can masquerade as isolated thrombocytopenia.
12. Petechiae = low number
Bleeding pattern localizes the lesion. Petechiae, purpura, mucosal/gingival bleeding, and epistaxis reflect a QUANTITATIVE platelet problem (low number) or qualitative platelet dysfunction — i.e., primary hemostasis. By contrast, deep bleeds, hemarthroses, and delayed bleeding point to a coagulation factor (secondary hemostasis) defect. Petechiae appearing only when count is very low (<10–20K) reflects number, not platelet function.
⚠️ Don't interpret petechiae as a clotting-factor problem — petechiae/mucosal bleeding = platelet (primary hemostasis); hemarthrosis and deep muscle bleeds = factor deficiency (hemophilia). Mixing these up sends the workup down the wrong pathway.
13. ITP = diagnosis of exclusion
ITP is immune-mediated platelet destruction (anti-GPIIb/IIIa antibodies) AND impaired production, diagnosed by ISOLATED thrombocytopenia with a normal smear after excluding drugs, infection (HIV, HCV, H. pylori), and other causes — there is no confirmatory test. First-line therapy is corticosteroids (prednisone or dexamethasone) ± IVIG (1 g/kg) for rapid rise or active bleeding; second-line includes TPO agonists (eltrombopag, romiplostim), rituximab, or splenectomy.
⚠️ Don't diagnose ITP without checking HIV and HCV — both classically present as 'ITP' and are reversible/treatable causes; immunosuppressing an undiagnosed HIV patient is the trap. Also test before steroids, since steroids can mask/worsen an underlying infection or lymphoma.
14. Giant platelets mimic ITP
Inherited macrothrombocytopenias (MYH9-related disorders, Bernard-Soulier) produce giant platelets (larger than RBCs, high MPV) and a chronically low count that mimics ITP. Clues to inherited disease: lifelong low counts (check old CBCs), family history, syndromic features (MYH9: Döhle-like leukocyte inclusions, nephritis, deafness, cataracts), and NO response to steroids/IVIG/splenectomy. Bernard-Soulier is a GPIb deficiency causing giant platelets + bleeding.
⚠️ Don't immunosuppress an inherited giant-platelet disorder as if it were ITP — these patients won't respond to steroids/IVIG and shouldn't have a splenectomy. The discriminator is reviewing PRIOR platelet counts (lifelong low) and family history; chronic, treatment-refractory 'ITP' should prompt this reconsideration.
