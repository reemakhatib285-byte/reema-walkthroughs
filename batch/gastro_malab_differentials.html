📖 Read-through — all 16 symbols
1. Whipple — PAS macrophages
Whipple disease = infection with Tropheryma whipplei; small-bowel biopsy shows PAS-positive foamy macrophages. Middle-aged white men. Classic tetrad weight loss, diarrhea, arthralgia, abdominal pain; arthralgia often precedes GI by years.
2. Whipple — CNS & cardiac
Whipple has prominent extra-intestinal features: CNS (dementia, ophthalmoplegia, the pathognomonic oculomasticatory myorhythmia) and CARDIAC (culture-negative endocarditis). Arthralgias can precede diagnosis by ~6 years. CNS relapse is feared.
3. Whipple — long antibiotics
Treat Whipple with PROLONGED antibiotics: IV ceftriaxone (CNS-penetrating) 2 weeks, then oral TMP-SMX for ~1 year. Under-treatment risks CNS relapse. Lifelong follow-up.
4. Tropical sprue
Tropical sprue = post-infectious malabsorption in/after travel to tropics, causing folate and B12 deficiency (megaloblastic anemia) and villous changes. Diffuse small-bowel involvement.
5. Sprue: antibiotics + folate
Treat tropical sprue with antibiotics (tetracycline ~3-6 months) PLUS folate (and B12). It responds to antibiotics — confirming an infectious etiology. Replete the deficiencies.
6. Gluten-free fails (X)
A gluten-free diet does NOT help tropical sprue — that is celiac's treatment. Tropical sprue needs antibiotics + folate. Mixing up the two is the classic trap.
⚠️ "Treat tropical sprue with a gluten-free diet" → wrong; gluten-free is for celiac. Tropical sprue responds to antibiotics (tetracycline) plus folate/B12.
7. Pancreatic insufficiency
Pancreatic exocrine insufficiency (chronic pancreatitis, CF, cancer) -> maldigestion of fat -> steatorrhea, weight loss, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency. Note: in pure pancreatic insufficiency the bowel mucosa is normal so amino-acid/D-xylose absorption is intact.
8. Low fecal elastase + enzymes
Diagnose with LOW fecal elastase-1 (and high fecal fat). Treat with pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (lipase with meals) plus a PPI, plus fat-soluble vitamin repletion. Manage the underlying pancreatitis.
9. Steatorrhea
Steatorrhea = bulky, greasy, foul, floating stools from fat malabsorption; quantified by 72h fecal fat or qualitative Sudan stain. Common to pancreatic insufficiency, bile-salt problems, and mucosal disease.
10. Vit A — night blindness
Fat-soluble vitamin A deficiency -> night blindness, xerophthalmia, Bitot spots. Replete vitamin A. Part of the ADEK malabsorption cluster.
11. Vit D — osteoporosis
Vitamin D deficiency -> osteomalacia/osteoporosis, hypocalcemia, bone pain. Replete vitamin D + calcium. Check in any chronic malabsorption.
12. Vit E — ataxia/neuropathy
Vitamin E deficiency -> spinocerebellar ataxia, peripheral neuropathy, hemolysis. A neurologic mimic; replete vitamin E.
13. Vit K — high INR
Vitamin K deficiency -> impaired factors II, VII, IX, X -> elevated INR and bleeding. Correct with vitamin K. A clue that fat-soluble vitamin absorption is impaired.
14. Ileal resection >100 cm
Terminal ileal resection >100 cm causes BILE-SALT depletion (liver can't keep up) -> fat malabsorption and steatorrhea. Manage with a LOW-FAT diet (+ fat-soluble vitamins, B12); here cholestyramine binds the already-scarce bile salts and WORSENS steatorrhea.
⚠️ "Give cholestyramine for >100 cm ileal resection" → wrong; with extensive resection bile-salt pool is depleted, so cholestyramine worsens fat malabsorption. Use a low-fat diet.
15. <100 cm → cholestyramine helps
Limited ileal resection <100 cm causes bile-SALT (choleretic) diarrhea: unabsorbed bile acids irritate the colon, but the bile-salt pool is preserved. Cholestyramine binds the offending bile acids and HELPS. The length cutoff (100 cm) flips management.
16. Low-fat diet (X for <100cm context)
For the SHORT (<100 cm) resection with bile-salt diarrhea, the fix is cholestyramine (not a low-fat diet); the low-fat diet belongs to the >100 cm fat-malabsorption scenario. Matching the right intervention to the resection length is the tested point.
⚠️ "Low-fat diet for short (<100 cm) ileal resection" → wrong; that scenario is bile-salt diarrhea treated with cholestyramine. Low-fat diet is for >100 cm fat malabsorption.
