📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Vegetation on valve
Vegetations are masses of platelets, fibrin, and microorganisms on valve leaflets. They form on a damaged endothelial surface where a sterile platelet-fibrin nidus is seeded during bacteremia.
⚠️ A bland thrombus without organisms is non-bacterial thrombotic (marantic) endocarditis, not infective.
2. Damaged endocardium nidus
Turbulent flow or prior valve damage creates a torn patch; sterile vegetation forms first, then bacteremia seeds it. Explains why abnormal/prosthetic valves are high risk.
⚠️ Normal valves can still be infected by virulent S. aureus — absence of valve disease does not exclude IE.
3. Prolonged therapy clock
Vegetations are avascular, so antibiotics penetrate poorly — IE needs prolonged IV bactericidal therapy (typically 4-6 weeks). High inoculum within fibrin shields organisms.
⚠️ Short oral courses fail in IE — the avascular vegetation demands prolonged IV bactericidal drugs.
4. Oral entry — viridans strep
Oral/dental source seeds viridans group streptococci, the classic cause of subacute IE on previously abnormal native valves. Indolent course.
⚠️ Subacute IE after dental work points to viridans strep, not S. aureus.
5. Skin entry — S. aureus
Skin/IV drug use seeds Staphylococcus aureus — the most common overall cause and the agent of acute IE, including right-sided tricuspid disease in IVDU.
⚠️ Acute IE with rapid valve destruction is S. aureus; a tricuspid lesion in an IVDU is the discriminator.
6. GU tract — enterococcus
GU/GI instrumentation seeds enterococci, especially in older men after urologic procedures.
⚠️ Enterococcal IE after a urologic procedure in an elderly man is the classic GU-source vignette.
7. GI tract — S. bovis & colon CA
Streptococcus gallolyticus (bovis) IE is linked to colonic adenoma/carcinoma — mandates colonoscopy. ~5-15% of cases.
⚠️ S. bovis bacteremia/IE = colonoscopy to find colon cancer; missing the GI workup is the trap.
8. Culture-negative organisms
Culture-negative IE (~5-15%) from prior antibiotics or fastidious organisms — HACEK, Coxiella (Q fever), Bartonella, Chlamydia. Needs serology/PCR.
⚠️ Negative blood cultures do NOT exclude IE — pursue serology for fastidious organisms.
9. Aortic regurgitation
Valve destruction causes new regurgitation; acute aortic regurgitation from IE can precipitate flash pulmonary edema and needs urgent surgery.
⚠️ A new regurgitant murmur with heart failure signals valve destruction/abscess — escalate to surgery, not just antibiotics.
10. AV block — root abscess
Perivalvular extension to an aortic root abscess disrupts conduction, producing new AV block on ECG.
⚠️ New PR prolongation/AV block in IE means perivalvular abscess — an indication for surgery.
11. Septic emboli
Vegetation fragments embolize: left-sided to brain/spleen/kidney, right-sided to lungs (septic pulmonary emboli). Large mobile vegetations raise embolic risk.
⚠️ Septic pulmonary emboli (multiple cavitary nodules) indicate right-sided IE, typical of IVDU.
12. Janeway lesions
Janeway lesions are painless erythematous macules on palms/soles from septic microemboli — a vascular phenomenon.
⚠️ Janeway = painless and septic (palms/soles); do not confuse with painful Osler nodes.
13. Osler nodes
Osler nodes are painful, tender nodules on finger/toe pads from immune-complex deposition — an immunologic phenomenon.
⚠️ Osler = Ouch/painful and immune; Janeway = painless/septic — the classic discriminator pair.
14. Roth spots & emboli organs
Roth spots (retinal hemorrhage with pale center), splinter hemorrhages, glomerulonephritis, splenic infarcts, and mycotic aneurysms reflect emboli plus immune complexes.
⚠️ Hematuria with IE can be immune-complex glomerulonephritis, not just embolic infarct.
15. Pacemaker/lead-related IE
Cardiac device leads can harbor IE; S. aureus and coagulase-negative staph predominate. Often requires complete device/lead extraction.
⚠️ Device-related IE usually needs hardware removal — antibiotics alone fail with retained infected leads.
