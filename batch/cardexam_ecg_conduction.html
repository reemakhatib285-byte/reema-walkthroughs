📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. RBBB tracing (V1)
RBBB produces a WIDE QRS (>=120 ms) with an rSR' pattern in V1 — the classic 'rabbit ears.' The right ventricle depolarizes late because the right bundle is blocked, so the terminal forces point rightward and anteriorly toward V1. Look for the second R wave (R') in the right precordial leads to call it.
⚠️ Don't read the rSR' as just artifact or a normal variant — a wide QRS with rabbit ears in V1 is a true conduction block.
2. Rabbit (rSR' rabbit-ears)
The rabbit anchors the rSR' 'rabbit-ear' morphology of RBBB in V1. Two upright deflections (r and R') separated by the S wave are the visual hallmark. RBBB alone does not block the main left-sided forces, so it rarely hides an MI the way LBBB does.
⚠️ Rabbit ears belong in V1 (right precordial). Expecting them in the lateral leads is wrong — there you instead see a wide, slurred S.
3. Wide slurred S (V6)
In RBBB the late right-ventricular forces travel away from the left-lateral leads, so V6 (and lead I) show a WIDE, slurred S wave. This is the lateral-lead counterpart to the V1 rabbit ears. Together — rSR' in V1 plus broad S in V6 — they confirm right bundle branch block.
⚠️ A slurred S in V6 is RBBB, not LBBB — in LBBB V6 shows a broad notched R wave instead, with no S.
4. LBBB tracing (V6)
LBBB gives a WIDE QRS with a broad, notched (or slurred) R wave in the lateral leads V6, I and aVL, and characteristically NO septal q wave. The septum normally depolarizes left-to-right, producing that little q; LBBB reverses septal activation, so the q disappears. NEW LBBB with chest pain is treated as a STEMI equivalent.
⚠️ Absence of the normal septal q in I/V6 is expected in LBBB — don't mistake it for an old infarct erasing the q wave.
5. Bulldog conductor (LBBB)
The bulldog conductor marks LEFT bundle branch block — the blocked 'conductor' of the left-sided tracks. Because LBBB scrambles ventricular activation, you cannot read ST segments for ischemia normally, which is why new LBBB plus symptoms is managed as a STEMI. Sgarbossa criteria are used to find MI within an LBBB.
⚠️ Don't dismiss a stable-looking LBBB tracing in a patient with chest pain — new LBBB + chest pain = activate the cath lab.
6. NEW LBBB + CHEST PAIN = STEMI banner
This is the urgent rule: a NEW (or presumed-new) LBBB in a patient with ischemic chest pain is a STEMI equivalent and warrants emergent reperfusion. The conduction block masks the ST elevation, so you treat on clinical grounds. The red light and alarm flag the time-critical, do-not-wait nature of the call.
⚠️ Waiting for 'classic' ST elevation before acting is the trap — in new LBBB the elevation is hidden, so delay costs myocardium.
7. LAFB strip — qR in aVL
Left anterior fascicular block (LAFB) shows a qR complex in lead aVL — a small q followed by an R. Activation reaches the high-lateral wall late and superiorly, giving aVL its qR shape. LAFB is a common, often benign cause of marked left axis deviation.
⚠️ A qR in aVL here reflects fascicular block, not lateral infarction — don't over-call an old MI from the small q.
8. LAFB strip — rS in II/III/aVF
In LAFB the inferior leads (II, III, aVF) show an rS pattern because the depolarization wavefront moves up and to the left, away from those inferior leads. This rS-inferior / qR-aVL combination is what produces the LEFT axis deviation of LAFB (axis more negative than -45 degrees).
⚠️ rS in the inferior leads is the LAFB signature — don't read the deep inferior S waves as evidence of inferior ischemia.
9. Yoga dancers — LEFT axis deviation
The dancers leaning up-and-left depict LEFT axis deviation, the defining feature of LAFB. Axis is leftward (beyond -45) because the anterior fascicle is blocked and the wavefront swings superiorly. Lead I is upright while II, III and aVF are negative.
⚠️ Left axis deviation has many causes; pairing it with qR-aVL plus rS-inferior is what specifically points to LAFB rather than inferior MI or LVH.
10. LAFB sign
LAFB = Left Anterior Fascicular Block. Diagnostic triad: left axis deviation, qR in aVL, and rS in II/III/aVF, with normal or only slightly widened QRS. It localizes the lesion to the anterior division of the left bundle, not the whole left bundle.
⚠️ LAFB widens the QRS minimally; if the QRS is clearly wide with lateral notching, think full LBBB instead of a fascicular block.
11. Bodybuilder — LVH (deep S V1, tall R V5-6)
The hypertrophied muscle man is LEFT VENTRICULAR HYPERTROPHY: high QRS voltage — a DEEP S in V1 plus a TALL R in V5-V6 (Sokolow-Lyon and Cornell criteria). The thick left ventricle generates oversized voltages. Causes shown are aortic stenosis and hypertension.
⚠️ Tall voltages alone are just LVH by voltage; don't automatically add 'strain' unless the ST-T changes are present.
12. Strain banner (I, aVL, V5, V6)
LVH WITH STRAIN adds downsloping ST depression and asymmetric T-wave inversion in the lateral leads I, aVL, V5 and V6. It reflects the pressure-overloaded thick ventricle (classically aortic stenosis or hypertension), not acute coronary occlusion. Recognizing strain prevents mislabeling these repolarization changes as ischemia.
⚠️ LVH-strain ST/T changes are repeatedly mistaken for ISCHEMIA — the morphology is strain (asymmetric, lateral, with high voltage), so don't call it an NSTEMI.
13. NOT ISCHEMIA crossed-out signs
These crossed-out labels hammer the key teaching point: the lateral ST depression and T inversion of LVH with strain are NOT ischemia. They are a secondary repolarization effect of the hypertrophied myocardium. Treat the underlying load (AS, HTN), not as an acute coronary syndrome.
⚠️ Stress-testing or cathing a patient purely for LVH-strain ST-T changes is the trap — strain is a chronic, expected pattern.
14. Fat man on train — RVH / cor pulmonale
RIGHT ventricular hypertrophy / cor pulmonale shows RIGHT axis deviation and a dominant tall R wave in V1 (the reverse of normal). The pressure-loaded right ventricle (e.g., from chronic lung disease, the smoking steam train) drags the axis rightward. Often accompanied by RV strain T inversions in V1-V3.
⚠️ A tall R in V1 can be RVH, posterior MI, RBBB or WPW — pair it with right axis deviation and P pulmonale to land on RVH.
15. P pulmonale (tall peaked P in II)
P pulmonale is a tall (>2.5 mm), peaked P wave in lead II signaling RIGHT ATRIAL ENLARGEMENT, the atrial companion of RVH/cor pulmonale. The enlarged right atrium depolarizes first and big, peaking the early part of the P wave. It supports a right-sided pressure-overload picture.
⚠️ Don't confuse P pulmonale (tall, peaked, lead II = right atrium) with P mitrale (wide, notched, M-shaped = left atrium).
