📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Nephrotoxin Factory
Master scene organizing drug/toxin-induced AKI into five mechanisms: (1) hemodynamic (altered glomerular autoregulation), (2) tubular ATN (direct epithelial toxicity), (3) crystal nephropathy (intratubular obstruction), (4) thrombotic microangiopathy (endothelial injury), and (5) dose-limiting/preventable injury (contrast, pigment). Recognizing the mechanism predicts the timing, the urine sediment, and the prevention/treatment strategy on exam questions.
⚠️ Don't lump all drug nephrotoxicity as 'ATN' — the discriminator is mechanism: hemodynamic AKI has a bland sediment and reverses with stopping the drug, ATN shows muddy-brown casts, crystal nephropathy shows crystalluria, and TMA shows schistocytes/thrombocytopenia.
2. Hemodynamic workshop
Hemodynamic AKI results from drugs that impair the kidney's ability to autoregulate glomerular pressure. The 'triple whammy' = NSAID + ACE-I/ARB + diuretic: the NSAID removes afferent vasodilation, the ACE-I/ARB removes efferent vasoconstriction, and the diuretic drops volume — together they collapse glomerular filtration pressure. This is functional (prerenal-type) AKI with a BLAND urine sediment and FENa <1%, and it reverses when the offending drug is stopped.
⚠️ WRONG to call triple-whammy AKI 'ATN' — there is no tubular cell death, the sediment is bland, and creatinine rebounds within days of stopping the drugs. Treating it like ATN (just supportive waiting) misses the immediate fix: hold the NSAID/ACE-I and rehydrate.
3. NSAID afferent
NSAIDs inhibit cyclooxygenase, blocking the vasodilatory prostaglandins (PGE2, PGI2) that dilate the AFFERENT arteriole. In volume-depleted states (CHF, cirrhosis, dehydration, true hypovolemia) renal perfusion is prostaglandin-dependent, so NSAIDs cause afferent constriction → fall in GFR. NSAIDs can also cause acute interstitial nephritis and minimal-change/membranous nephropathy (nephrotic syndrome).
⚠️ The dangerous patient is the dehydrated/CHF/cirrhotic one — in a euvolemic healthy person NSAIDs rarely cause AKI, so the vignette discriminator is an NSAID started on top of volume depletion or diuretics.
4. ACE-I/ARB efferent
ACE-inhibitors/ARBs block angiotensin-II-mediated constriction of the EFFERENT arteriole, lowering intraglomerular pressure (this is renoprotective long-term in CKD/diabetes). But when glomerular filtration depends on efferent constriction — bilateral renal artery stenosis (or stenosis of a solitary kidney) — these drugs cause an abrupt GFR drop. A creatinine rise up to ~30% after starting is acceptable and expected; more than 30% warrants stopping and evaluating for RAS.
⚠️ WRONG to permanently discontinue an ACE-I for a small (<30%) creatinine bump — that rise reflects intended efferent dilation, not nephrotoxicity. But a sharp >30% rise, especially with hyperkalemia, should prompt suspicion of bilateral renal artery stenosis.
5. Tubular ATN workshop
Acute tubular necrosis from direct nephrotoxins injures the proximal tubule (and thick ascending limb) epithelium. Damaged tubules can't reabsorb sodium, so FENa is >2% (>1% with urine Na >20) and urine osmolality is isosthenuric (~300). The pathognomonic sediment is muddy-brown (pigmented) granular casts and renal tubular epithelial cells. Recovery follows the ATN phases: initiation → maintenance (oliguric, the dangerous electrolyte phase) → recovery (polyuric).
⚠️ FENa <1% does NOT exclude ATN if the patient is on diuretics (use FEurea <35% instead) or in early/nonoliguric contrast and pigment nephropathy — these ATN variants can paradoxically show a low FENa.
6. Aminoglycosides 5-7d
Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin) accumulate in proximal tubule cells and cause ATN classically 5–7 days into therapy. It is typically NON-OLIGURIC and accompanied by potassium and magnesium wasting. Risk rises with prolonged courses, high troughs, and concurrent nephrotoxins; once-daily dosing and trough monitoring reduce risk because toxicity is trough-dependent while killing is peak-dependent.
⚠️ WRONG answer is 'oliguric AKI on day 1' — aminoglycoside nephrotoxicity is delayed (5–7 days) and non-oliguric. Day-1 oliguria points elsewhere (contrast, ischemia, obstruction).
7. Vanco / Ampho B
Vancomycin causes dose-dependent ATN, markedly potentiated when combined with aminoglycosides or piperacillin-tazobactam; keep troughs/AUC in range and monitor creatinine. Amphotericin B (especially the deoxycholate form) is intensely nephrotoxic via afferent vasoconstriction plus distal tubular membrane injury, causing potassium and magnesium wasting and a distal (type I) RTA; liposomal formulations and saline preloading reduce toxicity.
⚠️ Don't forget amphotericin causes BOTH hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia AND a distal RTA — and vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam together raise AKI risk more than either alone, a common 'which combination is worst' exam point.
8. Crystal workshop
Crystal nephropathy occurs when drugs/metabolites precipitate inside tubules and cause intrarenal obstruction. Culprits: acyclovir (IV, in dehydration), sulfonamides, indinavir/atazanavir, methotrexate (high-dose), and uric acid/calcium phosphate in tumor lysis syndrome. Urine shows crystals; prevention is aggressive IV hydration to maintain high tubular flow, plus urinary alkalinization for methotrexate and uric acid.
⚠️ Tumor lysis syndrome AKI: the discriminator labs are HIGH uric acid, HIGH potassium, HIGH phosphate, and LOW calcium — treat/prevent with hydration plus rasburicase (not just allopurinol, which only blocks new uric acid and doesn't clear existing load).
9. TMA workshop
Drug-induced thrombotic microangiopathy injures the glomerular/arteriolar endothelium → platelet microthrombi → microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes), thrombocytopenia, and AKI. Key distinguishing point from TTP: ADAMTS13 activity is NORMAL in drug-induced TMA (it is severely deficient, <10%, in TTP). Management is stopping the offending drug; plasma exchange helps TTP, not most drug-induced TMA.
⚠️ WRONG to assume ADAMTS13 is deficient in drug-induced TMA — it is normal. Severe ADAMTS13 deficiency (<10%) defines TTP and is the indication for urgent plasma exchange; checking ADAMTS13 is how you separate the two MAHA syndromes.
10. TMA drugs
Drugs that classically cause TMA: gemcitabine (chemotherapy), VEGF inhibitors (bevacizumab — also cause hypertension and proteinuria via podocyte/endothelial injury), calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine), quinine, and ticlopidine/clopidogrel. VEGF blockade is the mechanistic clue linking anti-angiogenic cancer therapy to hypertension, proteinuria, and TMA.
⚠️ New hypertension + proteinuria + falling platelets in a cancer patient on bevacizumab is drug-induced TMA, not 'just chemo side effects' — recognizing it triggers holding the VEGF inhibitor.
11. Calcineurin inhibitors
Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine) cause AKI by two mechanisms: acute, dose-dependent, reversible AFFERENT arteriolar vasoconstriction (functional/hemodynamic — improves with dose reduction) and chronic, irreversible TMA/interstitial fibrosis with arteriolar hyalinosis. They also cause hypertension, hyperkalemia (type IV RTA), and hypomagnesemia. Levels guide dosing because toxicity is concentration-dependent.
⚠️ In a transplant patient with rising creatinine, the discriminator between CNI toxicity and acute rejection is the drug level (high → toxicity) and often biopsy — empirically increasing immunosuppression for presumed rejection when it's actually CNI toxicity worsens the kidney.
12. Dose-limiting workshop
Preventable/dose-related injuries: contrast-associated nephropathy and pigment nephropathy (rhabdomyolysis-induced myoglobin, hemolysis-induced hemoglobin). Risk is reduced by IV isotonic saline hydration, minimizing contrast dose, and avoiding concurrent nephrotoxins. Contrast nephropathy peaks at 3–5 days and usually recovers; pigment nephropathy needs aggressive volume to flush the tubules.
⚠️ Routine N-acetylcysteine and sodium bicarbonate for contrast prophylaxis are NOT proven superior to IV saline alone (PRESERVE trial) — the right answer for contrast nephropathy prevention is isotonic saline hydration, not NAC.
13. FENa over 1
ATN urine indices: FENa >2% (FEurea >50% if on diuretics), urine Na >40, urine osmolality near serum (~300, isosthenuric), and muddy-brown granular casts. Proximal tubule injury can produce a Fanconi-type picture: glucosuria, phosphaturia, aminoaciduria, and a proximal (type II) RTA. Recovery often passes through a polyuric phase with ongoing electrolyte wasting.
⚠️ FENa <1% suggests PRERENAL azotemia, but several ATN variants (contrast, pigment, sepsis, and any ATN on diuretics) break this rule — confirm with the sediment (muddy-brown casts = ATN) rather than relying on FENa alone.
14. Acute interstitial nephritis
A sixth high-yield drug mechanism worth flagging: acute interstitial nephritis (AIN), an allergic tubulointerstitial reaction to drugs — classically beta-lactams, NSAIDs, sulfonamides, and PROTON PUMP INHIBITORS (an increasingly common cause). The classic triad of fever, rash, and eosinophilia is present in a minority; urine shows WBCs, WBC casts, and sometimes eosinophiluria. Treatment is stopping the drug, with corticosteroids if no rapid recovery.
⚠️ The full triad (fever + rash + eosinophilia) is present in fewer than 10–15% of AIN cases, so its ABSENCE does not exclude AIN — and don't forget PPIs/NSAIDs, the 'silent' causes without a dramatic allergic picture.
