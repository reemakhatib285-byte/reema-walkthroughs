📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Sensitivity ~75%
Exercise ECG has ~75% sensitivity, so a normal result cannot exclude CAD — about a quarter of true disease is missed, especially single-vessel and circumflex territory. Add imaging when clinical suspicion remains high.
⚠️ Ruling out CAD on a negative exercise ECG — ~25% false-negative rate; high suspicion still needs imaging/angiography.
2. Flat/downsloping ≥1mm = positive
A positive exercise ECG is ≥1mm (0.1mV) horizontal or downsloping ST depression measured 0.08s past the J-point. Upsloping depression does not count. This is the objective threshold for an ischemic ECG response.
⚠️ Upsloping ST depression read as positive — only horizontal/downsloping ≥1mm at 80ms past J-point is diagnostic.
3. LCx blind spot → false negative
The standard 12-lead ECG poorly represents the posterolateral wall, so left circumflex-territory ischemia is a classic source of false-negative exercise ECG. A normal ECG response does not exclude LCx disease.
⚠️ Trusting a normal exercise ECG to exclude circumflex disease — the posterolateral blind spot causes false negatives.
4. Male >50 with typical angina ~98% PTP
A man over 50 with typical angina has very high pre-test probability (~90%+). With such high prior probability, a negative stress test cannot meaningfully lower post-test probability — proceed toward angiography rather than stress testing.
⚠️ Stress-testing a high-PTP man with typical angina — a normal test won't reassure; go to coronary angiography.
5. Low PTP → false-positive risk
Stress testing is most useful at intermediate pre-test probability. At low PTP most positives are false; at very high PTP most negatives are false. Pre-test probability, not the raw result, determines how to interpret and act.
⚠️ Applying stress tests across all PTP — they only refine intermediate probability; extremes mislead in both directions.
6. Bruce protocol treadmill
The Bruce protocol is the standard incremental treadmill test; a diagnostic study requires ≥85% of maximum predicted heart rate. Exercise duration and the Duke treadmill score are independently prognostic.
⚠️ Calling a submaximal (<85% MPHR) test negative — inadequate workload yields a non-diagnostic, falsely reassuring result.
7. BP drop >10 mmHg → stop, high risk
A fall in systolic BP >10 mmHg during exercise indicates exertional LV dysfunction from severe multivessel or left main disease — a high-risk sign mandating test termination and prompt angiography.
⚠️ Pushing the test through exertional hypotension — falling systolic BP signals severe ischemia; stop immediately.
8. Alarm bell — ST recovery >5 min
ST depression persisting more than 5 minutes into recovery reflects extensive ischemia and is a high-risk finding that should trigger angiography. Prolonged recovery time tracks with more severe coronary anatomy.
⚠️ Treating slow ST recovery as benign — depression lasting >5 min into recovery is a high-risk prognostic marker.
9. Stage II incomplete = high risk
Inability to complete even Bruce stage II (low workload) indicates poor functional capacity and is associated with high risk and adverse prognosis. Low exercise tolerance is itself a powerful predictor of cardiac events.
⚠️ Reassuring a patient who fails early-stage exercise — poor functional capacity (failing stage II) is high risk, not deconditioning alone.
10. Pulmonary uptake → worst prognosis
Increased lung uptake of perfusion tracer reflects stress-induced LV dysfunction and elevated filling pressures — the single most dangerous prognostic marker, indicating extensive ischemia and high event risk.
⚠️ Focusing only on defect size — pulmonary tracer uptake is the strongest high-risk prognostic flag on perfusion imaging.
11. Stage III + normal scan = very low risk
Achieving Bruce stage III with a normal perfusion scan and good exercise capacity confers very low risk and excellent prognosis — no further testing or revascularization required. High functional capacity is strongly protective.
⚠️ Ordering angiography after a high-workload normal scan — excellent exercise tolerance with normal imaging is reassuringly low risk.
12. Cannot exercise → pharmacologic stress
Patients who cannot exercise adequately need pharmacologic stress: vasodilators (adenosine/regadenoson/dipyridamole) with perfusion imaging, or dobutamine for stress echo. Exercise is preferred when possible because capacity adds prognostic data.
⚠️ Defaulting to exercise testing in patients who cannot walk adequately — submaximal effort is non-diagnostic; use pharmacologic stress.
