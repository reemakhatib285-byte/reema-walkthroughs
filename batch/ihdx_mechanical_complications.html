📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Bimodal timing — 24h & 3-5 days
Mechanical complications (VSR, free-wall rupture, papillary muscle rupture) peak bimodally: within 24h (early) and at 3-5 days (when necrotic tissue is weakest before scar forms). Reperfusion has reduced overall incidence.
⚠️ Day 3-5 is the classic 'rupture' window — sudden deterioration days after MI should trigger an echo for mechanical complication, not just re-MI.
2. VSR — ventricular septal rupture
Ventricular septal rupture creates a left-to-right shunt → harsh holosystolic murmur, often with a thrill, and an oxygen step-up from RA to RV. More common in anterior MI (LAD). Treat with surgical repair (± temporary mechanical support).
⚠️ VSR shows an O2 step-up RA→RV and a thrill; papillary muscle rupture does NOT step up and gives acute pulmonary edema with a softer murmur.
3. O2 step-up RA→RV / thrill
The hallmark of VSR is an oxygen saturation step-up between right atrium and right ventricle on right-heart catheterization, plus a palpable thrill. This distinguishes it from acute mitral regurgitation.
⚠️ O2 step-up = VSR. Acute MR from papillary rupture has NO step-up — use this to separate the two on the boards.
4. Free wall rupture — pericardium/Beck's
Free-wall rupture causes acute hemopericardium → tamponade with Beck's triad (hypotension, distended neck veins, muffled heart sounds), often pulseless electrical activity and sudden death. Emergent pericardiocentesis + surgery.
⚠️ Sudden PEA arrest or shock with tamponade physiology days after MI = free-wall rupture; it kills fast and needs the OR, not just pericardiocentesis.
5. BP cuff / 60 mmHg — tamponade hypotension
Free-wall rupture produces tamponade with profound hypotension; unlike VSR there is no intracardiac shunt or O2 step-up. The presentation is obstructive shock/sudden collapse.
⚠️ No O2 step-up here — that finding belongs to VSR. Free-wall rupture = tamponade, not a shunt.
6. Papillary muscle rupture — acute MR
Papillary muscle rupture causes acute severe mitral regurgitation → flash pulmonary edema (wet crackles), new soft systolic murmur, large V-waves and elevated PCWP. Posteromedial papillary muscle (single LAD or RCA supply) is most vulnerable.
⚠️ Posteromedial papillary muscle ruptures more often (single blood supply) — inferior MI is a classic setup, not anterior.
7. Giant V-wave / elevated PCWP
Acute MR generates tall regurgitant V-waves on the PCWP tracing and markedly elevated wedge pressure with flash pulmonary edema. Urgent surgical valve repair/replacement is required.
⚠️ Tall V-waves on PCWP point to acute MR (papillary rupture); they do not occur in VSR, which instead shows an O2 step-up.
8. Killip classification ladder
Killip class stratifies acute-MI mortality by exam: I = no failure, II = rales/S3, III = pulmonary edema, IV = cardiogenic shock. Higher class predicts steeply higher mortality.
⚠️ Killip is a clinical bedside score (rales, edema, shock) — don't confuse it with hemodynamic Forrester classes from PCWP/cardiac index.
9. Killip IV — cardiogenic shock 60-95%
Killip IV (cardiogenic shock) carries the highest mortality (historically 60-95%). Management: urgent revascularization, vasopressors/inotropes, and mechanical circulatory support.
⚠️ Cardiogenic shock is Killip IV with the worst prognosis — beta-blockers are contraindicated in this class.
10. RV infarction — clear lungs, no rales
RV infarction (inferior STEMI) gives hypotension with CLEAR lungs and elevated JVP; treat with IV fluids and avoid nitrates/diuretics that drop preload. ECG: ST elevation in V4R.
⚠️ RV infarct = hypotension + clear lungs + raised JVP → give fluids; nitrates and diuretics are harmful here.
11. Pericarditis corner — ASA, not steroids
Post-MI pericarditis (early peri-infarction or late Dressler) is treated with high-dose aspirin; NSAIDs and corticosteroids are avoided early because they impair myocardial healing and promote rupture/aneurysm.
⚠️ Use ASA for post-MI pericarditis — NSAIDs/steroids early can impair infarct healing and increase rupture/aneurysm risk.
12. True vs false aneurysm comparison
True aneurysm = wide-mouthed, full-thickness, low rupture risk (causes HF, arrhythmia, mural thrombus). Pseudoaneurysm = contained rupture, narrow neck, HIGH rupture risk → needs surgery.
⚠️ Narrow-necked pseudoaneurysm is a contained rupture at high risk of bursting — operate; a wide-mouthed true aneurysm is far more stable.
13. AIVR exhibit — accelerated idioventricular rhythm
Accelerated idioventricular rhythm (wide complexes, rate 60-100) is a benign reperfusion marker after successful thrombolysis/PCI. It is usually transient and needs no treatment.
⚠️ AIVR is benign and is NOT treated/suppressed — it signals successful reperfusion, not a dangerous VT.
