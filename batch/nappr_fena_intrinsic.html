📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. FeNa <1% = prerenal
FeNa = (UNa x PCr)/(PNa x UCr) x 100. <1% means avid tubular Na reabsorption from a healthy tubule responding to hypoperfusion = prerenal azotemia. >2% implies tubular injury (ATN). Validated only in oliguric AKI.
⚠️ Calling every low FeNa 'prerenal' — contrast nephropathy and early sepsis can give FeNa <1% from preserved tubular Na avidity despite intrinsic insult.
2. Early/intact tubular Na reabsorption
Prerenal physiology: ↓effective arterial volume → RAAS + ADH → maximal proximal and distal Na/water reabsorption. Hence low UNa (<20), high urine osm (>500), high BUN/Cr ratio (>20:1). The tubule itself is structurally intact.
⚠️ Confusing low UNa with low FeNa after diuretics — diuretics raise UNa/FeNa; use FeUrea instead.
3. Contrast can give <1%
Contrast-induced nephropathy classically produces a LOW FeNa (<1%) despite being intrinsic, because intense renal vasoconstriction mimics prerenal Na avidity. Cr peaks at 3–5 days. This is a key exception that breaks the FeNa rule.
⚠️ Picking ATN/high FeNa for contrast nephropathy — the discriminator is the low FeNa plus the 24–48h onset, 3–5 day peak timeline.
4. Rhabdo / myoglobin cast <1%
Pigment nephropathy (rhabdomyolysis) is another intrinsic cause that can show FeNa <1% early because vasoconstriction and proximal Na avidity dominate before frank ATN. Look for CK >5000, dipstick blood without RBCs, ↑K, ↑PO4, ↓Ca.
⚠️ Trusting low FeNa to exclude tubular injury here — muddy brown / pigmented casts plus dipstick-blood-negative-RBC is the real discriminator.
5. Sepsis early <1%
Early sepsis can give FeNa <1% (prerenal phase from vasodilation/hypovolemia) before evolving into septic ATN with FeNa >2% and muddy brown casts. Timing matters: the same patient migrates across categories.
⚠️ Anchoring on a single FeNa value — serial FeNa plus sediment is needed; a rising FeNa with muddy casts signals transition to ATN.
6. FeUrea when on diuretics
FeUrea = (Uurea x PCr)/(BUN x UCr) x 100. Use when patient is on loop/thiazide diuretics that artificially raise FeNa. FeUrea <35% = prerenal; urea reabsorption is less diuretic-dependent than Na.
⚠️ Reporting FeNa >1% as 'ATN' in a diuretic-treated patient — FeUrea <35% reveals the true prerenal state.
7. CKD baseline raises FeNa
Chronic kidney disease patients have a chronically elevated baseline FeNa (each nephron excretes more Na) so FeNa loses prerenal specificity. Interpret cautiously and lean on clinical volume status and sediment.
⚠️ Misreading an elevated FeNa in CKD as acute tubular necrosis — the elevation is chronic and expected.
8. Pigment nephropathy: heme+, no RBCs
Myoglobin and hemoglobin make the urine dipstick positive for blood while microscopy shows NO red cells. This dissociation is pathognomonic for pigment nephropathy (rhabdo or hemolysis) causing tubular injury.
⚠️ Diagnosing glomerulonephritis on a positive dipstick — absence of RBCs on micro rules out hematuria and points to myoglobin/hemoglobin.
9. Hematuria needs RBCs (not pigment)
True hematuria requires intact RBCs on microscopy. Dysmorphic RBCs/RBC casts = glomerular; isomorphic = post-renal/urologic. Pigmenturia (heme+, RBC-negative) is explicitly NOT hematuria.
⚠️ Working up 'hematuria' urologically when micro shows no RBCs — that's pigmenturia, work up rhabdo/hemolysis instead.
10. S3 proximal tubule = most vulnerable
The S3 segment (outer medulla) and medullary thick ascending limb sit in a low-O2 watershed, making them the first to die in ischemic/toxic ATN. 'Broken pipe' = sloughed brush border → muddy brown granular casts.
⚠️ Locating ischemic ATN damage in the cortex/glomerulus — the outer medullary S3 segment is the lesion site.
11. Muddy brown casts → ATN
Pigmented (muddy brown) granular casts and renal tubular epithelial cells are the sediment signature of ATN. Their presence shifts the diagnosis from prerenal to intrinsic regardless of a low FeNa.
⚠️ Calling muddy brown casts 'normal granular casts' — they specifically indicate tubular epithelial necrosis (ATN).
12. WBC casts → AIN
WBC casts (especially with eosinophiluria, fever, rash, recent drug like PPI/NSAID/beta-lactam) indicate acute interstitial nephritis. Sterile pyuria with WBC casts and no bacteria supports AIN over UTI.
⚠️ Attributing WBC casts to pyelonephritis only — drug-induced AIN gives WBC casts with eosinophils and a culprit medication.
13. RBC casts → GN / vasculitis
RBC casts and dysmorphic RBCs are diagnostic of glomerulonephritis / vasculitis. Pair with proteinuria, hypertension, ↓C3/C4 or positive ANCA to localize the glomerular lesion.
⚠️ Dismissing RBC casts as contamination — they are a glomerular red flag mandating serologies and often biopsy.
14. Hyaline casts → prerenal/benign
Hyaline casts are acellular Tamm-Horsfall protein, seen in concentrated urine, volume depletion, exercise, or diuretics. They support a prerenal/benign picture and are not specific for injury.
⚠️ Reading bland hyaline casts as evidence of intrinsic disease — they actually favor prerenal azotemia with intact tubules.
