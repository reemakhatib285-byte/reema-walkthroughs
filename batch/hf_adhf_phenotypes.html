⚠️ Board traps 13
  1. Typical ADHF — bed 1 — WRONG answer: 'give high-dose IV furosemide as the primary treatment for a hypertensive flash-pulmonary-edema patient.' Discriminator: in hypertensive/vascular ADHF the problem is afterload-driven fluid shift, so aggressive diuresis of a near-euvolemic patient causes hypotension and AKI — the correct first move is a vasodilator (nitrates ± NIV).
  2. Normotensive typical — WRONG answer: 'treat every normotensive ADHF patient like flash edema with vasodilators only and minimal diuresis.' Discriminator: normotensive gradual-onset ADHF reflects true total-body volume overload — diuresis to euvolemia is the priority, unlike the redistribution physiology of hypertensive ADHF.
  3. Pulmonary edema phenotype — WRONG answer: 'intubate immediately and start broad-spectrum antibiotics for the bilateral infiltrates.' Discriminator: bilateral symmetric perihilar edema with elevated JVP and S3 is cardiogenic — NIV plus nitrates usually averts intubation, and antibiotics treat pneumonia (asymmetric, fever, leukocytosis), not flash edema.
  4. Opiates / NIV — WRONG answer: 'give IV morphine liberally to calm the dyspneic flash-edema patient.' Discriminator: routine morphine in ADHF is associated with respiratory depression, more intubations, and worse outcomes — NIV and nitrates are the evidence-based preload-reducing tools.
  5. Low output phenotype — WRONG answer: 'the cool extremities and low urine output mean the patient is dehydrated — give a fluid bolus.' Discriminator: in cold-wet ADHF hypoperfusion is from pump failure WITH congestion, not hypovolemia — fluids worsen pulmonary edema; the patient needs decongestion ± inotropic support, not volume.
  6. Inotropes if low BP / diuretic-refractory — WRONG answer: 'add an inotrope to any ADHF patient who is still congested.' Discriminator: inotropes are only for low-output/hypoperfusion or diuretic-refractory states — in a warm-wet patient with adequate BP they add arrhythmia and mortality risk with no decongestion benefit (OPTIME-CHF showed harm with routine milrinone).
  7. Cardiogenic shock — bed 4 — WRONG answer: 'in MI-related cardiogenic shock, perform immediate complete multivessel revascularization of all stenoses.' Discriminator: CULPRIT-SHOCK showed culprit-lesion-only PCI lowered 30-day death/renal failure vs immediate multivessel PCI — stage non-culprit lesions later.
  8. Inotropes/pressors + MCS — WRONG answer: 'start dopamine as the first-line vasopressor and place a routine IABP to improve survival in MI cardiogenic shock.' Discriminator: norepinephrine beats dopamine (SOAP-II) and routine IABP gave no mortality benefit (IABP-SHOCK II) — MCS is selective, not reflexive.
  9. Precipitants — always ask — WRONG answer: 'just treat the congestion with diuretics and discharge — the trigger does not matter.' Discriminator: missing AF with RVR, ACS, or a new severe valve lesion leads to early readmission or death; identifying/correcting the precipitant is a board-mandated step in ADHF workup.
  10. BUN > 43 — WRONG answer: 'an elevated BUN in ADHF is just dehydration and is prognostically meaningless.' Discriminator: BUN >43 is the top node of the ADHERE mortality tree — it is a high-risk marker reflecting neurohormonal activation/hypoperfusion, not a benign lab.
  11. SBP < 115 — WRONG answer: 'a low SBP on ADHF admission is reassuring because the patient is not hypertensive.' Discriminator: low admission SBP marks low cardiac output and predicts worse outcomes; the hypertensive (high-SBP) phenotype carries the better prognosis.
  12. Cr > 2.75 / rising troponin — WRONG answer: 'a detectable troponin in an ADHF patient without chest pain is always a STEMI requiring emergent cath.' Discriminator: low-level troponin elevation is common in ADHF from demand ischemia/wall stress and is prognostic — distinguish a flat low-level elevation from a dynamic rise-and-fall pattern that signals true type 1 MI.
  13. Swan-Ganz — selective only — WRONG answer: 'place a PA catheter routinely in all ADHF admissions to optimize fluid management and improve outcomes.' Discriminator: ESCAPE proved routine PA catheterization gives no outcome benefit and adds harm — it is a selective tool for shock or refractory/uncertain hemodynamics only.
📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Typical ADHF — bed 1
Hypertensive ('vascular') ADHF is the most common ER presentation: rapid-onset 'flash' pulmonary edema driven by an abrupt rise in afterload and fluid REDISTRIBUTION into the lungs, NOT whole-body fluid accumulation — these patients are often warm-wet but near euvolemic in total body water. SBP is typically >140. Cornerstone therapy is IV vasodilators (nitroglycerin starting ~5–10 mcg/min titrated up, or nitroprusside) to drop preload/afterload; diuretics are secondary because there is little extra total-body volume to remove. Mnemonic: 'high pressure, redistribute not deplete.'
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'give high-dose IV furosemide as the primary treatment for a hypertensive flash-pulmonary-edema patient.' Discriminator: in hypertensive/vascular ADHF the problem is afterload-driven fluid shift, so aggressive diuresis of a near-euvolemic patient causes hypotension and AKI — the correct first move is a vasodilator (nitrates ± NIV).
2. Normotensive typical
Normotensive ADHF (SBP ~ normal) is the classic gradual 'cardiac' decompensation: days-to-weeks of progressive sodium/water retention with rising weight, edema, and elevated JVP — these patients ARE truly volume overloaded. IV loop diuretic is the cornerstone; per DOSE trial give an IV dose ~2.5× the chronic oral loop dose and target a falling JVP/weight. Distinguish from hypertensive ADHF where total body water is near normal.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'treat every normotensive ADHF patient like flash edema with vasodilators only and minimal diuresis.' Discriminator: normotensive gradual-onset ADHF reflects true total-body volume overload — diuresis to euvolemia is the priority, unlike the redistribution physiology of hypertensive ADHF.
3. Pulmonary edema phenotype
Acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema = severe respiratory distress with hypoxia, diffuse crackles, and bilateral 'bat-wing' infiltrates on CXR. Management bundle (mnemonic LMNOP-ish): sit Upright, high-flow O2/Noninvasive ventilation (CPAP/BiPAP), IV Nitrates for preload reduction, and diuretics if volume overloaded; morphine only cautiously. Target SpO2 ≥90%. NIV reduces intubation rates and work of breathing.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'intubate immediately and start broad-spectrum antibiotics for the bilateral infiltrates.' Discriminator: bilateral symmetric perihilar edema with elevated JVP and S3 is cardiogenic — NIV plus nitrates usually averts intubation, and antibiotics treat pneumonia (asymmetric, fever, leukocytosis), not flash edema.
4. Opiates / NIV
NIV (CPAP/BiPAP) is first-line adjunct in cardiogenic pulmonary edema: positive pressure recruits alveoli, lowers preload AND afterload, and reduces work of breathing and intubation rate. Opiates (low-dose IV morphine) modestly reduce preload and anxiety but are now DISCOURAGED for routine use — registry data link morphine to more ICU admissions, mechanical ventilation, and possibly higher mortality.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'give IV morphine liberally to calm the dyspneic flash-edema patient.' Discriminator: routine morphine in ADHF is associated with respiratory depression, more intubations, and worse outcomes — NIV and nitrates are the evidence-based preload-reducing tools.
5. Low output phenotype
Low-output ('cold') ADHF = inadequate cardiac output with hypoperfusion: cool/clammy extremities, NARROW pulse pressure (proportional pulse pressure <25%), altered mentation, rising creatinine, and congested liver. This is the 'cold-wet' Stevenson quadrant (low perfusion + congestion). Confirm hemodynamics; in unclear cases or shock a PA catheter can guide. Cardiorenal and cardiohepatic dysfunction are markers of severity.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'the cool extremities and low urine output mean the patient is dehydrated — give a fluid bolus.' Discriminator: in cold-wet ADHF hypoperfusion is from pump failure WITH congestion, not hypovolemia — fluids worsen pulmonary edema; the patient needs decongestion ± inotropic support, not volume.
6. Inotropes if low BP / diuretic-refractory
Inotropes (dobutamine ~2–10 mcg/kg/min, or milrinone 0.125–0.75 mcg/kg/min) are reserved for the cold/low-output patient with hypotension causing end-organ hypoperfusion OR genuine diuretic refractoriness from poor renal perfusion. They improve contractility and forward flow as a bridge, but increase myocardial O2 demand and arrhythmias and do NOT improve survival. Milrinone is renally cleared and a pulmonary vasodilator (good for RV failure/pulmonary HTN); avoid milrinone in profound hypotension.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'add an inotrope to any ADHF patient who is still congested.' Discriminator: inotropes are only for low-output/hypoperfusion or diuretic-refractory states — in a warm-wet patient with adequate BP they add arrhythmia and mortality risk with no decongestion benefit (OPTIME-CHF showed harm with routine milrinone).
7. Cardiogenic shock — bed 4
Cardiogenic shock = sustained hypotension (SBP <90 for >30 min or pressors needed) PLUS end-organ hypoperfusion (lactate >2, oliguria, cold mottled skin, altered mentation) with cardiac index typically <2.2 L/min/m² and PCWP >15. Mortality ~40–50%. Find and fix the cause — ACS is #1, so emergent revascularization (PCI) per the CULPRIT-SHOCK trial: revascularize the culprit lesion ONLY, not all vessels.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'in MI-related cardiogenic shock, perform immediate complete multivessel revascularization of all stenoses.' Discriminator: CULPRIT-SHOCK showed culprit-lesion-only PCI lowered 30-day death/renal failure vs immediate multivessel PCI — stage non-culprit lesions later.
8. Inotropes/pressors + MCS
Cardiogenic shock support: vasopressor of choice is NOREPINEPHRINE (SOAP-II — dopamine caused more arrhythmias and trended to higher mortality), plus an inotrope (dobutamine) for contractility. Add mechanical circulatory support — IABP, Impella (pVAD), or VA-ECMO — for refractory shock as a bridge to recovery/transplant. Note: the IABP-SHOCK II trial showed routine IABP did NOT reduce mortality in MI shock.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'start dopamine as the first-line vasopressor and place a routine IABP to improve survival in MI cardiogenic shock.' Discriminator: norepinephrine beats dopamine (SOAP-II) and routine IABP gave no mortality benefit (IABP-SHOCK II) — MCS is selective, not reflexive.
9. Precipitants — always ask
Every ADHF admission demands a hunt for the precipitant because treating it changes outcomes. Mnemonic FAILURE / 'CHAMP': Forgot meds/Faild compliance, Arrhythmia (esp new AF with RVR), Ischemia/ACS, Lifestyle (dietary salt/fluid, NSAIDs, alcohol), Upregulation (thyrotoxicosis, pregnancy, anemia), Rheumatic/valve disease, Embolism (PE), plus infection/sepsis. CHAMP = Coronary, Hypertension emergency, Arrhythmia, acute Mechanical (papillary rupture, VSD), PE.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'just treat the congestion with diuretics and discharge — the trigger does not matter.' Discriminator: missing AF with RVR, ACS, or a new severe valve lesion leads to early readmission or death; identifying/correcting the precipitant is a board-mandated step in ADHF workup.
10. BUN > 43
BUN >43 mg/dL is the single STRONGEST predictor of in-hospital mortality in the ADHERE registry risk tree — it reflects both renal hypoperfusion and intense neurohormonal (RAAS/ADH) activation. The ADHERE decision tree splits first on BUN ≥43, then SBP <115, then creatinine ≥2.75; the highest-risk branch (all three) carries ~22% in-hospital mortality vs ~2% for none.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'an elevated BUN in ADHF is just dehydration and is prognostically meaningless.' Discriminator: BUN >43 is the top node of the ADHERE mortality tree — it is a high-risk marker reflecting neurohormonal activation/hypoperfusion, not a benign lab.
11. SBP < 115
Admission SBP <115 mmHg is the SECOND node of the ADHERE risk tree and a powerful adverse prognostic marker — LOW blood pressure in ADHF signals a low-output, poorly perfusing state, the opposite of the better-prognosis hypertensive phenotype. Higher admission SBP actually correlates with LOWER mortality in ADHF.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'a low SBP on ADHF admission is reassuring because the patient is not hypertensive.' Discriminator: low admission SBP marks low cardiac output and predicts worse outcomes; the hypertensive (high-SBP) phenotype carries the better prognosis.
12. Cr > 2.75 / rising troponin
Creatinine ≥2.75 mg/dL is the third splitting node of the ADHERE tree (cardiorenal injury). A detectable/rising troponin in ADHF — even without overt ACS — independently predicts higher mortality (ongoing myocyte injury), and a rising BNP/NT-proBNP trend tracks worsening wall stress. Together these mark the highest-risk patients.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'a detectable troponin in an ADHF patient without chest pain is always a STEMI requiring emergent cath.' Discriminator: low-level troponin elevation is common in ADHF from demand ischemia/wall stress and is prognostic — distinguish a flat low-level elevation from a dynamic rise-and-fall pattern that signals true type 1 MI.
13. Swan-Ganz — selective only
The pulmonary artery (Swan-Ganz) catheter is NOT used routinely: the ESCAPE trial showed no improvement in mortality or days alive out of hospital, with more catheter-related adverse events. Reserve it for selected cases — cardiogenic shock with unclear hemodynamics, diuretic-refractory congestion, suspected mixed/discordant shock, or evaluation before transplant/MCS — where it guides tailored therapy (measures PCWP, CO, SVR).
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'place a PA catheter routinely in all ADHF admissions to optimize fluid management and improve outcomes.' Discriminator: ESCAPE proved routine PA catheterization gives no outcome benefit and adds harm — it is a selective tool for shock or refractory/uncertain hemodynamics only.
