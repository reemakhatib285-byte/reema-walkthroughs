📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. GPIb-alpha + Von Willebrand Factor (adhesion)
STEP 1 ADHESION: after vessel injury, subendothelial collagen exposes VWF, which bridges GPIb-alpha on the resting platelet to the wall under high shear. This is the very first tethering step. Her note 'no drug here' flags that no routine antiplatelet acts at GPIb/VWF adhesion.
⚠️ Board may claim aspirin or clopidogrel block initial adhesion — wrong. Adhesion is GPIb-VWF (and GPVI/alpha2beta1 to collagen); standard antiplatelets act downstream at TXA2, P2Y12, or GPIIb/IIIa, not at the VWF tether.
2. GPVI & alpha2beta1 (collagen receptors)
Collagen binds directly via GPVI (signaling) and alpha2beta1 (firm anchorage), reinforcing adhesion and triggering activation. GPVI is the key collagen signaling receptor that helps shape-change begin.
⚠️ Don't confuse alpha2beta1 (collagen, integrin) with alphaIIbbeta3 = GPIIb/IIIa (fibrinogen, final aggregation). Tirofiban/eptifibatide/abciximab block GPIIb/IIIa, NOT the collagen receptors.
3. ADP granule release
STEP 2 ACTIVATION: activated platelets degranulate and release ADP from dense granules, which feeds the P2Y12 amplification loop. ADP is the autocrine/paracrine signal that recruits and activates more platelets.
⚠️ ADP acts on P2Y12 (and P2Y1) — clopidogrel/prasugrel/ticagrelor target P2Y12. A trap is attributing ADP signaling to the TXA2/COX pathway (that's aspirin's lane).
4. TXA2 + COX-1 (the grill)
STEP 2: activation triggers COX-1 to convert arachidonic acid to thromboxane A2 (TXA2), a potent secreted agonist that amplifies aggregation and causes vasoconstriction. This is exactly aspirin's target.
⚠️ Aspirin IRREVERSIBLY acetylates COX-1, so the effect lasts the platelet lifespan (~7-10 days) because platelets have no nucleus to resynthesize COX-1. NSAIDs (ibuprofen) reversibly block COX and can competitively block aspirin's access.
5. P2Y12 gate (amplification)
STEP 3 AMPLIFICATION: ADP acting on the P2Y12 receptor sustains activation, stabilizes the aggregate, and recruits additional platelets. P2Y12 is the central amplification node for thienopyridines and ticagrelor.
⚠️ Clopidogrel and prasugrel are irreversible prodrugs needing CYP activation; ticagrelor is a reversible, direct (non-prodrug) P2Y12 blocker. Mixing up reversibility/prodrug status is the classic discriminator.
6. PAR-1 / thrombin (THROM-BEAVER)
STEP 3: thrombin (from the TF/coagulation cascade) is the most potent platelet agonist, acting through PAR-1 to amplify aggregation and recruit platelets. Vorapaxar is the PAR-1 antagonist drawn here.
⚠️ Vorapaxar (PAR-1 blocker) is contraindicated with any history of stroke/TIA or ICH because of intracranial bleeding risk — a frequent board contraindication trap.
7. GPIIb/IIIa opens (inside-out signaling)
STEP 4: all agonist pathways converge to activate GPIIb/IIIa (integrin alphaIIbbeta3) through inside-out signaling — a conformational change exposing the fibrinogen binding site. This is the FINAL COMMON PATHWAY of aggregation.
⚠️ GPIIb/IIIa is the common final pathway — abciximab/eptifibatide/tirofiban block it. Board trap: confusing 'inside-out' activation with adhesion; this step is aggregation (platelet-platelet) not wall adhesion.
8. GPIIb/IIIa cross-linking by fibrinogen
Active GPIIb/IIIa binds fibrinogen (and VWF) to cross-link adjacent platelets — the actual aggregation event building the platelet plug. Final common pathway: every agonist ends here.
⚠️ Fibrinogen (not fibrin) is the bridging ligand for GPIIb/IIIa aggregation. The fibrin mesh comes later (step 6, thrombin-driven). Don't swap fibrinogen-bridging with fibrin-stabilization.
9. STEP 6 Stabilization — fibrin thrombus
STEP 6 STABILIZATION: thrombin converts fibrinogen to fibrin strands that weave through the aggregate, yielding a stable white (platelet-rich) thrombus. This is the FINAL COMMON PATHWAY shown twice.
⚠️ White (platelet-rich, arterial) thrombus is treated with antiplatelets; red (fibrin/RBC, venous/stasis) thrombus is treated with anticoagulants. Her annotation contrasts 'white artery / antiPLT' vs 'red vein / fibrin + RBC / anticoag' — a key board discriminator.
10. Prostacyclin (PGI2) -> cAMP
Healthy endothelium releases prostacyclin (PGI2), raising platelet cAMP to inhibit activation — a natural antiaggregatory brake. High-dose aspirin can blunt endothelial PGI2 (the pro-aggregatory shift), which is why low dose is preferred.
⚠️ Low-dose aspirin spares endothelial PGI2 (favoring antiplatelet effect); high-dose (~1g/day) also blocks endothelial COX-derived PGI2 = pro-aggregatory shift. 'More aspirin is better' is the trap.
11. Nitric oxide (NO) -> cGMP
Endothelial nitric oxide raises platelet cGMP, inhibiting activation and causing vasodilation — a second natural antiplatelet defense lost after vascular injury (her note: 'all lost after vascular injury').
⚠️ NO/cGMP and PGI2/cAMP are the two endothelial brakes — both are LOST at injured endothelium, which is why thrombosis proceeds there. Don't attribute platelet inhibition here to a drug; these are endogenous.
12. CD39 / ADPase
Endothelial CD39 (an ADPase) degrades extracellular ADP, removing the P2Y12 agonist and damping platelet recruitment. Another endogenous antiplatelet defense of intact endothelium.
⚠️ CD39/ADPase clears ADP — it is endogenous, not a drug. Board trap is calling this clopidogrel's mechanism; clopidogrel blocks the P2Y12 RECEPTOR, it does not enzymatically degrade ADP.
13. Irreversible vs reversible (platelet lifespan)
Because platelets lack a nucleus, irreversible drugs (aspirin on COX-1; clopidogrel/prasugrel on P2Y12) inhibit for the platelet lifespan (~7-10 days). Recovery requires new platelet production, hence the 5-7 day pre-surgery hold.
⚠️ Ticagrelor is REVERSIBLE — its effect wanes with drug half-life, but it is still held ~5 days pre-op. The trap is assuming all P2Y12 inhibitors are irreversible or have identical washout.
