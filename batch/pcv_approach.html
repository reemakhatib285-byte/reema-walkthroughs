⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. Erythrocytosis detected — A WRONG move is to start phlebotomy or order a JAK2/EPO panel off one mildly elevated Hct. The discriminator: repeat the CBC and assess plasma volume first — transient dehydration or lab error mimics erythrocytosis, and treating it commits the patient to an unnecessary MPN workup.
  2. Plasma volume / RCM — The trap is assuming every high Hct means more red cells. WRONG — if plasma volume is contracted (the small plasma bag), red cell mass can be normal. Discriminator: a normal calculated/measured RCM with low plasma volume = relative polycythemia, which needs hydration and med review, not a hematologic workup.
  3. Relative (Gaisböck) — A classic boards stem: hypertensive overweight male smoker with mildly high Hct — the WRONG answer is polycythemia vera with phlebotomy. The discriminator is a NORMAL red cell mass and absence of splenomegaly/pruritus/JAK2; this is relative polycythemia, treated with lifestyle and addressing diuretics, not bloodletting.
  4. Gate 1: need RCM — WRONG to demand a chromium-51 RCM study in a patient with Hct 65% before acting — at extreme values RCM is presumed elevated. Discriminator: reserve formal RCM for borderline counts; chasing it at extreme values delays diagnosis and the isotope test is rarely available.
  5. JAK2 detector >95% — The trap: a positive JAK2 V617F is NOT specific for PV — it also appears in ~50–60% of ET and ~50% of primary myelofibrosis. So JAK2+ alone does not equal PV. Discriminator: combine JAK2 with a SUPPRESSED EPO and red-cell predominance; JAK2+ with high EPO and isolated thrombocytosis points elsewhere.
  6. EPO level meter — High EPO does NOT mean PV. WRONG answer — students invert the logic. In PV the marrow makes red cells on its own and feedback SUPPRESSES EPO (low). High/normal EPO points to a SECONDARY cause (hypoxia, tumor, doping). Discriminator: low EPO + JAK2+ = PV; high EPO = go hunting for hypoxia or an EPO source.
  7. Low EPO → PV — WRONG to call any erythrocytosis with low EPO 'PV' without confirming JAK2 — congenital high-O2-affinity hemoglobinopathies and EPO-receptor mutations can also have low EPO. Discriminator: PV needs the JAK2 mutation (or exon 12); JAK2-negative low-EPO erythrocytosis in a young patient suggests a congenital cause.
  8. Secondary / exclude PV — The trap is treating secondary polycythemia like PV with aggressive phlebotomy. WRONG — in hypoxia-driven secondary polycythemia the high Hct is compensatory; aggressive phlebotomy can worsen tissue oxygen delivery. Discriminator: fix the underlying cause (oxygen, CPAP, resect tumor); phlebotomy is reserved for symptomatic hyperviscosity.
  9. Hypoxia — A boards trap: a heavy smoker with high Hct and a NORMAL pulse-ox SaO2 — calling it PV is WRONG. Pulse oximetry cannot detect carboxyhemoglobin; the discriminator is an elevated carboxyhemoglobin level (co-oximetry), revealing smoker's polycythemia, which resolves with cessation.
  10. EPO-secreting tumors — WRONG to stop at 'secondary polycythemia' once EPO is high without imaging for a tumor. Discriminator: a non-smoker with high EPO, no hypoxia, and a flank mass or new headache needs CT abdomen/MRI brain — missing an EPO-secreting RCC or cerebellar hemangioblastoma (think von Hippel–Lindau) is the tested error.
  11. Renal causes — Post-transplant erythrocytosis treated with phlebotomy alone is the WRONG approach. Discriminator: first-line therapy is an ACE inhibitor or ARB, which lowers EPO and Hct; reserving phlebotomy for refractory/symptomatic cases is the tested point.
  12. Drugs (testosterone) — An older man on testosterone replacement with Hct 56% being diagnosed as PV is the WRONG call. Discriminator: medication history reveals exogenous androgen; management is dose reduction/holding testosterone and therapeutic phlebotomy — not committing to lifelong MPN therapy. EPO is typically LOW here too (androgens suppress endogenous EPO), so don't be fooled into the PV pathway.
  13. Familial / high-affinity Hb — A young adult with lifelong high Hct and JAK2-negative testing labeled 'PV' is WRONG. Discriminator: order a hemoglobin-oxygen dissociation curve (p50) and family studies — a low p50 indicates a high-affinity hemoglobin, a benign congenital condition, not a clonal MPN.
  14. Arterial O2 saturation probe — Relying on a normal finger pulse-ox to exclude hypoxia is the WRONG move. Discriminator: pulse oximetry misses carboxyhemoglobin (smokers) and overnight desaturation (OSA); the tested next steps are co-oximetry/ABG and, when suspected, a sleep study before concluding the polycythemia is unexplained.
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Erythrocytosis detected
Entry point: confirm a TRUE, sustained elevation before launching a workup. Diagnostic thresholds for erythrocytosis are Hgb >16.5 g/dL or Hct >49% in men, and Hgb >16.0 g/dL or Hct >48% in women (2016 WHO PV criteria). Always repeat the CBC — a single high value may be hemoconcentration. Mnemonic: 'TRUE before CHASE' — prove the red cell mass is really up before you order EPO, JAK2, and imaging.
⚠️ A WRONG move is to start phlebotomy or order a JAK2/EPO panel off one mildly elevated Hct. The discriminator: repeat the CBC and assess plasma volume first — transient dehydration or lab error mimics erythrocytosis, and treating it commits the patient to an unnecessary MPN workup.
2. Plasma volume / RCM
Gate 1 separates TRUE polycythemia (absolute rise in red cell mass) from RELATIVE/spurious polycythemia (normal red cell mass with a contracted plasma volume). Hct is a ratio, so a shrunken plasma denominator falsely raises it. The historic gold standard is a chromium-51–labeled red cell mass study, but in practice very high Hgb/Hct (men Hgb >18.5, women >16.5) is taken as presumptive true erythrocytosis and RCM testing is reserved for borderline cases. Mnemonic: 'numerator vs denominator.'
⚠️ The trap is assuming every high Hct means more red cells. WRONG — if plasma volume is contracted (the small plasma bag), red cell mass can be normal. Discriminator: a normal calculated/measured RCM with low plasma volume = relative polycythemia, which needs hydration and med review, not a hematologic workup.
3. Relative (Gaisböck)
Relative (spurious/apparent) polycythemia = NORMAL red cell mass with reduced plasma volume. Causes: dehydration, diuretics, vomiting/burns, and Gaisböck syndrome (stress erythrocytosis in middle-aged hypertensive overweight male smokers). Management is treating the cause — rehydrate, stop diuretics, control BP, smoking cessation — not phlebotomy. Mnemonic: 'Gaisböck = the stressed, smoking, hypertensive businessman.'
⚠️ A classic boards stem: hypertensive overweight male smoker with mildly high Hct — the WRONG answer is polycythemia vera with phlebotomy. The discriminator is a NORMAL red cell mass and absence of splenomegaly/pruritus/JAK2; this is relative polycythemia, treated with lifestyle and addressing diuretics, not bloodletting.
4. Gate 1: need RCM
When values are very high (e.g., Hct >60% in men, >56% in women), red cell mass is essentially always elevated and formal RCM measurement is unnecessary. In the intermediate zone (the warning-triangle Hgb <20 / Hct <60% range), an isotope red cell mass study can confirm true erythrocytosis when the diagnosis is unclear. Mnemonic: 'sky-high Hct skips the RCM step.'
⚠️ WRONG to demand a chromium-51 RCM study in a patient with Hct 65% before acting — at extreme values RCM is presumed elevated. Discriminator: reserve formal RCM for borderline counts; chasing it at extreme values delays diagnosis and the isotope test is rarely available.
5. JAK2 detector >95%
Gate 2 molecular testing: JAK2 V617F (exon 14) is present in ~95% of polycythemia vera; the remaining ~3–4% carry JAK2 exon 12 mutations, so a V617F-negative but clinically suspicious case gets reflex exon 12 testing. JAK2 is a constitutively active tyrosine kinase driving EPO-independent erythroid proliferation. A positive JAK2 is a MAJOR WHO criterion for PV. Mnemonic: 'JAK2 — the master switch jammed ON.'
⚠️ The trap: a positive JAK2 V617F is NOT specific for PV — it also appears in ~50–60% of ET and ~50% of primary myelofibrosis. So JAK2+ alone does not equal PV. Discriminator: combine JAK2 with a SUPPRESSED EPO and red-cell predominance; JAK2+ with high EPO and isolated thrombocytosis points elsewhere.
6. EPO level meter
Serum EPO is the pivotal discriminating test. In PV the autonomous JAK2-driven erythropoiesis suppresses EPO via negative feedback, so EPO is LOW (or low-normal). In secondary polycythemia the rise is EPO-DRIVEN, so EPO is HIGH or inappropriately normal. A low EPO is itself a MINOR WHO criterion for PV. Mnemonic: 'LOW EPO = Lesion in the marrow (PV); HIGH EPO = Hypoxia/tumor (secondary).'
⚠️ High EPO does NOT mean PV. WRONG answer — students invert the logic. In PV the marrow makes red cells on its own and feedback SUPPRESSES EPO (low). High/normal EPO points to a SECONDARY cause (hypoxia, tumor, doping). Discriminator: low EPO + JAK2+ = PV; high EPO = go hunting for hypoxia or an EPO source.
7. Low EPO → PV
Suppressed EPO + JAK2 mutation = polycythemia vera. WHO 2016 PV diagnosis = all 3 major OR first 2 major + the minor criterion: (1) elevated Hgb/Hct or RCM, (2) bone marrow trilineage panmyelosis with pleomorphic megakaryocytes, (3) JAK2 mutation; minor = subnormal EPO. Treat with phlebotomy to Hct <45% plus low-dose aspirin; add hydroxyurea (or ruxolitinib/interferon) if high-risk (age >60 or prior thrombosis). Mnemonic: 'PV = Pruritus, Plethora, Phlebotomy.'
⚠️ WRONG to call any erythrocytosis with low EPO 'PV' without confirming JAK2 — congenital high-O2-affinity hemoglobinopathies and EPO-receptor mutations can also have low EPO. Discriminator: PV needs the JAK2 mutation (or exon 12); JAK2-negative low-EPO erythrocytosis in a young patient suggests a congenital cause.
8. Secondary / exclude PV
An elevated or inappropriately normal EPO shifts the diagnosis to SECONDARY polycythemia and effectively excludes PV (where EPO should be suppressed). Next steps: pulse oximetry/ABG and carboxyhemoglobin for hypoxia, then imaging (renal/hepatic/CNS) to hunt for an EPO-secreting tumor if no hypoxia is found. Mnemonic: 'EPO up → look UP the airway and INSIDE the organs.'
⚠️ The trap is treating secondary polycythemia like PV with aggressive phlebotomy. WRONG — in hypoxia-driven secondary polycythemia the high Hct is compensatory; aggressive phlebotomy can worsen tissue oxygen delivery. Discriminator: fix the underlying cause (oxygen, CPAP, resect tumor); phlebotomy is reserved for symptomatic hyperviscosity.
9. Hypoxia
Most common cause of secondary polycythemia: chronic tissue hypoxia driving appropriate EPO release. Causes: COPD, obstructive sleep apnea (Pickwickian/obesity-hypoventilation), high-altitude residence, cyanotic congenital heart disease with right-to-left shunt, and smoking (carboxyhemoglobin causes a left-shift and functional hypoxia). Check SaO2 and carboxyhemoglobin. Mnemonic: 'high CO from cigarettes hides as normal SaO2.'
⚠️ A boards trap: a heavy smoker with high Hct and a NORMAL pulse-ox SaO2 — calling it PV is WRONG. Pulse oximetry cannot detect carboxyhemoglobin; the discriminator is an elevated carboxyhemoglobin level (co-oximetry), revealing smoker's polycythemia, which resolves with cessation.
10. EPO-secreting tumors
Tumors that autonomously secrete EPO cause secondary polycythemia with HIGH EPO and no hypoxia. Classic four: Renal cell carcinoma, Hepatocellular carcinoma, cerebellar Hemangioblastoma, and uterine leiomyomata (fibroids); also pheochromocytoma and parathyroid adenomas. Mnemonic: 'EPO tumors — Renal, Hepatic, Hemangioblastoma, uterine fibroids (the 4 H-driven sources).'
⚠️ WRONG to stop at 'secondary polycythemia' once EPO is high without imaging for a tumor. Discriminator: a non-smoker with high EPO, no hypoxia, and a flank mass or new headache needs CT abdomen/MRI brain — missing an EPO-secreting RCC or cerebellar hemangioblastoma (think von Hippel–Lindau) is the tested error.
11. Renal causes
Renal pathology raises EPO either by local hypoxia or autonomous production: post-renal-transplant erythrocytosis (10–15% of transplants, treat with ACE inhibitor/ARB), renal artery stenosis, polycystic kidney disease, hydronephrosis, and renal cell carcinoma. Mnemonic: 'a kidney that's squeezed, cystic, or cancerous cranks out EPO.'
⚠️ Post-transplant erythrocytosis treated with phlebotomy alone is the WRONG approach. Discriminator: first-line therapy is an ACE inhibitor or ARB, which lowers EPO and Hct; reserving phlebotomy for refractory/symptomatic cases is the tested point.
12. Drugs (testosterone)
Drug/iatrogenic erythrocytosis: exogenous testosterone and anabolic-androgenic steroids stimulate erythropoiesis (a dose-limiting side effect of testosterone replacement — monitor Hct and hold if >54%); recombinant EPO/darbepoetin doping in athletes; and SGLT2 inhibitors mildly raise Hct. Mnemonic: 'the gym/anti-aging clinic patient with a high Hct = testosterone.'
⚠️ An older man on testosterone replacement with Hct 56% being diagnosed as PV is the WRONG call. Discriminator: medication history reveals exogenous androgen; management is dose reduction/holding testosterone and therapeutic phlebotomy — not committing to lifelong MPN therapy. EPO is typically LOW here too (androgens suppress endogenous EPO), so don't be fooled into the PV pathway.
13. Familial / high-affinity Hb
Congenital erythrocytosis: high-oxygen-affinity hemoglobin variants (left-shifted O2 dissociation curve, low p50 — Hb releases O2 poorly so tissues sense hypoxia), EPO-receptor truncation mutations (primary familial polycythemia, LOW EPO), 2,3-BPG deficiency, and VHL Chuvash polycythemia. Suspect in young patients with a family history. Mnemonic: 'young + family history + low p50 = high-affinity Hb.'
⚠️ A young adult with lifelong high Hct and JAK2-negative testing labeled 'PV' is WRONG. Discriminator: order a hemoglobin-oxygen dissociation curve (p50) and family studies — a low p50 indicates a high-affinity hemoglobin, a benign congenital condition, not a clonal MPN.
14. Arterial O2 saturation probe
After EPO is found high/normal, measure arterial oxygenation: SaO2 by ABG (not just pulse ox) and carboxyhemoglobin by co-oximetry to detect smoker's/CO-mediated hypoxia. SaO2 <92% supports a hypoxic secondary cause. If oxygenation is normal, pivot to imaging for an EPO-secreting tumor. Mnemonic: 'EPO high → check the O2 probe → if normal, hunt the tumor.'
⚠️ Relying on a normal finger pulse-ox to exclude hypoxia is the WRONG move. Discriminator: pulse oximetry misses carboxyhemoglobin (smokers) and overnight desaturation (OSA); the tested next steps are co-oximetry/ABG and, when suspected, a sleep study before concluding the polycythemia is unexplained.
