📖 Read-through — all 16 symbols
1. HCM phenotype
HCM phenotype = asymmetric septal hypertrophy with a small or normal LV cavity and preserved or hyperdynamic EF — the problem is too much wall, not a weak pump. Classifying phenotype is the mandatory first clinical step because it dictates everything downstream. Echo shows septal wall thickness with no other cause (no HTN, no aortic stenosis).
⚠️ Board contrasts HCM (small cavity, ↑EF) against DCM (dilated cavity, ↓EF). Discriminator: cavity size and EF. HCM treatment REDUCES contractility; DCM treatment INCREASES it — opposite philosophies.
2. DCM phenotype
DCM = dilated LV with reduced EF — a weak pump. Treatment increases contractility and reduces afterload (HFrEF GDMT). This is the mirror image of HCM. The window/phenotype panel forces the correct classification before any drug choice.
⚠️ Giving an HCM patient an inotrope or afterload reducer because you mistook the phenotype. In DCM these help; in HCM they worsen the gradient. Always classify cavity + EF first.
3. Septal hypertrophy
Asymmetric septal hypertrophy is the anatomic substrate of HCM. The thick septum narrows the LV outflow tract and sets up the Venturi effect that drives SAM. Most cases are autosomal dominant sarcomere gene mutations (e.g. beta-myosin heavy chain). Septal myectomy or alcohol septal ablation removes this obstruction when drugs fail.
⚠️ Concentric symmetric hypertrophy points to hypertensive heart or aortic stenosis, NOT HCM. HCM is classically ASYMMETRIC septal. Discriminator: septum-to-posterior-wall thickness ratio and absence of pressure-overload cause.
4. SAM
Systolic anterior motion (SAM) of the anterior mitral leaflet: rapid early ejection across the narrowed outflow tract creates a Venturi effect that pulls the enlarged anterior leaflet toward the hypertrophied septum during systole. When the leaflet contacts the septum it obstructs the LVOT. SAM is the mechanism of dynamic obstruction in HCM.
⚠️ SAM produces dynamic LVOT obstruction AND a posteriorly directed MR jet simultaneously — both from the same event. If you see a posteriorly directed MR jet with HCM, the cause is SAM, not primary valve disease.
5. Posterior MR jet
Because SAM pulls the anterior leaflet away from coaptation, the mitral valve closes incompletely and produces a POSTERIORLY directed regurgitant jet — occurring together with the LVOT obstruction. The MR is secondary and dynamic; it tracks with the degree of SAM/obstruction.
⚠️ An ANTERIORLY or centrally directed MR jet suggests intrinsic mitral pathology, not SAM. The posterior direction is the fingerprint of SAM-mediated MR in HCM.
6. Worsens (Valsalva/stand)
Obstruction WORSENS whenever the LV cavity shrinks: Valsalva, standing, dehydration, nitrates, tachycardia, and positive inotropes. The unifying rule — anything that reduces preload OR increases contractility shrinks the cavity and increases the gradient. The murmur of HCM gets LOUDER with Valsalva and standing.
⚠️ Aortic stenosis and MR murmurs get SOFTER with Valsalva (less flow). HCM murmur gets LOUDER. Valsalva/standing response is the bedside discriminator that separates HCM from fixed AS.
7. Improves (squat/β-block)
Obstruction IMPROVES when the cavity enlarges: squatting, beta-blockade, volume loading, and avoiding vasodilators. Squatting increases both preload and afterload, enlarging the cavity and softening the murmur. Any maneuver that raises preload or lowers contractility helps.
⚠️ Handgrip (↑afterload) softens the HCM murmur and louds MR/VSD — useful to distinguish. Squat-to-stand louds HCM. Knowing the maneuver direction is the high-yield discriminator.
8. β-blocker (1st)
Beta-blocker is FIRST-LINE for symptomatic HCM obstruction: it lowers heart rate and contractility while preserving preload, reducing the dynamic gradient. It is also the correct treatment for angina in HCM. Slowing HR prolongs diastolic filling and reduces the Venturi effect.
⚠️ Board trap: HCM patient with angina — do NOT give nitroglycerin. Use beta-blockers. Nitrates reduce preload, shrink the cavity, and worsen the very obstruction causing the angina (demand ischemia, not coronary supply).
9. Verapamil/Diltiazem (2nd)
Verapamil or diltiazem (non-dihydropyridine CCBs) are SECOND-LINE, used when beta-blockers are not tolerated — same dual mechanism of negative chronotropy and inotropy. They reduce the gradient and improve diastolic filling.
⚠️ Avoid dihydropyridine CCBs (nifedipine, amlodipine) — they are vasodilators that drop afterload and WORSEN obstruction. Only the rate-limiting non-DHP CCBs are appropriate in HCM.
10. Disopyramide (3rd)
Disopyramide is THIRD-LINE: a potent negative inotrope (class Ia antiarrhythmic) used for persistent obstruction despite beta-blocker or CCB. Its strong negative inotropy reduces the systolic gradient. Added on top of a rate-controlling agent.
⚠️ Disopyramide has anticholinergic side effects and can accelerate AV nodal conduction in AF — usually combined with a beta-blocker/CCB. It is for persistent obstruction, not a first step.
11. Mavacamten (4th)
Mavacamten is FOURTH-LINE: a cardiac myosin inhibitor that reduces actin-myosin cross-bridge formation, lowering hyperdynamic contractility and the LVOT gradient. It requires serial echo EF monitoring because it can reduce systolic function. Escalate to it before invasive septal reduction.
⚠️ Board tests the EXACT order (β-blocker → verapamil/diltiazem → disopyramide → mavacamten) before myectomy/alcohol ablation. Mavacamten needs echo EF surveillance — forgetting the monitoring requirement is a trap.
12. BANNED counter
BANNED in obstructive HCM: nitrates, digoxin, dehydration, and pure vasodilators — all worsen the LVOT obstruction. This counter collects every wrong-answer drug into one rule: nothing that shrinks the cavity or boosts ejection.
⚠️ The classic vignette offers a 'reasonable' cardiac drug (nitrate for chest pain, digoxin for symptoms, ACEi/vasodilator for HTN). In obstructive HCM all are wrong because they worsen the gradient. Recognize the trap by phenotype.
13. Nitrates ✗
Nitrates reduce preload → smaller LV cavity → worse obstruction. Contraindicated in obstructive HCM even for angina. The angina in HCM is demand-driven from the hypertrophy, not a fixed coronary lesion, so nitrates make it worse.
⚠️ HCM + chest pain → answer is beta-blocker, NOT nitroglycerin. Nitrates are the single most tested banned drug in HCM vignettes.
14. Digoxin ✗
Digoxin is a positive inotrope → more forceful systolic ejection → more SAM → worse obstruction. Contraindicated in obstructive HCM. Positive inotropy is exactly what you want to avoid here.
⚠️ Digoxin may be offered for an HCM patient who develops AF. In OBSTRUCTIVE HCM it worsens the gradient — rate control with beta-blocker/verapamil is preferred instead.
15. Vasodilators ✗
Pure vasodilators drop afterload → the LV empties more aggressively → larger systolic gradient. Contraindicated in obstructive HCM. This includes dihydropyridine CCBs and arterial vasodilators.
⚠️ An HCM patient with hypertension may be offered a vasodilator/ACEi. In obstructive HCM, afterload reduction worsens the gradient; control BP with beta-blockers/verapamil instead.
16. SCD risk / ICD
HCM is a leading cause of sudden cardiac death in young athletes. SCD risk factors that drive ICD consideration: family history of SCD, unexplained syncope, massive LV wall thickness (≥30 mm), NSVT on monitoring, and abnormal blood-pressure response to exercise. ICD for primary prevention in high-risk patients; advise against competitive high-intensity sports.
⚠️ SCD risk in HCM is driven by these factors, NOT by the LVOT gradient severity. A patient can have severe obstruction but low SCD risk, or no obstruction yet high SCD risk. Don't equate gradient with arrhythmic risk.
