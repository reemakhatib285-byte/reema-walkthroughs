The hyperkalemia risk is essentially the SAME because both reduce aldosterone; ARBs only fix the bradykinin-related cough/angioedema, not the potassium. 📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. 3 lanes of drugs
Drugs cause hyperkalemia along three mechanistic lanes that all converge on impaired distal K+ secretion: (1) RAAS BLOCKADE (less aldosterone is made), (2) ALDOSTERONE-RECEPTOR (mineralocorticoid receptor) blockade, and (3) ENaC blockade in the collecting duct. A few additional drugs cause a transcellular SHIFT (beta-blockers, digoxin).
⚠️ WRONG answer = assume every drug-induced hyperkalemia is from an ACEi/ARB. Map the drug to its lane (RAAS vs MR vs ENaC vs shift) — and remember risk multiplies when agents from different lanes are combined, especially in CKD or diabetes.
2. ACE inhibitors
ACE inhibitors (-pril) block angiotensin-converting enzyme → less angiotensin II → less aldosterone → reduced distal K+ secretion → hyperkalemia. Risk is highest in CKD, diabetes, volume depletion, or when combined with other RAAS/ENaC blockers. Monitor K+ and creatinine ~1–2 weeks after starting or uptitrating.
⚠️ WRONG answer = stop an ACEi permanently for a small, expected creatinine rise (<30%) without checking potassium. In proteinuric CKD the renoprotective benefit is worth tolerating mild Cr/K+ rises; severe or progressive hyperkalemia, however, does warrant dose reduction/discontinuation.
3. ARBs
ARBs (-sartan) block the angiotensin II type-1 (AT1) receptor → less aldosterone → hyperkalemia. Same downstream effect as ACEi but without bradykinin accumulation (so no cough/angioedema risk).
⚠️ WRONG answer = switch an ACEi to an ARB to avoid hyperkalemia. The hyperkalemia risk is essentially the SAME because both reduce aldosterone; ARBs only fix the bradykinin-related cough/angioedema, not the potassium. Never combine an ACEi WITH an ARB (additive hyperkalemia/AKI, no benefit).
4. Renin inhibitor
Direct renin inhibitor (aliskiren) blocks renin at the top of the RAAS cascade → less angiotensin II and aldosterone → hyperkalemia.
⚠️ WRONG answer = add aliskiren to an ACEi or ARB in a diabetic/CKD patient. The ALTITUDE trial showed dual RAAS blockade with aliskiren caused excess hyperkalemia, hypotension, and renal events — it is contraindicated in diabetes with an ACEi/ARB.
5. NSAIDs
NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandin (PGE2) synthesis. PGE2 normally stimulates renin, so blocking it → low renin → low aldosterone (hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism) → hyperkalemia. NSAIDs also reduce renal blood flow/GFR, compounding the effect.
⚠️ WRONG answer = overlook OTC NSAIDs as a hyperkalemia (and HTN/AKI) cause. NSAIDs act like a functional RAAS blocker; in a patient already on an ACEi + diuretic, adding an NSAID forms the dangerous 'triple whammy' (ACEi/ARB + diuretic + NSAID → AKI and hyperkalemia).
6. Heparin / LMWH
Heparin (unfractionated and LMWH) directly inhibits aldosterone synthesis in the adrenal zona glomerulosa (reduces angiotensin-II receptor number and inhibits the final synthetic steps) → hyperkalemia, even at prophylactic doses and within days.
⚠️ WRONG answer = exclude heparin as a cause because 'it's just an anticoagulant' or because the dose is low. Heparin blocks aldosterone DIRECTLY at the adrenal (not at a receptor) and can cause hyperkalemia at any dose — a classic, easily missed cause in hospitalized patients.
7. Spironolactone
Spironolactone is a non-selective mineralocorticoid (aldosterone) receptor antagonist in the collecting duct → blocks aldosterone action → K+-sparing → hyperkalemia. Used in HFrEF (RALES), resistant HTN (PATHWAY-2), primary aldosteronism, and cirrhotic ascites.
⚠️ WRONG answer = combine spironolactone with an ACEi/ARB in a patient with CKD or diabetes without close monitoring. This is a high-risk combination for severe hyperkalemia. Spironolactone's non-selective action also causes gynecomastia — switch to eplerenone if that occurs.
8. Eplerenone
Eplerenone is a SELECTIVE mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist → blocks aldosterone with far fewer antiandrogen/progestin side effects (less gynecomastia) than spironolactone, but the same hyperkalemia risk.
⚠️ WRONG answer = assume eplerenone is 'safer' for potassium because it is selective. Selectivity only spares the sex-hormone side effects; the hyperkalemia risk is comparable to spironolactone and still requires K+ monitoring.
9. Drospirenone
Drospirenone (the progestin in some combined oral contraceptives, e.g. Yaz/Yasmin) has antimineralocorticoid (spironolactone-like) activity → can raise serum K+.
⚠️ WRONG answer = forget the OCP when evaluating hyperkalemia in a young woman, especially if she is also on an ACEi/ARB, NSAID, or K-sparing diuretic. Drospirenone-containing pills carry a label caution about combining with potassium-raising drugs.
10. Amiloride/triamterene
Amiloride and triamterene are K+-sparing diuretics that directly block the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC) in the collecting duct principal cells. Blocking Na+ reabsorption removes the favorable lumen-negative gradient that drives K+ secretion → hyperkalemia. They act independently of aldosterone (unlike spironolactone).
⚠️ WRONG answer = treat amiloride-induced hyperkalemia with fludrocortisone. ENaC blockade is downstream of aldosterone, so giving more mineralocorticoid won't help — stop the drug. Note: amiloride/triamterene block the channel directly (vs spironolactone, which blocks the receptor).
11. Trimethoprim
Trimethoprim (in TMP-SMX/Bactrim) blocks ENaC exactly like amiloride → hyperkalemia, especially at high doses (PCP treatment), in CKD, or with other RAAS/K-sparing agents. Effect appears within days of starting.
⚠️ WRONG answer = miss TMP-SMX as the cause of new hyperkalemia in a patient treated for a UTI, cellulitis, or PCP. Trimethoprim is a classic, frequently tested ENaC blocker — think of it (and pentamidine) whenever an HIV/PCP patient becomes hyperkalemic.
12. Pentamidine
Pentamidine (used for PCP prophylaxis/treatment when TMP-SMX cannot be used) blocks ENaC like amiloride → hyperkalemia. Classic in the HIV/PCP context; also causes nephrotoxicity and hypoglycemia.
⚠️ WRONG answer = assume an HIV patient's hyperkalemia is only from their renal disease while overlooking pentamidine (or TMP-SMX). Both ENaC-blocking antimicrobials are common culprits in this exact population.
13. Calcineurin inhibitors
Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus) cause hyperkalemia by multiple distal mechanisms: they impair aldosterone responsiveness, downregulate ENaC and the Na-K-ATPase, and can produce a type-4-RTA-like state — a common problem in transplant recipients.
⚠️ WRONG answer = attribute a transplant patient's hyperkalemia solely to allograft dysfunction. CNIs directly impair distal K+ handling even with adequate graft function; review tacrolimus/cyclosporine levels and co-prescribed RAAS/ENaC blockers.
14. Deadly combos
High-risk drug combinations that multiply hyperkalemia risk: ACEi/ARB + spironolactone; ACEi/ARB + NSAID (+ diuretic = the 'triple whammy' for AKI); and TMP-SMX + a K-sparing diuretic or RAAS blocker. Risk compounds further in CKD, diabetes, and the elderly.
⚠️ WRONG answer = add a second potassium-raising drug from a different lane without rechecking potassium and renal function. Always reconcile the full medication list (including OTC NSAIDs and OCPs) — combinations from different lanes are additive and are the most common cause of severe drug-induced hyperkalemia.
