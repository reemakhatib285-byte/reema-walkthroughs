📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Rivaroxaban (River-toga) — once daily
RIVAROXABAN ('river-toga') is a direct factor Xa inhibitor dosed mostly ONCE DAILY for AF (20 mg, or 15 mg if CrCl 15–50) and TAKEN WITH FOOD to ensure absorption. It is the all-oral, single-drug option for VTE that starts with a higher loading dose then steps down. Renally adjusted; avoid in severe renal impairment.
⚠️ Rivaroxaban must be taken WITH FOOD (esp. the 15/20 mg doses) for reliable absorption — apixaban does not have this requirement. Don't forget the CrCl-based dose reduction.
2. Apixaban (Bee-helmet) — BID
APIXABAN ('bee-helmet') is a direct factor Xa inhibitor dosed TWICE DAILY (5 mg BID; reduce to 2.5 mg BID if ≥2 of: age ≥80, weight ≤60 kg, creatinine ≥1.5). It has the LEAST renal dependence of the DOACs (~27% renal) and the most favorable bleeding profile, making it preferred in CKD and elderly patients.
⚠️ Apixaban 2.5 mg BID requires ≥2 of 3 criteria (age ≥80, ≤60 kg, Cr ≥1.5) — applying just one criterion and under-dosing risks thrombosis. Apixaban is preferred over rivaroxaban/dabigatran when renal function is poor.
3. Edoxaban (Edge-armor) — BID/QD
EDOXABAN ('edge-armor') is a once-daily factor Xa inhibitor (60 mg, reduced to 30 mg for CrCl 15–50, weight ≤60 kg, or potent P-gp inhibitors). Notably it must NOT be used when CrCl is TOO HIGH (>95 mL/min) because reduced exposure led to higher stroke rates in AF trials.
⚠️ Edoxaban is the DOAC that is contraindicated for AF when renal function is TOO GOOD (CrCl >95) — the opposite of the usual 'reduce in renal failure' rule. This 'too clean' ceiling is a favorite board point.
4. Dabigatran (Gator) — BID
DABIGATRAN ('gator') is the direct THROMBIN (IIa) inhibitor, dosed 150 mg BID (110/75 mg for age/renal reduction). It is the MOST renally cleared DOAC (~80%), so it accumulates in renal failure and is the one with a specific antibody antidote (idarucizumab). Requires VTE treatment to be LED IN with parenteral heparin first.
⚠️ Dabigatran is the only listed DOAC that needs a 5–10 day parenteral (heparin/LMWH) LEAD-IN before starting (like edoxaban) — apixaban and rivaroxaban are started orally with no lead-in. Also most renal-dependent → avoid in CKD.
5. AF dosing wall — pick 2 of 3
The AF DOSING WALL summarizes nonvalvular-AF stroke-prevention dosing and the dose-reduction triad for apixaban: pick 2 of 3 (age ≥80, weight ≤60 kg, creatinine ≥1.5) to drop to the lower dose. Each DOAC has its own renal/weight/age adjustment table — these are heavily tested.
⚠️ Reduction criteria are drug-specific — don't apply apixaban's 'pick 2 of 3' to edoxaban or dabigatran, which use CrCl thresholds and other rules.
6. No bridge needed
Because DOACs have rapid onset/offset, no heparin BRIDGING is needed when starting or stopping them around procedures — you simply hold and restart. This contrasts with warfarin's slow kinetics.
⚠️ Bridging logic belongs to warfarin, not DOACs — bridging a DOAC just raises bleeding risk.
7. Parenteral-first lead-in (5–10 days)
VTE TREATMENT — orange (parenteral-first) lane: DABIGATRAN and EDOXABAN require an initial 5–10 day course of parenteral anticoagulation (LMWH/UFH) BEFORE the oral DOAC is started. This lead-in mirrors how those two drugs were studied.
⚠️ The lead-in DOACs are dabigatran and edoxaban. Apixaban/rivaroxaban are the all-oral, single-drug regimens (the 'green lane') — mixing these up changes the entire VTE plan.
8. All-oral green lane
VTE TREATMENT — green (all-oral) lane: APIXABAN and RIVAROXABAN can be started immediately as monotherapy with NO parenteral lead-in, using a higher loading phase then a maintenance dose (apixaban 10 mg BID ×7 d then 5 mg BID; rivaroxaban 15 mg BID ×21 d then 20 mg daily).
⚠️ Don't give apixaban/rivaroxaban a heparin lead-in — they were designed as single-drug oral therapy from day 1. The loading-then-maintenance step-down is the key dosing detail.
9. VTE treatment corridor
The whole corridor frames acute VTE (DVT/PE) treatment: choose either the all-oral lane (apixaban/rivaroxaban) or the parenteral-lead-in lane (dabigatran/edoxaban), then continue anticoagulation for a defined duration (typically ≥3 months, longer/indefinite for unprovoked or ongoing risk).
⚠️ DOACs are first-line for most VTE but are NOT for severe renal failure, antiphospholipid syndrome (use warfarin), or mechanical valves (use warfarin) — know these exclusions.
10. Prophylaxis shelf (hip/knee)
Post-orthopedic (hip/knee arthroplasty) VTE PROPHYLAXIS: rivaroxaban 10 mg once daily, apixaban 2.5 mg BID, dabigatran 220 mg once daily are all options. Duration is longer for HIP (~35 days) than KNEE (~10–14 days). Aspirin 81 mg is an accepted alternative in selected low-risk patients after an initial DOAC course.
⚠️ Prophylaxis doses are LOWER than treatment doses (e.g., apixaban 2.5 BID for prophylaxis vs 5 BID for AF) — don't confuse the two. Hip needs longer prophylaxis duration than knee.
11. Routine monitoring? NO
A defining DOAC advantage: NO routine coagulation monitoring and no dose titration to a lab target. Fixed dosing with periodic renal/hepatic function checks is all that is required. INR/PT is NOT used to dose or monitor DOACs.
⚠️ Ordering INR to 'check' a DOAC is wrong — INR is for warfarin. A normal INR does NOT exclude a therapeutic DOAC effect.
12. PT/INR is useless for DOACs
PT/INR and aPTT respond unpredictably to DOACs and cannot quantify their effect. If drug levels truly must be measured (e.g., before emergency surgery, suspected accumulation, extremes of weight), use a DRUG-SPECIFIC assay: anti-Xa calibrated to the specific Xa inhibitor, or dilute thrombin time / ecarin for dabigatran.
⚠️ A normal PT/aPTT does NOT rule out DOAC presence; conversely a mildly abnormal PT doesn't quantify it. Use the calibrated anti-Xa (for -xabans) or dilute thrombin time (for dabigatran) when a level is genuinely needed.
13. Anti-Xa assay (when needed)
For the factor Xa inhibitors (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban), a DRUG-CALIBRATED ANTI-Xa level is the appropriate test if measurement is required, and it should be checked relative to dose timing (peak ~1–4 h, trough before next dose). Useful in renal failure, bleeding, urgent surgery, or compliance questions.
⚠️ A generic (LMWH-calibrated) anti-Xa is not interchangeable — the assay must be calibrated to the specific DOAC. Routine levels are unnecessary; this is only for special situations.
14. Dilute thrombin time for dabigatran
Dabigatran (anti-IIa) is best assessed, when needed, by a DILUTE THROMBIN TIME (dTT) or ECARIN clotting time, which correlate with drug concentration. A normal thrombin time (TT) effectively excludes clinically relevant dabigatran, because TT is exquisitely sensitive to thrombin inhibition.
⚠️ Use thrombin-based assays for dabigatran and anti-Xa assays for the -xabans — don't swap them. A normal standard TT rules OUT meaningful dabigatran (high sensitivity), unlike PT/aPTT.
15. Monitoring corner takeaway
Bottom line locked in: DOACs are given at fixed doses with NO routine lab monitoring; check renal/liver function periodically and adjust dose by CrCl. Reserve drug-specific assays (anti-Xa for -xabans, dilute TT/ecarin for dabigatran) for emergencies, accumulation, or extremes of body weight — NEVER PT/INR.
⚠️ The exam reward is recognizing that 'no monitoring' is a benefit but NOT 'never measurable' — in the right emergency you still use the correct calibrated assay, just not PT/INR/aPTT.
