📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Myoglobin barrel
Rhabdomyolysis (crush injury, prolonged immobilization, seizures, statins/fibrates, cocaine, extreme exertion, NMS/malignant hyperthermia) breaks down skeletal muscle, releasing myoglobin and markedly elevating CK — usually >5,000 and AKI risk climbs sharply above ~15,000–20,000 U/L. Associated labs: hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, HYPOcalcemia early, and a later rebound hypercalcemia. Myoglobin is a heme pigment that is filtered and damages tubules.
⚠️ Diagnosing rhabdomyolysis off a positive blood dipstick alone — the discriminator is a high CK with FEW RBCs on micro; a normal CK essentially excludes it, and the pigment, not RBCs, drives the dipstick reaction.
2. Rhabdo mechanisms
Myoglobin causes AKI by three mechanisms: (1) renal vasoconstriction reducing medullary blood flow, (2) direct heme-mediated proximal tubular toxicity via oxidative injury, and (3) intratubular cast obstruction when myoglobin binds Tamm-Horsfall protein. ACIDIC urine promotes cast precipitation and amplifies toxicity, which is the rationale for urine alkalinization.
⚠️ Forgetting that aciduria worsens pigment cast formation — students recall 'give fluids' but miss that acidic urine accelerates injury, which is why alkalinizing the urine (raising pH) is considered to keep myoglobin soluble.
3. Rhabdo treatment
The cornerstone of rhabdomyolysis management is early, aggressive IV isotonic fluid resuscitation (often several liters, titrated to a urine output of ~200–300 mL/h) to dilute and flush pigment and casts and restore perfusion. Sodium bicarbonate to alkalinize urine (target pH >6.5) is considered to reduce cast formation, though evidence is debated. Treat hyperkalemia urgently; avoid calcium unless symptomatic given rebound hypercalcemia.
⚠️ Reaching for mannitol or bicarbonate before adequate volume repletion — aggressive crystalloid is the proven first-line; mannitol/bicarbonate are adjuncts, and giving calcium for asymptomatic early hypocalcemia risks precipitating calcium-phosphate and rebound hypercalcemia.
4. Hemoglobin barrel
Massive intravascular hemolysis releases free hemoglobin that, once haptoglobin is saturated, is filtered and causes pigment nephropathy by the same mechanisms as myoglobin. Causes include ABO-incompatible transfusion reactions, mechanical heart valves, paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria, severe malaria (blackwater fever), and G6PD-deficiency oxidative crises. Plasma appears pink-red in hemoglobinemia.
⚠️ Confusing hemoglobinuria with myoglobinuria — both give a blood-positive dipstick with few RBCs; the tell is pink plasma and low haptoglobin/high LDH in hemolysis versus clear plasma and very high CK in rhabdomyolysis.
5. Low haptoglobin
Haptoglobin binds free hemoglobin for clearance, so a LOW haptoglobin is a sensitive marker of intravascular hemolysis. The full hemolysis panel shows low haptoglobin, elevated LDH, elevated indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin, and reticulocytosis; schistocytes on smear indicate a microangiopathic/mechanical cause (TMA, mechanical valve).
⚠️ Using low haptoglobin to claim rhabdomyolysis — haptoglobin binds hemoglobin, not myoglobin, so it is a hemolysis marker; in pure rhabdo, haptoglobin is normal and CK is the discriminating test.
6. Dipstick + few RBCs
Pigment nephropathy fingerprint: the urine dipstick is POSITIVE for blood (the heme moiety of myoglobin/hemoglobin triggers the peroxidase pad) but microscopy shows FEW or NO red blood cells. This dipstick-positive/RBC-negative dissociation distinguishes pigment from true hematuria (where intact RBCs are seen).
⚠️ Reading a blood-positive dipstick as confirmed hematuria and launching a urologic bleed workup — with few RBCs on micro, the answer is pigmenturia (myoglobin or hemoglobin), confirmed by CK and a hemolysis panel rather than cystoscopy.
7. Dipstick negative / SSA
The urine dipstick protein pad detects ALBUMIN, not immunoglobulin light chains, so a patient with heavy Bence Jones (light-chain) proteinuria can have a NEGATIVE dipstick. The sulfosalicylic acid (SSA) test precipitates ALL urine proteins including light chains, so a dipstick-negative but SSA-positive urine reveals non-albumin protein — the classic myeloma clue. Confirm with urine protein electrophoresis/immunofixation.
⚠️ Concluding 'no proteinuria' from a negative dipstick in a patient with bone pain, anemia, and hypercalcemia — the dipstick misses light chains; the discriminator is a positive SSA test or UPEP, and missing it overlooks myeloma cast nephropathy.
8. Light chains / myeloma
In multiple myeloma, overproduced monoclonal free light chains are filtered and form obstructing casts with Tamm-Horsfall protein (cast nephropathy), the leading cause of myeloma AKI. The diagnostic combination is dipstick-negative proteinuria but SSA-positive, plus serum/urine free light chains and an M-spike on electrophoresis. Suspect with the CRAB features: hyperCalcemia, Renal failure, Anemia, Bone lesions.
⚠️ Attributing myeloma AKI purely to hypercalcemia and stopping there — cast nephropathy from light chains is the dominant and often irreversible mechanism, so treatment targets the plasma-cell clone and reduces light-chain load, not just the calcium.
9. Myeloma mechanisms
Myeloma injures the kidney through multiple pathways: (1) cast nephropathy from light chains obstructing distal tubules, (2) direct proximal tubular toxicity from reabsorbed light chains (can cause acquired Fanconi syndrome), and (3) hypercalcemia-induced afferent vasoconstriction and volume depletion (nephrogenic DI). Amyloidosis (AL) and light-chain deposition disease add chronic glomerular damage.
⚠️ Giving IV contrast or NSAIDs to a dehydrated myeloma patient — these precipitate cast nephropathy; the right move is aggressive hydration, avoiding nephrotoxins, correcting hypercalcemia, and urgently lowering the light-chain burden.
10. Uric acid / TLS
Tumor lysis syndrome follows cytotoxic therapy (or spontaneously) in bulky, high-turnover malignancies (Burkitt lymphoma, ALL, high-grade lymphomas), dumping intracellular contents into the blood. The metabolic signature is HYPERkalemia, HYPERphosphatemia, HYPERuricemia, and HYPOcalcemia (phosphate binds calcium). Uric acid and calcium-phosphate crystals precipitate in tubules causing obstructive AKI.
⚠️ Replacing calcium for the hypocalcemia of TLS — giving calcium in the face of hyperphosphatemia precipitates calcium-phosphate in tissues and tubules, worsening injury; only treat hypocalcemia if symptomatic (tetany, arrhythmia).
11. Crystal obstruction
TLS-related AKI is intratubular crystal obstruction by uric acid (worse in acidic urine) and calcium-phosphate (worse in alkaline urine). Prevention/treatment: aggressive IV hydration to maintain high urine flow; rasburicase (recombinant urate oxidase) for high-risk or established TLS to enzymatically degrade uric acid; allopurinol or febuxostat to block new urate formation in lower-risk patients.
⚠️ Routinely alkalinizing urine in TLS as you would for rhabdo — alkalinization helps uric acid solubility but promotes calcium-phosphate precipitation, so it is no longer recommended; the answer is hydration plus rasburicase, not bicarbonate.
12. Ethylene glycol
Ethylene glycol (antifreeze) is metabolized by alcohol dehydrogenase to glycolic and oxalic acids, producing a HIGH anion-gap metabolic acidosis (an 'M' in MUDPILES) with an elevated OSMOLAR gap early. Oxalate binds calcium forming calcium-oxalate crystals (envelope/needle-shaped) that cause AKI and hypocalcemia; urine may fluoresce under Wood's lamp. Methanol, by contrast, causes visual loss/optic injury.
⚠️ Confusing ethylene glycol with methanol — both give a high anion-gap acidosis with an osmolar gap and are treated with fomepizole, but ethylene glycol causes AKI and calcium-oxalate crystalluria/hypocalcemia, whereas methanol causes blindness and optic disc hyperemia.
13. Toxic alcohol Rx
Treat toxic-alcohol (ethylene glycol or methanol) poisoning by blocking alcohol dehydrogenase with FOMEPIZOLE (preferred) or ethanol infusion to stop formation of toxic metabolites. Add hemodialysis for severe metabolic acidosis, end-organ injury (AKI, visual changes), or high measured levels (often cited around >50 mg/dL, with treatment thresholds and the depicted >15–20 range varying by source); give cofactors — thiamine/pyridoxine for ethylene glycol, folate/folinic acid for methanol.
⚠️ Withholding fomepizole until a confirmatory level returns — empiric fomepizole is started on clinical suspicion (high anion-gap and osmolar gap, history) because waiting allows ongoing toxic-metabolite production and irreversible AKI or blindness.
