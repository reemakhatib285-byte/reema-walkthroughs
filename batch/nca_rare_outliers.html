📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. PTHrP — humoral hypercalcemia
PTHrP from squamous cell lung, breast, renal cell, and head/neck cancers drives humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy. PTHrP mimics PTH at the receptor: high Ca, LOW phosphate, but PTH itself is suppressed and 1,25-vitD is low. This is the most common mechanism of malignancy-associated hypercalcemia.
⚠️ Low PTH with high Ca looks like the tumor 'makes PTH' — but ectopic true PTH is vanishingly rare; PTHrP (a separate assay) is the answer.
2. Ectopic PTH secretion
Genuine ectopic secretion of intact PTH (not PTHrP) is a true rarity, reported in ovarian, lung, and neuroendocrine tumors. Here the standard PTH assay is inappropriately elevated despite hypercalcemia, mimicking primary hyperparathyroidism.
⚠️ Don't reflexively call high-PTH hypercalcemia 'primary hyperparathyroidism' — imaging-occult ectopic PTH tumor is the rare alternative.
3. Tertiary hyperparathyroidism
Long-standing secondary hyperparathyroidism in CKD/after transplant becomes autonomous: parathyroid glands hypertrophy and keep oversecreting PTH even after the renal stimulus resolves, producing HIGH Ca + HIGH PTH. Treat with cinacalcet or parathyroidectomy.
⚠️ Secondary hyperPTH has LOW/normal Ca; tertiary is distinguished by autonomous hypercalcemia after renal correction/transplant.
4. Immobilization hypercalcemia
Prolonged immobilization with high baseline bone turnover (adolescents, Paget disease, spinal cord injury) uncouples resorption from formation, releasing skeletal Ca. PTH is suppressed; treat with bisphosphonates and mobilization.
⚠️ Suppressed PTH + immobilized young patient points to bone-resorption hypercalcemia, not malignancy.
5. Williams syndrome
Williams syndrome (7q11.23 elastin deletion) causes idiopathic infantile hypercalcemia with elfin facies, supravalvular aortic stenosis, and a hypersocial 'cocktail party' personality. Hypercalcemia is from abnormal vitamin D sensitivity.
⚠️ Elfin facies + hypercalcemia + supravalvular AS = Williams, not DiGeorge (which causes HYPOcalcemia).
6. Rhabdomyolysis recovery phase
Acute rhabdomyolysis causes EARLY hypocalcemia (Ca deposits in damaged muscle). In the RECOVERY/diuretic phase, that calcium is remobilized causing rebound HYPERcalcemia, often with rising vitamin D.
⚠️ Rhabdo is classically hypocalcemic — the rebound hypercalcemia appears only during recovery, a frequently tested reversal.
7. Pregnancy / lactation PTHrP
Breast tissue and placenta can produce PTHrP; massive mammary hyperplasia or lactation rarely cause hypercalcemia. PTH is suppressed, PTHrP elevated. Resolves with delivery or weaning.
⚠️ Hypercalcemia in lactation isn't always malignancy — benign PTHrP from breast tissue is the rare physiologic cause.
8. Pseudohypercalcemia
Severe hyperalbuminemia (dehydration) or paraproteins (myeloma) raise TOTAL calcium while IONIZED calcium is normal — a spurious result. Always check ionized Ca or correct for albumin before treating.
⚠️ Treating a high total Ca without checking ionized Ca/albumin risks iatrogenic hypocalcemia when the elevation was artefactual.
9. Adrenal insufficiency (Addisonian)
Cortisol deficiency causes mild hypercalcemia via reduced renal Ca excretion and hemoconcentration plus increased bone resorption. Resolves rapidly with glucocorticoid replacement.
⚠️ Hypercalcemia + fatigue + hyponatremia + hyperkalemia should prompt an adrenal-insufficiency work-up, not just a malignancy hunt.
10. Familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia (FHH)
Inactivating CaSR mutation raises the set-point so PTH stays mildly high and the kidney reabsorbs Ca avidly: mild hypercalcemia, mild high PTH, and a LOW urine Ca/creatinine clearance ratio (<0.01). Benign, autosomal dominant, needs NO parathyroidectomy.
⚠️ FHH mimics primary hyperPTH — the 24-h urine calcium (low in FHH, high in primary hyperPTH) is the discriminator that prevents needless surgery.
11. Granulomatous / sarcoid 1-alpha-hydroxylase
Sarcoid, TB, and other granulomas express extrarenal 1-alpha-hydroxylase, converting 25-vitD to active 1,25-vitD autonomously. Result: hypercalcemia with HIGH 1,25-vitD, LOW PTH, often worsened by sunlight. Treat with steroids.
⚠️ Granulomatous hypercalcemia is driven by 1,25-vitD (active), not 25-vitD — and responds to glucocorticoids, unlike PTH-driven disease.
12. Milk-alkali / thiazide
Milk-alkali (calcium carbonate + alkali) gives hypercalcemia, metabolic alkalosis, and AKI. Thiazides reduce urinary Ca excretion, unmasking or worsening hypercalcemia. Both are reversible by stopping the agent.
⚠️ A patient on thiazide + calcium supplements with hypercalcemia may not have an endocrine tumor — stop the drugs and recheck first.
13. Carpopedal spasm — severe hypocalcemia
Acute severe hypocalcemia produces neuromuscular irritability: Chvostek and Trousseau signs, carpopedal spasm, laryngospasm, prolonged QT, and seizures. Emergency treatment is IV calcium gluconate.
⚠️ Prolonged QT and tetany in a post-thyroidectomy patient = hypocalcemia, not a primary arrhythmia — give IV calcium, not antiarrhythmics.
14. Bone-image dominant infiltration
Osteolytic metastases and myeloma release Ca by direct bone destruction (local osteolytic hypercalcemia), with suppressed PTH and high bone-resorption markers; bisphosphonates/denosumab are key.
⚠️ Local osteolytic hypercalcemia (direct lysis) is mechanistically distinct from PTHrP humoral hypercalcemia — both occur in cancer but PTHrP gives low phosphate.
15. Acute kidney injury phases
AKI alters calcium handling: oliguric phase tends toward hypocalcemia (phosphate retention), while recovery/diuretic phases and rhabdo-AKI can flip to hypercalcemia as deposits remobilize.
⚠️ Calcium direction in AKI depends on the phase — don't assume a single trend across the illness course.
