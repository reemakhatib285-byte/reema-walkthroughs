📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Macro-ovalocyte
Oval macrocytes (large, egg-shaped RBCs) signal MEGALOBLASTIC anemia — B12 or folate deficiency — and MDS. They appear with hypersegmented neutrophils because impaired DNA synthesis affects all marrow lineages. Both deficiencies raise homocysteine; only B12 deficiency also raises methylmalonic acid (MMA).
⚠️ WRONG answer: treating presumed megaloblastic anemia with folate alone. Discriminator: if it's actually B12 deficiency, folate masks the anemia while subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord progresses — confirm B12 with MMA and replace B12 first.
2. Spherocyte
Spherocytes are small, round, hyperchromatic RBCs lacking central pallor, from loss of membrane surface area. Two big causes: hereditary spherocytosis (membrane cytoskeleton defect, e.g., ankyrin/spectrin) and warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia (macrophages bite off antibody-coated membrane). MCHC is often high; osmotic fragility/EMA binding test for HS.
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming spherocytes always mean hereditary spherocytosis. Discriminator: the direct Coombs (DAT) separates them — POSITIVE = warm AIHA; NEGATIVE with a family history/splenomegaly = hereditary spherocytosis (confirm with EMA binding or osmotic fragility).
3. Schistocyte
Schistocytes (helmet/fragmented cells) mean RBCs are sheared mechanically: microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (TTP, HUS, DIC, HELLP, malignant hypertension, scleroderma renal crisis) or macroangiopathic shear from a prosthetic heart valve. TTP pentad: MAHA + thrombocytopenia + neuro changes + renal + fever (ADAMTS13 deficiency).
⚠️ WRONG answer: giving a platelet transfusion to a TTP patient with low platelets. Discriminator: in TTP, platelet transfusion 'fuels the fire' (more microthrombi) and is contraindicated unless life-threatening bleeding — treat with urgent plasma exchange. DIC (unlike TTP) has prolonged PT/PTT, low fibrinogen, high D-dimer.
4. Sickle cell
Sickle/crescent-shaped cells result from HbS polymerization on deoxygenation (Glu→Val substitution at β6). Seen in sickle cell disease, with vaso-occlusive crises, and often accompanied by Howell-Jolly bodies and target cells from autosplenectomy. Confirm with Hb electrophoresis (HbS predominant, no/low HbA in SS disease).
⚠️ WRONG answer: ordering a sickle solubility test (Sickledex) alone to diagnose disease vs trait. Discriminator: the solubility test is positive in both trait and disease and cannot distinguish them — use Hb electrophoresis to quantify HbS vs HbA and identify trait (HbAS) vs disease (HbSS).
5. Target cell
Target cells (codocytes) have a central bullseye from excess membrane relative to hemoglobin. Causes — mnemonic 'HALT': Hemoglobinopathies (thalassemia, HbC), Asplenia, Liver disease, Thalassemia. Also seen post-splenectomy.
⚠️ WRONG answer: interpreting a few target cells as diagnostic of a specific disease. Discriminator: target cells are nonspecific — correlate with MCV (low → thalassemia/HbC) vs liver disease (normal/high MCV, abnormal LFTs) and the rest of the smear/electrophoresis.
6. Basophilic stippling
Coarse basophilic stippling = punctate blue ribosomal/RNA aggregates throughout the RBC. Classic for LEAD poisoning (lead inhibits rRNA degradation via ferrochelatase/pyrimidine-5'-nucleotidase) and also thalassemia and sideroblastic anemia. Lead poisoning presents with abdominal pain, neuropathy/encephalopathy, microcytic anemia.
⚠️ WRONG answer: confusing basophilic stippling (diffuse, RNA, lead/thal) with a single Howell-Jolly body (one round DNA remnant, asplenia) or Pappenheimer bodies (iron, sideroblastic). Discriminator: stippling = MULTIPLE fine blue dots throughout the cell; Howell-Jolly = ONE solid purple inclusion = asplenia.
7. Howell-Jolly
Howell-Jolly bodies are single, round, dense nuclear (DNA) remnants normally pitted out by a functioning spleen. Their presence indicates ASPLENIA or functional hyposplenism (post-splenectomy, sickle cell autosplenectomy, celiac disease).
⚠️ WRONG answer: ignoring the infection risk implied by Howell-Jolly bodies. Discriminator: asplenia means high risk of overwhelming sepsis from encapsulated organisms (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, N. meningitidis) — these patients need vaccination and prompt antibiotics for febrile illness.
8. Teardrop cell
Teardrop cells (dacrocytes) form as RBCs are squeezed exiting a fibrotic/infiltrated marrow. Hallmark of primary MYELOFIBROSIS and other myelophthisic processes (marrow metastases, leukemia), typically with a leukoerythroblastic smear (immature myeloid cells + nucleated RBCs).
⚠️ WRONG answer: attempting a marrow ASPIRATE first in suspected myelofibrosis. Discriminator: aspiration gives a 'dry tap' because of fibrosis — you need a core BIOPSY (with reticulin stain) to show fibrosis. Primary myelofibrosis is associated with JAK2 mutation.
9. Rouleaux
Rouleaux = RBCs stacked like coins, caused by high plasma paraprotein/fibrinogen reducing surface charge. Classic for multiple myeloma/MGUS/Waldenström, also seen with marked inflammation. Prompts SPEP/UPEP, serum free light chains, and a high total-protein/elevated ESR.
⚠️ WRONG answer: mistaking rouleaux for autoagglutination. Discriminator: rouleaux is orderly coin-stacking (paraprotein, dispersible on dilution); agglutination is irregular clumping (cold agglutinin/IgM-mediated AIHA, positive Coombs for C3) — different causes and workup.
10. Hypersegmented neutrophil
Hypersegmented neutrophils (≥5–6 nuclear lobes) are an early, sensitive sign of MEGALOBLASTIC anemia from B12/folate deficiency (impaired DNA synthesis). They can appear before macrocytosis or anemia develop.
⚠️ WRONG answer: requiring a high MCV before considering B12/folate deficiency. Discriminator: hypersegmented neutrophils can precede a high MCV, and coexisting iron deficiency can normalize the MCV — check B12/folate (and MMA) even with a normal MCV if the smear shows hypersegmentation.
11. Toxic granulation / Döhle
Reactive/'left-shift' neutrophil changes of severe infection/sepsis: toxic granulation (coarse dark primary granules), Döhle bodies (blue cytoplasmic RER inclusions), cytoplasmic vacuoles, and bands/immature forms. Reflect accelerated marrow output under inflammatory stress.
⚠️ WRONG answer: interpreting a marked neutrophilia with left shift automatically as leukemia (CML). Discriminator: a leukemoid REACTION (infection) has high leukocyte alkaline phosphatase (LAP) and toxic changes, whereas CML has LOW LAP and the BCR-ABL/Philadelphia chromosome with basophilia.
12. Smudge cell
Smudge (basket) cells are fragile lymphocytes ruptured during smearing — characteristic of chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL), alongside an absolute lymphocytosis of small mature lymphocytes. Diagnose/confirm by flow cytometry (CD5+, CD19/CD20+, CD23+).
⚠️ WRONG answer: diagnosing CLL or acute leukemia from smudge cells alone on the smear. Discriminator: smudge cells suggest CLL but flow cytometry is required for diagnosis; do not confuse mature lymphocytosis (CLL) with blasts (acute leukemia) — blasts demand urgent marrow evaluation.
13. Marrow: biopsy vs aspirate
Core BIOPSY preserves marrow architecture — assesses cellularity, fibrosis (reticulin stain), and infiltration/metastases. ASPIRATE smears show individual cell morphology, blast percentage, and are used for flow cytometry, cytogenetics, and the M:E ratio. They are complementary; biopsy is done before aspiration in the sketch's workflow.
⚠️ WRONG answer: relying on aspirate alone when fibrosis or a 'dry tap' is suspected. Discriminator: fibrotic/infiltrated marrow (myelofibrosis, mets) yields a dry tap and needs the core BIOPSY to show fibrosis/architecture — aspirate cannot quantify fibrosis or cellularity.
14. M:E ratio 2.5:1
Normal marrow myeloid:erythroid (M:E) ratio is ~2–3:1 (≈2.5:1). A HIGH ratio = myeloid hyperplasia or erythroid hypoplasia (infection, CML, pure red cell aplasia); a LOW ratio = erythroid hyperplasia (hemolysis, blood loss recovery, B12/folate replacement) or myeloid suppression. Blasts ≥20% define acute leukemia.
⚠️ WRONG answer: reading a high M:E ratio as 'too many red cell precursors.' Discriminator: a HIGH M:E means relatively MORE myeloid (or fewer erythroid) cells; erythroid hyperplasia LOWERS the ratio. Always interpret M:E together with overall cellularity and the blast percentage.
