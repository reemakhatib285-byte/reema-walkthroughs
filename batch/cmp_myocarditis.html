📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. MI impersonator
Myocarditis is the great STEMI mimic: it presents with chest pain, ST elevation, and elevated troponin — identical to an acute MI. The discriminating clue is a YOUNG patient with a viral illness days to weeks prior. The board presents this scenario and asks the next step.
⚠️ The trap is calling it STEMI and giving thrombolytics or rushing to PCI. Discriminator: young age + viral prodrome + (when cath'd) normal coronaries. Next step is CMR, not thrombolytics.
2. Troponin (steak)
Troponin is elevated in myocarditis from myocyte injury and supports the diagnosis, but it is NOT specific — it rises in MI, myocarditis, Takotsubo, and MINOCA alike. Diagnosis requires the full picture: typical presentation + elevated troponin + CMR showing a nonischemic injury pattern.
⚠️ Trending troponin or relying on it to confirm myocarditis is wrong — it cannot distinguish myocarditis from MI. The confirming test is CMR, not a troponin trend or stress test.
3. ST-elevation
Myocarditis can produce ST elevation on ECG, completing the STEMI mimicry. Combined with chest pain and troponin, the ECG looks ischemic. The key is that the injury does not follow a single coronary territory.
⚠️ Diffuse or non-coronary-distribution ST changes (and PR depression if pericarditis overlaps) hint away from STEMI. A regional ST elevation matching one coronary territory plus an occluded vessel = true MI.
4. Normal coronaries / MINOCA
When ST elevation and troponin rise but catheterization shows clean coronaries, this is MINOCA (Myocardial Infarction with Non-Obstructive Coronary Arteries). Myocarditis accounts for up to one-third of MINOCA cases and is the most common cause in young patients with a viral prodrome.
⚠️ MINOCA differential also includes Takotsubo, coronary spasm, and SCAD. In a 28-year-old post-viral patient with normal coronaries, the most likely diagnosis is MYOCARDITIS — confirm with CMR.
5. CMR gold standard ★
Cardiac MRI is the GOLD STANDARD non-invasive test. It shows myocardial edema on T2 imaging and nonischemic late gadolinium enhancement — subepicardial or midmyocardial, NOT subendocardial as in infarct (Lake Louise criteria). It also characterizes fibrosis for risk stratification.
⚠️ Best next test to confirm myocarditis = CMR. NOT endomyocardial biopsy, not a troponin trend, not a stress test. Subendocardial LGE = infarct; subepicardial/midmyocardial LGE = myocarditis.
6. Nonischemic LGE pattern
The late gadolinium enhancement in myocarditis is NONISCHEMIC: it sits subepicardially or midmyocardially and crosses coronary territories, because the injury is inflammatory rather than from a vessel occlusion. This pattern plus T2 edema confirms the diagnosis.
⚠️ Subendocardial-to-transmural LGE in a coronary distribution = infarction. If LGE follows a single coronary territory from the subendocardium out, think MI, not myocarditis.
7. Biopsy bouncer (4 types)
Endomyocardial biopsy is NOT routine. It is reserved for four scenarios where it changes management: giant-cell myocarditis, eosinophilic myocarditis, ICI (checkpoint-inhibitor) myocarditis, and rapid progression / arrhythmia or conduction block when CMR is nondiagnostic.
⚠️ Board trap: presumed viral myocarditis with NSVT — is biopsy needed? NO. NSVT alone in a typical viral context does not mandate biopsy. Biopsy only when one of the four specific indications applies.
8. Giant-cell myocarditis
Giant-cell myocarditis is aggressive and often fatal, presenting with refractory heart failure, ventricular arrhythmias, or heart block. It MUST be identified by biopsy because it requires immunosuppression (and often transplant evaluation). This is the prototype of a biopsy-mandating myocarditis.
⚠️ A young patient with NEW myocarditis plus high-grade AV block or refractory VT should trigger biopsy for giant-cell — do not assume benign viral. Missing giant-cell means missing life-saving immunosuppression.
9. Three-tap severity
Severity drives management. GREEN/uncomplicated (EF ≥50%, no significant arrhythmia, no shock): supportive care + NSAIDs for pain, excellent prognosis (~95% transplant-free survival at 5 years). AMBER/complicated (EF <50%): HFrEF GDMT — ACEi, beta-blocker, MRA. RED/fulminant (cardiogenic shock): ICU, inotropes, mechanical circulatory support.
⚠️ Do NOT give exercise/competitive sports during active myocarditis (arrhythmia/SCD risk) — abstain ~3-6 months. And NSAIDs are for pain in mild cases; in HFrEF myocarditis use GDMT, not NSAIDs.
10. Fulminant recovers
Fulminant myocarditis (cardiogenic shock) paradoxically has a GOOD prognosis if the patient is bridged through the acute phase — roughly 50% recover to near-normal EF. Aggressive support (ICU, inotropes, MCS such as ECMO/Impella) buys time for recovery.
⚠️ Do not withdraw support prematurely in fulminant myocarditis — early appearance is dramatic but recovery is common with support. This is the opposite of the intuitive 'too sick to save' answer.
11. Steroids locked (no viral)
Do NOT routinely immunosuppress presumed viral myocarditis. Corticosteroids impair viral clearance and worsen outcomes in standard lymphocytic/viral myocarditis. The locked cabinet = steroids off-limits for the common viral case.
⚠️ Steroids ARE appropriate only for the biopsy-confirmed types (giant-cell, eosinophilic, ICI, sarcoid-related). Reflexively steroid-treating viral myocarditis is the trap — it worsens outcomes.
12. ICI myocarditis = EMERGENCY
Immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) myocarditis is a true EMERGENCY: a cancer patient on a checkpoint inhibitor develops troponin elevation with arrhythmia or heart block. Management = STOP immunotherapy immediately + high-dose corticosteroids. Mortality is substantial and delay is fatal.
⚠️ ICI myocarditis is the ONE setting where high-dose steroids are urgently correct — the opposite of viral myocarditis. The vignette is a cancer patient on pembrolizumab/nivolumab with new troponin/heart block.
13. Chagas (DCM + arrhythmia)
Chagas disease (Trypanosoma cruzi) is endemic in Latin America and causes a dilated cardiomyopathy with prominent ventricular arrhythmias and a classic RBBB pattern (often with left anterior fascicular block / apical aneurysm). Consider it in any DCM patient from Latin America — it has a specific antiparasitic protocol.
⚠️ DCM + ventricular arrhythmias + RBBB in a patient from Latin America = think Chagas, not idiopathic DCM. Missing the epidemiologic clue means missing specific treatment.
14. Viral titers useless
Routine viral serology (Coxsackie titers, adenovirus titers) has NO diagnostic value in acute myocarditis — low sensitivity, low specificity, and it does not change management. Do not send viral titers; do CMR instead.
⚠️ Ordering viral titers to 'confirm viral myocarditis' is a classic wrong answer. The serology won't change management — the correct next step is CMR.
15. Eosinophilic myocarditis
Eosinophilic myocarditis is one of the four biopsy-mandating types and RESPONDS to steroids. It is associated with hypersensitivity/drug reactions, hypereosinophilic syndromes, and parasitic disease, often with peripheral eosinophilia. Biopsy confirms and steroids treat it.
⚠️ Peripheral eosinophilia + new heart failure/myocarditis = eosinophilic myocarditis, a steroid-responsive type — unlike viral myocarditis where steroids are harmful. The eosinophil clue flips the steroid decision.
