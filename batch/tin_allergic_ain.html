📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. NSAID (angry pill)
NSAIDs are a leading cause of drug-induced acute interstitial nephritis (AIN). Unlike antibiotic-induced AIN, NSAID AIN tends to have a LONGER latency (months), MINIMAL allergic features (often no fever/rash/eosinophilia), and uniquely can coexist with NEPHROTIC-range proteinuria from concurrent minimal change disease (MCD). Mechanism is T-cell mediated (type IV) hypersensitivity to the drug-modified tubular antigen, not dose-dependent toxicity. NSAIDs also cause hemodynamic AKI (afferent vasoconstriction) — a separate mechanism.
⚠️ Heavy (nephrotic-range) proteinuria does not exclude AIN — NSAID AIN can present with MCD-pattern nephrotic syndrome; calling it 'primary glomerular disease' and missing the NSAID is the trap.
2. Antibiotics list
Antibiotics are the classic and most common drug cause of AIN. High-yield offenders: beta-lactams (methicillin is the historic prototype; penicillins, cephalosporins), sulfonamides (including TMP-SMX), fluoroquinolones, vancomycin, and rifampin. The reaction is idiosyncratic/hypersensitivity (NOT dose-related) and can recur faster and more severely on re-exposure.
⚠️ AIN is a hypersensitivity reaction, not dose-dependent toxicity — assuming a 'normal/therapeutic dose' rules out drug AIN is wrong; even a single standard course can trigger it.
3. PD-1 checkpoint inhibitor
Immune checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1 nivolumab/pembrolizumab, anti-PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4 ipilimumab) are an increasingly common cause of AIN as an immune-related adverse event. Onset is often DELAYED (weeks to months after starting), and concurrent PPI/NSAID use raises risk. Management may require holding the checkpoint inhibitor and high-dose glucocorticoids, often without permanent discontinuation depending on severity.
⚠️ In an oncology patient on immunotherapy, rising creatinine is easily blamed on contrast or pre-renal causes — missing checkpoint-inhibitor AIN (which needs steroids, not just fluids) is the trap.
4. PPI sleeping pharmacist
Proton pump inhibitors (omeprazole and the other '-prazoles') are a very common, often OVERLOOKED cause of AIN. Onset is typically insidious/late (weeks to many months) with few classic allergic features, so it is easily missed. PPIs are also linked to chronic interstitial nephritis and CKD with prolonged use. Always review the med list for PPIs in unexplained AKI.
⚠️ Because PPIs are OTC and seem benign, they are forgotten on the med reconciliation — overlooking a chronic PPI as the AIN trigger is a classic miss.
5. Sulfonamides/cipro/rifampin
Sulfonamides, fluoroquinolones, and rifampin are reinforced offenders. Note sulfonamides have a DUAL renal toxicity — hypersensitivity AIN AND crystal-induced (crystalluria/tubular obstruction) injury. Rifampin is notable for causing severe AIN especially with intermittent or interrupted-then-resumed dosing (re-challenge).
⚠️ Sulfa-related AKI can be crystal nephropathy rather than AIN — distinguish by urinalysis (crystals/hematuria with obstruction vs sterile pyuria/WBC casts of AIN); lumping them together is the trap.
6. Fever + rash + eosinophilia triad
The classic hypersensitivity triad of AIN is FEVER + RASH (maculopapular) + EOSINOPHILIA, often with arthralgias and peripheral eosinophilia on CBC. Critically, the COMPLETE triad is present in only a MINORITY (~10% or fewer) of patients — most have one or none. Fever is the single most common component; the full triad is the exception, not the rule.
⚠️ Requiring the full triad before diagnosing AIN is wrong — the complete fever+rash+eosinophilia combination occurs in only ~10% of cases, so its absence cannot be used to exclude AIN.
7. NO RASH NO EOS
Absence of rash and a normal eosinophil count do NOT rule out AIN — these features are insensitive, and many biopsy-proven cases (especially NSAID- and PPI-induced) lack them entirely. The diagnosis rests on the clinical picture (offending drug + AKI + bland-to-pyuric sediment) and is confirmed by renal BIOPSY when needed. Maintain a low threshold to suspect AIN in unexplained AKI.
⚠️ 'Eosinophils are normal, so it can't be AIN' is a classic distractor — normal eosinophils (and no rash) absolutely do not exclude AIN; biopsy is the gold standard.
8. Urine eos not useful
Urine eosinophils (Hansel/Wright stain) have POOR sensitivity AND specificity for AIN (also positive in pyelonephritis, atheroembolic disease, prostatitis, cystitis) and are NO LONGER recommended as a diagnostic test. They neither confirm nor exclude AIN. Renal biopsy remains the definitive test when the diagnosis is uncertain.
⚠️ Ordering urinary eosinophils to confirm or rule out AIN is outdated — a negative result does not exclude it and a positive result is nonspecific; relying on the test is the trap.
9. Sterile pyuria + WBC casts
The characteristic urinalysis in AIN is STERILE PYURIA (white cells with negative culture), WBC CASTS, hematuria, and mild (sub-nephrotic) proteinuria. WBC casts point to tubulointerstitial inflammation. Eosinophil casts are NOT a reliable finding. Contrast this with the muddy-brown granular casts of ATN and the RBC/dysmorphic-RBC casts of glomerulonephritis.
⚠️ Sterile pyuria + WBC casts in the right drug context is AIN — misreading it as a 'culture-negative UTI/pyelonephritis' and treating with more antibiotics (potentially the offending drug class) is the trap.
10. 7-10 days latency
Typical latency from drug exposure to AIN is days to weeks — classically ~7–10 days after FIRST exposure, but it can be as short as 1–2 days on RE-exposure (sensitized) and as long as weeks-to-months for NSAIDs/PPIs. The temporal link to a new drug is a key diagnostic clue; always build a careful medication timeline.
⚠️ AKI appearing only a couple of days after starting a drug doesn't argue against AIN if the patient was previously sensitized — accelerated onset on re-exposure is expected, so a short latency does not exclude AIN.
11. Stop drug — first step
The single most important first-line management step is to IDENTIFY and STOP the offending drug — many patients recover renal function spontaneously after withdrawal. Observe for improvement over roughly a week. Avoid re-introducing the culprit (and cross-reacting agents). Provide supportive care for the AKI in the interim.
⚠️ Adding steroids while continuing the offending drug is wrong — discontinuation of the culprit is the essential first step; steroids without stopping the drug will not resolve AIN.
12. Steroids ± dialysis
If renal function does not recover after stopping the drug (or if biopsy shows severe/extensive inflammation), give GLUCOCORTICOIDS (e.g., prednisone ~0.5–1 mg/kg/day, or IV methylprednisolone pulse for severe disease, tapered over several weeks). EARLY treatment matters — delayed steroid initiation (beyond ~1–2 weeks, or progression to fibrosis) predicts worse, sometimes irreversible, renal recovery. Dialysis is supportive for severe AKI/uremia.
⚠️ Indefinitely 'watching' a non-recovering AIN before starting steroids is harmful — delaying glucocorticoids allows interstitial fibrosis and permanent CKD; timely steroids are key once drug withdrawal alone fails.
13. Rifampin re-challenge
Rifampin is a special, severe cause of immune AIN, classically triggered by INTERMITTENT or interrupted-then-resumed dosing ('re-challenge'). It is associated with rifampin-dependent antibodies and can produce an abrupt, severe immunoallergic reaction (fever, AKI, sometimes hemolysis/thrombocytopenia/flu-like syndrome). Never re-challenge with rifampin after a suspected reaction.
⚠️ Restarting rifampin after a prior interruption (assuming the earlier reaction was unrelated) can precipitate fulminant immune AIN — re-challenge is contraindicated, and treating the reaction as a simple 'flu' is the trap.
