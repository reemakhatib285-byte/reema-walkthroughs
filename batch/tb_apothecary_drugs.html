📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Standard regimen: 2 HRZE + 4 HR
Drug-susceptible active TB: 2-month intensive phase with Rifampin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol, then 4-month continuation with Rifampin + Isoniazid (total 6 months).
⚠️ Don't drop to 2 drugs in the intensive phase — 4 drugs prevent resistance until susceptibilities return.
2. Hepatotoxicity stop rule
INH, RIF, and PZA are all hepatotoxic. Stop all if ALT/AST >3× ULN with symptoms or >5× ULN without symptoms, then reintroduce one drug at a time.
⚠️ Asymptomatic mild transaminase rise does NOT require stopping — threshold is higher (5× vs 3×) when asymptomatic.
3. Reintroduce one at a time / Rifampin first
After drug-induced hepatitis resolves, rechallenge sequentially. Rifampin is reintroduced first because it is the least hepatotoxic of the three.
⚠️ Restarting all drugs at once hides which agent caused hepatitis — reintroduce singly.
4. Isoniazid → B6 / peripheral neuropathy
INH causes pyridoxine (B6) deficiency → peripheral neuropathy. Give prophylactic B6 to those at risk: pregnant, diabetic, alcoholic, malnourished, HIV, uremic.
⚠️ Neuropathy is treated/prevented with B6, not by stopping INH; INH also causes drug-induced LUPUS and hepatitis.
5. INH drug-induced lupus
INH is a classic cause of drug-induced lupus (anti-histone antibodies), along with procainamide and hydralazine.
⚠️ Drug-induced lupus from INH spares kidneys/CNS and is anti-histone positive; it resolves on stopping the drug.
6. Rifampin = CYP inducer
Rifampin is a potent CYP450 INDUCER, lowering levels of warfarin, OCPs, protease inhibitors, methadone. It also causes orange body-fluid discoloration and thrombocytopenia.
⚠️ Rifampin INDUCES (not inhibits) CYP — it speeds metabolism and lowers drug levels; INH is the CYP inhibitor.
7. Rifampin → orange secretions
Rifampin harmlessly turns urine, sweat, and tears orange-red and can permanently stain soft contact lenses.
⚠️ Orange fluids are benign and expected — not a reason to stop therapy; reassure the patient.
8. Pyrazinamide most hepatotoxic
PZA is the most hepatotoxic first-line agent and also causes hyperuricemia/arthralgia. It works in acidic intracellular/caseous environments.
⚠️ PZA-related arthralgia/hyperuricemia is usually NOT true gout and you continue the drug; stop only for true hepatotoxicity.
9. PZA hyperuricemia → continue
PZA inhibits renal urate excretion causing hyperuricemia and arthralgias; this is expected and you continue therapy.
⚠️ Asymptomatic hyperuricemia on PZA does NOT need treatment or drug cessation.
10. PZA avoided in pregnancy
In US practice PZA is often avoided in pregnancy (safety data limited); the pregnancy regimen is RIF + INH + EMB for 9 months. INH, RIF, EMB are considered safe.
⚠️ Streptomycin is the agent absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy (ototoxic to fetus), not the oral first-line drugs.
11. Ethambutol → optic neuritis
Ethambutol causes dose-dependent optic neuritis with red-green color blindness and decreased acuity. Check visual acuity/color monthly and STOP permanently if it occurs.
⚠️ Optic neuritis is the EMB (ethambutol) side effect — the panel's label is a deliberate distractor; EMB toxicity is irreversible-risk so stop, don't rechallenge.
12. MDR-TB / BPaLM regimen
MDR-TB (resistant to INH + RIF) is treated with the 6-month BPaLM regimen: Bedaquiline, Pretomanid, Linezolid, Moxifloxacin. XDR adds fluoroquinolone/second-line injectable resistance.
⚠️ Bedaquiline and moxifloxacin prolong QT — BPaLM is contraindicated if QTc >500 ms.
13. QTc >500 ms contraindicated
Bedaquiline (and moxifloxacin) cause QT prolongation; monitor ECG and avoid initiating if QTc exceeds 500 ms or with other QT-prolonging drugs.
⚠️ This QT rule is for the MDR (BPaLM) drugs, not first-line RIPE.
14. LTBI regimens: 3HP / 4R / INH
Latent TB options: 3HP (weekly INH+rifapentine ×12, preferred), 4R (rifampin ×4 months), or INH ×9 months. Shorter rifamycin-based regimens improve completion.
⚠️ Exclude ACTIVE TB before starting LTBI monotherapy — treating active disease with one drug breeds resistance.
15. Exclude active TB before TPT
Always rule out active TB (symptoms, CXR, sputum) before starting single/dual-drug preventive therapy; otherwise you select for resistance.
⚠️ Never give LTBI monotherapy to someone with undiagnosed active disease.
