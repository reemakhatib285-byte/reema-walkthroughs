📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Rifaximin bottle (gold star)
Rifaximin is the gold-standard antibiotic for IBS-D (non-constipation diarrhea-predominant). Non-absorbed (gut-luminal) so minimal systemic effect; targets small-bowel bacterial overgrowth. Typical course 550 mg TID × 14 days; it works again on retreatment after relapse.
2. Works after stopping glass
Rifaximin's benefit persists after a finite course, and a repeat course is effective if symptoms recur — no tolerance to its antibacterial effect. This durable, re-treatable response is a key tested feature.
3. Gut-brain catwalk bridge
The gut-brain axis is the therapeutic target for neuromodulators. Low-dose TCAs (e.g., amitriptyline 10–25 mg) provide visceral analgesia and slow transit (helpful in IBS-D) through this axis — at doses far below antidepressant dosing.
4. Mood crossed out (red X)
Low-dose TCAs in IBS work via gut-brain visceral analgesia, NOT via their antidepressant/mood effect. The analgesic mechanism is independent of and at lower dose than the mood effect — a classic board distinction.
⚠️ "TCAs help IBS through their antidepressant effect" → wrong; benefit is low-dose visceral analgesia via the gut-brain axis.
5. Shelf agents: psyllium / PEG / cholestyramine
Adjuncts: soluble psyllium fiber for global/IBS-C symptoms; PEG osmotic laxative for IBS-C constipation; cholestyramine (bile-acid sequestrant) for bile-acid diarrhea presenting as IBS-D. For IBS-C also linaclotide and lubiprostone (secretagogues).
6. Bartender gut
The friendly gut anchors the IBS patient receiving subtype-tailored therapy. Treatment is matched to subtype: IBS-D (rifaximin, loperamide, cholestyramine, low-dose TCA) vs IBS-C (PEG, fiber, linaclotide/lubiprostone).
7. Low-FODMAP helps (green check)
A low-FODMAP diet genuinely reduces IBS symptoms (bloating, pain, diarrhea) and is evidence-based first-line dietary therapy. Reintroduction phase identifies triggers; it is implemented with a dietitian to avoid nutritional gaps.
8. Doubter 'would not work' (red X)
FALSE claim that low-FODMAP won't help. Evidence supports low-FODMAP as effective first-line dietary management of IBS — the doubter is wrong.
⚠️ "Low-FODMAP diet won't help IBS" → wrong; it is evidence-based and effective first-line dietary therapy.
9. Ciprofloxacin bottle (red X)
Ciprofloxacin is NOT the IBS-D antibiotic — rifaximin is. Systemic fluoroquinolones carry tendon/QT/aortic and C. difficile risks and have no role in functional IBS.
⚠️ "Ciprofloxacin treats IBS-D" → wrong; rifaximin is the IBS-D antibiotic.
10. Metronidazole bottle (red X)
Metronidazole is not used for IBS-D; it targets anaerobes/protozoa (e.g., C. diff, Giardia). Rifaximin is the correct non-absorbed agent for IBS-D.
⚠️ "Metronidazole is the IBS-D antibiotic" → wrong; rifaximin is the one.
11. Fidaxomicin bottle (red X)
Fidaxomicin is for C. difficile infection, not IBS-D. Although also gut-selective, it is not an IBS therapy — rifaximin is.
⚠️ "Fidaxomicin treats IBS-D" → wrong; it is for C. difficile, not IBS; rifaximin is correct.
12. Loperamide 'same dose forever' capsule
Loperamide controls IBS-D diarrhea with NO tolerance and NO dose escalation (the crossed-out staircase). It slows transit via gut μ-opioid receptors without central effect. Does not treat pain, so pair with a neuromodulator if pain dominant.
⚠️ "Loperamide requires progressive dose escalation" → wrong; it works at a stable dose with no tolerance.
