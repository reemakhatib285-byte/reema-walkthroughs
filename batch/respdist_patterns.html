📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. IPF / fibrosis patient
Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis = RESTRICTIVE: ↓TLC, ↓FRC, ↓RV, with a NORMAL or HIGH FEV1/FVC ratio (both numerator and denominator fall), and a LOW DLCO from membrane destruction. CT shows basal/subpleural reticulation, traction bronchiectasis, and honeycombing (UIP pattern); diagnosed >50–60 yrs, treated with antifibrotics (pirfenidone, nintedanib).
⚠️ Low DLCO + restriction = fibrosis/ILD, distinguishing it from chest-wall/neuromuscular restriction (NORMAL DLCO). DLCO often drops before lung volumes do, so it's the earliest marker.
2. IPF ↓DLCO tag
DLCO is reduced in fibrosis because alveolar surface area is destroyed and the membrane is thickened — a diffusion limitation that also widens the A–a gradient and causes exertional desaturation.
⚠️ A NORMAL DLCO essentially argues against significant interstitial fibrosis — if restriction has a normal DLCO, look extrapulmonary.
3. Myasthenia gravis
Neuromuscular weakness (MG, ALS, Guillain-Barré, myopathy) = RESTRICTION with a NORMAL DLCO and normal FEV1/FVC; TLC and FVC fall, RV may rise (incomplete exhalation), and a supine FVC drop signals diaphragm weakness. Confirm MG with anti-AChR antibodies.
⚠️ Normal DLCO with restriction = neuromuscular/chest-wall cause, NOT parenchymal lung disease. Check maximal inspiratory/expiratory pressures (MIP/MEP) and supine FVC to localize.
4. Broken pump / filter fine
In neuromuscular disease the gas-exchange 'filter' (alveolar membrane) is intact while the muscular 'pump' fails. The result is hypoventilation: rising PaCO2 with a NORMAL A–a gradient and preserved DLCO.
⚠️ Hypoxemia here is HYPOVENTILATION with a NORMAL A–a gradient, not a diffusion defect — and the treatment is ventilatory support (NIV), not just oxygen.
5. Obesity patient
Obesity: ↓FRC and ↓ERV first (chest-wall and abdominal mass), then mildly ↓TLC; FEV1/FVC is preserved and DLCO is NORMAL (or high). Can progress to obesity hypoventilation syndrome (daytime hypercapnia + OSA).
⚠️ Obesity gives a restrictive-LOOKING pattern but with a NORMAL/high DLCO — not fibrosis. The earliest change is a fall in ERV/FRC, not TLC.
6. FRC ↓ in obesity
Reduced FRC and ERV from chest-wall mass push tidal breathing toward closing volume, causing basal atelectasis and mild V/Q mismatch → mild hypoxemia that is WORSE supine.
⚠️ The EARLIEST obesity PFT change is a fall in ERV/FRC — TLC and the FEV1/FVC ratio stay relatively preserved until late.
7. Spirometry/plethysmograph
Spirometry measures FEV1, FVC, and flows: FEV1/FVC <0.7 confirms OBSTRUCTION. To prove RESTRICTION you need lung volumes (TLC) by body plethysmography or gas dilution — spirometry alone cannot measure TLC, FRC, or RV.
⚠️ Spirometry diagnoses obstruction; a low FVC on spirometry only SUGGESTS restriction — you must confirm with a low TLC (plethysmography), or it may be pseudo-restriction/air trapping.
8. Emphysema patient
Emphysema = OBSTRUCTION with hyperinflation: ↓FEV1/FVC, ↑TLC, ↑↑RV (here 310%), ↑FRC, and DLCO ↓↓↓ from destroyed alveolar surface area. Loss of elastic recoil drives air trapping; consider alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency if early-onset or basilar/panacinar.
⚠️ Low DLCO separates emphysema from chronic bronchitis and asthma, where DLCO is NORMAL or high — the single best discriminator among obstructive diseases.
9. FVC ↓ from RV too high
In severe emphysema, air trapping raises RV so much that there's less volume left to exhale, so FVC FALLS — producing a low FVC that can masquerade as restriction (pseudo-restriction). But TLC is HIGH, not low.
⚠️ A low FVC in emphysema is air TRAPPING (high TLC), NOT coexisting true restriction (which requires a low TLC). Check TLC to tell them apart.
10. Central airway collapse spike
Emphysema's flow-volume loop shows a brief initial expiratory spike followed by rapid airway collapse and a markedly CONCAVE ('scooped') expiratory limb — loss of recoil collapses small airways early in exhalation.
⚠️ The scooped/concave expiratory limb signals diffuse SMALL-airway obstruction, NOT upper/central-airway obstruction (which gives flat plateaus).
11. Restriction confirmed (MD)
Restriction is CONFIRMED by a low TLC (here 60%) with a preserved/high FEV1/FVC ratio; once confirmed, DLCO localizes the cause (low = parenchymal ILD; normal = chest wall/neuromuscular/obesity).
⚠️ A low FVC with a normal ratio is restriction ONLY if TLC is also low — otherwise it's pseudo-restriction (air trapping/poor effort), so always confirm with lung volumes.
12. Pulmonary HTN patient
Pulmonary hypertension / pulmonary vascular disease: normal spirometry + normal lung volumes + an ISOLATED low DLCO (loss of the pulmonary capillary bed for CO uptake). Suspect with exertional dyspnea, loud P2, and a low DLCO out of proportion to other PFTs.
⚠️ Isolated low DLCO with normal volumes and normal spirometry points to VASCULAR disease (PAH/CTEPH), not parenchymal or airway disease.
13. Isolated DLCO drop sign
An isolated DLCO reduction with otherwise normal PFTs is the classic clue to pulmonary VASCULAR disease (PAH, chronic PE/CTEPH), and can also appear in EARLY ILD or with anemia (correct for Hgb first).
⚠️ Don't dismiss an isolated low DLCO as artifact — after Hgb correction it is a real, high-yield pointer to pulmonary vascular disease or early interstitial disease.
