📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Strep — penicillin/ceftriaxone (green snake + star)
Penicillin-susceptible viridans strep / S. gallolyticus IE: penicillin G or ceftriaxone 2 g IV daily x4 weeks, or a 2-week course with added gentamicin synergy for uncomplicated native-valve disease. Ceftriaxone's once-daily dosing suits outpatient parenteral therapy; penicillin allergy → vancomycin.
2. Gentamicin = synergy only (small knight)
Aminoglycosides are used only for SYNERGY, never as monotherapy: they are not bactericidal alone against strep and only work alongside a cell-wall agent. Gentamicin can shorten susceptible-strep therapy to 2 weeks and is REQUIRED for enterococcal cure, but routine gentamicin is NO LONGER recommended for native-valve S. aureus (nephrotoxicity without benefit).
⚠️ 'Add gentamicin to native-valve S. aureus for synergy' -> wrong; routine aminoglycoside is no longer recommended in native-valve staph IE — its role is strep dose-shortening and enterococcal synergy, and used alone it is not bactericidal against strep.
3. MSSA — nafcillin (not vanc)
Methicillin-SUSCEPTIBLE S. aureus native valve: nafcillin or oxacillin 2 g IV q4h (or cefazolin 2 g q8h) for 6 weeks. Beta-lactams are bactericidal and clinically SUPERIOR to vancomycin for MSSA (faster clearance, fewer relapses), so empiric vancomycin must be de-escalated once MSSA is confirmed.
⚠️ 'Use vancomycin for MSSA IE because it covers staph' -> wrong; vancomycin is INFERIOR to nafcillin/oxacillin/cefazolin for MSSA (slower kill, more failures) — reserve vancomycin for MRSA or true severe beta-lactam allergy, hence the crossed-out mallet.
4. MRSA — vancomycin/daptomycin (spiky sun)
Methicillin-RESISTANT S. aureus native valve: vancomycin (trough 15–20 or AUC/MIC ≥400) OR daptomycin (≥8–10 mg/kg IV daily) for 6 weeks. Daptomycin is preferred for vancomycin MIC ≥2 or treatment failure; it is inactivated by lung surfactant, so it is fine for right-sided IE with pulmonary emboli but NOT for pneumonia.
⚠️ 'If vancomycin fails or the MIC creeps up, just push higher vancomycin doses' -> wrong; switch to daptomycin (or ceftaroline-based salvage) for vancomycin MIC ≥2 or persistent MRSA bacteremia — and remember daptomycin is useless for pneumonia due to surfactant inactivation.
5. Prosthetic valve — backbone + rifampin + gentamicin
Staphylococcal PROSTHETIC-valve endocarditis needs triple therapy: backbone (nafcillin/cefazolin for MSSA, vancomycin for MRSA) PLUS rifampin (penetrates/sterilizes biofilm on foreign material) PLUS gentamicin for the first 2 weeks, with backbone + rifampin continued ≥6 weeks. Start rifampin only AFTER bacteremia is controlled (to avoid selecting resistance).
⚠️ 'Add rifampin to native-valve S. aureus' / 'start rifampin first or alone' -> wrong; rifampin is reserved for PROSTHETIC/foreign-material (biofilm) infection and must be added after the backbone controls bacteremia — it has no role in native-valve staph IE and risks resistance/hepatotoxicity, hence the red X.
6. Surgery bell — emergent/urgent/elective
Surgical urgency is tiered. EMERGENT (same day): refractory pulmonary edema or cardiogenic shock from acute regurgitation — heart failure is the leading indication and cause of death. URGENT (1-2 days): perivalvular abscess/new heart block, persistent infection despite ≥5-7 days of appropriate antibiotics, large mobile vegetation with recurrent emboli, prosthetic dehiscence, fungal/resistant organisms. ELECTIVE: stable patients with a firm indication who can finish more antibiotics first. Still, >50% of IE (especially right-sided IVDU) is cured medically.
⚠️ 'Delay surgery to finish the full antibiotic course before operating on complicated IE' -> wrong; in HF, abscess, persistent infection, or large embolizing vegetations, early surgery improves survival and remaining antibiotics are given postop — but don't over-call surgery for uncomplicated/right-sided disease, which is usually medical.
7. Prophylaxis — highest-risk only (gate bouncer)
Antibiotic prophylaxis is ONLY for conditions at highest risk of an adverse IE outcome: (1) prosthetic valve or prosthetic repair material, (2) prior IE, (3) unrepaired cyanotic congenital heart disease, (4) repaired CHD with prosthetic material within the first 6 months, (5) repaired CHD with a residual defect at/near the prosthetic site, (6) cardiac transplant recipients with valvulopathy. Since 2007 (AHA), prophylaxis for the general valve population was dropped — most bacteremia comes from daily activities, not procedures.
8. Starred hearts admitted (prosthetic / prior IE / cyanotic CHD)
The classic admitted three: ALL prosthetic valve types (mechanical, bioprosthetic, TAVI/TAVR, repair material) — any prosthetic material is a high-risk nidus; PRIOR IE — a permanent indication because it strongly predicts recurrence; and unrepaired CYANOTIC congenital heart disease (TOF, transposition, single-ventricle, palliative shunts). Also on the list: repaired CHD with prosthetic material <6 months, residual defect near a patch, and transplant valvulopathy.
⚠️ 'Only mechanical valves (not bioprosthetic/TAVI) get prophylaxis' or 'a recovered prior-IE patient no longer needs it' -> wrong; ALL prosthetic valve types qualify equally, and prior IE is a PERMANENT high-risk indication regardless of how long ago recovery occurred.
9. Red-X hearts barred (MVP / pacemaker / native AS-MS)
NOT high-risk, so NO prophylaxis: mitral valve prolapse (even with regurgitation), HCM, an isolated pacemaker/ICD without valve disease, and acquired native-valve disease (mitral stenosis, aortic stenosis, rheumatic disease, bicuspid aortic valve). These were covered pre-2007, which is exactly why they are favorite distractors.
⚠️ 'Give amoxicillin before dental work for MVP, HCM, a pacemaker, or rheumatic mitral/aortic stenosis' -> wrong; none of these are on the high-risk list — only prosthetic valves/material, prior IE, specific cyanotic CHD, and transplant valvulopathy qualify. Over-prescribing prophylaxis is the most tested error.
10. Dental procedures only (giant tooth)
Even in high-risk patients, prophylaxis is given ONLY for procedures manipulating GINGIVAL tissue or the PERIAPICAL region, or PERFORATING oral mucosa (extractions, periodontal surgery, implants, scaling), and certain invasive respiratory-tract procedures. Routine cleanings, simple fillings, and shedding deciduous teeth do NOT qualify. GI/GU procedures (colonoscopy, cystoscopy, EGD) do NOT require prophylaxis unless there is active infection.
⚠️ 'A high-risk patient needs prophylaxis before a screening colonoscopy/cystoscopy, or a routine cleaning' -> wrong; only gingival/periapical/oral-mucosa-perforating dental (and some respiratory) procedures qualify — GI/GU endoscopy and routine cleanings do not unless active infection is present.
11. Amoxicillin 2 g PO single dose
First-line prophylaxis: amoxicillin 2 g PO (children 50 mg/kg, max 2 g) as a SINGLE dose 30–60 minutes before the procedure (can be given up to 2 h after if missed). If NPO: ampicillin 2 g IV/IM, or cefazolin/ceftriaxone 1 g IV/IM. Penicillin allergy: cephalexin 2 g PO (avoid if anaphylaxis), azithromycin/clarithromycin 500 mg, or doxycycline 100 mg.
⚠️ 'Give a multi-day course of amoxicillin around the procedure' or 'an NPO patient just skips prophylaxis' -> wrong; prophylaxis is a SINGLE pre-procedure dose, and NPO patients switch to a parenteral agent (ampicillin 2 g IV/IM or cefazolin/ceftriaxone 1 g) rather than omitting it.
12. Clindamycin removed (red-X pills)
Clindamycin has been REMOVED from IE prophylaxis (2021 AHA update) because of disproportionate C. difficile colitis and adverse events. Current penicillin-allergy options are cephalexin (non-anaphylactic), azithromycin/clarithromycin, or doxycycline.
⚠️ 'Use clindamycin for penicillin-allergic IE prophylaxis' -> wrong; clindamycin is no longer recommended (C. diff/toxicity) — the current allergy alternatives are cephalexin, a macrolide, or doxycycline.
