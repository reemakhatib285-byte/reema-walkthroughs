📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Duration + A1c banner
Like all microvascular complications, neuropathy risk climbs with diabetes DURATION and chronic hyperglycemia (A1c). Tight glycemic control clearly prevents/slows neuropathy in T1DM (DCCT); the effect is more modest in T2DM. Neuropathy is the most common chronic complication of diabetes and underlies most diabetic foot ulcers and non-traumatic amputations.
⚠️ In T2DM, intensive glucose control has only a MODEST effect on preventing neuropathy compared to T1DM — the trap is assuming aggressive A1c lowering will reverse established neuropathy; once present, treatment is symptomatic, and over-aggressive control risks hypoglycemia.
2. Diabetic amyotrophy ghost
Diabetic amyotrophy (lumbosacral radiculoplexus neuropathy / Bruns-Garland syndrome) presents as ACUTE, often asymmetric PROXIMAL thigh/hip pain followed by quadriceps WEAKNESS and wasting, frequently with weight loss. It is immune/microvasculitic, usually MONOPHASIC and self-limited over 6-12 months with supportive care and PT.
⚠️ Amyotrophy is PROXIMAL and predominantly MOTOR — the trap is confusing it with the distal symmetric SENSORY polyneuropathy (DSPN). Proximal asymmetric weakness with wasting is amyotrophy, not stocking-glove DSPN, and is not a reason to escalate to insulin urgently.
3. Sensation thief / stocking
The core of distal symmetric polyneuropathy (DSPN) is length-dependent loss of sensation in a STOCKING-GLOVE distribution — feet first (longest axons), then hands once leg involvement reaches the knees. Small fibers (pain/temperature) and large fibers (vibration/proprioception) are both affected.
⚠️ An ASYMMETRIC or focal sensory deficit is NOT typical DSPN — the trap is labeling a unilateral foot drop or single-nerve deficit as diabetic polyneuropathy; that pattern suggests mononeuropathy, radiculopathy, or compression instead.
4. Worse at night / numbness-tingling-burning
Painful DSPN: symmetric distal NUMBNESS, TINGLING/paresthesias, and BURNING/lancinating pain, classically WORSE AT NIGHT. Positive (painful) and negative (numb) symptoms coexist. Loss of pain sensation is what makes feet vulnerable to unnoticed ulcers.
⚠️ Numbness with painlessness is dangerous, not reassuring — the trap is assuming a patient with no foot pain is low-risk; LOSS of protective sensation is precisely what predicts ulceration and amputation.
5. DSPN label on bed
Distal symmetric polyneuropathy is the MOST COMMON form of diabetic neuropathy (~75% of cases). It is a clinical diagnosis; nerve conduction studies are reserved for atypical features. Always exclude other reversible causes of polyneuropathy (B12 deficiency, hypothyroidism, alcohol, uremia, monoclonal gammopathy).
⚠️ Don't reflexively attribute every diabetic's neuropathy to diabetes — the trap is missing a coexisting B12 deficiency (especially in metformin users, which lowers B12). Check B12, TSH, and other reversible causes before declaring it purely diabetic.
6. LOPS / 10g monofilament / vibration
Screen for LOSS OF PROTECTIVE SENSATION (LOPS) with the 10-gram Semmes-Weinstein MONOFILAMENT plus one of: 128 Hz tuning fork (vibration), pinprick, ankle reflexes, or temperature. LOPS predicts ulcer and amputation risk; positive screen → annual comprehensive foot exam and protective footwear.
⚠️ A single normal test does not exclude LOPS — the trap is relying on the monofilament alone; combine it with vibration/pinprick/ankle reflex. Also, the goal of the foot exam is risk stratification for ULCERS, not diagnosing the neuropathy subtype.
7. CV autonomic (resting tachy / silent MI)
Cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy (CAN): RESTING TACHYCARDIA (early vagal loss), loss of heart-rate variability, exercise intolerance, and SILENT (painless) myocardial ischemia — raising risk of unrecognized MI, perioperative instability, and SUDDEN cardiac DEATH.
⚠️ A diabetic with new dyspnea, nausea, or fatigue but NO chest pain can be having a silent MI — the trap is ruling out ACS because there's no typical anginal pain; autonomic neuropathy blunts the pain signal, so get an ECG/troponin.
8. Orthostatic / midodrine
Autonomic neuropathy causes ORTHOSTATIC HYPOTENSION (drop without compensatory tachycardia). Treat non-pharmacologically first (rise slowly, fluids/salt, compression stockings), then MIDODRINE (alpha-1 agonist) or fludrocortisone; droxidopa is another option.
⚠️ Watch for SUPINE HYPERTENSION when treating orthostasis — the trap is dosing midodrine/fludrocortisone at bedtime; give midodrine during the day and avoid late doses to prevent dangerous supine BP spikes.
9. GI: gastroparesis
Gastroparesis = delayed gastric emptying → early satiety, bloating, nausea, vomiting, and erratic glucose (mismatched insulin/food). Diagnose with a 4-hour gastric emptying scintigraphy after excluding obstruction. Manage with small, frequent, low-fat/low-fiber meals and prokinetics (metoclopramide short-term; erythromycin).
⚠️ GLP-1 receptor agonists SLOW gastric emptying and worsen gastroparesis — the trap is choosing/continuing a GLP-1 RA in a diabetic with gastroparesis; pramlintide (also slows emptying) is similarly contraindicated. Also limit metoclopramide duration (tardive dyskinesia).
10. GU: neurogenic bladder
Diabetic autonomic (neurogenic) bladder: impaired sensation of fullness → large bladder capacity, incomplete emptying, high post-void residual, and OVERFLOW incontinence with UTIs. Manage with timed/scheduled voiding, double voiding, and clean intermittent catheterization for high residuals.
⚠️ Overflow incontinence from a hypotonic neurogenic bladder is NOT treated with anticholinergics (oxybutynin) — the trap is treating it like urge incontinence; anticholinergics worsen retention. Confirm with post-void residual; treat by emptying the bladder, not relaxing it further.
11. ED / PDE-5
Erectile dysfunction is an early, common autonomic/vascular manifestation in diabetic men. First-line therapy is a PDE-5 inhibitor (sildenafil, tadalafil); second-line includes intracavernosal injections or vacuum devices.
⚠️ PDE-5 inhibitors are absolutely CONTRAINDICATED with nitrates (and caution with alpha-blockers) — the trap is prescribing sildenafil to a diabetic with CAD who is on nitrates, risking life-threatening hypotension.
12. Sudomotor (anhidrosis/hyperhidrosis)
Sudomotor (sweat) dysfunction: distal/LOWER-body ANHIDROSIS (dry, cracked, fissured feet that predispose to ulcers/infection) with compensatory UPPER-body HYPERHIDROSIS. Gustatory sweating (facial sweating with eating) is another autonomic clue.
⚠️ Dry, cracked feet here are an AUTONOMIC sign, not just poor hygiene — the trap is overlooking anhidrosis as a contributor to skin breakdown and ulcer risk; emollients and foot care matter.
13. Pupil spared (CN III)
Diabetic third-nerve (CN III) palsy is classically PUPIL-SPARING: down-and-out eye, ptosis, and diplopia but a REACTIVE pupil. Reason: ischemic microvascular infarction damages the central somatic motor fibers while sparing the peripheral parasympathetic (pupillomotor) fibers that run on the nerve's surface. Usually resolves over weeks-months.
⚠️ A pupil-INVOLVING (blown, fixed, dilated) CN III palsy is a compressive lesion — a posterior communicating artery ANEURYSM until proven otherwise — and a neurosurgical emergency needing urgent CTA/MRA. The trap is attributing a dilated, unreactive pupil to diabetes; diabetic CN III palsy spares the pupil.
14. Screen sign
Screen for distal symmetric polyneuropathy starting at 5 years after T1DM diagnosis and AT DIAGNOSIS for T2DM (silent prior years), then ANNUALLY thereafter. Also assess for autonomic neuropathy symptoms. Use history plus 10 g monofilament and one additional modality.
⚠️ Mirroring the nephropathy/retinopathy rule: T2DM is screened at the time of DIAGNOSIS (not 5 years later) — the trap is applying the T1DM '5-year' delay to a T2DM patient, missing existing neuropathy present from undiagnosed disease.
15. Treatment bottles (no opioids)
First-line for painful DSPN: pregabalin (75-150 mg BID, FDA-approved), duloxetine (60 mg/day, SNRI, FDA-approved), gabapentin (titrate to 1800-3600 mg/day), and TCAs (amitriptyline — caution: anticholinergic, orthostasis, arrhythmia). Add daily foot self-inspection and well-fitted footwear to prevent ulcers.
⚠️ OPIOIDS (including tramadol/tapentadol) are NOT first-line for diabetic neuropathic pain — the trap is selecting an opioid for refractory burning feet; they carry dependence/overdose risk with limited long-term benefit. Optimize gabapentinoids/SNRIs/TCAs first.
