⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. Tubules row — Prerenal AKI and ATN are unrelated diagnoses. WRONG — they are a continuum; untreated prerenal hypoperfusion progresses to ischemic ATN once tubular cells die. The discriminator is reversibility with fluids (prerenal) vs not (ATN) plus muddy-brown casts and FENa >2% in ATN.
  2. Tubules triggers — Vancomycin and aminoglycoside nephrotoxicity look identical. WRONG — aminoglycoside ATN is classically NONoliguric with proximal tubular wasting (hypoMg, hypoK); pinning the mechanism requires the exposure timeline and urine sediment, not assuming all 'antibiotic AKI' is the same.
  3. Tubules urine clue — FENa is always >2% in ATN. WRONG — early sepsis, contrast-induced AKI, and pigment nephropathy are intrinsic/tubular but keep FENa <1%; muddy-brown casts and clinical context, not FENa alone, clinch ATN.

  5. Interstitium triggers — Stopping the offending drug and giving steroids isn't urgent in AIN. WRONG — the key intervention is prompt withdrawal of the culprit drug; early corticosteroids may aid recovery, and delay risks irreversible interstitial fibrosis.
  6. Interstitium urine clue — Negative urine eosinophils excludes AIN, and positive eosinophiluria confirms it. WRONG on both counts — eosinophiluria has poor sensitivity AND specificity (also seen in atheroembolic disease, UTI, GN); diagnosis rests on drug history, sterile pyuria/WBC casts, and biopsy when needed.
  7. Glomeruli row — Glomerular AKI is identified by FENa/urine Na like ATN. WRONG — GN often shows a prerenal-pattern FENa <1% despite intrinsic injury; the discriminator is the ACTIVE sediment (RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs) plus serologies, not the sodium indices.
  8. Glomeruli triggers — A young adult with hematuria days after a sore throat has post-streptococcal GN. WRONG — synpharyngitic hematuria (within 1–5 days of URI) is IgA nephropathy; post-strep GN has a 1–3 WEEK latency with LOW complement that normalizes by ~8 weeks. Complement levels and timing separate them.
  9. Glomeruli urine clue — Hematuria with RBC casts could be from a kidney stone or bladder cancer. WRONG — RBC casts form only in the tubules and signal GLOMERULAR bleeding; isomorphic RBCs without casts suggest a urologic (stone/tumor) source. The presence of casts redirects the workup to GN, not urology.
  10. Renal vessels row — Microangiopathic anemia with low platelets and AKI is automatically TTP needing plasma exchange. WRONG — distinguish TTP (ADAMTS13 <10%, neuro signs) from HUS (Shiga-toxin diarrhea/E. coli O157, children — supportive care, NOT routine plasma exchange) and atypical HUS (complement → eculizumab).   11. Renal vessels clue — AKI plus eosinophilia after a cardiac catheterization is contrast nephropathy or AIN. WRONG — cholesterol (atheroembolic) AKI presents days-to-weeks post-procedure with blue toes, livedo, transient eosinophilia, and low complement; contrast AKI peaks at 3–5 days and resolves, and AIN is drug-triggered.   14. Board meaning column — You can localize intrinsic AKI from FENa and BUN:Cr alone. WRONG — those indices mainly separate prerenal vs ATN and have many exceptions; the URINE SEDIMENT is what distinguishes ATN vs AIN vs GN vs vascular vs pigment. Always examine the sediment in intrinsic AKI.
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Tubules row
The tubular compartment = acute tubular necrosis (ATN), the single most common cause of hospital-acquired intrinsic AKI. ATN has two flavors: ISCHEMIC (prolonged prerenal hypoperfusion that crosses into tubular cell death) and NEPHROTOXIC (direct toxins). It evolves through initiation → maintenance (oliguric, days–weeks) → recovery (polyuric) phases.
⚠️ Prerenal AKI and ATN are unrelated diagnoses. WRONG — they are a continuum; untreated prerenal hypoperfusion progresses to ischemic ATN once tubular cells die. The discriminator is reversibility with fluids (prerenal) vs not (ATN) plus muddy-brown casts and FENa >2% in ATN.
2. Tubules triggers
Ischemic triggers: hypotension/shock, sepsis, major surgery, hemorrhage. Nephrotoxic triggers: contrast, aminoglycosides (5–10 day lag, nonoliguric, with Mg/K wasting), amphotericin B, cisplatin, vancomycin, and endogenous pigments (myoglobin in rhabdo, hemoglobin in hemolysis). Tumor lysis (uric acid) and ethylene glycol (oxalate crystals) also injure tubules.
⚠️ Vancomycin and aminoglycoside nephrotoxicity look identical. WRONG — aminoglycoside ATN is classically NONoliguric with proximal tubular wasting (hypoMg, hypoK); pinning the mechanism requires the exposure timeline and urine sediment, not assuming all 'antibiotic AKI' is the same.
3. Tubules urine clue
Hallmark sediment: MUDDY-BROWN granular casts and renal tubular epithelial cell casts. Indices favor ATN: FENa >2%, urine Na >40, urine osm ~300 (isosthenuria — injured tubules can't concentrate), BUN:Cr ~10–15 (normal). Note the important exceptions where ATN shows FENa <1%: early sepsis, contrast, and rhabdo/pigment.
⚠️ FENa is always >2% in ATN. WRONG — early sepsis, contrast-induced AKI, and pigment nephropathy are intrinsic/tubular but keep FENa <1%; muddy-brown casts and clinical context, not FENa alone, clinch ATN.
4. Interstitium row
The interstitial compartment = acute interstitial nephritis (AIN), an immune/hypersensitivity inflammation of the renal interstitium. Classic (but present in <10–30%) triad: fever, rash, eosinophilia. Onset is typically days to weeks after a new drug; AKI is often accompanied by sterile pyuria and mild proteinuria.
⚠️ Absence of the fever-rash-eosinophilia triad rules out AIN. WRONG — the full triad appears in a minority of cases; AIN remains the diagnosis with a compatible drug exposure, WBC casts/sterile pyuria, and AKI. Renal biopsy is definitive when needed.
5. Interstitium triggers
Drug-induced (most common): PPIs, NSAIDs, beta-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins), sulfonamides, rifampin, allopurinol, and immune-checkpoint inhibitors (a growing oncology cause). Also infections (Legionella, Leptospira) and autoimmune (sarcoidosis, Sjögren, lupus, TINU). NSAID-AIN is unique in causing AIN PLUS nephrotic-range proteinuria (minimal change).
⚠️ Stopping the offending drug and giving steroids isn't urgent in AIN. WRONG — the key intervention is prompt withdrawal of the culprit drug; early corticosteroids may aid recovery, and delay risks irreversible interstitial fibrosis.
6. Interstitium urine clue
Sediment: WBC casts and sterile pyuria (pyuria with negative culture) ± hematuria and mild proteinuria. Urine eosinophils (Hansel stain) are the classic teaching clue but are neither sensitive nor specific. AKI with a bland-to-pyuric sediment after a new PPI/NSAID/antibiotic should raise AIN.
⚠️ Negative urine eosinophils excludes AIN, and positive eosinophiluria confirms it. WRONG on both counts — eosinophiluria has poor sensitivity AND specificity (also seen in atheroembolic disease, UTI, GN); diagnosis rests on drug history, sterile pyuria/WBC casts, and biopsy when needed.
7. Glomeruli row
The glomerular compartment = glomerulonephritis / vasculitis, a NEPHRITIC process: inflammation of the glomerular tuft causing hematuria with RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs, hypertension, edema, and sub-nephrotic proteinuria. Rapidly progressive GN (RPGN) with crescents is the can't-miss emergency requiring urgent biopsy and immunosuppression.
⚠️ Glomerular AKI is identified by FENa/urine Na like ATN. WRONG — GN often shows a prerenal-pattern FENa <1% despite intrinsic injury; the discriminator is the ACTIVE sediment (RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs) plus serologies, not the sodium indices.
8. Glomeruli triggers
Serologic workup localizes the GN: ANCA (granulomatosis with polyangiitis, microscopic polyangiitis — pauci-immune); anti-GBM (Goodpasture — linear IgG, hemoptysis + AKI); ANA/dsDNA + LOW complement (lupus nephritis); low complement also in post-strep GN (recent pharyngitis/impetigo, weeks prior) and MPGN/cryoglobulinemia (hepatitis C). Normal complement: IgA nephropathy, ANCA, anti-GBM.
⚠️ A young adult with hematuria days after a sore throat has post-streptococcal GN. WRONG — synpharyngitic hematuria (within 1–5 days of URI) is IgA nephropathy; post-strep GN has a 1–3 WEEK latency with LOW complement that normalizes by ~8 weeks. Complement levels and timing separate them.
9. Glomeruli urine clue
Hallmark: RBC casts and DYSMORPHIC (acanthocyte) RBCs = true glomerular bleeding (nephritic). Proteinuria is usually sub-nephrotic (<3.5 g/day); nephrotic-range with bland sediment instead points to a non-inflammatory glomerulopathy (membranous, FSGS, minimal change). RBC casts are essentially pathognomonic for GN/vasculitis.
⚠️ Hematuria with RBC casts could be from a kidney stone or bladder cancer. WRONG — RBC casts form only in the tubules and signal GLOMERULAR bleeding; isomorphic RBCs without casts suggest a urologic (stone/tumor) source. The presence of casts redirects the workup to GN, not urology.
10. Renal vessels row
The vascular compartment splits into MICROvascular (thrombotic microangiopathy — TTP, HUS, DIC, scleroderma renal crisis, malignant HTN, APS) and MACROvascular (atheroembolic/cholesterol emboli, renal artery thrombosis/dissection, large-vessel occlusion). Each has distinct systemic clues beyond the kidney.
⚠️ Microangiopathic anemia with low platelets and AKI is automatically TTP needing plasma exchange. WRONG — distinguish TTP (ADAMTS13 <10%, neuro signs) from HUS (Shiga-toxin diarrhea/E. coli O157, children — supportive care, NOT routine plasma exchange) and atypical HUS (complement → eculizumab). Treatment diverges sharply.
11. Renal vessels clue
TMA clues: microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes), thrombocytopenia, elevated LDH, low haptoglobin, normal coags (vs DIC). Atheroembolic clues: livedo reticularis, blue/purple toes with intact pulses, EOSINOPHILIA + low complement + eosinophiluria, days–weeks AFTER a vascular procedure/angiography or anticoagulation. Malignant HTN: BP often >180/120 with retinal/CNS findings.
⚠️ AKI plus eosinophilia after a cardiac catheterization is contrast nephropathy or AIN. WRONG — cholesterol (atheroembolic) AKI presents days-to-weeks post-procedure with blue toes, livedo, transient eosinophilia, and low complement; contrast AKI peaks at 3–5 days and resolves, and AIN is drug-triggered. The skin findings and timing distinguish them.
12. Pigment injury row
Pigment nephropathy is a TUBULAR subtype: rhabdomyolysis releases myoglobin (or massive hemolysis releases hemoglobin) which is directly tubulotoxic, forms casts with Tamm-Horsfall protein, and causes intrarenal vasoconstriction. Clues to rhabdo: CK in the thousands–hundreds of thousands, hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, early HYPOcalcemia then late hypercalcemia, and a LOW/normal BUN:Cr (muscle dumps creatinine).
⚠️ Pigment (rhabdo) AKI shows a high BUN:Cr and high FENa like typical ATN. WRONG — rhabdo gives a LOW/normal BUN:Cr (direct creatinine release) and can show FENa <1% (intact tubular avidity early); the giveaways are CK elevation and heme-positive dipstick without RBCs.
13. Pigment urine clue
Signature finding: a dipstick POSITIVE for blood/heme but FEW or NO RBCs on microscopy — because the dipstick detects the heme moiety of myoglobin and hemoglobin, not intact erythrocytes. Pair with red-brown ('tea/cola-colored') urine, markedly elevated CK (rhabdo) or low haptoglobin/high LDH (hemolysis) to confirm.
⚠️ A heme-positive dipstick always means true hematuria. WRONG — myoglobin and free hemoglobin also turn the dipstick positive; if microscopy shows no RBCs, think pigment nephropathy and send CK, haptoglobin, and LDH rather than chasing a urologic bleeding source.
14. Board meaning column
The exam logic: each compartment maps to a SIGNATURE sediment, so the urine microscopy IS the localizing test for intrinsic AKI — muddy-brown casts = ATN (tubules); WBC casts/sterile pyuria = AIN (interstitium); RBC casts/dysmorphic RBCs = GN (glomeruli); schistocytes/blue toes = vascular; heme-positive without RBCs = pigment. Order: assess pre/post-renal first, then use sediment to pin the intrinsic compartment.
⚠️ You can localize intrinsic AKI from FENa and BUN:Cr alone. WRONG — those indices mainly separate prerenal vs ATN and have many exceptions; the URINE SEDIMENT is what distinguishes ATN vs AIN vs GN vs vascular vs pigment. Always examine the sediment in intrinsic AKI.
