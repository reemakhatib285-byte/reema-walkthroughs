⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. All four required — WRONG to assume normal serum aldosterone guarantees normal K+ excretion. Discriminator: even with intact aldosterone, K+ retention occurs if distal Na+ delivery is low (CHF, cirrhosis, volume depletion), flow is low (oliguria), or ENaC is blocked (amiloride/triamterene) — all four pillars must be intact.
  2. Renin — WRONG to overlook drugs that suppress renin as a cause of hyperkalemia. Discriminator: NSAIDs and beta-blockers lower renin (hyporeninemic states), reducing downstream aldosterone and K+ secretion — these are common, easily missed contributors in CKD/diabetic patients.
  3. Angiotensin II — WRONG ANSWER: failing to attribute hyperkalemia to ACE inhibitors/ARBs. Discriminator: ACEi (block AT-II formation) and ARBs (block AT-1 receptor) both reduce aldosterone-driven K+ secretion — they are leading drug causes of hyperkalemia, especially combined or in CKD/diabetes.
  4. Aldosterone — WRONG to forget that hyperkalemia itself is a direct stimulus to aldosterone independent of the renin axis. Discriminator: a high K+ should drive aldosterone up directly; if it doesn't (Type 4 RTA, MRA use, Addison's), suspect an adrenal or receptor problem — check aldosterone and renin to localize the lesion.
  5. Pillar 1: Aldo — WRONG to expect spironolactone to spare K+ only mildly. Discriminator: MR antagonists (spironolactone/eplerenone) directly block this pillar and are potent causes of hyperkalemia, especially in CHF/CKD or combined with ACEi/ARB — monitor K+ closely after starting or up-titrating.
  6. Pillar 2: ENaC — WRONG to assume amiloride/triamterene cause hyperkalemia by an aldosterone mechanism. Discriminator: these block ENaC DIRECTLY (downstream of the receptor), so they cause hyperkalemia even when aldosterone is normal/high — contrast with spironolactone, which works at the MR. Note Liddle syndrome = ENaC gain-of-function (HYPOkalemia).
  7. Na/K-ATPase — WRONG to overlook Na/K-ATPase inhibition as a mechanism of hyperkalemia. Discriminator: digoxin toxicity blocks the Na/K-ATPase body-wide, causing a transcellular shift of K+ OUT of cells — hyperkalemia in acute digoxin toxicity is a marker of severity and an indication for digoxin-specific Fab.
  8. ROMK — WRONG to ignore the loop-of-Henle link to ROMK. Discriminator: ROMK in the thick ascending limb is essential for the NKCC2 cotransporter; loss-of-function (Bartter syndrome type 2) causes a loop-diuretic-like salt wasting with HYPOkalemia — distinguish channel location/function when reasoning about K+ direction.
  9. Lumen-neg voltage — WRONG to expect normal K+ secretion when distal Na+ reabsorption is impaired. Discriminator: anything that reduces ENaC-driven Na+ reabsorption (low distal Na+, ENaC blockers, low aldosterone) collapses the lumen-negative voltage and the K+-secretory drive — explaining hyperkalemia in those states.
  10. Pillar 3: distal Na+ — WRONG ANSWER: blaming hyperkalemia in CHF or cirrhosis purely on the kidney itself. Discriminator: in CHF, cirrhosis, and volume depletion, intense proximal Na+ reabsorption leaves little distal Na+ — no Na+ delivery means no ENaC activity and no K+ secretion, so K+ is retained despite (often high) aldosterone.
  11. Pillar 4: urine flow — WRONG to expect adequate K+ excretion in an oliguric patient even if aldosterone and Na+ delivery are fine. Discriminator: low urine flow (oliguria, dehydration) abolishes the gradient and halts K+ secretion — a key reason AKI/oliguria causes hyperkalemia and why restoring urine flow (fluids, loop diuretics) helps.
  12. Urine flow > 40 — WRONG to assume hyperkalemia must be from intake when urine K+ is low. Discriminator: an inappropriately low urine K+ during hyperkalemia indicts impaired renal secretion (low aldosterone, ENaC block, low Na+/flow), whereas a high urine K+ with high serum K+ suggests an overwhelming load or shift with intact kidneys.
  13. TTKG > 8 — WRONG ANSWER: a low TTKG in hyperkalemia means the kidney is responding normally. Discriminator: in hyperkalemia an INAPPROPRIATELY LOW TTKG (< 5-7) signals a secretory defect — hypoaldosteronism (Type 4 RTA), MR/ENaC blockade — not a load; a fludrocortisone challenge that raises TTKG confirms aldosterone deficiency/resistance. (Note: TTKG is now considered less reliable but remains testable.)

Discriminator: they are serial, non-redundant requirements — a single failed pillar is sufficient to cause hyperkalemia, so identify WHICH pillar is broken to target therapy (e.g., fludrocortisone for aldosterone deficiency, fluids/diuretics to restore Na+ delivery and flow).
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. All four required
Distal nephron K+ secretion (principal cells of the late distal tubule/collecting duct) requires all four pillars: aldosterone effect, functioning ENaC, adequate distal Na+ delivery, and adequate urine flow. These are serial dependencies — each sets up the next, and the lumen-negative voltage from ENaC-mediated Na+ reabsorption is what drives K+ out through ROMK. Knocking out any one pillar impairs secretion and causes hyperkalemia.
⚠️ WRONG to assume normal serum aldosterone guarantees normal K+ excretion. Discriminator: even with intact aldosterone, K+ retention occurs if distal Na+ delivery is low (CHF, cirrhosis, volume depletion), flow is low (oliguria), or ENaC is blocked (amiloride/triamterene) — all four pillars must be intact.
2. Renin
Renin, released from juxtaglomerular cells of the afferent arteriole, is the rate-limiting first step of the RAAS. It is stimulated by low renal perfusion pressure, low distal Na+ (macula densa), and sympathetic (beta-1) activity. Renin cleaves angiotensinogen to angiotensin I, initiating the cascade toward aldosterone.
⚠️ WRONG to overlook drugs that suppress renin as a cause of hyperkalemia. Discriminator: NSAIDs and beta-blockers lower renin (hyporeninemic states), reducing downstream aldosterone and K+ secretion — these are common, easily missed contributors in CKD/diabetic patients.
3. Angiotensin II
ACE (mainly in pulmonary endothelium) converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II, which stimulates the adrenal zona glomerulosa to synthesize aldosterone and also drives proximal Na+/water reabsorption and efferent arteriolar constriction. It is the pivotal effector linking volume status to aldosterone. Blocking it impairs distal K+ secretion.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: failing to attribute hyperkalemia to ACE inhibitors/ARBs. Discriminator: ACEi (block AT-II formation) and ARBs (block AT-1 receptor) both reduce aldosterone-driven K+ secretion — they are leading drug causes of hyperkalemia, especially combined or in CKD/diabetes.
4. Aldosterone
Aldosterone, made in the adrenal zona glomerulosa, is the master hormone for distal K+ secretion: it upregulates ENaC, the basolateral Na/K-ATPase, and ROMK in principal cells. It is stimulated directly by hyperkalemia and by angiotensin II. Deficiency (Addison's, hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism/Type 4 RTA) or receptor blockade causes hyperkalemia.
⚠️ WRONG to forget that hyperkalemia itself is a direct stimulus to aldosterone independent of the renin axis. Discriminator: a high K+ should drive aldosterone up directly; if it doesn't (Type 4 RTA, MRA use, Addison's), suspect an adrenal or receptor problem — check aldosterone and renin to localize the lesion.
5. Pillar 1: Aldo
Pillar 1: aldosterone binds the cytosolic mineralocorticoid receptor in principal cells, increasing transcription of ENaC, Na/K-ATPase, and ROMK. The net effect is enhanced Na+ reabsorption and K+ secretion. This is the target of spironolactone/eplerenone (MR antagonists).
⚠️ WRONG to expect spironolactone to spare K+ only mildly. Discriminator: MR antagonists (spironolactone/eplerenone) directly block this pillar and are potent causes of hyperkalemia, especially in CHF/CKD or combined with ACEi/ARB — monitor K+ closely after starting or up-titrating.
6. Pillar 2: ENaC
Pillar 2: the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC) on the apical principal-cell membrane lets luminal Na+ enter the cell, generating the lumen-negative transepithelial voltage that drives K+ secretion through ROMK. ENaC activity depends on aldosterone and on adequate luminal Na+. It is blocked by amiloride and triamterene.
⚠️ WRONG to assume amiloride/triamterene cause hyperkalemia by an aldosterone mechanism. Discriminator: these block ENaC DIRECTLY (downstream of the receptor), so they cause hyperkalemia even when aldosterone is normal/high — contrast with spironolactone, which works at the MR. Note Liddle syndrome = ENaC gain-of-function (HYPOkalemia).
7. Na/K-ATPase
The basolateral Na/K-ATPase pumps Na+ out of and K+ into the principal cell, keeping intracellular K+ high so K+ can flow down its gradient into the lumen via ROMK. It is the energy-dependent engine that loads the cell with K+ for secretion. Aldosterone upregulates it; digoxin and severe ischemia inhibit it.
⚠️ WRONG to overlook Na/K-ATPase inhibition as a mechanism of hyperkalemia. Discriminator: digoxin toxicity blocks the Na/K-ATPase body-wide, causing a transcellular shift of K+ OUT of cells — hyperkalemia in acute digoxin toxicity is a marker of severity and an indication for digoxin-specific Fab.
8. ROMK
ROMK (renal outer medullary K+ channel) is the apical channel through which K+ exits the principal cell into the urine, driven by the lumen-negative voltage. Its activity rises with aldosterone, high distal flow, and high tubular K+. It is the final common conduit for regulated K+ secretion.
⚠️ WRONG to ignore the loop-of-Henle link to ROMK. Discriminator: ROMK in the thick ascending limb is essential for the NKCC2 cotransporter; loss-of-function (Bartter syndrome type 2) causes a loop-diuretic-like salt wasting with HYPOkalemia — distinguish channel location/function when reasoning about K+ direction.
9. Lumen-neg voltage
ENaC-mediated Na+ reabsorption leaves the lumen electrically negative, and this lumen-negative voltage is the electrochemical force that pulls K+ (and H+) out of the cell into the urine. Without Na+ reabsorption there is no driving voltage, and K+ secretion stalls. This couples Na+ handling directly to K+ excretion.
⚠️ WRONG to expect normal K+ secretion when distal Na+ reabsorption is impaired. Discriminator: anything that reduces ENaC-driven Na+ reabsorption (low distal Na+, ENaC blockers, low aldosterone) collapses the lumen-negative voltage and the K+-secretory drive — explaining hyperkalemia in those states.
10. Pillar 3: distal Na+
Pillar 3: adequate distal Na+ delivery (urine Na+ > ~25 mM) is needed to fuel ENaC and generate the lumen-negative voltage. In avid-reabsorption states the proximal nephron grabs Na+ first, starving the distal segment. Thiazides and loops increase distal Na+ delivery, which is partly why they promote K+ wasting.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: blaming hyperkalemia in CHF or cirrhosis purely on the kidney itself. Discriminator: in CHF, cirrhosis, and volume depletion, intense proximal Na+ reabsorption leaves little distal Na+ — no Na+ delivery means no ENaC activity and no K+ secretion, so K+ is retained despite (often high) aldosterone.
11. Pillar 4: urine flow
Pillar 4: brisk tubular flow continuously sweeps secreted K+ away from the apical membrane, maintaining a steep cell-to-lumen K+ gradient that sustains ongoing secretion. High flow also activates flow-sensitive maxi-K (BK) channels for additional K+ excretion. Low flow lets luminal K+ accumulate and shuts secretion down.
⚠️ WRONG to expect adequate K+ excretion in an oliguric patient even if aldosterone and Na+ delivery are fine. Discriminator: low urine flow (oliguria, dehydration) abolishes the gradient and halts K+ secretion — a key reason AKI/oliguria causes hyperkalemia and why restoring urine flow (fluids, loop diuretics) helps.
12. Urine flow > 40
Normal urinary K+ excretion is roughly > 40-80 mM/day, reflecting balance with dietary intake of ~40-120 mM. A spot urine K+ that is inappropriately LOW (< ~20 mM) in the face of hyperkalemia points to a renal/tubular secretory defect rather than a load problem. Urine K+ helps separate renal from extrarenal causes.
⚠️ WRONG to assume hyperkalemia must be from intake when urine K+ is low. Discriminator: an inappropriately low urine K+ during hyperkalemia indicts impaired renal secretion (low aldosterone, ENaC block, low Na+/flow), whereas a high urine K+ with high serum K+ suggests an overwhelming load or shift with intact kidneys.
13. TTKG > 8
The transtubular potassium gradient (TTKG = [urine K+/plasma K+] / [urine osm/plasma osm]) estimates distal K+ secretory drive. In hyperkalemia, a TTKG > ~7-8 indicates an appropriate kidney response (intact aldosterone/secretion), implicating a load or shift. Validity requires urine osm > plasma osm and urine Na+ > 25.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: a low TTKG in hyperkalemia means the kidney is responding normally. Discriminator: in hyperkalemia an INAPPROPRIATELY LOW TTKG (< 5-7) signals a secretory defect — hypoaldosteronism (Type 4 RTA), MR/ENaC blockade — not a load; a fludrocortisone challenge that raises TTKG confirms aldosterone deficiency/resistance. (Note: TTKG is now considered less reliable but remains testable.)
14. 4-pillar scroll
Bottom line: renal K+ secretion needs aldosterone + ENaC + distal Na+ delivery + urine flow, and failure of ANY single pillar causes hyperkalemia. Map each clinical cause to its pillar — Addison's/Type 4 RTA (aldosterone), amiloride/triamterene (ENaC), CHF/cirrhosis/volume depletion (distal Na+), oliguria/AKI (flow). This framework turns a long hyperkalemia differential into four mechanistic buckets.
⚠️ WRONG to treat the four pillars as redundant backups for one another. Discriminator: they are serial, non-redundant requirements — a single failed pillar is sufficient to cause hyperkalemia, so identify WHICH pillar is broken to target therapy (e.g., fludrocortisone for aldosterone deficiency, fluids/diuretics to restore Na+ delivery and flow).
