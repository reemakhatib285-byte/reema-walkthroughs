⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. Step 1: confirm hemolysis — WRONG: 'A high reticulocyte count plus anemia confirms hemolysis.' Recent/ongoing blood loss and recovery from a nutritional deficiency also raise reticulocytes without hemolysis. Discriminator: hemolysis requires the biochemical signature (high LDH, high indirect bili, LOW haptoglobin) — blood loss leaves haptoglobin/LDH normal.
  2. LDH most important — WRONG: 'A high LDH by itself confirms hemolysis.' LDH is sensitive but nonspecific — it rises in MI, tumor lysis, megaloblastic/ineffective erythropoiesis, and tissue injury. Discriminator: pair it with a LOW haptoglobin (the most specific marker) and high indirect bilirubin to confirm true hemolysis.
  3. Step 2: where? intra vs extra — WRONG: 'Hemoglobinuria can occur in either intravascular or extravascular hemolysis.' Hemoglobinuria/hemosiderinuria require free Hb in plasma, which is specific to INTRAvascular hemolysis. Discriminator: extravascular hemolysis produces indirect hyperbilirubinemia and splenomegaly, not hemoglobinuria.
  4. Hemosiderinuria — WRONG: 'Negative hemosiderinuria on day 1 of dark urine rules out intravascular hemolysis/PNH.' Hemosiderinuria lags by several days because it depends on tubular cell turnover; early on you see hemoglobinuria instead. Discriminator: check free plasma Hb/hemoglobinuria acutely, and repeat urine hemosiderin (Prussian blue) after a few days for chronic intravascular hemolysis.
  5. Step 3: inherited vs acquired — WRONG: 'New-onset hemolysis in a 70-year-old is most likely a missed inherited disorder.' Late-onset hemolysis without prior history points to ACQUIRED causes — warm AIHA (consider underlying CLL/lymphoma), drugs, MAHA, or infection. Discriminator: age of onset, family history, and DAT (positive = acquired immune) steer inherited vs acquired.
  6. Smear = best weapon — WRONG: 'The smear is optional once the hemolysis labs are abnormal.' Morphology often clinches the diagnosis and is mandatory — finding schistocytes can trigger emergent plasma exchange for TTP. Discriminator: always review the smear; it determines the specific confirmatory test and can identify a hematologic emergency.
  7. Spherocytes — WRONG: 'Spherocytes on the smear mean hereditary spherocytosis.' Spherocytes are NOT specific — warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia produces them too. Discriminator: order the DAT first — DAT-positive spherocytes = warm AIHA; DAT-negative spherocytes with high MCHC/family history = hereditary spherocytosis (confirm with EMA-binding/osmotic fragility).
  8. Bite cells → G6PD — WRONG: 'A normal G6PD level during an acute hemolytic episode excludes G6PD deficiency.' During acute hemolysis the oldest, most deficient cells lyse first and reticulocytes have higher enzyme, so the assay can be falsely normal. Discriminator: repeat the quantitative G6PD assay 2-3 months after the episode when RBCs have re-aged.
  9. Schistocytes → MAHA — WRONG: 'Schistocytes with thrombocytopenia mean autoimmune hemolytic anemia — start steroids and check the DAT.' Schistocytes indicate MAHA, where the DAT is NEGATIVE and the emergency is TTP. Discriminator: schistocytes + thrombocytopenia + organ injury = MAHA → urgent ADAMTS13 and plasma exchange for TTP, NOT immune hemolysis.
  10. Elliptocytes — WRONG: 'Elliptocytes always indicate a severe inherited hemolytic anemia needing splenectomy.' Hereditary elliptocytosis is usually mild/asymptomatic and rarely needs treatment. Discriminator: assess for actual hemolysis (retic, LDH, bili); reserve splenectomy for the uncommon symptomatic/transfusion-dependent cases, and note elliptocytes can be nonspecific (iron deficiency, thalassemia).
  11. Basophilic stippling — WRONG: 'Basophilic stippling with microcytic anemia is iron deficiency.' Coarse basophilic stippling points to lead poisoning or P5'N deficiency, not simple iron deficiency. Discriminator: check a blood lead level (and zinc protoporphyrin); iron deficiency lacks coarse stippling and shows low ferritin/high TIBC.
  12. DAT/Coombs branch — WRONG: 'A negative DAT excludes autoimmune hemolytic anemia.' ~5-10% of warm AIHA is 'DAT-negative' (low-affinity IgG, IgA, or antibody below the assay threshold). Discriminator: if clinical AIHA is strong despite a negative DAT, use enhanced/more-sensitive techniques and consider an empiric steroid trial; also exclude PNH/HS.
  13. Inherited vs acquired file — WRONG: 'PNH is an inherited hemolytic anemia because it is a genetic mutation.' PNH is ACQUIRED — a somatic PIGA mutation in a hematopoietic stem cell clone (not germline/inherited), causing GPI-anchor (CD55/CD59) loss and complement-mediated intravascular hemolysis. Discriminator: diagnose PNH by flow cytometry for CD55/CD59 deficiency (FLAER), not by family history.
  14. IVH wanted poster — WRONG: 'Warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia is a classic cause of intravascular hemolysis.' Warm AIHA is predominantly EXTRAvascular (splenic macrophage clearance of IgG-coated RBCs) — it does not typically cause hemoglobinuria. Discriminator: warm AIHA shows spherocytes, splenomegaly, and indirect hyperbilirubinemia; intravascular hallmarks (hemoglobinuria, hemosiderinuria) point to PNH/MAHA/G6PD/ABO instead.
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Step 1: confirm hemolysis
Step 1 is to confirm hemolysis biochemically before chasing a cause. The four pillars: HIGH LDH (released from lysed RBCs), HIGH indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin (heme catabolism), LOW haptoglobin (binds free Hb then is cleared), and HIGH reticulocytes (marrow compensation). The classic high-yield combo is high LDH + low haptoglobin, which together are ~90% specific for hemolysis.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A high reticulocyte count plus anemia confirms hemolysis.' Recent/ongoing blood loss and recovery from a nutritional deficiency also raise reticulocytes without hemolysis. Discriminator: hemolysis requires the biochemical signature (high LDH, high indirect bili, LOW haptoglobin) — blood loss leaves haptoglobin/LDH normal.
2. LDH most important
LDH rises because it is abundant inside RBCs and spills out when they lyse; it is very sensitive (especially in intravascular hemolysis) but not specific (also up in MI, tumor lysis, liver/muscle injury, megaloblastic anemia). The most specific single marker for hemolysis is a LOW haptoglobin; combining a high LDH with a low haptoglobin gives the best diagnostic yield.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A high LDH by itself confirms hemolysis.' LDH is sensitive but nonspecific — it rises in MI, tumor lysis, megaloblastic/ineffective erythropoiesis, and tissue injury. Discriminator: pair it with a LOW haptoglobin (the most specific marker) and high indirect bilirubin to confirm true hemolysis.
3. Step 2: where? intra vs extra
Step 2 localizes the destruction. Intravascular (lysis within vessels): markedly low/absent haptoglobin, free plasma hemoglobin, hemoglobinuria, hemosiderinuria, very high LDH — causes include PNH, G6PD crisis, MAHA, ABO incompatibility, infections. Extravascular (splenic/hepatic macrophage clearance): predominant high indirect bilirubin, splenomegaly, spherocytes; causes include warm AIHA, hereditary spherocytosis, pyruvate kinase deficiency.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Hemoglobinuria can occur in either intravascular or extravascular hemolysis.' Hemoglobinuria/hemosiderinuria require free Hb in plasma, which is specific to INTRAvascular hemolysis. Discriminator: extravascular hemolysis produces indirect hyperbilirubinemia and splenomegaly, not hemoglobinuria.
4. Hemosiderinuria
In chronic intravascular hemolysis, filtered free hemoglobin is reabsorbed by renal tubular cells, converted to hemosiderin, and shed in the urine as the cells slough — detected by Prussian blue stain of urine sediment. It appears days after onset (after tubular cells turn over) and is the classic chronic marker of PNH; acute intravascular hemolysis shows hemoglobinuria first.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Negative hemosiderinuria on day 1 of dark urine rules out intravascular hemolysis/PNH.' Hemosiderinuria lags by several days because it depends on tubular cell turnover; early on you see hemoglobinuria instead. Discriminator: check free plasma Hb/hemoglobinuria acutely, and repeat urine hemosiderin (Prussian blue) after a few days for chronic intravascular hemolysis.
5. Step 3: inherited vs acquired
Step 3 separates inherited from acquired. Clues to inherited: lifelong/childhood anemia, positive family history, ethnicity (Mediterranean/African/SE Asian), splenomegaly, gallstones at a young age, abnormal smear morphology (spherocytes, elliptocytes, sickle, target cells). Acquired causes present later and are often immune (DAT+), microangiopathic, infectious, or clonal (PNH).
⚠️ WRONG: 'New-onset hemolysis in a 70-year-old is most likely a missed inherited disorder.' Late-onset hemolysis without prior history points to ACQUIRED causes — warm AIHA (consider underlying CLL/lymphoma), drugs, MAHA, or infection. Discriminator: age of onset, family history, and DAT (positive = acquired immune) steer inherited vs acquired.
6. Smear = best weapon
The peripheral blood smear is the single most informative test in hemolytic anemia: schistocytes → MAHA, spherocytes → AIHA/HS, bite cells/Heinz bodies → G6PD, sickle cells → sickle disease, target cells → thalassemia/Hb disease, agglutination → cold agglutinin. The smear directs which confirmatory test to order (DAT, ADAMTS13, G6PD assay, flow cytometry, electrophoresis).
⚠️ WRONG: 'The smear is optional once the hemolysis labs are abnormal.' Morphology often clinches the diagnosis and is mandatory — finding schistocytes can trigger emergent plasma exchange for TTP. Discriminator: always review the smear; it determines the specific confirmatory test and can identify a hematologic emergency.
7. Spherocytes
Spherocytes (dense, small, no central pallor) form when membrane is partially removed by splenic macrophages (warm AIHA) or is intrinsically deficient (hereditary spherocytosis). The DAT distinguishes them: warm AIHA is DAT-positive (IgG ± C3), HS is DAT-NEGATIVE with a positive osmotic fragility / abnormal EMA-binding flow test and often a family history.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Spherocytes on the smear mean hereditary spherocytosis.' Spherocytes are NOT specific — warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia produces them too. Discriminator: order the DAT first — DAT-positive spherocytes = warm AIHA; DAT-negative spherocytes with high MCHC/family history = hereditary spherocytosis (confirm with EMA-binding/osmotic fragility).
8. Bite cells → G6PD
Bite cells and blister cells form when splenic macrophages remove Heinz bodies (precipitated, oxidized hemoglobin) in G6PD deficiency, an X-linked enzyme defect leaving RBCs unable to regenerate glutathione against oxidative stress. Triggers: infection, fava beans, oxidant drugs (dapsone, primaquine, sulfas, nitrofurantoin, rasburicase). Heinz bodies need a supravital stain (crystal violet).
⚠️ WRONG: 'A normal G6PD level during an acute hemolytic episode excludes G6PD deficiency.' During acute hemolysis the oldest, most deficient cells lyse first and reticulocytes have higher enzyme, so the assay can be falsely normal. Discriminator: repeat the quantitative G6PD assay 2-3 months after the episode when RBCs have re-aged.
9. Schistocytes → MAHA
Schistocytes (fragmented helmet/triangle cells) signal microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA): RBCs sheared by fibrin strands or mechanical force. Differential: TTP (low ADAMTS13, neuro), HUS (Shiga toxin/renal), DIC (consumptive coagulopathy with high PT/PTT/D-dimer), malignant hypertension, HELLP, scleroderma renal crisis, and prosthetic/mechanical heart valves.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Schistocytes with thrombocytopenia mean autoimmune hemolytic anemia — start steroids and check the DAT.' Schistocytes indicate MAHA, where the DAT is NEGATIVE and the emergency is TTP. Discriminator: schistocytes + thrombocytopenia + organ injury = MAHA → urgent ADAMTS13 and plasma exchange for TTP, NOT immune hemolysis.
10. Elliptocytes
Elliptocytes (cigar/oval RBCs) define hereditary elliptocytosis, a defect of horizontal membrane skeleton proteins (spectrin self-association, protein 4.1). Most patients are asymptomatic carriers; hemolysis is usually mild. The severe variant is hereditary pyropoikilocytosis. A few elliptocytes are also nonspecifically seen in iron deficiency, thalassemia, and myelofibrosis.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Elliptocytes always indicate a severe inherited hemolytic anemia needing splenectomy.' Hereditary elliptocytosis is usually mild/asymptomatic and rarely needs treatment. Discriminator: assess for actual hemolysis (retic, LDH, bili); reserve splenectomy for the uncommon symptomatic/transfusion-dependent cases, and note elliptocytes can be nonspecific (iron deficiency, thalassemia).
11. Basophilic stippling
Coarse basophilic stippling = ribosomal RNA aggregates in RBCs, classically from lead poisoning (lead inhibits ferrochelatase and pyrimidine-5'-nucleotidase) or hereditary pyrimidine-5'-nucleotidase deficiency. Lead poisoning also gives microcytic anemia, abdominal pain, neuropathy/encephalopathy, and a high zinc protoporphyrin; chelate with succimer (DMSA), EDTA, or dimercaprol (BAL) for severe cases.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Basophilic stippling with microcytic anemia is iron deficiency.' Coarse basophilic stippling points to lead poisoning or P5'N deficiency, not simple iron deficiency. Discriminator: check a blood lead level (and zinc protoporphyrin); iron deficiency lacks coarse stippling and shows low ferritin/high TIBC.
12. DAT/Coombs branch
The direct antiglobulin test (DAT/direct Coombs) detects IgG and/or C3 already bound to the patient's RBCs and is the pivotal branch point: POSITIVE = immune-mediated hemolysis (warm AIHA = IgG; cold agglutinin/PCH = C3/complement; drug-induced). NEGATIVE = non-immune (hereditary membrane/enzyme/Hb defects, MAHA, PNH, infection, hypersplenism).
⚠️ WRONG: 'A negative DAT excludes autoimmune hemolytic anemia.' ~5-10% of warm AIHA is 'DAT-negative' (low-affinity IgG, IgA, or antibody below the assay threshold). Discriminator: if clinical AIHA is strong despite a negative DAT, use enhanced/more-sensitive techniques and consider an empiric steroid trial; also exclude PNH/HS.
13. Inherited vs acquired file
Filing framework. INHERITED: membrane (hereditary spherocytosis, elliptocytosis), enzyme (G6PD, pyruvate kinase), hemoglobin (sickle cell, thalassemia). ACQUIRED: immune (warm/cold AIHA, drug-induced), microangiopathic (TTP/HUS/DIC), infection (malaria, Babesia, Clostridium), clonal (PNH), and mechanical/toxic. Mapping the cause to a category drives the confirmatory test.
⚠️ WRONG: 'PNH is an inherited hemolytic anemia because it is a genetic mutation.' PNH is ACQUIRED — a somatic PIGA mutation in a hematopoietic stem cell clone (not germline/inherited), causing GPI-anchor (CD55/CD59) loss and complement-mediated intravascular hemolysis. Discriminator: diagnose PNH by flow cytometry for CD55/CD59 deficiency (FLAER), not by family history.
14. IVH wanted poster
The intravascular hemolysis 'most wanted' list: PNH, G6PD crisis, MAHA (TTP/HUS/DIC) and march/march hemoglobinuria, ABO-incompatible transfusion reaction, prosthetic valve, and infections (malaria, Babesia, Clostridium perfringens sepsis). Hallmarks: hemoglobinemia, hemoglobinuria, hemosiderinuria, very low haptoglobin, very high LDH.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia is a classic cause of intravascular hemolysis.' Warm AIHA is predominantly EXTRAvascular (splenic macrophage clearance of IgG-coated RBCs) — it does not typically cause hemoglobinuria. Discriminator: warm AIHA shows spherocytes, splenomegaly, and indirect hyperbilirubinemia; intravascular hallmarks (hemoglobinuria, hemosiderinuria) point to PNH/MAHA/G6PD/ABO instead.
