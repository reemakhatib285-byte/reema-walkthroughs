📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. DAT — Coombs first
The Direct Antiglobulin Test (DAT/Coombs) is the first branch point in any hemolysis work-up: positive = immune (autoimmune, drug, alloimmune); negative = non-immune (membrane, enzyme, hemoglobinopathy, MAHA, PNH). Confirm hemolysis itself first with reticulocytes, LDH, haptoglobin and indirect bilirubin.
⚠️ Ordering an osmotic-fragility or G6PD assay before the DAT — the DAT splits immune from non-immune and reshapes the entire work-up; a positive DAT makes hereditary spherocytosis unlikely (HS is Coombs-negative).
2. Intravascular hemolysis
Intravascular hemolysis = RBCs lyse inside the vessels, releasing free hemoglobin. Hallmarks: very high LDH, very low/absent haptoglobin, hemoglobinemia → hemoglobinuria → hemosiderinuria. Causes: PNH, MAHA, severe G6PD, transfusion reactions, mechanical/prosthetic valves, march hemoglobinuria.
⚠️ Labeling splenomegaly + spherocytes as "intravascular" — those are extravascular signs; intravascular shows hemoglobinuria and hemosiderinuria.
3. Hemoglobinuria
Free hemoglobin saturates haptoglobin then filters into urine → red-brown urine that is dipstick-positive for blood but shows NO red cells on microscopy (separating it from true hematuria). Chronic loss causes hemosiderinuria and iron deficiency, classic in PNH.
⚠️ A positive urine "blood" dipstick with no RBCs read as hematuria — it is hemoglobinuria (or myoglobinuria), a marker of intravascular hemolysis.
4. LDH
LDH pours out of lysed red cells and is markedly elevated in intravascular hemolysis; high LDH with low haptoglobin is the most specific lab combination for hemolysis. It also rises in ineffective erythropoiesis (megaloblastic anemia).
⚠️ A mildly high LDH alone calling hemolysis — it must be paired with low haptoglobin, high reticulocytes and high indirect bilirubin.
5. Haptoglobin
Haptoglobin binds free hemoglobin and is cleared, so it falls (often undetectable) in intravascular hemolysis; low haptoglobin + high LDH is the most specific combo for hemolysis.
⚠️ Haptoglobin is an acute-phase reactant — inflammation can falsely normalize it, so a normal value does not exclude hemolysis.
6. Hemosiderin
Chronic intravascular hemolysis deposits iron in shed renal tubular cells → hemosiderinuria (Prussian-blue positive) appearing days after onset. It is a marker of ongoing intravascular hemolysis, classically PNH.
⚠️ Expecting it immediately — hemosiderinuria appears only days later as iron-laden tubular cells slough.
7. Extravascular (spleen)
Extravascular hemolysis = splenic/hepatic macrophages remove antibody- or abnormality-tagged RBCs. Hallmarks: high indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin, high urobilinogen, splenomegaly, near-normal haptoglobin, spherocytes. Causes: warm AIHA, hereditary spherocytosis, hemoglobinopathies.
⚠️ Expecting hemoglobinuria — it is NOT a feature of pure extravascular hemolysis.
8. Unconjugated bilirubin
Heme catabolism produces unconjugated (indirect) hyperbilirubinemia with jaundice and pigment gallstones; the conjugated fraction stays normal because the liver conjugates normally.
⚠️ A high CONJUGATED bilirubin pointing to hemolysis — hemolytic jaundice is unconjugated; conjugated hyperbilirubinemia means hepatobiliary disease.
9. Urobilinogen
The increased bilirubin load raises urinary and fecal urobilinogen, supporting extravascular hemolysis.
⚠️ (supportive sign only)
10. Spherocytes
Spherocytes on the smear mean part of the membrane was removed by the spleen; they occur in BOTH hereditary spherocytosis (Coombs-negative) and warm AIHA (Coombs-positive). The DAT distinguishes them.
⚠️ Reading spherocytes as automatically hereditary spherocytosis — warm AIHA is also spherocytic; check the DAT.
11. Splenomegaly
Splenic processing of tagged cells enlarges the spleen; common in extravascular hemolysis (HS, warm AIHA, thalassemia).
⚠️ (supportive sign only)
12. Reticulocytes high
The appropriate marrow response to hemolysis is reticulocytosis (corrected retic / RPI > 2–3). A hemolytic anemia with LOW reticulocytes signals a superimposed marrow problem.
⚠️ A normal/low retic count in acute hemolysis read as reassuring — it actually means the marrow is failing to compensate.
13. Parvovirus B19 — aplastic crisis
Parvovirus B19 infects erythroid progenitors causing transient pure red-cell aplasia. In chronic hemolysis (HS, sickle cell) the shortened RBC lifespan makes marrow shutdown cause a sudden APLASTIC CRISIS: Hb plummets with reticulocytopenia.
⚠️ A sudden Hb drop with LOW reticulocytes in a known hemolytic patient mistaken for a hemolytic flare — it is aplastic crisis (Parvovirus B19); a flare has HIGH retics.
