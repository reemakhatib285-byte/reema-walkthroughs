📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. DDAVP replaces AVP — monitor Na closely
Desmopressin (DDAVP) is the treatment for central DI (AVP deficiency), replacing the missing hormone via V2 receptors. The dominant safety issue is iatrogenic hyponatremia, so serum sodium must be monitored.
⚠️ DDAVP only helps central DI — using it for nephrogenic DI fails, and over-dosing any patient causes dilutional hyponatremia.
2. Four DDAVP routes
DDAVP is given IV/SC (1–2 mcg, onset ~15 min), intranasal (10–20 mcg BID), oral (100–400 mcg BID-TID), or oral melt/sublingual. Bioavailability differs greatly, so doses are NOT interchangeable mg-for-mg.
⚠️ Switching routes without re-titrating is dangerous — oral DDAVP needs far higher mcg than IV; copy-pasting the dose causes under- or over-treatment.
3. IV/SC route
Parenteral DDAVP (1–2 mcg SC/IV) has the fastest onset (~15 min) and highest potency — used acutely (post-op DI, unable to take orals). Requires careful fluid balance.
⚠️ Parenteral DDAVP is the most potent route — applying an oral mcg dose IV would massively overdose the patient.
4. Intranasal route
Intranasal DDAVP (10–20 mcg BID) is convenient for chronic central DI but absorption is erratic with rhinitis/mucosal disease. Titrate to allow a daily 'breakthrough' to avoid water retention.
⚠️ Nasal congestion alters intranasal absorption unpredictably — blaming 'treatment failure' may just be impaired mucosal uptake.
5. Oral / oral melt
Oral DDAVP (100–400 mcg) and sublingual melt have low bioavailability, requiring much larger mcg doses than nasal/parenteral. Preferred for stable outpatient central DI.
⚠️ Oral DDAVP mcg numbers are large precisely because absorption is poor — never equate them to the tiny IV/SC dose.
6. DDAVP escape — intermittent delay = transient aquaresis
Intentionally allowing the DDAVP effect to wane between doses ('DDAVP escape') lets the patient excrete free water periodically, preventing chronic water retention and hyponatremia. Build a daily breakthrough into dosing.
⚠️ Round-the-clock DDAVP with no gap removes the escape window and steadily lowers sodium — the most common complication of DDAVP therapy is hyponatremia.
7. Hyponatremia risk — most common complication
Hyponatremia is the chief adverse effect of DDAVP, from continued water intake plus antidiuresis. Risk rises with excess fluids and missed escape windows; severe (<130) hyponatremia can cause seizures.
⚠️ Symptomatic hyponatremia, not hypernatremia, is the DDAVP danger — counsel fluid moderation and check Na, especially when starting/escalating.
8. Hyponatremia severity — ~25% any, ~15% severe <130
Roughly a quarter of DDAVP-treated patients develop some hyponatremia and ~15% severe (<130 mmol/L). Drives the practice of periodic monitoring and built-in aquaresis windows.
⚠️ Even 'stable' DDAVP patients need periodic Na checks — a significant minority drift to severe hyponatremia silently.
9. Lithium NDI — amiloride blocks ENaC entry
For lithium-induced nephrogenic DI, amiloride blocks the ENaC channel through which lithium enters principal cells, limiting AQP2 downregulation. HCTZ reduces volume and paradoxically reduces urine output.
⚠️ Amiloride is the targeted agent for lithium NDI; adding a thiazide raises lithium levels (volume contraction), so monitor lithium for toxicity.
10. HCTZ + volume-contraction caution
Thiazides treat nephrogenic DI by inducing mild volume contraction that enhances proximal Na/water reabsorption, cutting distal water delivery and urine output. In lithium NDI they can raise lithium levels.
⚠️ Thiazide for nephrogenic DI works by VOLUME contraction — but in lithium patients that same contraction precipitates lithium toxicity, so check levels.
11. Nephrogenic DI treatment combo
Nephrogenic DI is managed with a low-solute (low-Na, low-protein) diet, thiazide diuretic, amiloride (esp. lithium), and an NSAID (indomethacin) which blunts prostaglandin antagonism of AVP. DDAVP is ineffective.
⚠️ NSAIDs reduce urine output in nephrogenic DI by removing prostaglandin inhibition of water reabsorption — but watch renal function; and remember DDAVP won't work here.
12. Partial resistance combo / monitor
Partial nephrogenic DI may respond to high-dose DDAVP combined with thiazide/amiloride and solute restriction. Monitor urine output and creatinine to titrate the combination.
⚠️ Partial resistance can show some DDAVP response, blurring central vs nephrogenic — copeptin testing clarifies before committing to a regimen.
13. Primary polydipsia — fluid restriction supportive
Primary polydipsia is treated by reducing water intake (behavioral fluid restriction) plus symptom aids (ice chips, hard candy for dry mouth). DDAVP is generally avoided due to hyponatremia risk.
⚠️ Giving DDAVP/AVP analogues to a primary-polydipsia patient who keeps drinking precipitates dangerous hyponatremia — restrict water instead.
14. See adipsic ward for adynamic thirst
Adipsic (adynamic-thirst) DI patients lack the thirst safeguard, so they cannot self-correct rising sodium — they need scheduled fixed water intake plus DDAVP and frequent Na monitoring.
⚠️ Adipsic DI is the highest-risk subtype: without thirst, both hyper- and hyponatremia occur easily — fixed fluid prescriptions, not 'drink to thirst', are required.
