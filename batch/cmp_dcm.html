📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. DCM is a phenotype, not a diagnosis
Dilated cardiomyopathy is a final common pathway that many diseases produce, not a diagnosis in itself. 'Idiopathic DCM' is only permissible after excluding all reversible and heritable causes. Board trap: in newly diagnosed DCM the most important next step is a thorough etiologic workup — beginning with ruling out ischemia — NOT immediate GDMT or ICD placement.
⚠️ The wrong first move is jumping to GDMT/ICD or labeling it idiopathic — the mandatory first step is asking WHY and excluding reversible causes.
2. Ischemic — rule out first
Ischemic cardiomyopathy is the MOST COMMON cause of DCM and must always be ruled out first with coronary imaging (angiography or CT coronary angiography). You cannot call a DCM 'nonischemic' until significant CAD is excluded, because revascularization can change management and prognosis.
⚠️ Don't label new DCM as nonischemic/idiopathic without coronary imaging — missing occult CAD is the classic error.
3. Genetic — TTN truncation
TTN (titin) truncating variants are the most common genetic cause of DCM, found in ~15–25% of familial and ~3% of sporadic cases. A family history of DCM or premature sudden death should prompt genetic evaluation and cascade screening of first-degree relatives. Certain genotypes (e.g., LMNA, others) carry distinct arrhythmic risk that changes ICD timing.
⚠️ The most common genetic cause is TTN truncation, not LMNA — LMNA is less common but disproportionately arrhythmic and drives earlier ICD decisions.
4. Post-myocarditis
Viral/post-myocarditis is a recognized cause of DCM; a viral prodrome preceding heart failure, troponin elevation, and characteristic late-gadolinium enhancement on cardiac MRI support it. Many cases improve with GDMT and time, reinforcing the 'identify and treat the trigger' principle before committing to device therapy.
⚠️ A recent viral illness plus new DCM is post-myocarditis to treat and reassess — not an automatic indication for immediate ICD on the basis of low EF.
5. Alcoholic CM — threshold
Alcoholic cardiomyopathy classically follows heavy intake of about 60–80 g/day of alcohol for 5 or more years. It is a diagnosis of an identifiable, reversible trigger: the dominant management lever is abstinence. Concurrent thiamine deficiency (wet beriberi) should be considered in heavy drinkers with high-output features.
⚠️ Light or moderate intake does not cause alcoholic CM — the tested threshold is roughly 60–80 g/day for at least 5 years.
6. Chemotherapy — anthracyclines
Anthracyclines (e.g., doxorubicin) cause dose-dependent cardiomyopathy; risk rises with cumulative dose and prior chest radiation. Management includes stopping/holding the offending agent plus GDMT; serial echo/strain surveillance during therapy helps catch early dysfunction. Trastuzumab cardiotoxicity, by contrast, is often reversible.
⚠️ Doxorubicin cardiotoxicity is largely dose-dependent and often irreversible — don't equate it with the typically reversible, non-dose-dependent trastuzumab effect.
7. Peripartum CM — timeline
Peripartum cardiomyopathy presents from the last month of pregnancy through ~5 months postpartum — that window is the diagnostic criterion. Most recover within ~6 months with GDMT (and bromocriptine in some protocols). It is a reversible-trigger DCM where recovery is common but not universal.
⚠️ Heart failure appearing in early/mid-pregnancy is more likely pre-existing cardiomyopathy unmasked, not peripartum CM — the window is last month to 5 months postpartum.
8. Subsequent pregnancy risk
If EF has fully normalized after peripartum CM, a subsequent pregnancy is possible but still carries risk. If EF remains reduced, subsequent pregnancy carries roughly a 25–50% risk of further deterioration and maternal mortality. Never approve a subsequent pregnancy without verified full EF normalization.
⚠️ Persistently reduced EF after peripartum CM is a strong contraindication to another pregnancy — don't clear the patient on symptom resolution alone; require documented EF recovery.
9. Tachycardia-induced CM
Persistent tachyarrhythmia (e.g., uncontrolled AF/flutter, frequent PVCs, incessant SVT) can drive a reversible DCM. Rate/rhythm control or ablation that removes the trigger frequently restores EF over weeks to months — a key 'treat the cause' scenario where premature ICD should be avoided.
⚠️ Low EF with an uncontrolled tachyarrhythmia may be tachycardia-induced and reversible — control the rhythm and reassess rather than implanting an ICD up front.
10. Alcoholic CM recovery zone
Alcoholic cardiomyopathy can recover substantially with abstinence — improvement is typically seen over 3–6 months, unlike most cardiomyopathies. The intervention IS abstinence: continued heavy drinking leads to progressive deterioration and poor prognosis. This reversibility is why ICD placement should be deferred until a GDMT-plus-abstinence trial.
⚠️ Don't write alcoholic CM off as fixed/irreversible — with abstinence plus GDMT, EF often improves over 3–6 months.
11. Grim reaper — continued drinking
The grim reaper encodes the alternative path: continued heavy alcohol use drives progressive myocardial deterioration and poor prognosis. The single most effective intervention is abstinence; without it, GDMT alone cannot reverse the disease and the patient declines.
⚠️ Escalating HF medications without enforcing abstinence misses the point — ongoing heavy drinking, not undertreatment, is what determines the poor outcome here.
12. GDMT first — defer ICD
In newly diagnosed DCM — especially alcoholic, peripartum, or tachycardia-induced — EF may improve substantially with 3–6 months of GDMT plus trigger removal. Placing an ICD immediately on a low EF at diagnosis risks unnecessary implantation. Board rule: wait at least 3 months of optimized GDMT before reassessing ICD eligibility in new DCM.
⚠️ A low EF at first diagnosis is NOT an immediate ICD indication — wait ≥3 months of optimized GDMT (unless a specific high-risk feature is present) and reassess.
13. LMNA — conduction disease before EF drops
LMNA (lamin A/C) cardiomyopathy follows a distinctive sequence: AV conduction disease first (first-degree → complete AV block), then supraventricular arrhythmias (AF), then ventricular arrhythmias, and only later LV systolic dysfunction. Classic vignette: middle-aged patient + AV block + family history of SCD + mild LV dysfunction → LMNA. ICD consideration arises EARLIER than the usual EF ≤35% threshold because arrhythmic sudden death precedes severe LV dysfunction.
⚠️ Waiting for EF ≤35% in LMNA is the trap — conduction disease and arrhythmic SCD risk come before EF drops, so consider an ICD early (don't apply the standard EF threshold).
14. 1/3 of nonischemic DCM recover
About one-third of recent-onset nonischemic DCM can recover substantially with optimal therapy — prognosis is not uniformly poor. Recovery levers: CRT for LBBB (resynchronization dramatically improves EF in LBBB-associated DCM), full GDMT, achieving euvolemia, and removing the trigger (alcohol abstinence, controlling tachycardia, treating hypothyroidism, stopping offending chemotherapy).
⚠️ Don't counsel uniformly grim prognosis in new nonischemic DCM — roughly one-third recover substantially, and CRT for LBBB plus trigger removal can normalize EF.
