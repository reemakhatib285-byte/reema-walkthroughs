⚠️ Board traps 17
  1. First-line: D-A-A-C — WRONG: choosing a beta-blocker as first-line for uncomplicated essential HTN. Discriminator — beta-blockers are NOT first-line absent a compelling indication (post-MI, HFrEF, AFib, angina); they are less effective at preventing stroke than the D-A-A-C classes.
  2. Thiazides — WRONG: continuing/starting a thiazide in a patient who develops gout or in one with recurrent calcium kidney stones being worked up — and confusing the calcium direction. Discriminator — thiazides cause hyperuricemia (precipitate gout) and HYPERcalcemia (loops cause hypocalcemia); if a stem shows low Na + low K + high Ca + high uric acid, think thiazide, not a loop diuretic.
  3. ACE inhibitors — WRONG: switching an ACEi-induced ANGIOEDEMA patient to an ARB. Discriminator — for the dry COUGH, switching to an ARB is correct (ARBs don't raise bradykinin); but after ANGIOEDEMA, ARBs carry cross-reactivity risk and the safer move is a different class entirely (e.g., CCB).
  4. ARBs — WRONG: assuming ARBs are safe in pregnancy because they 'aren't ACE inhibitors.' Discriminator — ARBs share the same fetal renal/skull toxicity and oligohydramnios as ACEi and are contraindicated in pregnancy; the difference from ACEi is only the cough/angioedema profile, not teratogenicity.
  5. NEVER combine ACEi+ARB — WRONG: adding an ARB to maximize renoprotection in a diabetic already on an ACEi with persistent proteinuria. Discriminator — dual RAAS blockade harms (AKI, hyperkalemia) without outcome benefit; instead optimize the single agent and add a different class (e.g., MRA, SGLT2 inhibitor) or a diuretic.
  6. DHP CCB — WRONG: treating amlodipine-induced ankle edema with a loop diuretic. Discriminator — DHP edema is from arteriolar dilation/capillary pressure, NOT volume overload, so diuretics don't help; the fix is dose reduction or adding an ACEi/ARB (venodilation balances the precapillary dilation).
  7. Non-DHP CCB — WRONG: adding verapamil or diltiazem to a patient already on a beta-blocker. Discriminator — both suppress the AV node and contractility; the combination risks profound bradycardia, complete heart block, and decompensated heart failure. Also avoid non-DHP CCBs in HFrEF.

Discriminator — switch to eplerenone for the gynecomastia (no anti-androgen effect); and combining an MRA with ACEi/ARB markedly raises hyperkalemia risk, so potassium and creatinine must be monitored closely.
  10. Gatekeeper / staging — WRONG: starting two drugs for every patient labeled 'hypertensive,' including low-risk Stage 1. 📖 Read-through — all 17 symbols
1. First-line: D-A-A-C
Four first-line classes for uncomplicated HTN: thiazide Diuretic, ACE inhibitor, ARB, and CCB (the 'D-A-A-C' shelf). Any one can start uncomplicated HTN; choice is individualized (e.g., ACEi/ARB in diabetes/CKD, thiazide/CCB often preferred in Black patients, CCB/thiazide in isolated systolic HTN of elderly). Stage 2 HTN typically begins with TWO agents from different classes.
⚠️ WRONG: choosing a beta-blocker as first-line for uncomplicated essential HTN. Discriminator — beta-blockers are NOT first-line absent a compelling indication (post-MI, HFrEF, AFib, angina); they are less effective at preventing stroke than the D-A-A-C classes.
2. Thiazides
Thiazides (hydrochlorothiazide 12.5-50 mg, chlorthalidone 12.5-25 mg, indapamide) block the Na-Cl cotransporter (NCC) in the distal convoluted tubule. Chlorthalidone is longer-acting and more potent than HCTZ. Metabolic effects: hypokalemia, hypoNATREMIA, metabolic alkalosis, hyperuricemia (gout), hyperGLYCEMIA, hyperlipidemia, and hyperCALCEMIA. Remember the calcium direction: thiazides RAISE calcium (used in calcium-stone prevention and hypercalciuria) whereas loops LOWER it.
⚠️ WRONG: continuing/starting a thiazide in a patient who develops gout or in one with recurrent calcium kidney stones being worked up — and confusing the calcium direction. Discriminator — thiazides cause hyperuricemia (precipitate gout) and HYPERcalcemia (loops cause hypocalcemia); if a stem shows low Na + low K + high Ca + high uric acid, think thiazide, not a loop diuretic.
3. ACE inhibitors
ACE inhibitors ('-pril': lisinopril 10-40 mg, enalapril, ramipril) block conversion of angiotensin I to II and reduce bradykinin breakdown. Effects: lower BP, reduce proteinuria, renoprotective in diabetic nephropathy/CKD, and improve survival in HFrEF and post-MI. Side effects: dry COUGH and ANGIOEDEMA (both bradykinin-mediated), HYPERKALEMIA, acute kidney injury in bilateral RAS, and teratogenicity. A mild creatinine rise up to ~30% is acceptable.
⚠️ WRONG: switching an ACEi-induced ANGIOEDEMA patient to an ARB. Discriminator — for the dry COUGH, switching to an ARB is correct (ARBs don't raise bradykinin); but after ANGIOEDEMA, ARBs carry cross-reactivity risk and the safer move is a different class entirely (e.g., CCB).
4. ARBs
ARBs ('-sartan': losartan 50-100 mg, valsartan, candesartan) block the angiotensin II type-1 receptor. Same hemodynamic and renoprotective benefits as ACEi but WITHOUT cough or significant bradykinin-mediated angioedema. They still raise potassium, can cause AKI in bilateral RAS, and are TERATOGENIC. First choice when an ACEi causes intolerable cough.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming ARBs are safe in pregnancy because they 'aren't ACE inhibitors.' Discriminator — ARBs share the same fetal renal/skull toxicity and oligohydramnios as ACEi and are contraindicated in pregnancy; the difference from ACEi is only the cough/angioedema profile, not teratogenicity.
5. NEVER combine ACEi+ARB
Do NOT combine an ACEi with an ARB. Dual RAAS blockade gives no added cardiovascular/renal benefit and significantly increases hyperkalemia, hypotension, and acute kidney injury (ONTARGET trial). The same caution applies to adding a direct renin inhibitor (aliskiren) to an ACEi/ARB, especially in diabetics.
⚠️ WRONG: adding an ARB to maximize renoprotection in a diabetic already on an ACEi with persistent proteinuria. Discriminator — dual RAAS blockade harms (AKI, hyperkalemia) without outcome benefit; instead optimize the single agent and add a different class (e.g., MRA, SGLT2 inhibitor) or a diuretic.
6. DHP CCB
Dihydropyridine CCBs ('-dipine': amlodipine 5-10 mg, nifedipine, felodipine) block vascular L-type calcium channels -> potent arterial VASODILATION with little effect on the heart's conduction. Side effects: dose-dependent peripheral/PEDAL EDEMA (from precapillary dilation, not fluid overload), flushing, headache, reflex tachycardia, and gingival hyperplasia. Good in isolated systolic HTN of the elderly and in Black patients.
⚠️ WRONG: treating amlodipine-induced ankle edema with a loop diuretic. Discriminator — DHP edema is from arteriolar dilation/capillary pressure, NOT volume overload, so diuretics don't help; the fix is dose reduction or adding an ACEi/ARB (venodilation balances the precapillary dilation).
7. Non-DHP CCB
Non-dihydropyridine CCBs — verapamil (most cardioselective, also constipation) and diltiazem — block cardiac L-type calcium channels: they slow the SA/AV node (negative chronotrope/dromotrope) and reduce contractility (negative inotrope). Useful for rate control in AFib/SVT and for angina. Side effects: bradycardia, AV block, worsening HFrEF, and constipation (verapamil).
⚠️ WRONG: adding verapamil or diltiazem to a patient already on a beta-blocker. Discriminator — both suppress the AV node and contractility; the combination risks profound bradycardia, complete heart block, and decompensated heart failure. Also avoid non-DHP CCBs in HFrEF.
8. Beta-blockers
Beta-blockers (metoprolol, carvedilol, bisoprolol, atenolol) reduce HR, contractility, and renin release. NOT first-line for uncomplicated HTN, but indicated with compelling reasons: post-MI, HFrEF (carvedilol/metoprolol succinate/bisoprolol), rate control in AFib, angina, and certain tachyarrhythmias. Side effects: bradycardia, fatigue, masking of hypoglycemia symptoms, bronchospasm (non-selective), and rebound HTN/tachycardia if stopped abruptly.
⚠️ WRONG: abruptly discontinuing a beta-blocker in a patient with coronary disease. Discriminator — abrupt withdrawal causes receptor up-regulation and rebound tachycardia/HTN, precipitating angina, MI, or arrhythmia; beta-blockers must be tapered.
9. Spironolactone / MRA
Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists — spironolactone 25-50 mg (eplerenone is more selective) — block aldosterone at the distal nephron/collecting duct: potassium-sparing diuresis. They are the evidence-based ADD-ON of choice for RESISTANT HTN (uncontrolled on a thiazide + ACEi/ARB + CCB, per PATHWAY-2) and treat primary aldosteronism and HFrEF. Spironolactone also causes GYNECOMASTIA, breast tenderness, and menstrual irregularity (anti-androgen effect); eplerenone avoids these.
⚠️ WRONG: continuing spironolactone in a man who develops painful gynecomastia, or stacking it on an ACEi/ARB without checking K+. Discriminator — switch to eplerenone for the gynecomastia (no anti-androgen effect); and combining an MRA with ACEi/ARB markedly raises hyperkalemia risk, so potassium and creatinine must be monitored closely.
10. Gatekeeper / staging
ACC/AHA staging gates therapy: Normal <120/80; Elevated 120-129/<80; Stage 1 130-139 or 80-89; Stage 2 >=140 or >=90. Stage 1 with <10% 10-year ASCVD risk -> lifestyle first; Stage 1 with high risk (ASCVD, diabetes, CKD, >=10% risk) or any Stage 2 -> start drug therapy. Stage 2 usually starts with TWO drugs from different classes. General target <130/80.
⚠️ WRONG: starting two drugs for every patient labeled 'hypertensive,' including low-risk Stage 1. Discriminator — initiate two-drug therapy for Stage 2 (or markedly above goal); low-risk Stage 1 gets lifestyle first, then a single agent if needed. Threshold and intensity scale with stage AND CV risk.
11. Lifestyle is additive
Lifestyle interventions lower BP independently and ADDITIVELY — stacking DASH, sodium restriction, weight loss, exercise, potassium, and alcohol moderation can total 10-15+ mmHg, rivaling a drug. They are foundational at every stage and continue alongside medication; they also improve drug efficacy and CV risk profile.
⚠️ WRONG: abandoning lifestyle counseling once medication is started. Discriminator — lifestyle effects are additive to drug effects and reduce the number/dose of drugs needed; they remain part of the plan after pharmacotherapy begins.
12. DASH diet
The DASH diet (fruits, vegetables, low-fat dairy, whole grains; limited saturated fat/red meat/sweets) gives the LARGEST single lifestyle BP drop, up to ~11 mmHg SBP in hypertensives, and is even larger when combined with sodium restriction. It also raises dietary potassium and fiber.
⚠️ WRONG: equating a generic 'low-fat diet' with DASH. Discriminator — DASH's BP benefit comes from its specific high-produce, high-potassium, high-dairy, low-sodium pattern; a nonspecific low-fat diet does not reproduce the ~11 mmHg effect.
13. Sodium & weight
Sodium restriction to ~1500-2300 mg/day lowers SBP ~5 mmHg (more in salt-sensitive: older, Black, CKD, diabetic patients), and weight loss lowers SBP ~1 mmHg per kilogram lost. Both are dose-dependent and additive with DASH. Most sodium is from processed/restaurant foods.
⚠️ WRONG: expecting little benefit from modest weight loss in an obese patient. Discriminator — BP falls ~1 mmHg per kg, so even a 5-10% body-weight loss yields a meaningful drop; benefit is proportional and does not require reaching ideal weight.
14. Potassium / aerobic / alcohol
Three more additive measures each lower SBP ~5 mmHg: increasing dietary potassium (~3500-5000 mg/day, food-based), aerobic exercise (>=150 min/week moderate intensity), and reducing alcohol (men <=2/day, women <=1/day; abstinence best). Potassium's effect is larger in Black adults.
⚠️ WRONG: recommending potassium supplementation/salt substitutes to a CKD patient or one on an ACEi/ARB/MRA. Discriminator — these patients can't excrete a potassium load and risk dangerous hyperkalemia; food-based potassium counseling is fine only when renal function and RAAS-blockade allow.
15. Loop diuretics (volume/CKD)
NEW: Loop diuretics — furosemide, torsemide, bumetanide — block the Na-K-2Cl cotransporter in the thick ascending limb (most powerful natriuresis). In HTN they are not first-line but are PREFERRED over thiazides when GFR is low (eGFR <30) or with volume overload/heart failure, because thiazides lose efficacy in advanced CKD. Side effects: hypokalemia, hypocalcemia (calcium WASTING), hypomagnesemia, metabolic alkalosis, ototoxicity, and hyperuricemia.
⚠️ WRONG: choosing a thiazide for HTN in a patient with advanced CKD (eGFR <30). Discriminator — thiazides are largely ineffective at low GFR; switch to a loop diuretic. Also remember loops LOWER calcium (used for hypercalcemia) whereas thiazides RAISE it — opposite calcium effects.
16. Hydralazine / direct vasodilators
NEW: Direct arteriolar vasodilators. Hydralazine reduces afterload (used IV in pregnancy/hypertensive emergencies and in the hydralazine-isosorbide dinitrate combo for HFrEF in Black patients); it causes reflex tachycardia and DRUG-INDUCED LUPUS (anti-histone antibodies). Minoxidil is a potent K-channel-opener vasodilator reserved for refractory HTN; it causes marked fluid retention and HYPERTRICHOSIS (the basis for topical hair-loss use).
⚠️ WRONG: missing drug-induced lupus in a patient on hydralazine who presents with arthralgias, fever, and serositis. Discriminator — hydralazine (and procainamide, isoniazid) cause a lupus-like syndrome with anti-HISTONE antibodies that resolves on stopping the drug; it typically spares the CNS and kidneys unlike idiopathic SLE.
17. Alpha-1 blockers (-zosin)
NEW: Alpha-1 blockers ('-zosin': doxazosin, terazosin, prazosin) relax vascular and prostatic smooth muscle. Not first-line for HTN (ALLHAT showed worse heart-failure outcomes vs chlorthalidone), but they are the antihypertensive of choice when HTN coexists with BPH (treat both). Classic side effect: FIRST-DOSE orthostatic hypotension/syncope — start low, dose at bedtime.
⚠️ WRONG: selecting an alpha-blocker as first-line monotherapy for uncomplicated HTN. Discriminator — ALLHAT demonstrated increased heart failure with doxazosin vs a thiazide; reserve alpha-blockers for the BPH-plus-HTN patient, and warn about first-dose orthostatic hypotension.
