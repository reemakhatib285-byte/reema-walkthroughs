📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. HBsAg — active infection
HBsAg (surface antigen) positive = current active HBV infection, acute or chronic, with virus/antigen present in blood. It is the FIRST serologic marker to appear (1-10 weeks after exposure, before symptoms) and the screening test for active infection. It is the antigen the vaccine is made from (recombinant HBsAg).
⚠️ HBsAg persisting >6 months DEFINES chronic infection. A common trap: HBsAg can be transiently positive for up to ~3 weeks after HBV vaccination — but the vaccine never produces anti-HBc, which distinguishes it from real infection.
2. Anti-HBs — immunity
Anti-HBs (surface antibody) = IMMUNITY/protection, arising either from resolved natural infection OR from vaccination. A titer ≥10 mIU/mL is considered protective. Its appearance after the window period marks recovery and non-infectivity.
⚠️ Anti-HBs is the ONLY HBV marker present after vaccination. If a stem shows anti-HBs positive but anti-HBc NEGATIVE, the patient is vaccinated, not recovered from infection.
3. Anti-HBc — real infection only
Anti-HBc (core antibody) appears only with true HBV infection — NEVER from vaccine (the vaccine contains no core antigen). IgM anti-HBc = acute/recent infection (and is the sole marker during the window period); IgG anti-HBc = past or chronic infection and persists for life.
⚠️ Anti-HBc is the marker that distinguishes vaccinated (anti-HBc NEGATIVE) from naturally resolved infection (anti-HBc POSITIVE). 'Isolated anti-HBc positive' = window period, distant resolved infection with waned anti-HBs, or false positive — order HBV DNA to sort it out.
4. HBeAg — high replication
HBeAg (envelope/secreted antigen) = active viral replication, high HBV DNA, and high infectivity. Its presence in a chronic carrier marks the immune-active, treatment-relevant, highly contagious phase and the highest perinatal transmission risk.
⚠️ HBeAg positivity = highly contagious; an HBeAg+ pregnant mother confers very high perinatal transmission, mandating HBIG + vaccine to the neonate. Don't equate HBeAg-negative with low DNA — see the pre-core mutant.
5. Anti-HBe — low replication
Anti-HBe appears after HBeAg seroconversion and generally signals declining replication, lower HBV DNA, and reduced infectivity — often the transition to the inactive carrier state.
⚠️ Anti-HBe usually means low replication, BUT a pre-core/basal-core promoter mutant can be anti-HBe positive yet still have HIGH HBV DNA and active disease. Always confirm with HBV DNA before calling a patient 'inactive.'
6. IgM anti-HBc excludes acute
IgM anti-HBc is the marker of ACUTE/recent HBV: positive confirms acute infection, negative essentially rules out acute infection. It is also the ONLY positive marker during the window period when HBsAg has cleared but anti-HBs has not yet risen.
⚠️ In the window period, IgM anti-HBc may be the SOLE positive marker (HBsAg negative, anti-HBs negative) — so a negative HBsAg does not exclude recent acute HBV. Always include core antibody, not just HBsAg, when working up acute hepatitis.
7. Acute HBV ship
Acute HBV profile: HBsAg+, IgM anti-HBc+, HBeAg+ (high DNA, high infectivity). In immunocompetent adults >95% clear spontaneously within ~6 months, developing anti-HBs and IgG anti-HBc.
⚠️ Duration defines outcome: <6 months = acute; HBsAg persistence beyond 6 months = chronic. Acute HBV in a healthy adult is usually NOT treated with antivirals (it self-resolves) — treatment is reserved for severe/fulminant or chronic disease.
8. Chronic HBV — replicative
Chronic replicative (immune-active) HBV: HBsAg+ >6 months, HBeAg+, HIGH HBV DNA, IgG anti-HBc+, and elevated ALT. This phase carries the highest risk of progression and is the treatment-eligible group — first-line agents are entecavir, tenofovir (TDF/TAF), or pegylated interferon.
⚠️ HBeAg+ chronic with high DNA + elevated ALT = treat. Don't withhold therapy here. Conversely, don't treat the immune-tolerant phase (HBeAg+, high DNA, but persistently normal ALT) reflexively — ALT and fibrosis guide treatment timing.
9. Chronic HBV — non-replicative
Inactive carrier (non-replicative): HBsAg+, HBeAg negative, anti-HBe+, low/undetectable HBV DNA (<2000 IU/mL), and persistently normal ALT. Usually monitored rather than treated, with periodic ALT/DNA and HCC surveillance.
⚠️ Low DNA + normal ALT = surveillance, not antivirals. BUT an inactive carrier can reactivate (e.g., with chemo, rituximab, steroids) — give prophylactic entecavir/tenofovir before immunosuppression, since HBsAg+ patients can reactivate fatally.
10. Window period ship
Window period: HBsAg has disappeared but anti-HBs has not yet risen, so the ONLY positive serology is anti-HBc (IgM). It represents the gap between clearance of antigen and detectable protective antibody.
⚠️ THE classic exam trap: a patient with acute hepatitis whose HBsAg AND anti-HBs are both negative — diagnosis hinges on the isolated IgM anti-HBc. Missing the window period leads to wrongly excluding HBV.
11. Resolved ship
Resolved (past) infection with natural immunity: anti-HBs+ AND IgG anti-HBc+, with HBsAg negative. Both antibodies present = the body cleared a real infection and is now immune.
⚠️ Both anti-HBs and anti-HBc positive = PAST INFECTION, not vaccination. The presence of anti-HBc is the tell — vaccine never makes anti-HBc.
12. Vaccinated ship
Vaccinated profile: anti-HBs+ ONLY (HBsAg negative, anti-HBc negative). The recombinant HBsAg vaccine generates protective surface antibody without ever exposing the immune system to core antigen.
⚠️ Isolated anti-HBs = vaccinated; add a positive anti-HBc and it becomes resolved natural infection. The single discriminator between vaccine immunity and recovered immunity is anti-HBc.
13. Pre-core mutant ship
Pre-core / basal-core promoter mutant: a strain unable to make HBeAg, so it is HBeAg-NEGATIVE and anti-HBe-positive yet still has HIGH HBV DNA and active necroinflammatory disease (elevated ALT). It is treatment-eligible like other active chronic HBV.
⚠️ Don't be fooled by negative HBeAg into calling the patient inactive — HBV DNA OVERRIDES HBeAg status. HBeAg-negative chronic hepatitis B with high DNA and elevated ALT still needs antiviral therapy.
