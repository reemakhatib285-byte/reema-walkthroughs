⚠️ Board traps 12
  1. Any organ / looks like cancer — WRONG answer: working up each organ mass as a separate primary malignancy. Discriminator: when an older man develops sequential or synchronous steroid-responsive 'tumors' across multiple organs with elevated serum IgG4, think a single unifying diagnosis — IgG4-RD — and biopsy the most accessible site rather than pursuing multiple cancer resections.
  2. Sausage-shaped pancreas (Type 1 AIP) — WRONG answer: proceeding to Whipple resection for presumed pancreatic adenocarcinoma. Discriminator: Type 1 AIP shows a DIFFUSE sausage gland with capsule rim and duct narrowing (not a focal mass with upstream duct dilation), elevated IgG4, other-organ disease, and steroid responsiveness — confirm with EUS-FNA/biopsy and a steroid trial before resecting, since AIP is the great pancreatic-cancer mimic.
  3. Thyroid (Riedel / fibrosing) — WRONG answer: diagnosing anaplastic thyroid carcinoma based on a fixed, stony-hard, rapidly enlarging thyroid mass. Discriminator: anaplastic carcinoma occurs in the elderly with aggressive growth and a poor prognosis on FNA/biopsy showing pleomorphic malignant cells; Riedel thyroiditis shows bland dense fibrosis with IgG4+ plasma cells, is steroid-responsive, and often involves other IgG4 organs — open biopsy distinguishes them.
  4. Brain — hypophysitis / pachymeningitis — WRONG answer: calling a sellar mass with hormone deficiency a pituitary adenoma and planning transsphenoidal surgery. Discriminator: a pituitary adenoma rarely causes diabetes insipidus and grows as a discrete adenomatous mass, whereas IgG4 hypophysitis shows stalk thickening, DI, other-organ IgG4 involvement, and steroid responsiveness — recognize it to avoid unnecessary neurosurgery.
  5. Kidney — tubulointerstitial nephritis — WRONG answer: attributing renal masses + low complement to lupus nephritis or a renal malignancy. Discriminator: IgG4 renal disease shows TIN with IgG4+ plasma cells and TBM deposits (not the immune-complex glomerulonephritis of lupus, which has positive ANA/dsDNA and 'full-house' immunofluorescence); biopsy with IgG4 staining plus extrarenal IgG4 organ involvement clinches it.
  6. Retroperitoneal fibrosis — WRONG answer: working up rising creatinine as intrinsic renal disease and missing the obstruction. Discriminator: IgG4 retroperitoneal fibrosis silently entraps ureters → bilateral hydronephrosis and obstructive renal failure; image the retroperitoneum (and relieve obstruction with stents/nephrostomy) before it becomes irreversible, then treat the fibrosis with steroids ± rituximab.
  7. Periaortitis / aortitis — WRONG answer: labeling all aortic wall inflammation as giant cell or Takayasu arteritis. Discriminator: GCA/Takayasu are granulomatous large-vessel vasculitides (elevated ESR/CRP, claudication, age/sex patterns) typically without IgG4+ plasma-cell-rich storiform fibrosis; IgG4 periaortitis shows the characteristic histology, bland CRP, periaortic soft tissue, and association with other IgG4 organs.
  8. Lung — pleural / pseudotumor — WRONG answer: assuming a solitary lung nodule/mass plus mediastinal nodes is bronchogenic carcinoma or sarcoidosis without tissue. Discriminator: IgG4 pulmonary pseudotumor responds to steroids and shows IgG4+ plasma cells with storiform fibrosis on biopsy; sarcoid shows non-caseating granulomas and cancer shows malignant cells — biopsy is required because imaging alone cannot separate IgG4 pseudotumor from malignancy.
  9. Subacute, fibrosis-dominant late — WRONG answer: adopting a 'watch and wait' approach indefinitely because the disease is indolent. Discriminator: late fibrotic lesions have sparse plasma cells and respond poorly to immunosuppression, so the window for effective treatment is the inflammatory phase — treat before irreversible fibrosis sets in, particularly when vital organs are involved.
  10. Prednisone first-line — WRONG answer: continuing to call it IgG4-RD when there is NO steroid response. Discriminator: steroid responsiveness is so characteristic that failure to respond should prompt re-biopsy and reconsideration of malignancy (lymphoma, carcinoma) or another mimic — a non-responding 'IgG4 mass' is a red flag for missed cancer, not just refractory disease.
  11. Relapses common → rituximab — WRONG answer: reaching for a conventional DMARD (e.g., methotrexate or azathioprine) as the primary steroid-sparing/relapse therapy. Discriminator: in IgG4-RD the disease is B-cell/plasmablast-driven, and RITUXIMAB (anti-CD20) is the evidence-favored go-to for relapsing or refractory disease — conventional DMARDs are weakly effective and not the preferred answer on boards.
  12. Treat now vs watch — WRONG answer: 'watching' a patient whose disease threatens a vital organ (e.g., ureteral entrapment, biliary obstruction, aortic involvement). Discriminator: irreversible organ damage is the trigger to treat NOW — observation is only appropriate for mild, non-threatening disease; allowing threatened vital organs to progress to fibrosis is a management error because late fibrotic disease is steroid-refractory.
📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Any organ / looks like cancer
IgG4-RD is a systemic fibroinflammatory disease that can involve virtually ANY organ — pancreas, biliary tree, salivary/lacrimal glands, orbit, thyroid, kidney, retroperitoneum, aorta, lung, pituitary, lymph nodes, meninges — typically forming mass lesions or diffuse organ enlargement that mimic tumors. The shared histology across sites is storiform fibrosis + IgG4+ lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate + obliterative phlebitis. Hallmark unifying behavior: tumor-like masses that respond dramatically to corticosteroids.
⚠️ WRONG answer: working up each organ mass as a separate primary malignancy. Discriminator: when an older man develops sequential or synchronous steroid-responsive 'tumors' across multiple organs with elevated serum IgG4, think a single unifying diagnosis — IgG4-RD — and biopsy the most accessible site rather than pursuing multiple cancer resections.
2. Sausage-shaped pancreas (Type 1 AIP)
Type 1 autoimmune pancreatitis is the prototypical IgG4 organ lesion: a diffusely enlarged, sausage-shaped pancreas with a hypoattenuating capsule-like rim and irregular main-duct narrowing, presenting with painless obstructive jaundice, weight loss, and frequently new-onset diabetes from islet involvement. Serum IgG4 is often high; duct involvement may cause IgG4-related sclerosing cholangitis. It melts with steroids.
⚠️ WRONG answer: proceeding to Whipple resection for presumed pancreatic adenocarcinoma. Discriminator: Type 1 AIP shows a DIFFUSE sausage gland with capsule rim and duct narrowing (not a focal mass with upstream duct dilation), elevated IgG4, other-organ disease, and steroid responsiveness — confirm with EUS-FNA/biopsy and a steroid trial before resecting, since AIP is the great pancreatic-cancer mimic.
3. Thyroid (Riedel / fibrosing)
Riedel thyroiditis (invasive fibrosing thyroiditis) is part of the IgG4 spectrum — the gland becomes rock-hard and woody, with dense fibrosis extending beyond the capsule into surrounding neck structures, causing compressive symptoms (dysphagia, stridor, hoarseness) and often hypothyroidism. The IgG4-related fibrosing variant of Hashimoto thyroiditis is also recognized.
⚠️ WRONG answer: diagnosing anaplastic thyroid carcinoma based on a fixed, stony-hard, rapidly enlarging thyroid mass. Discriminator: anaplastic carcinoma occurs in the elderly with aggressive growth and a poor prognosis on FNA/biopsy showing pleomorphic malignant cells; Riedel thyroiditis shows bland dense fibrosis with IgG4+ plasma cells, is steroid-responsive, and often involves other IgG4 organs — open biopsy distinguishes them.
4. Brain — hypophysitis / pachymeningitis
IgG4 hypophysitis causes pituitary stalk thickening and gland enlargement with anterior hypopituitarism and/or posterior diabetes insipidus (polyuria, polydipsia, hypernatremia). IgG4-related hypertrophic pachymeningitis causes dural thickening with headaches, cranial neuropathies, and mass effect. Both respond to glucocorticoids, often with improvement in hormone deficits if treated early.
⚠️ WRONG answer: calling a sellar mass with hormone deficiency a pituitary adenoma and planning transsphenoidal surgery. Discriminator: a pituitary adenoma rarely causes diabetes insipidus and grows as a discrete adenomatous mass, whereas IgG4 hypophysitis shows stalk thickening, DI, other-organ IgG4 involvement, and steroid responsiveness — recognize it to avoid unnecessary neurosurgery.
5. Kidney — tubulointerstitial nephritis
The dominant renal lesion of IgG4-RD is IgG4-related tubulointerstitial nephritis (TIN) — patchy, often radiologically apparent cortical lesions or masses, with a dense IgG4+ plasma-cell interstitial infiltrate and tubular basement membrane immune deposits. Hypocomplementemia (low C3/C4) is common and signals active disease. Membranous nephropathy can also occur. It responds to steroids.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing renal masses + low complement to lupus nephritis or a renal malignancy. Discriminator: IgG4 renal disease shows TIN with IgG4+ plasma cells and TBM deposits (not the immune-complex glomerulonephritis of lupus, which has positive ANA/dsDNA and 'full-house' immunofluorescence); biopsy with IgG4 staining plus extrarenal IgG4 organ involvement clinches it.
6. Retroperitoneal fibrosis
Retroperitoneal fibrosis (Ormond disease, often IgG4-related) is a fibrous plaque encasing the abdominal aorta, IVC, and ureters. It is frequently SILENT until it entraps the ureters, causing hydronephrosis and obstructive (post-renal) acute kidney injury, or causes vague back/flank/abdominal pain and venous obstruction. Imaging (CT/MRI/PET) shows a periaortic soft-tissue rind; it is steroid-responsive, and ureteral stents may be needed acutely.
⚠️ WRONG answer: working up rising creatinine as intrinsic renal disease and missing the obstruction. Discriminator: IgG4 retroperitoneal fibrosis silently entraps ureters → bilateral hydronephrosis and obstructive renal failure; image the retroperitoneum (and relieve obstruction with stents/nephrostomy) before it becomes irreversible, then treat the fibrosis with steroids ± rituximab.
7. Periaortitis / aortitis
Chronic periaortitis/aortitis from IgG4-RD causes circumferential aortic wall thickening (a periaortic 'cuff'), most often in the infrarenal abdominal aorta, and is a recognized cause of inflammatory (non-infectious, non-atherosclerotic) aortic aneurysm and dissection risk. It frequently coexists with retroperitoneal fibrosis. Recognizing it allows immunosuppression and aneurysm surveillance.
⚠️ WRONG answer: labeling all aortic wall inflammation as giant cell or Takayasu arteritis. Discriminator: GCA/Takayasu are granulomatous large-vessel vasculitides (elevated ESR/CRP, claudication, age/sex patterns) typically without IgG4+ plasma-cell-rich storiform fibrosis; IgG4 periaortitis shows the characteristic histology, bland CRP, periaortic soft tissue, and association with other IgG4 organs.
8. Lung — pleural / pseudotumor
Pulmonary IgG4-RD is protean: solid nodules/masses (inflammatory pseudotumor), peribronchovascular and septal thickening, ground-glass opacities, interstitial fibrosis, hilar/mediastinal lymphadenopathy, and pleural thickening/effusions. Many patients are asymptomatic with incidental findings; symptoms include cough and dyspnea. Lesions respond to steroids.
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming a solitary lung nodule/mass plus mediastinal nodes is bronchogenic carcinoma or sarcoidosis without tissue. Discriminator: IgG4 pulmonary pseudotumor responds to steroids and shows IgG4+ plasma cells with storiform fibrosis on biopsy; sarcoid shows non-caseating granulomas and cancer shows malignant cells — biopsy is required because imaging alone cannot separate IgG4 pseudotumor from malignancy.
9. Subacute, fibrosis-dominant late
IgG4-RD runs a subacute, indolent course over months to years, with sequential organ involvement and tumor-like lesions; it is rarely acutely life-threatening but causes cumulative organ damage. Early disease is inflammation-rich (plasma cells, plasmablasts) and very steroid-responsive; untreated late disease becomes fibrosis-DOMINANT with few plasma cells, making it steroid-refractory and producing irreversible organ failure.
⚠️ WRONG answer: adopting a 'watch and wait' approach indefinitely because the disease is indolent. Discriminator: late fibrotic lesions have sparse plasma cells and respond poorly to immunosuppression, so the window for effective treatment is the inflammatory phase — treat before irreversible fibrosis sets in, particularly when vital organs are involved.
10. Prednisone first-line
Glucocorticoids are first-line induction therapy for active IgG4-RD: typically prednisone ~0.6 mg/kg/day (often around 30–40 mg/day) for 2–4 weeks, then a gradual taper over months. The response is rapid and dramatic — masses shrink, organ function recovers, and serum IgG4 falls — which is itself supportive of the diagnosis.
⚠️ WRONG answer: continuing to call it IgG4-RD when there is NO steroid response. Discriminator: steroid responsiveness is so characteristic that failure to respond should prompt re-biopsy and reconsideration of malignancy (lymphoma, carcinoma) or another mimic — a non-responding 'IgG4 mass' is a red flag for missed cancer, not just refractory disease.
11. Relapses common → rituximab
Relapse is common as steroids are tapered or stopped. Rituximab — an anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody that depletes B cells and plasmablasts — is the key steroid-sparing agent for relapsing, refractory, or steroid-intolerant disease, and is increasingly used for induction in severe multi-organ involvement. Plasmablast counts can guide and predict relapse. Conventional DMARDs (azathioprine, mycophenolate, methotrexate) have only a limited, adjunctive role.
⚠️ WRONG answer: reaching for a conventional DMARD (e.g., methotrexate or azathioprine) as the primary steroid-sparing/relapse therapy. Discriminator: in IgG4-RD the disease is B-cell/plasmablast-driven, and RITUXIMAB (anti-CD20) is the evidence-favored go-to for relapsing or refractory disease — conventional DMARDs are weakly effective and not the preferred answer on boards.
12. Treat now vs watch
Treatment urgency is risk-stratified. TREAT NOW when vital/critical organs are threatened — aortitis with aneurysm risk, retroperitoneal fibrosis entrapping ureters, autoimmune pancreatitis/cholangitis with biliary obstruction, tubulointerstitial nephritis, hypophysitis, pachymeningitis. WATCH (with close monitoring) for mild, non-organ-threatening disease such as isolated lymphadenopathy or asymptomatic single-gland involvement. The threshold to treat is the threat of irreversible organ damage.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'watching' a patient whose disease threatens a vital organ (e.g., ureteral entrapment, biliary obstruction, aortic involvement). Discriminator: irreversible organ damage is the trigger to treat NOW — observation is only appropriate for mild, non-threatening disease; allowing threatened vital organs to progress to fibrosis is a management error because late fibrotic disease is steroid-refractory.
