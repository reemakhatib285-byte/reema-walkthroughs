⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. Symmetric proximal > distal — WRONG answer: choosing distal and/or ASYMMETRIC weakness (e.g., finger flexors and quadriceps in an older man) and calling it polymyositis. Discriminator: that pattern is inclusion body myositis (IBM), which is distal, asymmetric, slowly progressive, and steroid-refractory — the opposite of the symmetric proximal DM/PM pattern.
  2. Heliotrope rash — WRONG answer: labeling it a malar (butterfly) rash and diagnosing SLE. Discriminator: the malar rash of lupus spares the nasolabial folds and centers on the cheeks/bridge of nose; the heliotrope rash is PERIORBITAL on the eyelids and is violaceous, not pink-red — eyelid involvement points to DM, not lupus.
  3. Gottron papules — WRONG answer: confusing the distribution with lupus. Discriminator: DM rash sits ON the knuckles (Gottron papules over the MCP/PIP joints) and SPARES the skin between joints; SLE does the reverse — it spares the knuckles and affects the phalanges between the joints. 'Over the joints = DM, between the joints = lupus.'
  4. DM patient — WRONG answer: making the diagnosis DM but then skipping the cancer hunt because the patient 'looks well.' Discriminator: adult DM is strongly paraneoplastic (ovarian, lung, GI, nasopharyngeal, breast) — failure to perform age-appropriate malignancy screening (and consider CT chest/abdomen/pelvis ± pelvic/mammography) is the classic management error.
  5. Juvenile + calcinosis — WRONG answer: ordering an exhaustive paraneoplastic/cancer workup in a child with DM. Discriminator: the malignancy association is an ADULT phenomenon — juvenile DM is not paraneoplastic, so calcinosis and vasculopathy, not cancer, drive the workup and prognosis.
  6. Always biopsy — WRONG answer: starting long-term immunosuppression on the basis of an autoantibody panel alone without tissue (or, where appropriate, a confirmatory MRI/EMG). Discriminator: many mimics (steroid myopathy, statin toxicity, hypothyroid myopathy, IBM) require tissue or other confirmation — committing a patient to chronic steroids without confirming inflammatory myopathy is the trap.
  7. PM patient — WRONG answer: diagnosing PM in a patient who actually has subtle cutaneous disease. Discriminator: if ANY characteristic skin finding is present (even subclinical Gottron or nailfold changes), the diagnosis is dermatomyositis, not polymyositis — examine the skin and nailfolds carefully before settling on PM.
  8. If normal → think IBM — WRONG answer: continuing to escalate steroids/immunosuppression in a 'PM' patient who is not responding. Discriminator: lack of response plus a near-normal CK and distal/asymmetric weakness = IBM, which is essentially refractory to immunosuppression — more steroids only add steroid myopathy and side effects.
  9. CK <10x — WRONG answer: excluding myositis because the CK is normal, or treating purely off the CK. Discriminator: a normal CK does NOT rule out inflammatory myopathy (DM can have normal CK, and IBM often does) — rely on the clinical exam, EMG, MRI, and biopsy rather than the enzyme alone.
  10. Anti-Mi-2 — WRONG answer: equating all DM antibodies with high cancer risk and ordering aggressive surveillance for an anti-Mi-2 patient. Discriminator: anti-Mi-2 carries low malignancy risk and good prognosis — the high-cancer antibodies are anti-TIF1-gamma and anti-NXP2, not anti-Mi-2.
  11. Anti-TIF1-gamma — WRONG answer: reassuring an anti-TIF1-gamma-positive adult that cancer is unlikely. Discriminator: this antibody carries the HIGHEST malignancy association in adult DM — it should trigger comprehensive, age-appropriate cancer screening (CT chest/abdomen/pelvis, mammography, pelvic exam, colonoscopy as indicated), not reassurance.
  12. CK severity dial — WRONG answer: reflexively calling a sky-high CK 'polymyositis.' Discriminator: the highest CK levels (often >10–50x) belong to necrotizing myopathy (anti-SRP/anti-HMGCR), while a low/normal CK with weakness points to IBM — let the CK magnitude steer subtype, not just the label PM.
  13. 5-yr survival 70-85% — WRONG answer: assuming inflammatory myopathy is a benign 'just weakness' disease. Discriminator: prognosis is dictated by ILD, cardiac disease, dysphagia/aspiration, and malignancy — overlooking these systemic associations, not the muscle weakness, is what kills patients.
  14. Shawl/V-sign rash — WRONG answer: dismissing a photodistributed chest/back rash as ordinary sun damage or polymorphous light eruption. Discriminator: in a patient with proximal weakness, a violaceous V-sign or shawl-sign distribution is a dermatomyositis skin marker and changes the diagnosis from PM to DM (and triggers the malignancy screen).
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Symmetric proximal > distal
The defining motor pattern of dermatomyositis and polymyositis is SYMMETRIC, PROXIMAL weakness that develops subacutely over weeks to months — trouble rising from a chair, climbing stairs, combing hair, lifting overhead. Bulbar/pharyngeal muscles can be involved (dysphagia, aspiration risk), but ocular muscles are spared (unlike myasthenia gravis). Sensation, reflexes, and the distal hands/feet are characteristically preserved early. Mnemonic: 'proximal, painless-ish, progressive, symmetric.'
⚠️ WRONG answer: choosing distal and/or ASYMMETRIC weakness (e.g., finger flexors and quadriceps in an older man) and calling it polymyositis. Discriminator: that pattern is inclusion body myositis (IBM), which is distal, asymmetric, slowly progressive, and steroid-refractory — the opposite of the symmetric proximal DM/PM pattern.
2. Heliotrope rash
Heliotrope rash = a violaceous (lilac/purple) discoloration of the upper eyelids, often with periorbital edema, named for the purple heliotrope flower. It is highly specific for dermatomyositis and is one of the recognized cutaneous hallmarks. The rash is photosensitive and frequently accompanies other DM skin findings (Gottron, shawl/V signs). Its presence makes the diagnosis DM rather than PM even if muscle findings are identical.
⚠️ WRONG answer: labeling it a malar (butterfly) rash and diagnosing SLE. Discriminator: the malar rash of lupus spares the nasolabial folds and centers on the cheeks/bridge of nose; the heliotrope rash is PERIORBITAL on the eyelids and is violaceous, not pink-red — eyelid involvement points to DM, not lupus.
3. Gottron papules
Gottron papules are flat-topped violaceous papules located directly OVER the bony extensor surfaces — MCP, PIP, and DIP joints (also elbows, knees). Together with the heliotrope rash they are considered pathognomonic for dermatomyositis. The flat macular form over the same joints is called Gottron sign. They reflect the photosensitive, interface dermatitis of DM.
⚠️ WRONG answer: confusing the distribution with lupus. Discriminator: DM rash sits ON the knuckles (Gottron papules over the MCP/PIP joints) and SPARES the skin between joints; SLE does the reverse — it spares the knuckles and affects the phalanges between the joints. 'Over the joints = DM, between the joints = lupus.'
4. DM patient
Dermatomyositis = characteristic SKIN findings (Gottron, heliotrope, shawl/V signs) PLUS proximal symmetric weakness. Biopsy shows PERIFASCICULAR atrophy with PERIMYSIAL/perivascular CD4+ and B-cell infiltrate and complement (MAC) deposition on capillaries — a humoral, microangiopathic process. Adult DM carries a markedly increased malignancy risk, so every adult needs an age- and risk-appropriate cancer screen at diagnosis.
⚠️ WRONG answer: making the diagnosis DM but then skipping the cancer hunt because the patient 'looks well.' Discriminator: adult DM is strongly paraneoplastic (ovarian, lung, GI, nasopharyngeal, breast) — failure to perform age-appropriate malignancy screening (and consider CT chest/abdomen/pelvis ± pelvic/mammography) is the classic management error.
5. Juvenile + calcinosis
Juvenile dermatomyositis (JDM) is the pediatric form, characterized by a vasculopathy and a strong tendency toward CALCINOSIS CUTIS (subcutaneous calcium deposits) and lipodystrophy. Unlike adult DM, JDM is NOT associated with underlying malignancy. Anti-NXP2 and anti-MDA5 antibodies are seen in children; treatment is steroids plus steroid-sparing agents to limit calcinosis.
⚠️ WRONG answer: ordering an exhaustive paraneoplastic/cancer workup in a child with DM. Discriminator: the malignancy association is an ADULT phenomenon — juvenile DM is not paraneoplastic, so calcinosis and vasculopathy, not cancer, drive the workup and prognosis.
6. Always biopsy
Muscle biopsy remains the gold-standard confirmatory test for inflammatory myopathy and distinguishes subtypes by pattern: perifascicular atrophy (DM), endomysial CD8+ invasion of non-necrotic fibers (PM), rimmed vacuoles (IBM), and scattered necrosis with sparse inflammation (necrotizing myopathy). Pick a moderately weak muscle (not end-stage); EMG/MRI can guide the site. Shared marker = sarcolemmal MHC-1 upregulation.
⚠️ WRONG answer: starting long-term immunosuppression on the basis of an autoantibody panel alone without tissue (or, where appropriate, a confirmatory MRI/EMG). Discriminator: many mimics (steroid myopathy, statin toxicity, hypothyroid myopathy, IBM) require tissue or other confirmation — committing a patient to chronic steroids without confirming inflammatory myopathy is the trap.
7. PM patient
Polymyositis = proximal symmetric weakness WITHOUT the DM skin findings. Biopsy shows ENDOMYSIAL CD8+ cytotoxic T cells invading non-necrotic, MHC-1-expressing fibers (a cell-mediated process, contrasting with DM's humoral perimysial CD4 pattern). PM is a diagnosis of exclusion — it is over-diagnosed, and many 'PM' cases are actually IBM, necrotizing myopathy, or dystrophy.
⚠️ WRONG answer: diagnosing PM in a patient who actually has subtle cutaneous disease. Discriminator: if ANY characteristic skin finding is present (even subclinical Gottron or nailfold changes), the diagnosis is dermatomyositis, not polymyositis — examine the skin and nailfolds carefully before settling on PM.
8. If normal → think IBM
A CK that is only mildly elevated or near-normal, combined with a slowly progressive, treatment-RESISTANT course, should redirect you toward inclusion body myositis. IBM classically affects men over 50, with distal (finger flexor) and asymmetric (quadriceps) weakness, frequent dysphagia, and rimmed vacuoles with congophilic/p62/TDP-43 inclusions on biopsy.
⚠️ WRONG answer: continuing to escalate steroids/immunosuppression in a 'PM' patient who is not responding. Discriminator: lack of response plus a near-normal CK and distal/asymmetric weakness = IBM, which is essentially refractory to immunosuppression — more steroids only add steroid myopathy and side effects.
9. CK <10x
Creatine kinase (CK) is the most useful serum marker of muscle injury and is typically markedly elevated — often more than 10x the upper limit of normal — in active DM/PM, paralleling disease activity. Other muscle enzymes (aldolase, AST/ALT, LDH) also rise. CK is used to track flares but treatment is titrated to STRENGTH, not the number.
⚠️ WRONG answer: excluding myositis because the CK is normal, or treating purely off the CK. Discriminator: a normal CK does NOT rule out inflammatory myopathy (DM can have normal CK, and IBM often does) — rely on the clinical exam, EMG, MRI, and biopsy rather than the enzyme alone.
10. Anti-Mi-2
Anti-Mi-2 is a myositis-SPECIFIC antibody that defines a classic dermatomyositis phenotype with prominent cutaneous features (Gottron, heliotrope, V/shawl signs), a GOOD response to immunosuppression, and a comparatively LOW malignancy risk. It is one of the more favorable serologic groups in DM.
⚠️ WRONG answer: equating all DM antibodies with high cancer risk and ordering aggressive surveillance for an anti-Mi-2 patient. Discriminator: anti-Mi-2 carries low malignancy risk and good prognosis — the high-cancer antibodies are anti-TIF1-gamma and anti-NXP2, not anti-Mi-2.
11. Anti-TIF1-gamma
Anti-TIF1-gamma (anti-p155/140) and anti-NXP2 are the dermatomyositis antibodies most strongly linked to underlying MALIGNANCY in adults — TIF1-gamma positivity warrants thorough, often repeated, cancer screening. TIF1-gamma is also associated with severe cutaneous disease. (Anti-MDA5, by contrast, marks clinically amyopathic DM with rapidly progressive ILD and skin ulcers.)
⚠️ WRONG answer: reassuring an anti-TIF1-gamma-positive adult that cancer is unlikely. Discriminator: this antibody carries the HIGHEST malignancy association in adult DM — it should trigger comprehensive, age-appropriate cancer screening (CT chest/abdomen/pelvis, mammography, pelvic exam, colonoscopy as indicated), not reassurance.
12. CK severity dial
CK magnitude roughly maps to subtype: NORMAL or near-normal in IBM (and amyopathic DM), MILDLY-to-moderately elevated in DM, and VERY HIGH (>10x ULN) in immune-mediated necrotizing myopathy (IMNM, anti-SRP/anti-HMGCR). Polymyositis sits in the high range as well. The dial is a quick way to rank suspicion by enzyme level.
⚠️ WRONG answer: reflexively calling a sky-high CK 'polymyositis.' Discriminator: the highest CK levels (often >10–50x) belong to necrotizing myopathy (anti-SRP/anti-HMGCR), while a low/normal CK with weakness points to IBM — let the CK magnitude steer subtype, not just the label PM.
13. 5-yr survival 70-85%
Inflammatory myopathy carries meaningful mortality (roughly 70–85% 5-year survival in older series), driven not by limb weakness itself but by EXTRAMUSCULAR complications: interstitial lung disease, cardiac involvement (myocarditis, arrhythmia), aspiration from pharyngeal weakness, and associated MALIGNANCY. Prognosis improves with early treatment and cancer detection.
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming inflammatory myopathy is a benign 'just weakness' disease. Discriminator: prognosis is dictated by ILD, cardiac disease, dysphagia/aspiration, and malignancy — overlooking these systemic associations, not the muscle weakness, is what kills patients.
14. Shawl/V-sign rash
The V-sign (photodistributed erythema over the anterior neck/upper chest) and the shawl sign (erythema across the shoulders/upper back) are photosensitive cutaneous signs of dermatomyositis. Like the heliotrope and Gottron findings, they are sun-exposed and reinforce a DM (not PM) diagnosis; the holster sign (lateral thighs) is the analogous lower-body finding.
⚠️ WRONG answer: dismissing a photodistributed chest/back rash as ordinary sun damage or polymorphous light eruption. Discriminator: in a patient with proximal weakness, a violaceous V-sign or shawl-sign distribution is a dermatomyositis skin marker and changes the diagnosis from PM to DM (and triggers the malignancy screen).
