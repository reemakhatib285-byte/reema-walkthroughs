📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Microbiota / colonization
Pneumonia begins when normal upper-airway flora is disrupted and pathogens colonize. Streptococcus pneumoniae is the leading cause of community-acquired pneumonia. Colonization precedes invasion.
⚠️ Colonization alone is not infection — sputum culture growth without clinical signs can reflect colonization, especially with Gram-negatives in hospitalized patients.
2. Micro-aspiration
Micro-aspiration of oropharyngeal secretions during sleep is the dominant route by which organisms reach the alveoli, even in healthy people. Impaired clearance or large inoculum tips to pneumonia.
⚠️ Micro-aspiration (silent, common) differs from gross aspiration pneumonia — the latter favors dependent segments and anaerobes/abscess.
3. Inhalation route
Some pathogens reach the lung by direct inhalation of aerosols — classically atypicals (Legionella from water, Mycoplasma, viral). Droplet size determines alveolar deposition.
⚠️ Inhalation favors atypicals/Legionella (water exposure); micro-aspiration favors typical pneumococcus — exposure history guides empiric choice.
4. Mucosal barrier breach
The mucociliary escalator and mucosal immunity form the first defense. Viral infection, smoking, and COPD damage this barrier, predisposing to bacterial superinfection (e.g., post-influenza S. aureus/pneumococcus).
⚠️ Post-influenza pneumonia is classically S. aureus (including MRSA) and S. pneumoniae — a high-yield superinfection pairing.
5. Cytokine storm (IL-6, TNF, GM-CSF)
Pathogen recognition triggers IL-6, TNF, and GM-CSF release, driving fever, neutrophil recruitment, and the acute-phase response. CRP/procalcitonin rise. Excess cytokines cause systemic sepsis features.
⚠️ Procalcitonin elevation favors bacterial over viral pneumonia and can guide antibiotic stewardship, but should not solely dictate starting/stopping in severe illness.
6. Phase 1 — Edema (congestion)
The first ~24h shows congestion: protein-rich edema fluid and bacteria fill alveoli with few neutrophils. The lobe is heavy and hyperemic. This is the earliest histologic phase of lobar pneumonia.
⚠️ Edema/congestion is bacteria-rich but neutrophil-poor — the inverse of red hepatization which is RBC/neutrophil-rich.
7. Phase 2 — Red hepatization
Days 2–3: alveoli pack with red cells, fibrin, and neutrophils, giving a firm liver-like consistency ('red hepatization'). Extravasated RBCs give the red color.
⚠️ Red hepatization = RBCs + fibrin + neutrophils; gray hepatization = RBCs disintegrated, fibrinopurulent. The hallmark is the loss of red color over time.
8. Phase 3 — Gray hepatization
Days 4–6: red cells lyse and degrade, leaving a fibrinopurulent gray exudate; the lobe stays firm but turns gray. Macrophages begin to appear. Infection is being contained.
⚠️ Gray hepatization is dry/gray because RBCs have broken down — not because of new bacterial growth. Color change is a timing clue, not severity.
9. Phase 4 — Resolution
Macrophages clear debris and the enzymatic digestion of exudate restores normal aerated architecture, typically over ~1 week onward. Lobar pneumonia characteristically resolves without scarring if uncomplicated.
⚠️ Lobar pneumonia resolves with restored architecture; persistent consolidation or scarring should prompt search for obstruction, abscess, or organizing pneumonia.
10. Lobar pattern (CXR)
Lobar consolidation — a dense opacity confined to one lobe with air bronchograms — is the classic pattern of S. pneumoniae and Klebsiella. Reflects alveolar filling.
⚠️ Lobar consolidation with a 'bulging fissure' classically suggests Klebsiella; pneumococcus is the commonest lobar cause overall.
11. Bronchopneumonia pattern
Bronchopneumonia shows patchy, scattered, often bilateral opacities centered on airways — typical of S. aureus, Gram-negatives, and many nosocomial pathogens.
⚠️ Patchy multifocal pattern (bronchopneumonia) vs single dense lobe (lobar) helps narrow likely organisms — Staph/Gram-negatives vs pneumococcus.
12. Interstitial / atypical pattern
Diffuse ground-glass/interstitial infiltrates suggest atypical or viral pneumonia and Pneumocystis jirovecii (PJP) in immunocompromised hosts. CXR may look worse than the relatively mild exam in atypicals.
⚠️ Diffuse ground-glass in an HIV/immunosuppressed patient with hypoxia out of proportion = PJP — confirm with induced sputum/BAL and treat with TMP-SMX +/- steroids.
13. VAP / ventilator-associated
Ventilator-associated pneumonia (>48h after intubation) involves MDR organisms (Pseudomonas, MRSA, Acinetobacter). New/progressive infiltrate plus fever, leukocytosis, and purulent secretions define it.
⚠️ VAP requires broad empiric anti-pseudomonal/anti-MRSA cover; treating it like CAP is a major error. New infiltrate + clinical criteria, not infiltrate alone.
14. R→L shunt / hypoxemia
Consolidated alveoli are perfused but not ventilated, creating an intrapulmonary right-to-left shunt and hypoxemia that responds poorly to supplemental oxygen. V/Q mismatch worsens with filling.
⚠️ True shunt physiology (consolidation) corrects poorly with 100% O2, unlike simple V/Q mismatch — a key gas-exchange distinction.
15. Trigger / inoculum
Disease results when inoculum size and virulence overwhelm host defenses. Large aspirated bacterial load or impaired immunity (age, COPD, immunosuppression) converts colonization into invasive pneumonia.
⚠️ Host risk factors (age, COPD, immunosuppression, prior viral infection) determine progression more than mere exposure — risk-stratify before assuming severity.
