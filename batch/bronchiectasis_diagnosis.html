📖 Read-through — all 16 symbols
1. #1 autoimmune disease
Rheumatoid arthritis is the most common AUTOIMMUNE/connective-tissue cause of bronchiectasis, and it can predate or follow the joint disease. The mechanism combines immune-mediated airway inflammation with susceptibility to infection. Always screen for joint symptoms, RF, and anti-CCP in otherwise-unexplained bronchiectasis (other associations: Sjögren, IBD).
⚠️ Don't assume autoimmune bronchiectasis means lupus — RA is the #1 connective-tissue association; picking SLE over RA is the discriminator trap.
2. Lymphoma risk
Chronic immune dysregulation underlying bronchiectasis — particularly common variable immunodeficiency (CVID) — carries an elevated risk of lymphoma (and other malignancies/autoimmunity). This is another reason to actively identify an immunodeficiency cause rather than treating infections symptomatically.
⚠️ Recurrent infections + bronchiectasis with new lymphadenopathy/cytopenias should raise CVID-associated lymphoma — dismissing nodes as 'reactive' without considering the immunodeficiency link is the miss.
3. Black cat with medal
Mascot for the chronic, recurrent, 'award-winning best mimic' nature of bronchiectasis. The defining clinical hallmark is a CHRONIC daily productive cough with copious mucopurulent sputum punctuated by exacerbations — not a single acute illness. It is frequently mislabeled as 'recurrent bronchitis' or refractory asthma/COPD for years before HRCT reveals it.
⚠️ Treating chronic productive cough as repeated episodes of acute bronchitis (and just giving antibiotic courses) misses the underlying structural diagnosis — the chronicity between exacerbations is the tell.
4. Tram-track / railway
The tram-track sign = parallel, non-tapering, thickened bronchial walls seen LENGTHWISE (the dilated airway runs horizontally in the imaging plane). It appears on both chest X-ray and HRCT and reflects the same dilated, thick-walled airways that produce the signet-ring sign in cross-section.
⚠️ Tram-tracks are a sign of dilated thick-walled BRONCHI (bronchiectasis), not the Kerley B lines/interstitial markings of pulmonary edema or the reticulation of fibrosis — misreading them as edema/fibrosis is the imaging trap.
5. Signet-ring (ring + diamond)
The signet-ring sign is the diagnostic HRCT hallmark: a dilated bronchus (the ring) sits adjacent to its smaller accompanying pulmonary artery (the 'stone'), giving a broncho-arterial ratio >1 (roughly ≥1.5). Seen in CROSS-section, it is the cardinal CT criterion for bronchiectasis.
⚠️ Normally the bronchus is SMALLER than its paired artery — so a bronchus LARGER than the adjacent artery (BA ratio >1) defines bronchiectasis; reversing the normal relationship is the tested point.
6. Tree-in-bud
Tree-in-bud opacities are centrilobular nodules with branching lines representing mucus/pus/inflammation filling small airways. They signal ACTIVE small-airway infection or inflammation — classically MAC/NTM, but also any infective exacerbation or aspiration — rather than the structural dilation itself.
⚠️ Tree-in-bud indicates active airway INFECTION (think NTM, infectious exacerbation), not chronic structural change — if present, pursue sputum cultures including AFB rather than assuming inert scarring.
7. CT >1 criteria card
The three core HRCT diagnostic features: (1) broncho-arterial ratio >1 (signet ring), (2) lack of normal distal bronchial TAPERING, and (3) bronchi VISIBLE in the peripheral lung (within ~1 cm of the pleura). HRCT is the gold-standard confirmatory test for bronchiectasis.
⚠️ Seeing airways within ~1 cm of the pleura is ABNORMAL — peripheral bronchi shouldn't normally be visible, so calling visible peripheral airways 'normal' misses the diagnosis.
8. Clinical card (cough/sputum)
Clinical diagnosis rests on chronic productive cough with copious purulent/mucopurulent sputum, recurrent exacerbations, and sometimes hemoptysis; exam may reveal crackles and clubbing. This clinical picture should prompt confirmatory HRCT, and chronic Pseudomonas colonization predicts worse outcomes.
⚠️ A normal chest X-ray does NOT exclude bronchiectasis — if the clinical picture fits, get HRCT; relying on plain film alone under-diagnoses.
9. IgE thermometer (ABPA)
A very high total IgE (often >1000 IU/mL) in an asthmatic — especially with REFRACTORY/poorly controlled asthma and central bronchiectasis — should trigger an ABPA workup: Aspergillus-specific IgE/IgG, peripheral eosinophilia, and serum precipitins. Treatment is corticosteroids ± itraconazole.
⚠️ A high IgE in an asthmatic with new bronchiectasis is ABPA until proven otherwise — simply escalating inhaled asthma medication without checking Aspergillus serologies misses (and undertreats) ABPA.
10. CF FIRST jar
In a young patient (especially with upper-lobe disease, sinus disease, malabsorption, or infertility), test for cystic fibrosis FIRST — sweat chloride testing (>60 mmol/L abnormal) and CFTR genetic analysis. CF is high-yield because it is common, heritable, and changes management dramatically.
⚠️ Skipping CF testing in a young adult with bronchiectasis because they 'would have been diagnosed in childhood' is wrong — mild CFTR variants can present in adulthood, so sweat chloride/genetics stay first-line.
11. IgG / immunodeficiency jar
Measure serum immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM), and consider IgG subclasses and specific antibody/vaccine responses, to detect CVID, hypogammaglobulinemia, or selective IgA deficiency as causes of recurrent infection-driven bronchiectasis. Identifying these enables immunoglobulin replacement.
⚠️ Recurrent sinopulmonary infections with bronchiectasis warrant quantitative immunoglobulins — treating each infection in isolation and never measuring Ig levels misses a correctable immunodeficiency.
12. AAT (alpha-1) jar
Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency is a less common but testable cause; the unopposed neutrophil elastase classically produces BASAL panacinar EMPHYSEMA, and bronchiectasis may coexist. Check serum AAT level (and phenotype/genotype if low), particularly with early-onset basal disease or liver involvement.
⚠️ AAT deficiency classically causes LOWER-zone emphysema (vs the upper-zone disease of smoking-related COPD) — overlooking AAT testing in early-onset basal disease, especially a nonsmoker, is the workup trap.
13. Cilia biopsy
For suspected primary ciliary dyskinesia (bronchiectasis + chronic sinusitis ± situs inversus ± infertility), screen with low nasal nitric oxide and confirm with ciliary ULTRASTRUCTURE on biopsy (electron microscopy showing dynein-arm defects) and/or genetic testing.
⚠️ Don't confirm PCD with a sweat chloride (that's CF) — the right confirmatory tests are nasal NO and ciliary biopsy/genetics; using CF testing for a PCD picture is the mismatch trap.
14. IVIG drip (Q3-4 weeks)
Immunoglobulin replacement (IVIG, typically every 3–4 weeks, or weekly SCIG) is the treatment for the ANTIBODY-DEFICIENCY subgroup (CVID, agammaglobulinemia, significant hypogammaglobulinemia) and reduces the frequency of infections and exacerbations.
⚠️ IVIG is ONLY for the immunodeficient subgroup — it is NOT a general bronchiectasis therapy; prescribing it for idiopathic or post-infectious bronchiectasis with normal immunoglobulins is wrong.
15. Chronic not acute
Bronchiectasis is a CHRONIC, irreversible STRUCTURAL airway disease; symptoms (cough, sputum) persist between exacerbations, distinguishing it from a single resolving acute pneumonia. Management centers on long-term airway clearance, treating exacerbations, and addressing colonization (e.g., chronic macrolide for frequent exacerbators; inhaled antibiotics for Pseudomonas).
⚠️ Treating bronchiectasis as a one-off acute pneumonia — antibiotics then discharge with no airway-clearance/long-term plan — ignores its chronic structural nature and leads to recurrent admissions.
16. Mild-mod destruction (overlap)
Bronchiectasis severity grades morphologically from cylindrical (tubular, mild) to varicose (beaded, moderate) to cystic/saccular (severe), and these patterns often OVERLAP within the same lung. Severity (and Pseudomonas colonization) correlates with exacerbation frequency and prognosis, captured by indices like the Bronchiectasis Severity Index.
⚠️ Cylindrical (mild) bronchiectasis is still real, treatable disease — dismissing milder cylindrical changes as insignificant because they aren't cystic underestimates exacerbation risk.
