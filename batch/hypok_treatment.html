⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. Treat patient AND cause — WRONG: "Just give IV potassium fast to a stable patient with K 3.0." Stable, asymptomatic, able-to-swallow patients get ORAL KCl — IV is reserved for severe (<2.5), symptomatic, arrhythmia, or NPO patients. The discriminator is symptoms/severity and PO access, not the absolute number alone.
  2. Stable or severe fork — WRONG: choosing the route purely off the lab value. A patient with K 2.3 who is asymptomatic, eating, and hemodynamically stable can often start oral repletion; conversely a patient at 3.0 on digoxin with PVCs needs IV. Severity + symptoms + PO access determine the branch, not the number in isolation.
  3. Oral KCl first line — WRONG: "Use potassium bicarbonate/citrate for the vomiting patient with metabolic alkalosis." In chloride-depletion alkalosis (vomiting, NG suction, diuretics) you want KCl, which replaces both K and Cl. Reserve K-citrate/bicarbonate for hypokalemia WITH a metabolic acidosis (e.g., RTA, diarrhea).
  4. IV KCl + rates — WRONG: running 40 mEq/h peripherally to "fix it fast," or giving an IV K "bolus." Exceeding safe rates risks fatal hyperkalemia and cardiac arrest; high peripheral concentration causes phlebitis. There is no such thing as an IV potassium push — it is always a controlled infusion with monitoring.
  5. Telemetry if severe — WRONG: skipping telemetry in a digoxin patient because the K is "only mildly low." Hypokalemia markedly potentiates digoxin binding to the Na/K-ATPase and precipitates life-threatening arrhythmias even at modest K reductions — these patients must be monitored and aggressively repleted.
  6. Mg key station — WRONG: ordering only a potassium level and ignoring magnesium. Common causes (loop/thiazide diuretics, diarrhea, alcoholism, PPIs, cisplatin) deplete BOTH ions, and you will chase your tail repleting K while Mg-driven renal K wasting continues.
  7. K won't correct — replace Mg first — WRONG: "K isn't rising despite 200 mEq — give even more potassium." The board answer in a diuretic/alcoholic patient whose K won't budge is to CHECK AND REPLACE MAGNESIUM. Escalating K alone fails and risks rebound hyperkalemia once Mg is finally corrected.
  8. Dextrose trap — WRONG: choosing "KCl in D5W" for an acutely symptomatic hypokalemic patient. The insulin surge from dextrose shifts K into cells and acutely worsens the hypokalemia — the correct diluent is saline. (Note: this is the opposite scenario from treating hyperkalemia, where insulin+dextrose is therapeutic.)
  9. Rebound hyperK risk — WRONG: treating periodic-paralysis hypokalemia like a true deficit with large K loads. Once the trigger resolves the intracellular K re-emerges and the patient overshoots into hyperkalemia — give modest K and treat the underlying shift instead.
  10. TPP treatment — WRONG: "Load the TPP patient with high-dose IV potassium." Because the K is shifted (not lost), high doses cause rebound hyperkalemia when the attack breaks. Propranolol plus low-dose K plus treating the thyroid is the answer.
  11. Cause-specific: vomiting — WRONG: giving potassium without volume/chloride repletion in the vomiting patient. Until you restore Cl and volume, secondary hyperaldosteronism keeps wasting K — saline + KCl together, not KCl alone.
  12. Cause-specific: diuretic — WRONG: endlessly repleting oral K in a patient on a high-dose loop diuretic without addressing the drug. The durable fix is dose reduction and/or a K-sparing diuretic, plus correcting Mg — otherwise the wasting continues.
  13. Liddle vs primary aldo tx — WRONG: giving spironolactone for Liddle syndrome. Liddle is a channel defect with LOW aldosterone, so an aldosterone antagonist won't work — the answer is amiloride/triamterene (block ENaC directly). Spironolactone is correct only when aldosterone is the problem (primary hyperaldo).
  14. Severe deficit magnitude — WRONG: stopping repletion the instant serum K crosses into the normal range in a truly depleted patient. The intracellular compartment is still empty; under-replacement leads to recurrent hypokalemia. (The exception: shift states, where the deficit is apparent, not real.)
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Treat patient AND cause
Four pillars of repletion: (1) oral KCl is first line, (2) IV only if severe/symptomatic or NPO, (3) ALWAYS check and replace Mg, (4) never dilute K in dextrose alone. Treat the underlying cause (vomiting, diuretic, hyperaldo) — repletion alone fails if losses continue. As a rule of thumb each 10 mEq of K raises serum K by ~0.1 mEq/L, but the total-body deficit is far larger than the serum number implies.
⚠️ WRONG: "Just give IV potassium fast to a stable patient with K 3.0." Stable, asymptomatic, able-to-swallow patients get ORAL KCl — IV is reserved for severe (<2.5), symptomatic, arrhythmia, or NPO patients. The discriminator is symptoms/severity and PO access, not the absolute number alone.
2. Stable or severe fork
The treatment route forks on severity and PO access. Stable + able to take PO → oral KCl (left branch). Severe (K <2.5), symptomatic (weakness, ileus, arrhythmia, ECG changes), or unable to swallow → IV KCl (right branch). Both arms still require Mg checking and cause-directed therapy.
⚠️ WRONG: choosing the route purely off the lab value. A patient with K 2.3 who is asymptomatic, eating, and hemodynamically stable can often start oral repletion; conversely a patient at 3.0 on digoxin with PVCs needs IV. Severity + symptoms + PO access determine the branch, not the number in isolation.
3. Oral KCl first line
Oral KCl is preferred for mild-to-moderate hypokalemia (K roughly 2.5–3.5, no severe symptoms). It is safe, cannot cause an iatrogenic bolus, and the gut self-limits absorption. Typical dosing is 20–40 mEq per dose, repeated; potassium chloride is the salt of choice in most cases (corrects the common coexisting metabolic alkalosis/chloride loss).
⚠️ WRONG: "Use potassium bicarbonate/citrate for the vomiting patient with metabolic alkalosis." In chloride-depletion alkalosis (vomiting, NG suction, diuretics) you want KCl, which replaces both K and Cl. Reserve K-citrate/bicarbonate for hypokalemia WITH a metabolic acidosis (e.g., RTA, diarrhea).
4. IV KCl + rates
IV KCl rate limits: peripheral line up to ~10 mEq/h (higher concentrations cause phlebitis/pain); central line up to ~10–20 mEq/h with continuous cardiac monitoring. Maximum concentration is roughly 10 mEq/100 mL peripherally, up to 20–40 mEq/100 mL centrally. Use saline as the diluent, never dextrose.
⚠️ WRONG: running 40 mEq/h peripherally to "fix it fast," or giving an IV K "bolus." Exceeding safe rates risks fatal hyperkalemia and cardiac arrest; high peripheral concentration causes phlebitis. There is no such thing as an IV potassium push — it is always a controlled infusion with monitoring.
5. Telemetry if severe
Severe hypokalemia, symptomatic patients, ongoing IV repletion, or any patient on digoxin warrants continuous ECG/telemetry. Hypokalemia prolongs repolarization: ECG shows flattened T waves, U waves, ST depression, and QT/QU prolongation, predisposing to torsades and (with digoxin) to digoxin toxicity arrhythmias.
⚠️ WRONG: skipping telemetry in a digoxin patient because the K is "only mildly low." Hypokalemia markedly potentiates digoxin binding to the Na/K-ATPase and precipitates life-threatening arrhythmias even at modest K reductions — these patients must be monitored and aggressively repleted.
6. Mg key station
Intracellular magnesium normally plugs the ROMK channel in the distal nephron, limiting K secretion. When Mg is depleted, ROMK is disinhibited (left open) and the kidney wastes K continuously. This is why Mg status must be checked and corrected in every hypokalemic patient — it is the rate-limiting step in refractory cases.
⚠️ WRONG: ordering only a potassium level and ignoring magnesium. Common causes (loop/thiazide diuretics, diarrhea, alcoholism, PPIs, cisplatin) deplete BOTH ions, and you will chase your tail repleting K while Mg-driven renal K wasting continues.
7. K won't correct — replace Mg first
Refractory hypokalemia is hypomagnesemia until proven otherwise. With low Mg, ROMK stays open and renal K loss persists despite aggressive repletion. Correct Mg FIRST (e.g., IV magnesium sulfate 1–2 g) and the K will then respond; in coexisting deficiency repletion of both is given in parallel.
⚠️ WRONG: "K isn't rising despite 200 mEq — give even more potassium." The board answer in a diuretic/alcoholic patient whose K won't budge is to CHECK AND REPLACE MAGNESIUM. Escalating K alone fails and risks rebound hyperkalemia once Mg is finally corrected.
8. Dextrose trap
Never use dextrose-containing fluid as the K diluent: glucose triggers endogenous insulin release, which drives K intracellularly and can transiently DROP serum K further before repletion catches up. Mix KCl in normal saline (or half-normal saline) instead.
⚠️ WRONG: choosing "KCl in D5W" for an acutely symptomatic hypokalemic patient. The insulin surge from dextrose shifts K into cells and acutely worsens the hypokalemia — the correct diluent is saline. (Note: this is the opposite scenario from treating hyperkalemia, where insulin+dextrose is therapeutic.)
9. Rebound hyperK risk
In pure transcellular SHIFT states — thyrotoxic/hypokalemic periodic paralysis, beta-agonist excess, DKA before fluids/insulin equilibrate — total body K is normal and the K is merely hiding inside cells. Give SMALL, cautious doses: when the shift reverses, the K floods back out and aggressive repletion causes dangerous rebound hyperkalemia.
⚠️ WRONG: treating periodic-paralysis hypokalemia like a true deficit with large K loads. Once the trigger resolves the intracellular K re-emerges and the patient overshoots into hyperkalemia — give modest K and treat the underlying shift instead.
10. TPP treatment
Thyrotoxic periodic paralysis (classically a young Asian male with acute proximal weakness and hyperthyroidism) is a shift phenomenon. Give non-selective beta-blockade — propranolol — to block the beta-adrenergic Na/K-ATPase-driven K shift, give only modest K, and definitively treat the hyperthyroidism. Avoid large K doses (rebound hyperK).
⚠️ WRONG: "Load the TPP patient with high-dose IV potassium." Because the K is shifted (not lost), high doses cause rebound hyperkalemia when the attack breaks. Propranolol plus low-dose K plus treating the thyroid is the answer.
11. Cause-specific: vomiting
Vomiting/NG suction causes a chloride-responsive metabolic alkalosis with volume depletion and secondary hyperaldosteronism driving renal K loss. Treat with normal saline (restores volume and chloride, switches off aldosterone) PLUS KCl. Urine chloride is low (<20), confirming saline-responsiveness.
⚠️ WRONG: giving potassium without volume/chloride repletion in the vomiting patient. Until you restore Cl and volume, secondary hyperaldosteronism keeps wasting K — saline + KCl together, not KCl alone.
12. Cause-specific: diuretic
Loop and thiazide diuretics cause renal K (and Mg) wasting. Manage by repleting KCl (and Mg), reducing the diuretic dose if possible, and adding a K-sparing agent (amiloride, triamterene, or spironolactone) to offset ongoing loss. Always check Mg, which is commonly co-depleted.
⚠️ WRONG: endlessly repleting oral K in a patient on a high-dose loop diuretic without addressing the drug. The durable fix is dose reduction and/or a K-sparing diuretic, plus correcting Mg — otherwise the wasting continues.
13. Liddle vs primary aldo tx
Liddle syndrome is a gain-of-function ENaC mutation (constitutively open sodium channel) causing HTN + hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis with LOW aldosterone and LOW renin — treat with an ENaC blocker (amiloride or triamterene). Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn) has HIGH aldosterone/low renin — treat with adrenalectomy (adenoma) or a mineralocorticoid-receptor antagonist (spironolactone/eplerenone) for bilateral hyperplasia.
⚠️ WRONG: giving spironolactone for Liddle syndrome. Liddle is a channel defect with LOW aldosterone, so an aldosterone antagonist won't work — the answer is amiloride/triamterene (block ENaC directly). Spironolactone is correct only when aldosterone is the problem (primary hyperaldo).
14. Severe deficit magnitude
Serum K vastly underestimates total-body deficit because ~98% of body K is intracellular. As a guide, every ~0.3 mEq/L fall in serum K reflects roughly a 100 mEq total-body deficit, so a K of 3.0 may represent a 200–400 mEq shortfall. Repletion therefore takes more than the bare number suggests — except in pure shift states, where total body K is normal.
⚠️ WRONG: stopping repletion the instant serum K crosses into the normal range in a truly depleted patient. The intracellular compartment is still empty; under-replacement leads to recurrent hypokalemia. (The exception: shift states, where the deficit is apparent, not real.)
