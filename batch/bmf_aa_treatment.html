📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. HSCT transplant bay
Allogeneic HEMATOPOIETIC STEM CELL TRANSPLANT is FIRST-LINE for YOUNG patients (roughly <40–50 yr, fit) with SEVERE or VERY-SEVERE aplastic anemia who have a matched sibling donor. It is the only potentially CURATIVE therapy because it replaces the failed marrow.
⚠️ Transplant is NOT first-line for everyone — age and donor availability decide. Older or unfit patients, or those without a matched donor, get immunosuppressive therapy (IST) first. Choosing 'HSCT for all' is the classic distractor.
2. >90% children cure
Matched-sibling allo-HSCT cures >90% of children and young adults with severe AA, with the best long-term outcomes and (unlike IST) low risk of later clonal evolution to MDS/AML.
⚠️ Younger age = better transplant outcome and lower GVHD risk; this is precisely why upfront transplant is favored in the young while IST is preferred in older adults.
3. Matched sibling donor
The preferred graft source is an HLA-MATCHED SIBLING DONOR — best engraftment, least GVHD. Its availability (plus patient age/severity) is the pivotal branch point in the treatment algorithm.
⚠️ Young + severe AA + matched sibling → transplant first. No matched sibling → start IST while searching for an unrelated/alternative donor (matched-unrelated transplant is increasingly used upfront in the young when a quick MUD is available).
4. Donor doors MUD/CORD/HAPLO
When no matched sibling exists, alternative donor sources are matched UNRELATED donor (MUD), CORD blood, or HAPLOIDENTICAL donor — used for transplant after IST failure, or upfront in young patients in experienced centers.
⚠️ Alternative-donor transplants carry higher GVHD/graft-failure risk than matched-sibling grafts, so they are generally reserved for IST-refractory young patients rather than offered before a trial of immunosuppression.
5. No family blood
Avoid transfusing blood products from FAMILY MEMBERS in any AA patient who might be transplanted — shared HLA antigens increase alloimmunization and transfusion-associated GVHD and can compromise later engraftment.
⚠️ Counterintuitive but high-yield: family donors are WORSE, not better, for transfusion in a potential transplant candidate. Also minimize transfusions overall pre-transplant.
6. Conditioning radiation
A CONDITIONING regimen (e.g., cyclophosphamide ± ATG; radiation in some protocols) precedes the graft to ablate the recipient's autoreactive immune system and allow engraftment.
⚠️ Minimize pretransplant transfusions and use leukoreduced/irradiated products to reduce alloimmunization and graft rejection — sensitization from prior transfusions worsens engraftment.
7. ATG + Cyclosporine
IMMUNOSUPPRESSIVE THERAPY (IST) = antithymocyte globulin (ATG, horse/equine ATG preferred over rabbit) PLUS cyclosporine. It is FIRST-LINE for patients who are NOT transplant candidates: older adults, no matched sibling donor, or significant comorbidities. It suppresses the T-cell attack so endogenous marrow recovers.
⚠️ IST is the answer for OLDER/unfit patients or young patients lacking a donor — NOT first-line for a young, fit patient WITH a matched sibling (that patient gets transplant). Horse ATG outperforms rabbit ATG in AA.
8. Triple 70-80%
TRIPLE therapy (ATG + cyclosporine + eltrombopag) achieves response rates of ~70–80%, higher and faster than ATG+cyclosporine alone. Responses take weeks to months, so supportive care must continue meanwhile.
⚠️ IST improves counts but is NOT curative and does not replace the marrow — relapse and clonal evolution remain possible, requiring long-term follow-up.
9. Eltrombopag
ELTROMBOPAG is an oral THROMBOPOIETIN-RECEPTOR AGONIST that stimulates surviving stem cells; added to ATG+cyclosporine it boosts overall and complete response rates and speeds count recovery (approved upfront with IST in 2018).
⚠️ Mechanism is TPO-receptor agonism on the residual stem-cell pool — not immunosuppression. Monitor for clonal cytogenetic changes/evolution while on therapy.
10. Relapse / clonal evolution
After IST, ~10–15%+ relapse and there is a real long-term risk of CLONAL EVOLUTION to MDS, AML, or clinically significant PNH. Lifelong monitoring (CBC, flow for PNH, marrow/cytogenetics) is required.
⚠️ IST is NOT permanently curative — its major drawback versus transplant is relapse and late clonal evolution. This is a key reason upfront transplant is favored in the young.
11. Supportive transfusion
SUPPORTIVE CARE underpins all management: RBC transfusion for symptomatic anemia (restrictive threshold ~Hb 7 g/dL) and platelet transfusion for bleeding/very low counts — all products LEUKOREDUCED and IRRADIATED to limit alloimmunization and transfusion-associated GVHD.
⚠️ Always use leukoreduced + irradiated products and avoid family donors in transplant candidates; over-transfusion before transplant promotes alloimmunization and graft failure.
12. Antimicrobials VANC
Neutropenic patients need aggressive infection management: empiric BROAD-SPECTRUM antibiotics for febrile neutropenia, antifungal therapy for persistent fever, and vigilance for neutropenic enterocolitis (TYPHLITIS). Prophylaxis may be used in prolonged severe neutropenia.
⚠️ Febrile neutropenia is an emergency — empiric anti-pseudomonal antibiotics immediately. Avoid routine prophylactic platelet/granulocyte myths; G-CSF gives only transient, limited benefit and does not treat the underlying disease.
13. Iron chelation DFX
Chronic RBC transfusion causes IRON OVERLOAD; treat with iron chelation (DEFERASIROX/DFX, oral; or deferoxamine) once ferritin/transfusion burden is high, to prevent cardiac/hepatic/endocrine iron toxicity.
⚠️ Iron overload is from transfusions, not the disease itself — monitor ferritin and chelate transfusion-dependent patients. Relevant especially in IST-treated/relapsed patients with ongoing transfusion need.
14. Most effective = transplant
Bottom line: allo-HSCT is the ONLY CURATIVE option and the most effective definitive therapy (the 'CURE' key), while IST is non-curative but is preferred when transplant is too risky (older/unfit patients or no donor). The decision balances age, disease severity, and donor availability.
⚠️ Both can be 'right' depending on the patient: transplant for young + severe + matched sibling; IST for older/unfit or no donor. The exam tests this branching, not a single universal first-line.
