📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Voltage-mass dissociation
In HCM and hypertensive LVH a thick wall generates HIGH voltage (LVH criteria appear). In amyloid, infiltrate fills the extracellular space and does not conduct electricity, so you get massive wall thickness on echo with LOW or pseudo-normal voltage on ECG. This dissociation is the most specific clinical clue for cardiac amyloidosis. 'Thick LV + low voltage = amyloid' is a mandatory board rule.
⚠️ Thick walls with HIGH voltage point to HCM or hypertensive LVH, not amyloid — the discriminator is the low voltage despite the thick wall.
2. Apical sparing strain
Amyloid deposits are denser at the base and mid-wall while the thin-walled apex is relatively spared, producing apical sparing on longitudinal strain — pathognomonic for cardiac amyloidosis. This is the OPPOSITE of ischemic disease. It is the single best echocardiographic discriminator: apical sparing = amyloid until proven otherwise.
⚠️ Don't read this as ischemia — apical/regional wall-motion loss from CAD does not produce the base-abnormal, apex-spared bull's-eye that is specific to amyloid.
3. ATTR exhibit
ATTRwt (wild-type) is predominantly older men, 7th–9th decade. Musculoskeletal clues precede heart failure: bilateral carpal tunnel syndrome may come 5–8 years before HF, plus lumbar spinal stenosis and ruptured biceps tendon (Popeye deformity). Classic vignette: an 80-year-old man with HFpEF, bilateral carpal tunnel, and low ECG voltage = ATTRwt cardiac amyloidosis.
⚠️ Unilateral, work-related carpal tunnel in a young patient is not the clue — it is the BILATERAL CTS in an older man with HFpEF that flags ATTR.
4. Bilateral carpal tunnel
Bilateral carpal tunnel syndrome is the earliest systemic flag of ATTR and may precede cardiac failure by 5–8 years; it reflects TTR amyloid deposition in the flexor retinaculum. On boards, bilateral CTS + spinal stenosis + HFpEF in an elderly man should trigger an amyloid workup rather than a primary orthopedic explanation.
⚠️ Treating recurrent bilateral CTS as an isolated nerve-entrapment problem misses the systemic ATTR diagnosis it heralds.
5. Tafamidis (TTR stabilizer)
Tafamidis is the disease-modifying treatment for ATTR: it binds and stabilizes the transthyretin TETRAMER, preventing dissociation and misfolding into amyloid fibrils, slowing progression and reducing mortality/hospitalization. It is indicated for ATTR (wild-type or hereditary), NOT for AL amyloidosis.
⚠️ Tafamidis is wrong for AL — AL needs urgent plasma-cell-directed therapy; giving tafamidis to an AL patient wastes critical time.
6. AL exhibit — periorbital purpura
AL arises from a plasma-cell dyscrasia. Periorbital purpura (amyloid in periorbital capillaries) is nearly pathognomonic for AL, along with macroglossia and nephrotic-range proteinuria. AL is the URGENT subtype: untreated median survival is only MONTHS, so it demands urgent hematologic, plasma-cell-directed therapy (e.g., daratumumab-based regimens).
⚠️ Periorbital purpura + macroglossia is AL, not ATTR — don't anchor on tafamidis; the next step is urgent hematology referral.
7. AL = URGENT next step
Because AL progresses far faster than ATTR (cardiac involvement can be fatal within months untreated), confirmed or suspected AL is a medical urgency. The board trap: 'thick LV + apical sparing + periorbital purpura — what must happen next?' Answer = URGENT hematology and plasma-cell-directed treatment, NOT tafamidis.
⚠️ The wrong answer is starting an ATTR stabilizer or routine HF titration — AL mandates immediate hematologic referral because of months-level survival.
8. Diagnostic sequence — exclude AL first
Mandatory workup: Step 1 = serum free light chains; Step 2 = serum AND urine immunofixation. SPEP alone misses up to 40% of AL because the paraprotein may be too small — never rely on SPEP. Step 3 (only after both AL screens negative) = PYP/DPD bone-tracer scan. A positive scan with negative plasma-cell workup = ATTR, no biopsy needed. Abnormal step 1 or 2 → hematology referral and typing.
⚠️ Ordering SPEP only is the trap — it misses ~40% of AL; you must add free light chains plus serum and urine immunofixation.
9. PYP/DPD scan → ATTR
A grade 2–3 positive PYP (pyrophosphate) / DPD bone-scintigraphy scan in a patient with negative serum/urine light-chain and immunofixation testing confirms ATTR cardiac amyloidosis noninvasively — no endomyocardial biopsy required. The cardinal rule: NEVER diagnose ATTR by scan alone without first excluding AL, because AL can also show tracer uptake and missing it is fatal.
⚠️ A positive PYP scan does NOT by itself mean ATTR — without first excluding AL, a positive scan in an AL patient leads to dangerously wrong (and delayed) treatment.
10. Danger cabinet — digoxin
Digoxin binds avidly to amyloid fibrils, concentrating in the myocardium and producing toxicity at otherwise normal therapeutic serum levels — avoid in amyloid. This is a high-yield 'drug to avoid' point distinguishing amyloid management from ordinary HF.
⚠️ A 'normal' digoxin level falsely reassures — in amyloid, fibril binding causes toxicity despite therapeutic levels, so it should be avoided entirely.
11. Danger cabinet — BB / ACEi / ARB
Amyloid hearts are stiff and output is rate-dependent, so beta-blocker-induced bradycardia drops cardiac output — avoid or use cautiously. Amyloid patients also run low systemic vascular resistance and tolerate vasodilation poorly, so ACEi/ARBs frequently cause hypotension. Standard HFrEF GDMT is often poorly tolerated; loop diuretics for congestion are the mainstay.
⚠️ Don't reflexively uptitrate full HFrEF GDMT — beta-blockers and ACEi/ARBs often cause low output and hypotension in stiff, low-SVR amyloid hearts.
12. Prophylactic ICD red X
Prophylactic ICD is NOT routinely beneficial in cardiac amyloidosis because sudden death tends to come from bradyarrhythmia or pulseless electrical activity / electromechanical dissociation — not shockable VT/VF that an ICD can terminate. Consider an ICD only after a resuscitated arrest or for sustained VT (secondary prevention).
⚠️ A low EF alone does not justify a prophylactic ICD in amyloid — the lethal mechanism is non-shockable, so the device often cannot help.
13. AF in amyloid → anticoagulate
Amyloid infiltrates the ATRIAL myocardium and impairs mechanical atrial contraction even when LV systolic function/EF is preserved, so atrial thrombus forms readily. Anticoagulate ALWAYS in amyloid with AF, independent of the CHA2DS2-VASc score — the usual risk-score thresholds do not apply.
⚠️ Don't withhold anticoagulation for a low CHA2DS2-VASc or preserved EF — amyloid AF carries high thrombus risk regardless, so anticoagulate every time.
14. Congo red — apple-green birefringence
Definitive histologic diagnosis is Congo red staining showing apple-green birefringence under polarized light. When the noninvasive algorithm is inconclusive, tissue typing by mass spectrometry distinguishes ATTR from AL. Biopsy is reserved for cases not resolved by the light-chain workup plus scintigraphy pathway.
⚠️ Congo red confirms amyloid but does NOT tell you the type — ATTR vs AL typing requires mass spectrometry (or immunohistochemistry), not the stain alone.
