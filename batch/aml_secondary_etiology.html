📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Therapy-related ships
Therapy-related AML (t-AML) arises years after cytotoxic chemotherapy or radiation given for a prior malignancy or autoimmune disease and is uniformly classified as ADVERSE risk with poor response to standard therapy. The two archetypes are alkylator-induced (long latency, -5/-7, complex karyotype, antecedent MDS phase) and topoisomerase-II-induced (short latency, KMT2A/11q23, no MDS phase). CPX-351 is the preferred induction in older t-AML patients.
⚠️ All therapy-related AML is adverse-risk regardless of cytogenetics and is best considered for allo-HCT/clinical trial; CPX-351 outperforms conventional 7+3 here. Distinguishing the two t-AML types by latency and genetics is high-yield.
2. Alkylating ship 4-6 yr
Alkylating agents (cyclophosphamide, busulfan, melphalan, chlorambucil, platinums) cause t-AML after a LONG latency of about 5-7 years, typically preceded by a myelodysplastic (MDS) phase, and carry unbalanced deletions — monosomy 5/del(5q), monosomy 7/del(7q) — and complex karyotypes, often with TP53 mutation. This is the worse-prognosis of the two t-AML patterns.
⚠️ Long latency (~5-7 yr) + preceding MDS + del(5q)/del(7q)/complex karyotype = ALKYLATOR-induced t-AML. Pairing the wrong agent class with these features is the classic distractor.
3. Topo II ship 1-3 yr
Topoisomerase II inhibitors (etoposide, teniposide, anthracyclines) cause t-AML after a SHORT latency of about 1-3 years, with NO preceding MDS phase, and characteristically harbor balanced translocations involving KMT2A/MLL at 11q23 (or sometimes RUNX1/CBF rearrangements). This monocytic-leaning leukemia appears more abruptly than the alkylator type.
⚠️ Short latency (~1-3 yr), no MDS phase, balanced KMT2A/11q23 translocation = TOPO-II-induced t-AML — the mirror image of the alkylator pattern. The latency period is the quickest discriminator on the exam.
4. KMT2A 11q23
KMT2A (MLL) rearrangement at chromosome band 11q23 is the molecular signature of topoisomerase-II-inhibitor-related AML and is itself an adverse-risk lesion (except the intermediate t(9;11)). KMT2A rearrangements also occur in de novo infant leukemias and confer chemoresistance.
⚠️ 11q23/KMT2A links specifically to TOPO-II t-AML and is adverse — except t(9;11)(p21;q23)/KMT2A-MLLT3, the single intermediate-risk KMT2A rearrangement.
5. Multilineage dysplasia
AML with myelodysplasia-related changes — defined by an antecedent MDS/MDS-MPN, MDS-related cytogenetics, or multilineage dysplasia, often with secondary-type mutations (SRSF2, SF3B1, U2AF1, ZRSR2, ASXL1, EZH2, BCOR, STAG2) — is a secondary-type AML that is adverse-risk and responds poorly to conventional chemo. Like t-AML, older patients benefit from CPX-351.
⚠️ AML with myelodysplasia-related changes / secondary-type mutations is adverse-risk secondary AML, biologically distinct from de novo AML, and warrants transplant-directed or HMA-based strategies rather than expecting a good 7+3 response.
6. Epigenetic mutations
DNMT3A, TET2, and ASXL1 ('DTA') mutations are epigenetic regulators and the founding lesions of clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP) — clonal expansion without overt malignancy that raises the long-term risk of AML/MDS and of cardiovascular disease. ASXL1 in particular is an adverse-risk marker, and DTA mutations persist in remission so they are unreliable MRD markers.
⚠️ DTA mutations (DNMT3A/TET2/ASXL1) persist after remission and therefore are POOR MRD markers — their presence in CR does not equal residual disease. CHIP is a premalignant state, not leukemia, but predicts transformation and cardiovascular risk.
7. Monocytic clogging
Monocytic differentiation (FAB M4 myelomonocytic and M5 monoblastic/monocytic) drives tissue infiltration — gingival hypertrophy, leukemia cutis, CNS involvement — and a higher leukostasis risk. Monocytic blasts express CD14/CD11b/CD64 and are nonspecific-esterase positive (versus MPO-strong granulocytic blasts).
⚠️ Monocytic AML (M4/M5) is the subtype behind gum infiltration, skin involvement, and elevated leukostasis/CNS risk — and is distinguished by nonspecific esterase positivity rather than strong MPO.
8. Germline pier
Germline predisposition syndromes (RUNX1, CEBPA, GATA2, DDX41, and bone marrow failure syndromes such as Fanconi anemia, dyskeratosis congenita, telomere disorders) cause familial MDS/AML. Recognizing them matters because affected relatives must be excluded as stem-cell donors and because surveillance is needed — testing requires germline (e.g., cultured fibroblast/skin) DNA, not blood, since blood is clonally involved.
⚠️ Before using a RELATIVE as a transplant donor, screen for germline predisposition (RUNX1/CEBPA/GATA2/DDX41) — transplanting a sibling's predisposed marrow can transmit/recur leukemia. Germline testing needs non-hematopoietic tissue (fibroblasts), not a blood sample.
9. Benzene barrel
Benzene is the best-established occupational/environmental chemical cause of AML (and MDS/aplastic anemia), with risk related to cumulative exposure in industries like petroleum, rubber, and tanning, and from cigarette smoke. It causes clastogenic marrow damage leading to secondary-type cytogenetic abnormalities.
⚠️ Benzene (and chronic cigarette smoking) is a confirmed AML risk factor — an occupational history in petroleum/rubber/solvent work is the testable clue. Toluene and similar solvents are not equivalently linked.
10. Radiation symbol
Ionizing radiation (atomic-bomb survivors, radiotherapy, prior radioiodine) is an established AML cause; the combination of radiation plus alkylating chemotherapy yields the highest secondary-AML risk of all. Latency is typically several years, and the resulting leukemia carries adverse-risk genetics.
⚠️ Combined chemoradiation carries the highest t-AML risk — greater than either modality alone. CLL/lymphoma survivors treated with chemo + radiation are a classic high-risk population for later AML.
11. Chlor / chemical exposure
Beyond benzene, additional chemical/cytotoxic exposures contribute to secondary AML, and patients with a prior cancer treated with cytotoxic agents remain at lifelong elevated risk. A careful occupational, environmental, and prior-treatment history is part of every new AML workup to identify a secondary etiology.
⚠️ A secondary etiology (chemical, occupational, prior chemo/radiation) reclassifies AML as adverse-risk and changes management — always take the exposure and prior-treatment history, since de novo and secondary AML are treated differently.
12. 10x risk multiplier
An antecedent hematologic disorder — MDS, MPN (e.g., myelofibrosis, PV, ET), aplastic anemia, or PNH — markedly increases the risk of progression to AML and defines secondary AML, which is adverse-risk with worse outcomes than de novo disease. MPN-blast-phase (≥20% blasts) AML is especially treatment-resistant.
⚠️ AML evolving from MDS/MPN ('secondary AML') is adverse-risk and responds poorly to standard 7+3 — these patients are steered toward HMA-based therapy, CPX-351, or transplant rather than expecting de-novo-level cure rates.
