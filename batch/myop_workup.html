📖 Read-through — all 17 symbols
1. Prednisone 1st line
High-dose oral corticosteroids (prednisone ~0.75–1.5 mg/kg/day, commonly ~1 mg/kg) are FIRST-LINE for DM, PM, IMNM, and antisynthetase syndrome. Steroids are continued at the high dose until strength improves and CK normalizes, then SLOWLY tapered over months. A steroid-sparing agent is added early (often at the outset) to limit cumulative steroid toxicity. IBM is the exception — it does not respond.
⚠️ WRONG answer: tapering prednisone rapidly once the CK drops. Discriminator: too-fast tapering causes relapse; the taper must be gradual over months and guided by clinical strength, with a steroid-sparing agent on board to permit dose reduction.
2. IV methylpred pulse
Severe or rapidly progressive disease (profound weakness, dysphagia/aspiration risk, myocarditis, or rapidly progressive ILD) begins with PULSE IV methylprednisolone — e.g., 1 g IV daily for 3 days — before transitioning to high-dose oral prednisone. Pulse dosing achieves rapid immunosuppression in organ- or life-threatening presentations.
⚠️ WRONG answer: starting a patient with severe dysphagia and respiratory compromise on standard oral prednisone only. Discriminator: severe/fulminant disease warrants IV pulse methylprednisolone (± early IVIG) up front — relying on oral steroids alone delays control in a patient who is aspirating or in respiratory failure.
3. Treat strength not CK
Therapy is titrated to CLINICAL STRENGTH (and function), not to the CK number. CK may lag, rise transiently with exercise, or stay up while the patient is clearly improving; conversely a falling CK with worsening strength suggests steroid myopathy or a non-inflammatory process. Track manual muscle testing, functional tasks, and patient-reported activity alongside the enzymes.
⚠️ WRONG answer: escalating immunosuppression solely because the CK ticked up while the patient is getting stronger. Discriminator: an isolated CK rise without clinical worsening does not warrant more immunosuppression — treat the patient's strength, not the lab; over-treating off the CK adds toxicity.
4. Methotrexate
Methotrexate is a first-line steroid-sparing agent with relatively FAST onset, given weekly (with folic acid). It is effective for DM/PM muscle and skin disease. Key caveat: AVOID in significant interstitial lung disease because of methotrexate pneumonitis/fibrosis risk that can be confused with or worsen ILD.
⚠️ WRONG answer: choosing methotrexate as the steroid-sparing drug in an antisynthetase/anti-Jo-1 patient with ILD. Discriminator: with significant ILD, MTX is contraindicated/avoided (pulmonary toxicity) — use mycophenolate or rituximab instead; the lung status decides the agent.
5. Azathioprine
Azathioprine is a steroid-sparing alternative to methotrexate with SLOWER onset (months to full effect). Check TPMT (thiopurine methyltransferase) activity BEFORE starting — low/absent TPMT predicts severe myelosuppression — and monitor CBC and LFTs. It is a reasonable choice when MTX is contraindicated (e.g., mild ILD, liver disease, alcohol use).
⚠️ WRONG answer: starting full-dose azathioprine without checking TPMT. Discriminator: TPMT-deficient patients develop life-threatening bone-marrow suppression on standard azathioprine doses — TPMT testing (or genotyping) before initiation, plus CBC monitoring, is mandatory.
6. MMF drug of choice for ILD
Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) is the steroid-sparing agent of choice when INTERSTITIAL LUNG DISEASE accompanies myositis (antisynthetase syndrome, anti-MDA5 DM). It treats both muscle and lung, is generally well tolerated (GI upset, cytopenias), and is teratogenic so requires contraception. Rituximab is the main alternative/add-on for ILD-predominant or refractory disease.
⚠️ WRONG answer: defaulting to methotrexate for steroid-sparing in myositis-ILD. Discriminator: MMF (or rituximab) is preferred when ILD is present because MTX risks pulmonary toxicity — match the agent to the lung disease.
7. IVIG
IVIG has RAPID onset and is FDA-approved for dermatomyositis; it is especially valuable in DM, IMNM (notably anti-HMGCR), severe dysphagia, and when infection risk limits other immunosuppression (e.g., active infection, pregnancy). Given as a course every few weeks; watch for headache/aseptic meningitis, thrombosis, and renal effects.
⚠️ WRONG answer: viewing IVIG only as a last-line agent for refractory disease. Discriminator: IVIG is an early/first-line option in DM and anti-HMGCR IMNM (and for life-threatening dysphagia) because of its speed and favorable infection profile — not merely a salvage therapy.
8. Rituximab
Rituximab (anti-CD20 B-cell depletion) is used for REFRACTORY myositis and is favored in antisynthetase syndrome (especially ILD-predominant) and anti-SRP/anti-HMGCR necrotizing myopathy. Typical dosing mirrors other rheumatic indications (e.g., two 1 g infusions 2 weeks apart, or weekly x4). Screen for hepatitis B and check immunoglobulins before use.
⚠️ WRONG answer: reserving rituximab indiscriminately for all refractory cases including IBM. Discriminator: rituximab helps refractory DM/PM/antisynthetase/IMNM but does NOT work for IBM, which is immunotherapy-resistant — escalating to RTX in IBM exposes the patient to risk without benefit.
9. Steroid myopathy
Steroid (glucocorticoid) myopathy is a common iatrogenic MIMIC of disease relapse: new proximal weakness developing while on high-dose or prolonged steroids, but with a NORMAL CK and a NON-irritable (myopathic without spontaneous activity) EMG and no inflammation on biopsy. It is dose- and duration-related and improves when steroids are reduced.
⚠️ WRONG answer: increasing the steroid dose for new weakness in a patient on chronic high-dose prednisone. Discriminator: in true myositis flare the CK rises and EMG is irritable; in steroid myopathy the CK stays NORMAL and EMG is non-irritable — the correct move is to TAPER the steroid, not escalate it.
10. Treatment response table
Response to immunosuppression by subtype: DM, PM, and antisynthetase syndrome generally RESPOND; IMNM (especially anti-HMGCR) responds, often needing IVIG/rituximab, while anti-SRP can be refractory; IBM is essentially NON-responsive (X across the board). This table is the high-yield 'who gets immunosuppression' summary.
⚠️ WRONG answer: applying the standard steroid + steroid-sparing immunosuppression algorithm uniformly to all five subtypes including IBM. Discriminator: IBM is the exception — it does not meaningfully respond to immunosuppression, so the algorithm that works for DM/PM/ASyS/IMNM does not apply to it.
11. IBM only supportive
Inclusion body myositis: typically a man over 50, with DISTAL and ASYMMETRIC weakness (classically finger flexors and quadriceps), frequent dysphagia, slowly progressive course, near-normal CK, and rimmed vacuoles with p62/TDP-43 inclusions on biopsy. There is no effective immunotherapy — management is SUPPORTIVE (PT/OT, assistive devices, dysphagia/aspiration precautions, falls prevention).
⚠️ WRONG answer: trialing high-dose steroids and steroid-sparing agents in confirmed IBM hoping for response. Discriminator: IBM is steroid- and immunotherapy-REFRACTORY; immunosuppression adds toxicity (including steroid myopathy) without benefit — supportive care is the standard of care.
12. Myocarditis / cardiac
Cardiac involvement (myocarditis, conduction disease, arrhythmia, rarely heart failure) is a feared and prognosis-worsening complication of inflammatory myopathy, more common with anti-SRP disease and in checkpoint-inhibitor myositis. Screen with ECG and troponin (note troponin-T can rise from skeletal muscle — use troponin-I to confirm cardiac source), with echo/cardiac MRI as needed.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing an elevated troponin-T purely to cardiac injury in a myositis patient. Discriminator: skeletal muscle releases troponin-T, so an isolated troponin-T rise may be muscular — confirm cardiac involvement with the more cardiac-specific troponin-I plus ECG and imaging before diagnosing myocarditis.
13. ICI myositis TRIAD
Immune-checkpoint-inhibitor (ICI) myositis is an immune-related adverse event that classically presents as an OVERLAP TRIAD of myositis + MYOCARDITIS + myasthenia-gravis-like neuromuscular disease (ptosis, diplopia, bulbar/respiratory weakness). The myocarditis component carries high mortality, so any ICI patient with weakness needs urgent cardiac evaluation (ECG, troponin, echo/MRI).
⚠️ WRONG answer: treating ICI-related weakness as isolated myositis and skipping cardiac and neuromuscular assessment. Discriminator: ICI myositis overlaps with myocarditis and MG — missing the myocarditis (the lethal component) by not checking ECG/troponin is the dangerous error; evaluate the whole triad.
14. Stop ICI first
Management of ICI-induced myositis/myocarditis: STEP 1 — STOP the checkpoint inhibitor immediately; STEP 2 — high-dose corticosteroids (IV methylprednisolone for severe/cardiac disease) ± IVIG/plasmapheresis. Because severe irAEs (especially myocarditis) can be fatal and recur, RE-CHALLENGE with the ICI is generally avoided after high-grade myositis/myocarditis.
⚠️ WRONG answer: re-starting the checkpoint inhibitor once the myositis/myocarditis settles because the tumor was responding. Discriminator: after severe (grade 3–4) ICI myositis or any myocarditis, re-challenge is generally CONTRAINDICATED due to risk of fatal recurrence — permanent discontinuation plus immunosuppression is the rule.
15. EMG irritable myopathy
NEW element. Electromyography in active inflammatory myopathy shows an IRRITABLE MYOPATHIC pattern: short-duration, low-amplitude, polyphasic motor unit potentials PLUS spontaneous activity (fibrillations, positive sharp waves, complex repetitive discharges). EMG confirms a myopathic (not neuropathic) process, gauges activity, and helps localize a biopsy site; MRI (STIR edema) complements it for site selection.
⚠️ WRONG answer: interpreting a normal or non-irritable EMG as excluding myositis, or reading large/long motor units as confirming it. Discriminator: long-duration, high-amplitude units indicate a NEUROPATHIC process (e.g., ALS, neuropathy), not myopathy; and a patchy myopathy can spare the sampled muscle — a non-diagnostic EMG does not rule it out.
16. Hypothyroid myopathy mimic
NEW element. Hypothyroid myopathy is a key non-inflammatory MIMIC: proximal weakness, myalgia, cramps, slowed/delayed deep-tendon reflex relaxation ('hung-up' reflexes), and an ELEVATED CK — sometimes markedly — that can falsely suggest myositis. The fix is thyroid hormone replacement, not immunosuppression. Always check TSH in a new myopathy workup before committing to inflammatory myositis.
⚠️ WRONG answer: starting corticosteroids for proximal weakness and a high CK without checking thyroid function. Discriminator: hypothyroidism raises CK and causes proximal weakness with hung-up reflexes; a high TSH points to thyroid-replacement therapy, not steroids — missing the TSH is the classic avoidable error.
17. PMR vs myositis
NEW element. Polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) is the classic distractor: age >50 with proximal shoulder/hip-girdle PAIN and STIFFNESS (worse in the morning), high ESR/CRP, but a NORMAL CK and no true weakness (limited by pain, not power). It responds dramatically to LOW-dose prednisone (~15 mg/day) and may be associated with giant cell arteritis — quite different from the high-CK, true-weakness, high-dose-steroid inflammatory myopathies.
⚠️ WRONG answer: diagnosing polymyositis in an older patient with girdle pain, stiffness, and a high ESR. Discriminator: PMR has PAIN/STIFFNESS with NORMAL CK and intact strength and responds to LOW-dose steroids; polymyositis has true WEAKNESS with HIGH CK needing HIGH-dose steroids plus immunosuppression — the CK and the strength exam separate them.
