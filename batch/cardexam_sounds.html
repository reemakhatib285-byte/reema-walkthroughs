📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. S3 = MR, DCM, HF
S3 is a low-pitched EARLY-DIASTOLIC sound from RAPID VENTRICULAR FILLING into a volume-overloaded ventricle: severe MITRAL REGURGITATION, DILATED CARDIOMYOPATHY, and HEART FAILURE. It can be physiologic in young, healthy people.
⚠️ S3 = volume overload (MR/DCM/HF) and is early diastolic — don't attribute it to a stiff ventricle; that's S4.
2. Big drum / volume overload
The big floppy drum encodes the dilated, volume-loaded, compliant ventricle that generates S3 — blood rushing in during early diastole resonates in the over-filled chamber, giving the low-pitched 'Kentucky' gallop.
⚠️ A big, low, early-diastolic gallop is S3 (floppy/dilated), not the high-pitched late S4 of a stiff ventricle.
3. Opening snap = MS
An OPENING SNAP is a high-pitched EARLY-DIASTOLIC sound from the abrupt opening of a stenotic but still pliable MITRAL valve — the hallmark of MITRAL STENOSIS. A shorter S2–OS interval indicates more severe MS.
⚠️ Opening snap is MITRAL STENOSIS, NOT mitral regurgitation — and it is a diastolic opening sound, not a click.
4. Snap NOT MR
This rules out mitral regurgitation as the cause of an opening snap. The snap is the sudden checked opening of a fused, stenotic mitral valve (MS); MR produces a holosystolic murmur, not a diastolic snap.
⚠️ The trap answer is MR — an opening snap belongs to MS, not regurgitation.
5. Ejection click = bicuspid AV
An early-systolic EJECTION CLICK comes from a BICUSPID AORTIC VALVE — the doming of the abnormal two-leaflet valve as it opens. It is often followed by an aortic ejection murmur.
⚠️ An ejection click points to a bicuspid aortic valve (two leaflets), not the diastolic opening snap of MS.
6. Presystolic = MS
A PRESYSTOLIC MURMUR is the late-diastolic accentuation of the MITRAL STENOSIS rumble, caused by atrial contraction pushing blood through the narrowed valve just before S1. It requires sinus rhythm (lost in AFib).
⚠️ Presystolic accentuation is MITRAL STENOSIS — it disappears in atrial fibrillation because it depends on atrial kick.
7. Physiologic split
PHYSIOLOGIC splitting of S2 (A2 then P2) WIDENS on INSPIRATION — increased venous return delays pulmonic closure (P2). It narrows on expiration and is a normal finding.
⚠️ Normal split widens with inspiration; a split that doesn't move with respiration (fixed) is abnormal — don't call physiologic splitting pathologic.
8. Inspiration widens split
On inspiration, negative intrathoracic pressure increases RV filling and delays pulmonic valve closure, so P2 moves later and the A2–P2 gap widens — the basis of normal physiologic splitting.
⚠️ Respiratory variation is the normal behavior; loss of that variation (fixed split) is the abnormal ASD finding.
9. Fixed split = ASD
A FIXED split S2 (A2–P2 gap unchanged through the respiratory cycle) is the classic sign of an ATRIAL SEPTAL DEFECT — the left-to-right shunt equalizes RV loading across respiration, so the split no longer varies.
⚠️ Fixed split = ASD specifically; it does NOT widen with inspiration like a physiologic split.
10. Paradoxical = AS, LBBB
A PARADOXICAL split means A2 comes AFTER P2 (reversed order) because LV emptying is delayed: SEVERE AORTIC STENOSIS or LBBB. The split NARROWS on inspiration (because P2 delays toward the already-late A2).
⚠️ Paradoxical split = A2 AFTER P2 and narrows on inspiration — the reverse of normal; causes are severe AS or LBBB, not RBBB.
11. A2 after P2
In paradoxical splitting the AORTIC component is delayed past the pulmonic component, so the normal A2-then-P2 order is reversed. Inspiration delays P2 toward A2, paradoxically narrowing or closing the split.
⚠️ The defining feature is reversed component order (A2 follows P2) — recognizing the reversal is the tested point.
12. Wide split = RBBB
A WIDE split S2 (widely separated but still respiration-varying) is caused by RIGHT BUNDLE BRANCH BLOCK — delayed RV activation pushes P2 even later, widening the A2–P2 gap throughout the cycle while still widening further on inspiration.
⚠️ Wide (not fixed) split = RBBB; it still varies with respiration, distinguishing it from the fixed split of ASD.
13. S4 = AS, HOCM, HTN
S4 is a LATE-DIASTOLIC sound from ATRIAL CONTRACTION (atrial kick) pushing blood into a STIFF, non-compliant ventricle: AORTIC STENOSIS, HOCM, and HYPERTENSION (LVH). It requires organized atrial contraction (absent in AFib).
⚠️ S4 = STIFF ventricle (AS/HOCM/HTN), NOT MR or MS — and it is late diastolic ('Tennessee'), unlike the early-diastolic S3.
14. S4 NOT MR / MS
This explicitly rules out mitral regurgitation and mitral stenosis as causes of S4. S4 reflects decreased ventricular compliance (stiff LV), not a leaky or stenotic mitral valve.
⚠️ The trap answers are MR and MS — S4 is a stiff-LV sound (AS/HOCM/HTN), not a mitral-valve lesion.
15. S4 = late diastole / atrial kick
S4 falls in LATE DIASTOLE, immediately before S1, generated by the atrial kick striking a stiff ventricle. Its timing (late, presystolic) and its stiff-LV causes distinguish it from the early-diastolic S3 gallop.
⚠️ Timing matters: S4 is late diastolic (atrial kick into a stiff LV); placing it in early diastole confuses it with S3.
