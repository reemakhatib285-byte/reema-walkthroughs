📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. TAVI — transcatheter valve
Transcatheter aortic valve implantation delivers a prosthetic valve via a catheter (usually transfemoral), with no sternotomy and no cardiopulmonary bypass. The new valve is deployed inside the diseased native valve, so recovery is faster and it suits higher surgical-risk patients.
⚠️ Describing TAVI as open surgery — it is catheter-based valve-in-native deployment, distinct from SAVR.
2. SAVR — surgical replacement
Surgical aortic valve replacement is open-heart surgery via sternotomy on cardiopulmonary bypass, excising the native valve and sewing in a prosthesis. It allows direct repair and concomitant procedures but carries a bigger operative insult.
⚠️ Defaulting to SAVR for everyone — TAVI is now favored across many risk groups; SAVR is for specific scenarios.
3. Balloon-expandable valve
Balloon-expandable TAVI valves are positioned and then expanded by inflating a balloon, giving precise, controlled deployment. Valve type selection depends on annular anatomy and access.
⚠️ Treating all TAVI valves as identical — balloon-expandable vs self-expandable differ in deployment and conduction-block risk.
4. Self-expandable valve
Self-expandable valves use a nitinol frame that opens on its own as the sheath is withdrawn. They sit higher/deeper depending on design and are associated with a higher rate of conduction disturbance and pacemaker need.
⚠️ Ignoring valve type when predicting pacemaker risk — self-expandable valves carry the highest new AV-block rate.
5. CT for annulus sizing
Pre-TAVI gated cardiac CT measures the aortic annulus to size the prosthesis and plan access. Correct annular sizing minimizes paravalvular leak and prevents annular injury or valve embolization.
⚠️ Sizing TAVI off echo alone — CT annular measurement is the standard to avoid paravalvular leak and malsizing.
6. Paravalvular leak
Paravalvular (perivalvular) aortic regurgitation is leakage AROUND the prosthesis rather than through it, a hallmark complication of TAVI from incomplete sealing against a calcified, irregular annulus. Even moderate paravalvular AR worsens prognosis.
⚠️ Dismissing post-TAVI paravalvular leak — it is a key complication and more than mild regurgitation impairs survival.
7. New AV block
The valve sits directly over the conduction system, so TAVI can injure the AV node/His bundle and cause new high-grade AV block or left bundle branch block. This risk is higher with TAVI than SAVR and greatest with self-expandable valves.
⚠️ Underestimating conduction-block risk after TAVI — new high-grade AV block is common, especially with self-expandable valves.
8. Pacemaker requirement
New high-grade AV block after valve replacement frequently requires permanent pacemaker implantation. Pacemaker rates are higher after TAVI than SAVR and must be discussed in patient consent and valve choice.
⚠️ Quoting equal pacemaker rates for TAVI and SAVR — TAVI (especially self-expandable) needs pacemakers more often.
9. Symptomatic severe AS, EF<50% → replace
Symptomatic severe aortic stenosis is a Class I indication for valve replacement, as is severe AS with LV systolic dysfunction (EF below 50%). Once these triggers are met, intervene — medical therapy does not change the natural history.
⚠️ Delaying intervention in symptomatic severe AS or AS with EF<50% — both are Class I indications to replace the valve.
10. TAVI favored
TAVI has expanded from high-risk to intermediate- and selected low-risk patients and is increasingly the default for transfemoral-suitable anatomy, especially older patients. The Heart Team weighs anatomy, age, and risk.
⚠️ Restricting TAVI to inoperable patients only — it is now favored across many risk groups, not just prohibitive risk.
11. SAVR: low-risk young
Younger, low-surgical-risk patients are often steered to SAVR because of greater long-term durability data and concerns about lifetime management/valve durability with TAVI. Life expectancy and future re-intervention strategy drive this choice.
⚠️ Choosing TAVI reflexively for a young low-risk patient — SAVR durability often favors surgery in that group.
12. SAVR: bicuspid valve
Bicuspid aortic valve anatomy (asymmetric calcification, elliptical annulus, associated aortopathy) is less ideal for TAVI sealing and may favor SAVR, though TAVI is used in selected cases. Anatomy is a major decider.
⚠️ Ignoring bicuspid anatomy in valve choice — it raises paravalvular-leak risk and often tips toward SAVR.
13. SAVR: concomitant cardiac surgery
When the patient already needs another cardiac operation (e.g., CABG or another valve), SAVR is performed together with that surgery rather than doing a separate TAVI. One operation addresses everything.
⚠️ Planning TAVI when the patient needs concomitant cardiac surgery — combine valve replacement with the surgery (SAVR).
14. Don't ignore the leak
A core teaching point: never dismiss post-procedure paravalvular regurgitation. More-than-mild paravalvular leak after TAVI is associated with worse outcomes and must be assessed and managed.
⚠️ Ignoring paravalvular leak after TAVI — it is a prognostically important complication, not a benign finding.
15. Don't miss AV block
The paired warning: do not overlook new conduction disease after valve replacement. Monitor for high-grade AV block post-TAVI, as many patients need a permanent pacemaker.
⚠️ Missing new AV block after TAVI — vigilance for conduction disturbance and pacemaker need is essential.
