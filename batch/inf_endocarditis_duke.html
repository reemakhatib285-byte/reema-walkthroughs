📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Balance scale = weigh the criteria
Duke criteria sort patients into DEFINITE, POSSIBLE, or REJECTED by weighing MAJOR vs MINOR findings. Major = a typical organism in 2 separate cultures (or the Coxiella exception) AND endocardial involvement on imaging (vegetation, abscess, fistula, new prosthetic dehiscence, or new regurgitation). The five minor categories are predisposition, fever ≥38°C, vascular phenomena, immunologic phenomena, and microbiologic evidence not meeting major.
2. DEFINITE plaque (gold star)
DEFINITE clinical IE = ANY of: 2 MAJOR, OR 1 major + 3 minor, OR 5 minor criteria. Pathologic definite = organisms or active endocarditis demonstrated on the valve specimen at surgery/autopsy. The combinations are deliberately swappable on exams, so lock them in.
3. 5 minor = definite (man lifting)
FIVE minor criteria with NO major still equals DEFINITE IE. Don't dismiss a culture-negative, echo-negative patient — count predisposition, fever, vascular, immunologic, and microbiologic minors; five of them clinch the diagnosis without any major criterion.
⚠️ 'You must have at least one major criterion to call it definite IE' -> wrong; five minor criteria with zero major still equals definite IE, so the culture- and echo-negative patient should not be written off.
4. POSSIBLE plaque
POSSIBLE IE = 1 major + 1 minor, OR 3 minor criteria. This intermediate category is suggestive but not definite and MANDATES continued evaluation — repeat cultures and TEE — to reclassify as definite or rejected.
⚠️ 'Possible IE means stop the workup / no echo needed' -> wrong; possible IE specifically requires further evaluation (TEE, repeat cultures) — 3 minor criteria is possible, not negative.
5. REJECTED (red-X)
REJECTED IE = a firm alternative diagnosis explains the picture, OR symptoms resolve with ≤4 days of antibiotics, OR no pathologic evidence of IE at surgery/autopsy after ≤4 days of antibiotics, OR criteria for possible/definite are not met.
⚠️ 'Any IE workup that is culture-negative gets rejected' -> wrong; rejection requires a firm alternative diagnosis or resolution within ≤4 days of antibiotics, not merely negative cultures — culture-negative IE is real and pursued by serology.
6. Major: typical organism x2 sets
MAJOR microbiologic criterion: a TYPICAL IE organism from 2 SEPARATE blood-culture sets — viridans strep, S. gallolyticus (bovis), HACEK, S. aureus, or community-acquired enterococcus without a primary focus — OR persistently positive cultures (≥2 sets >12 h apart, or majority of ≥3). Draw 3 sets from separate venipunctures BEFORE antibiotics; IE bacteremia is continuous so fever timing is irrelevant.
⚠️ 'One positive culture for a typical organism is a major criterion' -> wrong; a single positive set is only a MINOR microbiologic criterion — you need 2 separate sets (the lone exception being Coxiella).
7. Coxiella vials — single = major
Coxiella burnetii (Q fever) is the special microbiologic exception: a SINGLE positive blood culture OR a phase I IgG titer ≥1:800 is a STANDALONE major Duke criterion, because Coxiella is culture-negative and diagnosed serologically. Exposure clue: livestock (cattle, sheep, goats).
⚠️ 'Coxiella needs 2 positive cultures like other typical organisms' -> wrong; for Coxiella a single positive culture or phase I IgG ≥1:800 alone is major — with farm-animal exposure, send serology rather than waiting on cultures.
8. Bartonella serology = major
Bartonella (B. henselae from cats, B. quintana from homelessness/body lice) is culture-negative and diagnosed serologically; high-titer IgG (≥1:800) or positive PCR serves as a major microbiologic criterion under the modified/ISCVID Duke criteria.
⚠️ 'Negative blood cultures rule out Bartonella IE' -> wrong; Bartonella is culture-negative, so diagnosis relies on serology/PCR — with cat exposure (henselae) or homelessness/lice (quintana), send serology, not just repeat cultures.
9. Echo vegetation = major imaging
MAJOR imaging criterion: echo evidence of endocardial involvement — VEGETATION, abscess, pseudoaneurysm, intracardiac fistula, valve perforation, new prosthetic dehiscence, OR new valvular regurgitation. The 2023 Duke-ISCVID update also accepts abnormal periprosthetic PET/CT activity (beyond ~3 months post-op) and paravalvular CT defects as major imaging.
⚠️ 'Worsening of a pre-existing murmur counts as the major imaging criterion' -> wrong; only NEW regurgitation on echo or a structural lesion qualifies — an old murmur getting louder does not.
10. TEE > TTE (gold-star screen)
Start with TTE — fast, noninvasive, but sensitivity only ~50–70% for native valves (lower for prosthetics). If TTE is non-diagnostic with ongoing suspicion, or for PROSTHETIC valves, suspected abscess/perivalvular extension, or device leads, go to TEE (>90% sensitivity, the test of choice). Repeat TEE in 3–7 days if initially negative but suspicion persists.
⚠️ 'A negative TTE rules out endocarditis' -> wrong; a normal TTE does NOT exclude IE — escalate to TEE and repeat it if still suspicious, and go straight to TEE up front for prosthetic valves or suspected abscess.
11. Predisposition (minor)
MINOR criterion #1 — Predisposition: a predisposing cardiac condition (prosthetic valve, prior IE, congenital heart disease, significant regurgitant/stenotic lesions, HCM) OR injection drug use. This is the structural/behavioral substrate that lets organisms seed the endocardium.
⚠️ 'IV drug use is a vascular phenomenon' -> wrong; IVDU and predisposing cardiac conditions are the PREDISPOSITION minor (substrate) — don't double-count it as a vascular phenomenon (which covers embolic/immune sequelae).
12. Fever ≥38°C (minor)
MINOR criterion #2 — Fever: temperature ≥38.0°C (100.4°F), present in ~90% of IE and the easiest minor to satisfy, but nonspecific on its own. Its absence does not exclude IE.
⚠️ 'Absence of fever excludes IE' -> wrong; subacute IE (viridans strep) and elderly/immunosuppressed or recently-antibiotic-treated patients may be afebrile — fever counts only when present, but its absence never rules IE out.
13. Vascular vs immunologic hands/eye
Two distinct minors. VASCULAR (embolic): arterial emboli, septic pulmonary infarcts, mycotic aneurysm, intracranial/conjunctival hemorrhage, JANEWAY lesions (painless macules on palms/soles). IMMUNOLOGIC: glomerulonephritis, OSLER nodes (painful finger/toe pulp), ROTH spots (retinal hemorrhages, pale centers), rheumatoid factor.
⚠️ 'Janeway lesions and Osler nodes are both immunologic' -> wrong; JANEWAY = painless, VASCULAR (septic emboli), palms/soles, while OSLER = painful, IMMUNOLOGIC, finger/toe pulp — 'Osler = Ouch (immune); Janeway = painless (vascular).'
14. Single culture / not-major micro (minor)
MINOR criterion #5 — Microbiologic evidence not meeting a major: e.g., a single positive blood culture (excluding common skin contaminants and the Coxiella exception), or serologic evidence of active infection with an IE-consistent organism that doesn't reach the major threshold.
⚠️ 'A single positive culture for coagulase-negative staph is automatically a criterion' -> wrong; common skin contaminants (CoNS, diphtheroids) in a single set are usually dismissed as contamination — a single positive counts as minor only when the organism is plausibly causative.
