⚠️ Board traps 15
  1. Chemo weapon rack — WRONG to use the same regimen for every B-cell lymphoma. The discriminator: match intensity to biology — indolent (watch-and-wait or BR), aggressive (R-CHOP), very high-grade Burkitt/double-hit (DA-EPOCH-R, never plain R-CHOP) — and remember T-cell lymphomas are CD20-NEGATIVE, so the 'R' is omitted entirely.
  2. R-CHOP (top rack) — WRONG to give R-CHOP to Burkitt or double-hit lymphoma. The discriminator: R-CHOP is INADEQUATE for the highest-grade tumors (Burkitt, high-grade B-cell with MYC+BCL2 rearrangements) — they need dose-intensive, CNS-penetrating regimens (DA-EPOCH-R, hyper-CVAD, CODOX-M/IVAC); using R-CHOP for Burkitt is a classic exam misstep that undertreats a curable disease.
  3. Indolent / BR — WRONG to rush to treat asymptomatic low-burden follicular lymphoma. The discriminator: follicular lymphoma is indolent and INCURABLE, and early treatment of asymptomatic low-burden disease does not improve survival (GELF criteria define when to treat) — so 'watch and wait' is the correct answer unless there is bulky disease, cytopenias, symptoms, or organ compromise.
  4. DA-EPOCH-R (middle) — WRONG to omit tumor-lysis and CNS prophylaxis when escalating to DA-EPOCH-R for Burkitt/high-grade disease. The discriminator: the same explosive proliferation that demands DA-EPOCH-R also creates the highest TLS risk and CNS-relapse risk — so rasburicase (not just allopurinol) for high-risk TLS plus intrathecal methotrexate/cytarabine are part of the package, not optional add-ons.
  5. Double-hit — WRONG to treat double-hit lymphoma as ordinary DLBCL with R-CHOP. The discriminator: confirm REARRANGEMENTS by FISH (genetic, not IHC) — double-hit requires DA-EPOCH-R-type intensification and CNS prophylaxis, whereas a mere double-expressor (protein by IHC) gets standard R-CHOP; conflating expressor with hit is a frequent trap.
  6. Legacy / R-CVP (bottom) — WRONG to substitute R-CVP for R-CHOP in a fit DLBCL patient just to avoid toxicity. The discriminator: the ANTHRACYCLINE (doxorubicin) is the curative backbone in aggressive lymphoma — omitting it (R-CVP) sacrifices cure rates, so reserve R-CVP for genuinely anthracycline-ineligible patients (poor cardiac function); fit DLBCL patients should get full R-CHOP with cardiac monitoring.
  7. Rituximab mechanism — WRONG to start rituximab without hepatitis B serologies. The discriminator: rituximab-induced B-cell depletion can REACTIVATE latent HBV (even in anti-HBc-positive, HBsAg-negative 'resolved' infection) leading to fulminant hepatitis — so HBsAg AND anti-HBc must be checked and prophylaxis started before the first dose; skipping this is a classic patient-safety exam trap.
  8. CD20 target — WRONG to expect rituximab/anti-CD20 to work in a T-cell lymphoma or a CD20-negative tumor. The discriminator: CD20 is a B-CELL marker — T-cell lymphomas (PTCL, ALCL, ATLL, mycosis fungoides) are CD20-negative, and even some B-cell tumors lose CD20 after prior rituximab; verify CD20 expression by flow/IHC before assuming the antibody will help.
  9. IT MTX / CNS prophylaxis — WRONG to give standard intravenous methotrexate doses and expect CNS coverage, or to forget leucovorin rescue. The discriminator: only HIGH-DOSE (≥3 g/m²) methotrexate penetrates the CNS, and it mandates leucovorin rescue + urinary alkalinization + drug-level monitoring (and avoiding interacting drugs like NSAIDs, PPIs, trimethoprim) to prevent fatal toxicity; intrathecal delivery is the alternative route.
  10. CNS-IPI rules board — WRONG to give CNS prophylaxis to every DLBCL patient or to omit it based on score alone in a high-risk SITE. The discriminator: routine CNS prophylaxis is reserved for HIGH CNS-IPI (4-6) and specific high-risk anatomic sites (testicular, breast, epidural) — over-prophylaxis exposes low-risk patients to methotrexate toxicity, while ignoring testicular involvement (which also needs scrotal radiation) undertreats a sanctuary site.
  11. CAR-T launch bay CD19 — WRONG to treat CAR-T cytokine release syndrome the same as ICANS. The discriminator: CRS (IL-6-driven fever/hypotension) responds to TOCILIZUMAB (anti-IL-6R) ± steroids, whereas ICANS (neurotoxicity — aphasia, encephalopathy, seizures) does NOT respond well to tocilizumab and is managed with CORTICOSTEROIDS — and tocilizumab can paradoxically worsen ICANS by raising CNS IL-6; knowing which drug for which syndrome is high-yield.
  12. CAR-T bays/lines — WRONG to default every relapsed DLBCL to salvage chemo + autologous transplant. The discriminator: for PRIMARY-REFRACTORY or EARLY-relapse (≤12 months) DLBCL, CAR-T is now preferred second-line (ZUMA-7/TRANSFORM), whereas autologous transplant after salvage chemo remains appropriate for LATE relapses that are still chemosensitive — timing of relapse decides the pathway.
  13. Bispecifics (CD20xCD3) — WRONG to assume bispecifics carry the same logistics as CAR-T. The discriminator: bispecific antibodies are OFF-THE-SHELF (immediately available, given as a drug with step-up dosing), whereas CAR-T requires apheresis, weeks of manufacturing, and lymphodepletion — making bispecifics an option for patients who cannot wait for or are ineligible for CAR-T, though both share CRS risk.
  14. ADC concept — WRONG to assume an ADC's toxicity comes from the antibody. The discriminator: ADC toxicity tracks the CYTOTOXIC PAYLOAD — polatuzumab's MMAE causes peripheral NEUROPATHY (like vincristine/brentuximab), so it's combined with R-CHP minus vincristine to avoid stacking neurotoxicity; recognizing the payload class predicts the side-effect profile.
  15. Brentuximab (BRENT) — WRONG to add brentuximab on top of a full vincristine-containing regimen, or to give it with bleomycin. The discriminator: brentuximab's MMAE payload causes neuropathy, so in PTCL it REPLACES vincristine (CHOP→CHP), and in Hodgkin frontline it replaces bleomycin (ABVD→AVD) to avoid additive lung toxicity — matching the ADC to the regimen it modifies is the high-yield point.
📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Chemo weapon rack
The unifying principle of B-cell NHL therapy is immunochemotherapy: an anti-CD20 antibody (RITUXIMAB, the 'R') added to a cytotoxic backbone, with regimen INTENSITY scaling to tumor aggressiveness. Aggressive B-cell disease (DLBCL) gets R-CHOP; indolent disease (follicular, marginal zone) gets gentler bendamustine-rituximab or watchful waiting; very high-grade disease (Burkitt, double-hit) gets dose-intensive DA-EPOCH-R or hyper-CVAD with CNS coverage. Across the board, screen HEPATITIS B before rituximab and give tumor-lysis prophylaxis for bulky/high-grade tumors.
⚠️ WRONG to use the same regimen for every B-cell lymphoma. The discriminator: match intensity to biology — indolent (watch-and-wait or BR), aggressive (R-CHOP), very high-grade Burkitt/double-hit (DA-EPOCH-R, never plain R-CHOP) — and remember T-cell lymphomas are CD20-NEGATIVE, so the 'R' is omitted entirely.
2. R-CHOP (top rack)
R-CHOP = Rituximab + Cyclophosphamide + Hydroxydaunorubicin (DOXORUBICIN) + Oncovin (VINCRISTINE) + Prednisone, given every 21 days for ~6 cycles; it is the standard frontline for DLBCL and most aggressive B-cell NHL and CURES ~60% of DLBCL. Key toxicities map to each drug: doxorubicin → CARDIOTOXICITY (dose-dependent; check baseline ejection fraction), vincristine → peripheral NEUROPATHY and constipation, cyclophosphamide → hemorrhagic cystitis/myelosuppression, prednisone → hyperglycemia/mood. The newer pola-R-CHP (polatuzumab replacing vincristine) modestly improved outcomes in POLARIX.
⚠️ WRONG to give R-CHOP to Burkitt or double-hit lymphoma. The discriminator: R-CHOP is INADEQUATE for the highest-grade tumors (Burkitt, high-grade B-cell with MYC+BCL2 rearrangements) — they need dose-intensive, CNS-penetrating regimens (DA-EPOCH-R, hyper-CVAD, CODOX-M/IVAC); using R-CHOP for Burkitt is a classic exam misstep that undertreats a curable disease.
3. Indolent / BR
Bendamustine + Rituximab (BR) is a standard frontline for symptomatic INDOLENT B-cell NHL — follicular, marginal-zone, and (in older/less-fit patients) mantle cell — and was non-inferior/better tolerated than R-CHOP in the StiL and BRIGHT trials. Bendamustine is a hybrid alkylator/purine-analog with notable LYMPHOPENIA and infection/opportunistic-infection risk (PJP, herpesvirus reactivation), so consider antimicrobial prophylaxis. Asymptomatic, low-tumor-burden follicular lymphoma is often just OBSERVED ('watch and wait') rather than treated.
⚠️ WRONG to rush to treat asymptomatic low-burden follicular lymphoma. The discriminator: follicular lymphoma is indolent and INCURABLE, and early treatment of asymptomatic low-burden disease does not improve survival (GELF criteria define when to treat) — so 'watch and wait' is the correct answer unless there is bulky disease, cytopenias, symptoms, or organ compromise.
4. DA-EPOCH-R (middle)
Dose-Adjusted EPOCH-R (Etoposide, Prednisone, Oncovin/vincristine, Cyclophosphamide, Hydroxydaunorubicin/doxorubicin + Rituximab) uses continuous-infusion dosing that is escalated by nadir counts; it is preferred for BURKITT lymphoma, HIGH-GRADE/DOUBLE-HIT B-cell lymphoma, and primary mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma. It delivers more dose intensity and better CNS coverage than R-CHOP. Because Burkitt's proliferation is near 100%, aggressive TUMOR-LYSIS prophylaxis (hydration, rasburicase/allopurinol) and intrathecal CNS prophylaxis are mandatory.
⚠️ WRONG to omit tumor-lysis and CNS prophylaxis when escalating to DA-EPOCH-R for Burkitt/high-grade disease. The discriminator: the same explosive proliferation that demands DA-EPOCH-R also creates the highest TLS risk and CNS-relapse risk — so rasburicase (not just allopurinol) for high-risk TLS plus intrathecal methotrexate/cytarabine are part of the package, not optional add-ons.
5. Double-hit
'Double-hit' (high-grade B-cell lymphoma with MYC + BCL2 and/or BCL6 REARRANGEMENTS on FISH) is biologically near-Burkitt in aggressiveness and behaves poorly with standard R-CHOP, so it is treated like Burkitt with intensive therapy (DA-EPOCH-R) plus CNS prophylaxis. It is distinct from a 'double-EXPRESSOR' lymphoma (MYC + BCL2 overexpressed by IHC without rearrangement), which is prognostically worse but still treated with R-CHOP. Every aggressive GCB-type DLBCL with high Ki-67 should be sent for MYC/BCL2/BCL6 FISH.
⚠️ WRONG to treat double-hit lymphoma as ordinary DLBCL with R-CHOP. The discriminator: confirm REARRANGEMENTS by FISH (genetic, not IHC) — double-hit requires DA-EPOCH-R-type intensification and CNS prophylaxis, whereas a mere double-expressor (protein by IHC) gets standard R-CHOP; conflating expressor with hit is a frequent trap.
6. Legacy / R-CVP (bottom)
R-CVP = Rituximab + Cyclophosphamide + Vincristine + Prednisone — essentially R-CHOP WITHOUT the anthracycline (no doxorubicin) — used as a gentler regimen for FRAIL/elderly patients or low-grade disease where cardiotoxicity must be avoided (e.g., reduced ejection fraction, prior anthracycline exposure). Dropping doxorubicin spares the heart but lowers efficacy in aggressive disease, so R-CVP is not the choice for fit DLBCL patients.
⚠️ WRONG to substitute R-CVP for R-CHOP in a fit DLBCL patient just to avoid toxicity. The discriminator: the ANTHRACYCLINE (doxorubicin) is the curative backbone in aggressive lymphoma — omitting it (R-CVP) sacrifices cure rates, so reserve R-CVP for genuinely anthracycline-ineligible patients (poor cardiac function); fit DLBCL patients should get full R-CHOP with cardiac monitoring.
7. Rituximab mechanism
Rituximab is a chimeric anti-CD20 IgG1 monoclonal antibody that depletes B-cells via four mechanisms: antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC), complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC), antibody-dependent phagocytosis, and direct apoptosis signaling. Because CD20 is expressed on most B-cell NHLs but NOT on plasma cells or stem cells, normal B-cell recovery eventually occurs. Mandatory pre-treatment step: screen HEPATITIS B (HBsAg + anti-HBc) and give antiviral prophylaxis (entecavir/tenofovir) for positives, because rituximab can cause fatal HBV REACTIVATION; also a rare risk of PML (JC virus).
⚠️ WRONG to start rituximab without hepatitis B serologies. The discriminator: rituximab-induced B-cell depletion can REACTIVATE latent HBV (even in anti-HBc-positive, HBsAg-negative 'resolved' infection) leading to fulminant hepatitis — so HBsAg AND anti-HBc must be checked and prophylaxis started before the first dose; skipping this is a classic patient-safety exam trap.
8. CD20 target
CD20 is a B-lineage-restricted surface phosphoprotein expressed from the pre-B stage through mature B-cells but lost on plasma cells and absent on stem cells — making it the canonical immunotherapy target in B-cell NHL. OBINUTUZUMAB is a glycoengineered, type-II humanized anti-CD20 with enhanced ADCC and direct cell death, used in follicular lymphoma and CLL. CD20-targeting is why T-cell lymphomas (CD20-negative) get NO benefit from rituximab.
⚠️ WRONG to expect rituximab/anti-CD20 to work in a T-cell lymphoma or a CD20-negative tumor. The discriminator: CD20 is a B-CELL marker — T-cell lymphomas (PTCL, ALCL, ATLL, mycosis fungoides) are CD20-negative, and even some B-cell tumors lose CD20 after prior rituximab; verify CD20 expression by flow/IHC before assuming the antibody will help.
9. IT MTX / CNS prophylaxis
CNS prophylaxis aims to prevent leptomeningeal/parenchymal relapse in lymphomas at high CNS risk, delivered as INTRATHECAL methotrexate/cytarabine and/or HIGH-DOSE systemic methotrexate (which crosses the blood-brain barrier). It is indicated for Burkitt, lymphoblastic lymphoma, high-grade/double-hit lymphoma, testicular DLBCL, and DLBCL with high CNS-IPI or involvement of high-risk sites (e.g., paranasal sinus, epidural, breast, bone marrow). High-dose methotrexate requires leucovorin rescue, urine alkalinization, and methotrexate-level monitoring.
⚠️ WRONG to give standard intravenous methotrexate doses and expect CNS coverage, or to forget leucovorin rescue. The discriminator: only HIGH-DOSE (≥3 g/m²) methotrexate penetrates the CNS, and it mandates leucovorin rescue + urinary alkalinization + drug-level monitoring (and avoiding interacting drugs like NSAIDs, PPIs, trimethoprim) to prevent fatal toxicity; intrathecal delivery is the alternative route.
10. CNS-IPI rules board
The CNS-IPI is a 6-point risk score for CNS relapse in DLBCL combining the 5 IPI factors (age >60, elevated LDH, ECOG performance status >1, stage III/IV, >1 extranodal site) PLUS adrenal/KIDNEY involvement. Scores stratify low (0-1, ~0.6%), intermediate (2-3, ~3.4%), and high (4-6, ~10%) CNS-relapse risk, identifying which DLBCL patients warrant CNS-directed prophylaxis. Certain anatomic sites (testis, breast, epidural, paranasal sinus) independently raise risk regardless of score.
⚠️ WRONG to give CNS prophylaxis to every DLBCL patient or to omit it based on score alone in a high-risk SITE. The discriminator: routine CNS prophylaxis is reserved for HIGH CNS-IPI (4-6) and specific high-risk anatomic sites (testicular, breast, epidural) — over-prophylaxis exposes low-risk patients to methotrexate toxicity, while ignoring testicular involvement (which also needs scrotal radiation) undertreats a sanctuary site.
11. CAR-T launch bay CD19
Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy genetically engineers the patient's own T-cells to express an anti-CD19 receptor; approved products for relapsed/refractory aggressive B-cell lymphoma are axicabtagene ciloleucel (axi-cel), tisagenlecleucel (tisa-cel), and lisocabtagene maraleucel (liso-cel); BREXUcabtagene autoleucel (brexu-cel) is for relapsed mantle cell lymphoma. Signature toxicities are CYTOKINE RELEASE SYNDROME (fever/hypotension/hypoxia, treated with tocilizumab ± steroids) and ICANS (immune-effector-cell-associated neurotoxicity, treated with steroids).
⚠️ WRONG to treat CAR-T cytokine release syndrome the same as ICANS. The discriminator: CRS (IL-6-driven fever/hypotension) responds to TOCILIZUMAB (anti-IL-6R) ± steroids, whereas ICANS (neurotoxicity — aphasia, encephalopathy, seizures) does NOT respond well to tocilizumab and is managed with CORTICOSTEROIDS — and tocilizumab can paradoxically worsen ICANS by raising CNS IL-6; knowing which drug for which syndrome is high-yield.
12. CAR-T bays/lines
CD19 CAR-T therapy has moved EARLIER in DLBCL: based on ZUMA-7 (axi-cel) and TRANSFORM (liso-cel), it is now SECOND-LINE for primary-refractory or early-relapsing (within 12 months) DLBCL, outperforming the old standard of salvage chemo + autologous stem-cell transplant. Patients relapsing LATER (>12 months) may still go to salvage chemo/autoSCT first. In mantle cell lymphoma, brexu-cel CAR-T is used after BTK-inhibitor failure.
⚠️ WRONG to default every relapsed DLBCL to salvage chemo + autologous transplant. The discriminator: for PRIMARY-REFRACTORY or EARLY-relapse (≤12 months) DLBCL, CAR-T is now preferred second-line (ZUMA-7/TRANSFORM), whereas autologous transplant after salvage chemo remains appropriate for LATE relapses that are still chemosensitive — timing of relapse decides the pathway.
13. Bispecifics (CD20xCD3)
Bispecific T-cell engagers (e.g., GLOFITAMAB, EPCORITAMAB, mosunetuzumab) are antibodies with one arm binding tumor CD20 and the other binding CD3 on T-cells, physically bridging T-cells to the tumor to trigger killing. They are 'OFF-THE-SHELF' (no manufacturing lag, unlike CAR-T) and used in relapsed/refractory follicular and large B-cell lymphoma. Like CAR-T they cause CYTOKINE RELEASE SYNDROME, mitigated by step-up dosing and prophylaxis; mosunetuzumab is approved for relapsed follicular lymphoma.
⚠️ WRONG to assume bispecifics carry the same logistics as CAR-T. The discriminator: bispecific antibodies are OFF-THE-SHELF (immediately available, given as a drug with step-up dosing), whereas CAR-T requires apheresis, weeks of manufacturing, and lymphodepletion — making bispecifics an option for patients who cannot wait for or are ineligible for CAR-T, though both share CRS risk.
14. ADC concept
Antibody-drug conjugates couple a tumor-antigen-targeting antibody to a cytotoxic payload, delivering chemo selectively to lymphoma cells. POLATUZUMAB VEDOTIN targets CD79b (with MMAE payload) and is used in pola-R-CHP frontline (POLARIX) and in relapsed DLBCL; LONCASTUXIMAB tesirine targets CD19 (with a PBD-dimer payload) for relapsed/refractory DLBCL. ADCs let a single B-cell express several druggable antigens (CD19, CD20, CD79b, CD30), enabling sequential targeting.
⚠️ WRONG to assume an ADC's toxicity comes from the antibody. The discriminator: ADC toxicity tracks the CYTOTOXIC PAYLOAD — polatuzumab's MMAE causes peripheral NEUROPATHY (like vincristine/brentuximab), so it's combined with R-CHP minus vincristine to avoid stacking neurotoxicity; recognizing the payload class predicts the side-effect profile.
15. Brentuximab (BRENT)
Brentuximab vedotin is an anti-CD30 antibody-drug conjugate carrying the antimitotic MMAE payload; it targets CD30+ tumors — frontline (with CHP) for CD30+ peripheral T-cell lymphomas including ALCL (ECHELON-2), and used in Hodgkin lymphoma (with AVD frontline per ECHELON-1, and in relapse). Its hallmark dose-limiting toxicity is peripheral SENSORY NEUROPATHY from the MMAE; combining it with bleomycin is avoided due to pulmonary toxicity.
⚠️ WRONG to add brentuximab on top of a full vincristine-containing regimen, or to give it with bleomycin. The discriminator: brentuximab's MMAE payload causes neuropathy, so in PTCL it REPLACES vincristine (CHOP→CHP), and in Hodgkin frontline it replaces bleomycin (ABVD→AVD) to avoid additive lung toxicity — matching the ADC to the regimen it modifies is the high-yield point.
