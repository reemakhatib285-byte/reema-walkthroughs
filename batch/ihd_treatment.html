📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Beta-blocker first-line
Beta-blockers (metoprolol succinate 25–200 mg/day, atenolol, bisoprolol, carvedilol) are FIRST-LINE antianginal therapy: by blocking beta-1 receptors they lower heart rate and contractility, reducing myocardial oxygen demand (↓MVO2), and the slower HR lengthens diastole to improve coronary perfusion. Titrate to a resting HR of ~55–60 bpm. They are the only antianginal class with proven mortality benefit post-MI and in HFrEF, so they are preferred when a comorbidity (prior MI, low EF) coexists.
⚠️ WRONG: a calcium channel blocker or nitrate is the first-line antianginal of choice in stable angina. Discriminator: beta-blockers are first-line (and uniquely lower mortality post-MI/in HFrEF); CCBs and nitrates are add-ons or alternatives when beta-blockers are contraindicated or insufficient.
2. BB abrupt stop → rebound
Never stop beta-blockers abruptly in IHD: chronic blockade upregulates beta-receptors, so sudden withdrawal causes rebound tachycardia, hypertension, and ↑MVO2 that can precipitate angina, MI, or arrhythmia. Taper gradually over ~1–2 weeks. This rebound phenomenon is also why beta-blockers should generally be continued perioperatively rather than discontinued.
⚠️ WRONG: beta-blockers can be stopped abruptly without consequence (e.g., before surgery). Discriminator: abrupt withdrawal triggers rebound tachycardia/ischemia and can precipitate MI — taper over 1–2 weeks; this withdrawal hazard is a favorite exam point.
3. Long-acting nitrate
Long-acting nitrates (isosorbide mononitrate, isosorbide dinitrate, transdermal nitroglycerin patch) are add-on antianginals. Nitrates donate NO → venodilation that reduces preload and ventricular wall stress (lowering MVO2), with some epicardial coronary dilation. They relieve symptoms but have NO mortality benefit. Add when a beta-blocker alone is insufficient or as initial therapy if beta-blockers are contraindicated.
⚠️ WRONG: long-acting nitrates reduce mortality in stable IHD. Discriminator: nitrates are purely symptomatic (no survival benefit) — only beta-blockers, and revascularization in select anatomy, affect mortality; nitrates are for angina relief.
4. Nitrate-free interval
Continuous nitrate exposure causes tolerance (tachyphylaxis) within ~24h via depleted sulfhydryl groups / oxidative stress, abolishing efficacy. Prevent it by building in a daily nitrate-free interval of 10–14 hours (e.g., remove the patch overnight, dose isosorbide mononitrate once daily / dinitrate in an asymmetric schedule). The nitrate-free window is usually scheduled at night when angina risk is lowest.
⚠️ WRONG: dosing long-acting nitrates around the clock for steady coverage is optimal. Discriminator: continuous dosing causes tolerance and loss of effect — you must include a daily 10–14h nitrate-free interval; round-the-clock nitrates defeat the therapy.
5. Sublingual NTG (acute)
Sublingual nitroglycerin (0.3–0.4 mg tablet or spray) is for ACUTE angina relief or prophylaxis just before exertion; onset is 1–3 min. Instruct: one dose, repeat every 5 min up to 3 doses; if pain persists after the first dose, call emergency services. Absolute contraindication: recent PDE5 inhibitor use (sildenafil/tadalafil/vardenafil) — the combination causes life-threatening hypotension.
⚠️ WRONG: sublingual nitroglycerin is safe to give a patient who recently took sildenafil for chest pain. Discriminator: nitrate + PDE5 inhibitor → profound, potentially fatal hypotension — always ask about sildenafil/tadalafil before any nitrate (sublingual or IV).
6. DHP CCB (amlodipine)
Dihydropyridine CCBs (amlodipine 5–10 mg, felodipine, long-acting nifedipine) are add-on antianginals that cause arterial/coronary vasodilation, reducing afterload and MVO2. Long-acting DHPs are preferred (smooth, sustained effect). They are first choice for vasospastic (Prinzmetal) angina and useful when beta-blockers are contraindicated or angina persists despite them.
⚠️ WRONG: dihydropyridine CCBs slow the heart rate and shouldn't be combined with beta-blockers. Discriminator: DHPs (amlodipine) have minimal AV-node effect — they don't slow HR (may cause reflex tachycardia) and combine SAFELY with beta-blockers; it's the NON-DHP CCBs (verapamil/diltiazem) that are dangerous with beta-blockers.
7. Amlodipine safe with BB
Long-acting DHP CCBs (amlodipine) combine SAFELY with beta-blockers because they have negligible effect on the SA/AV node — the beta-blocker conveniently blunts any reflex tachycardia from the DHP's vasodilation, making the pairing complementary. This is a common, effective two-drug antianginal regimen when monotherapy fails.
⚠️ WRONG: adding amlodipine to a beta-blocker risks heart block and bradycardia. Discriminator: DHP + beta-blocker is a safe, synergistic combination (the beta-blocker offsets reflex tachycardia) — the heart-block danger belongs to verapamil/diltiazem + beta-blocker, not amlodipine.
8. Avoid short-acting nifedipine
Immediate-release (short-acting) nifedipine causes rapid vasodilation and a surge of reflex sympathetic activation → tachycardia and ↑MVO2 that can WORSEN ischemia and precipitate MI; it increased mortality in trials. Always use long-acting DHP formulations in IHD. (Short-acting nifedipine is also no longer used for hypertensive urgency for the same reason.)
⚠️ WRONG: short-acting nifedipine is a good rapid-acting antianginal/antihypertensive. Discriminator: immediate-release nifedipine causes reflex tachycardia and can precipitate MI (harm, not benefit) — only long-acting DHPs are used in IHD.
9. Non-DHP CCB + BB danger
Non-dihydropyridine CCBs (verapamil, diltiazem) act on the SA/AV node to slow HR and depress contractility. Combined with a beta-blocker their effects are ADDITIVE → high risk of severe bradycardia, high-grade AV block, and decompensated heart failure. Avoid this combination; non-DHPs are also contraindicated in HFrEF because of their negative inotropy.
⚠️ WRONG: verapamil or diltiazem can be safely added to a beta-blocker for better rate control in angina. Discriminator: non-DHP CCB + beta-blocker → additive AV block/bradycardia/HF — this combination is dangerous; if a patient is on a beta-blocker and needs a CCB, use a DHP (amlodipine).
10. Verapamil = rebound if stopped
Non-DHP CCBs (verapamil/diltiazem) lower HR and contractility for rate control and are an alternative antianginal when beta-blockers are contraindicated (e.g., severe asthma) — but in HFrEF they worsen failure, and like beta-blockers their abrupt cessation can cause rebound ↑HR/↑MVO2 and ischemia. Verapamil also causes constipation and can raise digoxin levels.
⚠️ WRONG: non-DHP CCBs are safe and beneficial in patients with reduced-EF heart failure. Discriminator: verapamil/diltiazem are negative inotropes contraindicated in HFrEF (they worsen decompensation) — only DHPs (amlodipine/felodipine) are tolerated in HFrEF if a CCB is needed.
11. Ranolazine (add-on)
Ranolazine inhibits the late inward sodium current, reducing Na/Ca overload and improving diastolic relaxation/coronary flow — an antianginal that does NOT lower heart rate or blood pressure. This makes it ideal as add-on therapy when angina persists but HR/BP are already low and limit beta-blockers, nitrates, or CCBs. It has no proven mortality benefit.
⚠️ WRONG: ranolazine works by lowering heart rate and blood pressure like a beta-blocker. Discriminator: ranolazine's distinguishing feature is NO effect on HR or BP (it blocks the late Na current) — that's exactly why it's chosen when low HR/BP preclude other antianginals.
12. Ranolazine QT / interactions
Ranolazine is metabolized by CYP3A4, so strong CYP3A inhibitors (diltiazem, verapamil, ketoconazole, macrolides, grapefruit) raise its levels — and it prolongs the QT interval. Avoid with other QT-prolonging drugs and in significant hepatic impairment; reduce the dose with moderate CYP3A inhibitors. Despite QT prolongation it has not been shown to cause torsades clinically.
⚠️ WRONG: ranolazine is free of significant drug interactions and QT effects. Discriminator: ranolazine is a CYP3A substrate that prolongs QT — watch for interactions (verapamil/diltiazem, azoles, macrolides, grapefruit) and avoid stacking QT-prolonging drugs.
13. Ivabradine (if EF normal, sinus)
Ivabradine selectively inhibits the SA-node 'funny' (If) current, lowering heart rate WITHOUT affecting contractility or blood pressure. It is an add-on antianginal for patients in SINUS rhythm with HR still >70 despite a maximally tolerated beta-blocker, or when beta-blockers are not tolerated. Side effects: visual phosphenes (luminous brightness) and bradycardia. It has a role in chronic HFrEF (EF ≤35%, sinus, HR ≥70).
⚠️ WRONG: ivabradine works in atrial fibrillation and also boosts contractility/blood pressure. Discriminator: ivabradine only works in SINUS rhythm (it acts on the SA node — useless in AF) and purely slows HR with no inotropic or BP effect.
