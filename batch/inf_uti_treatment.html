📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Pregnancy-safe window
Pregnancy-SAFE agents: nitrofurantoin (avoid near term), amoxicillin/amoxicillin-clavulanate, cephalexin and other cephalosporins, and fosfomycin. Screen all pregnant women for asymptomatic bacteriuria and treat positives 4-7 days with a test-of-cure.
2. KERNIC crossed-out (avoid near term)
Near term / 3rd trimester AVOID sulfonamides (TMP-SMX) and nitrofurantoin: risk of neonatal kernicterus (sulfa displaces bilirubin) and hemolytic anemia (especially with G6PD deficiency). Throughout pregnancy AVOID fluoroquinolones (fetal cartilage) and TMP-SMX in the 1st trimester (folate antagonist -> neural tube defects).
⚠️ "TMP-SMX or nitrofurantoin near delivery" -> wrong; sulfa near term -> kernicterus, nitrofurantoin near term -> neonatal hemolysis — pick a cephalosporin or amoxicillin instead.
3. Nitrofurantoin 5d (1st-line, gold star)
Nitrofurantoin 100 mg BID x5 days is first-line for uncomplicated cystitis — it concentrates in URINE only, keeps excellent E. coli activity with low collateral resistance, and is generally pregnancy-safe (except near term).
⚠️ "nitrofurantoin for pyelonephritis/prostatitis" -> wrong; no tissue/parenchymal penetration. Also ineffective when CrCl <30 (weak 30-45) and avoid near term and in G6PD deficiency (hemolysis).
4. TMP-SMX 3d (1st-line if <20%)
TMP-SMX 1 DS tablet BID x3 days is first-line for uncomplicated cystitis WHERE local E. coli resistance is <20%. It penetrates tissue, so it also serves in pyelonephritis (10-14 d) and prostatitis.
⚠️ "empiric TMP-SMX when resistance >20% or used in last 3 months" -> wrong; pick another agent. Avoid 1st trimester (NTDs) and near term (kernicterus); watch hyperkalemia and warfarin interaction.
5. Fosfomycin 3g (gold-medal single dose)
Fosfomycin is a single 3 g oral dose first-line cystitis option — convenient and active against many resistant organisms including ESBL-producing E. coli and VRE. It concentrates in urine.
⚠️ "fosfomycin for febrile/upper-tract UTI" -> wrong; single-dose fosfomycin is CYSTITIS only and does not reach tissue levels for pyelonephritis or prostatitis.
6. Pivmecillinam (1st-line cystitis)
Pivmecillinam (a beta-lactam prodrug of mecillinam) is a first-line cystitis agent in Europe/Israel with low resistance and a good safety profile including pregnancy. Like the other cystitis drugs it treats lower-tract disease only.
⚠️ "pivmecillinam for pyelonephritis" -> wrong; reserve it for cystitis.
7. Oral beta-lactam (2nd-line, silver + red-X)
Oral beta-lactams (amoxicillin-clavulanate, cefpodoxime, cephalexin) are SECOND-line for cystitis — lower cure rates and more relapse than first-line agents, partly from incomplete eradication of vaginal/gut reservoirs. Use when first-line drugs can't be used.
⚠️ "plain amoxicillin/ampicillin empirically for cystitis" -> wrong; high E. coli resistance makes bare aminopenicillins a poor empiric choice.
8. Fluoroquinolone (red-X, reserved)
Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin) are RESERVED for pyelonephritis and complicated UTI/prostatitis where tissue penetration matters. FDA boxed warnings — tendinopathy/rupture, aortic aneurysm/dissection, irreversible peripheral neuropathy, CNS effects, QT prolongation, C. difficile — mean avoid when safer options exist.
⚠️ "fluoroquinolone first-line for simple cystitis" -> wrong; that is collateral damage. Also avoid in pregnancy and children (cartilage toxicity).
9. Fosfomycin red-X on mountain
The mountain = pyelonephritis (upper tract). Urine-only agents are explicitly OFF the table here: neither fosfomycin nor nitrofurantoin reach parenchymal levels. Upper-tract disease demands fluoroquinolones, tissue-penetrating beta-lactams, or TMP-SMX.
⚠️ "fosfomycin (or nitrofurantoin) for pyelonephritis" -> wrong; 'no tissue penetration' is the trap — choose a drug with renal parenchymal levels.
10. IV climb (inpatient pyelo)
Severe/inpatient pyelonephritis is treated IV: ceftriaxone or another broad beta-lactam (pip-tazo, cefepime, or a carbapenem if ESBL risk), plus an aminoglycoside for critically ill/resistant cases. De-escalate to oral once afebrile and culture-guided. Duration: pyelonephritis 5-7 d with a fluoroquinolone, 7 d for IV beta-lactam, 10-14 d for TMP-SMX/oral beta-lactam.
11. Vomiting / flank (admission criteria)
Admission criteria for pyelonephritis: inability to tolerate PO (vomiting), sepsis/instability, pregnancy, immunocompromise, suspected obstruction, or outpatient failure. No improvement within 48-72 h -> image with CT/ultrasound to exclude obstructing stone, perinephric abscess, or emphysematous change.
⚠️ "keep the same regimen past 72 h of fever" -> wrong; persistent fever on appropriate antibiotics means image for a complication (abscess/obstruction).
12. Methenamine (recurrent prophylaxis)
Recurrent UTI = >=2 in 6 months or >=3 in 12 months. Options: continuous low-dose prophylaxis (nitrofurantoin or TMP-SMX) for 6+ months, post-coital prophylaxis if intercourse-related, and patient-initiated self-treatment. Non-antibiotic: methenamine (urinary antiseptic), topical vaginal estrogen in postmenopausal women (restores lactobacilli/lowers pH), and behavioral measures.
⚠️ "cranberry / lifelong antibiotics first" -> wrong; evaluate for a correctable structural cause before long-term antibiotics, and cranberry has weak/inconsistent evidence.
13. Prostate forge (men)
UTI in a man is by definition complicated — always consider PROSTATITIS as the source and pick prostate-penetrating agents: fluoroquinolones or TMP-SMX. Acute bacterial prostatitis 2-4 weeks; chronic bacterial prostatitis 4-6 weeks because of poor penetration of the non-inflamed gland.
⚠️ "prostate massage in acute prostatitis" -> wrong; risks bacteremia. Don't use nitrofurantoin/fosfomycin (no prostate penetration), and a febrile UTI in a man warrants a prostatic/obstructive evaluation.
