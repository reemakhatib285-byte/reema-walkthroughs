📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Intubated ICU patient (ETT = #1 VAP risk)
VAP is pneumonia developing >=48 h after intubation (a subset of HAP, pneumonia >=48 h after admission). The endotracheal tube is the single biggest VAP risk: it holds the glottis open, bypasses upper-airway defenses, provides a biofilm surface, and lets secretions leak past the cuff. Risk is ~1-3% per ventilator-day, highest the first week.
⚠️ "early aspiration at intubation = VAP" -> wrong; pneumonia present before or within 48 h of intubation is not VAP.
2. Green biofilm + microaspiration
Bacteria coat the ETT lumen in a glycocalyx biofilm that shields them from immune cells and antibiotics and sheds emboli into the lung. Contaminated oropharyngeal/gastric secretions pool above the cuff and microaspirate past it — the principal VAP mechanism. The tube also damages tracheal mucosa, promoting colonization.
⚠️ "the cuff seals the airway" -> wrong; secretions track along channels in the cuff — only subglottic-suction ETTs drain the pool. Colonization is NOT infection: treat clinical VAP (new infiltrate + deterioration), not a positive aspirate alone.
3. Hand hygiene (GOLD STAR)
Hand hygiene (alcohol-based rub or soap-and-water) before/after every patient and equipment contact is the single most effective measure to prevent healthcare-associated transmission of MDR pathogens, and underpins every VAP bundle.
⚠️ "gloves/gowns/antibiotics" as the best measure to prevent nosocomial spread -> wrong; the answer is hand hygiene.
4. Head of bed >=30 (GOLD STAR)
Elevating the head of bed to 30-45 degrees (semi-recumbent) uses gravity to cut gastroesophageal reflux and microaspiration — a simple, free, evidence-based ventilator-bundle element.
⚠️ "lay the ventilated patient flat" -> wrong; supine positioning increases aspiration and VAP unless contraindicated.
5. Subglottic suction & sedation-hold shields
Specialized ETTs with a dorsal subglottic-suction port drain the pooled secretions above the cuff (use when ventilation expected >48-72 h). Daily sedation interruptions (spontaneous awakening trials) paired with spontaneous breathing trials shorten ventilation time — the most powerful VAP-prevention lever. NIV (CPAP/BiPAP) avoids the tube entirely.
⚠️ "keep the patient deeply sedated" -> wrong; continuous deep sedation prolongs intubation and increases VAP — use the lightest effective sedation with daily readiness checks.
6. Pseudomonas frog + MRSA skull (MDR)
Early VAP (<5-7 days, no MDR risk) is 'core' organisms (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, MSSA, sensitive Enterobacterales) treatable with narrower monotherapy. Late/high-risk VAP is dominated by MDR bugs: Pseudomonas, MRSA, Acinetobacter, resistant/ESBL Klebsiella. MDR risk factors: prior IV antibiotics within 90 days, >=5 days hospitalized, septic shock at onset, ARDS/dialysis before VAP, high local resistance.
⚠️ "onset-day alone decides coverage" -> wrong; prior antibiotics/other risk factors make MDR likely even before day 7 — risk factors trump the clock.
7. X'd shield — no reflexive broad coverage
With NO MDR risk, empiric VAP can be a SINGLE antipseudomonal agent covering MSSA (pip-tazo, cefepime, levofloxacin, imipenem, meropenem) — empiric MRSA and double gram-negative coverage are unnecessary. Prophylactic systemic antibiotics do NOT prevent VAP and only breed resistance.
⚠️ "vancomycin + double gram-negative coverage for all VAP" -> wrong; over-treating low-risk VAP selects resistance and adds toxicity (nephrotoxicity, C. difficile) — match breadth to risk.
8. 7-day hourglass + de-escalate
With MDR risk, start COMBINATION therapy: two antipseudomonal agents from different classes PLUS anti-MRSA (vancomycin or linezolid). Standard VAP duration is ~7 days, de-escalating to the narrowest effective agent once cultures return.
⚠️ "extend to 14-21 days" for an uncomplicated improving VAP, or failing to de-escalate at 48-72 h -> wrong; ~7 days suffices and non-de-escalation is a classic stewardship error.
9. Unconscious man aspirating (poor dentition)
Impaired consciousness/airway protection (alcohol, seizure, stroke, anesthesia, overdose, dysphagia) plus poor dentition (periodontal anaerobes: Peptostreptococcus, Prevotella, Fusobacterium, microaerophilic strep) is the classic aspiration setup — the alcoholic combines both. Witnessed aspiration of sterile gastric contents may be chemical pneumonitis (Mendelson) that resolves without antibiotics.
⚠️ "give antibiotics for every aspiration event" -> wrong; reserve them for a true bacterial infiltrate/abscess or aspiration of colonized/obstructed contents.
10. RT — right dependent segments
Aspiration goes to dependent segments: supine -> posterior segments of upper lobes and superior segments of lower lobes; upright -> basal lower lobes. The RIGHT lung is favored because the right mainstem bronchus is wider and more vertical. A lung abscess is a thick-walled cavity with an air-fluid level developing over ~1-2 weeks; clubbing signals chronicity.
⚠️ "apical upper-lobe cavitation is aspiration" -> wrong; non-dependent/apical posterior cavitation is classic TB, not aspiration.
11. Edentulous = no abscess (X'd skull)
Anaerobic lung abscess needs the anaerobic load in tooth-bearing gingiva, so a fully edentulous patient is the wrong host for a 'simple' anaerobic abscess. An edentulous patient who cavitates should prompt a search for an obstructing endobronchial cancer (post-obstructive infection), necrotizing pneumonia, TB, fungus, or septic emboli.
⚠️ "ordinary anaerobic aspiration" in an edentulous patient with a cavity -> wrong; rule out malignancy/bronchial obstruction with bronchoscopy + CT — a heavily tested discriminator.
12. Septic emboli heart
Multiple bilateral peripheral cavitary nodules point to SEPTIC PULMONARY EMBOLI, not aspiration. Sources: right-sided (tricuspid) endocarditis (IVDU, S. aureus) and Lemierre syndrome (Fusobacterium septic IJ thrombophlebitis after oropharyngeal infection).
⚠️ "simple aspiration" for multiple bilateral peripheral cavities -> wrong; aspiration gives a single cavity in a dependent segment — get blood cultures + echo for septic emboli/endocarditis.
13. Clinda & Amp-sulb knights
For aspiration/lung abscess use clindamycin OR a beta-lactam/beta-lactamase-inhibitor (ampicillin-sulbactam IV, amoxicillin-clavulanate PO) — preferred over plain penicillin because oral anaerobes (Prevotella, Fusobacterium) produce beta-lactamase. Moxifloxacin is the penicillin-allergy alternative (anaerobic activity). Abscesses need PROLONGED therapy (weeks, until radiographic resolution); >6 cm or non-response warrants drainage.
⚠️ "plain penicillin G/amoxicillin alone" for abscess -> wrong; anaerobic beta-lactamase inactivates it — use clindamycin or a BL/BLI combination.
14. X'd knight — metronidazole alone fails
Metronidazole has excellent anaerobic activity but NO activity against the microaerophilic/aerobic streptococci in the polymicrobial aspiration flora, so monotherapy fails (~50%). It must be paired with a streptococcal-covering agent (beta-lactam or clindamycin). Aspiration infections are polymicrobial and synergistic, so single-target therapy fails.
⚠️ "metronidazole alone" for a lung abscess -> wrong; it misses microaerophilic streptococci — a frequently tested wrong-drug choice.
