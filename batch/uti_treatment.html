📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. UTI Apothecary sign
Scene overview: antibiotic selection is driven by SITE (cystitis vs pyelonephritis vs prostatitis), HOST (pregnancy, men, recurrent), and local resistance. Core principle: lower-tract cystitis can use urine-concentrated agents (nitrofurantoin, fosfomycin) that don't penetrate tissue, whereas pyelonephritis/prostatitis need agents that achieve high PARENCHYMAL levels (fluoroquinolones, beta-lactams, TMP-SMX).
2. Nitro 5 days bottle
Nitrofurantoin (100 mg BID x5 days) is a first-line agent for uncomplicated cystitis — it concentrates in URINE only and retains excellent activity against E. coli with low collateral resistance. It is generally pregnancy-safe (except near term).
⚠️ Nitrofurantoin is WRONG for pyelonephritis or prostatitis (no tissue/parenchymal penetration) and is ineffective/contraindicated when CrCl <30 (and weak 30-45) because it can't concentrate in urine. Avoid near term (G6PD/neonatal hemolysis) and in known G6PD deficiency.
3. TMP-SMX 3 days
TMP-SMX (1 DS tablet BID x3 days) is first-line for uncomplicated cystitis WHERE local E. coli resistance is <20%. It penetrates tissue well, so it also serves in pyelonephritis (10-14 d) and prostatitis.
⚠️ Don't use TMP-SMX empirically if local resistance >20% or the patient used it for a UTI within the past 3 months. Avoid in the 1st trimester (folate antagonist → neural tube defects) and near term (kernicterus); also watch for hyperkalemia and interaction with warfarin.
4. Fosfomycin 3g single dose
Fosfomycin is a single 3 g oral dose first-line cystitis option — convenient and active against many resistant organisms including ESBL-producing E. coli and VRE. It concentrates in urine.
⚠️ Single-dose fosfomycin is for CYSTITIS only — it does NOT achieve tissue levels for pyelonephritis or prostatitis. Don't pick it for an upper-tract/febrile UTI.
5. Pivmecillinam 400
Pivmecillinam (a beta-lactam prodrug of mecillinam) is a first-line cystitis agent in Europe/Israel with low resistance and a good safety profile, including in pregnancy. Like the other cystitis-only agents, it is for lower-tract disease.
⚠️ Reserve pivmecillinam for cystitis — it is not used for pyelonephritis.
6. Beta-lactam PO less effective
Oral beta-lactams (amoxicillin-clavulanate, cefpodoxime, cephalexin) are SECOND-line for cystitis — they have lower cure rates and more relapse than the first-line agents, partly from incomplete eradication of vaginal/gut reservoirs. Use when first-line agents can't be used.
⚠️ Plain amoxicillin or ampicillin alone is a poor empiric choice (high E. coli resistance) — don't select it for empiric cystitis.
7. Fluoroquinolone FDA AVOID
Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin) are RESERVED for pyelonephritis and complicated UTI/prostatitis where their tissue penetration matters. The FDA boxed warnings — tendinopathy/rupture, aortic aneurysm/dissection, irreversible peripheral neuropathy, CNS effects, QT prolongation, and C. difficile — mean they should be avoided when safer options exist.
⚠️ Do NOT use a fluoroquinolone first-line for simple cystitis — that's collateral damage and the wrong board answer. Also avoid in pregnancy and children (cartilage toxicity).
8. Days clock / 7-14d
Duration by syndrome: uncomplicated cystitis 3-5 days; pyelonephritis 5-7 days with a fluoroquinolone, 7 days for an IV beta-lactam, or 10-14 days for TMP-SMX/oral beta-lactam. Acute prostatitis 2-4 weeks and chronic prostatitis 4-6 weeks because of poor prostate penetration. Asymptomatic bacteriuria in pregnancy is 4-7 days.
⚠️ Matching wrong duration to site is a classic distractor — a 3-day course is for cystitis, not pyelonephritis or prostatitis.
9. Inpatient IV beta-lactam
Severe or inpatient pyelonephritis is treated IV: ceftriaxone or another broad beta-lactam (piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, or a carbapenem if ESBL risk), with an aminoglycoside added for critically ill/resistant cases. De-escalate to oral once afebrile and culture-guided. Nitrofurantoin and fosfomycin are explicitly OFF the table here (no parenchymal penetration).
⚠️ Never pick nitrofurantoin or fosfomycin for inpatient/parenchymal pyelonephritis — 'no tissue penetration' is the trap. Choose an agent with renal tissue levels.
10. Outpatient Cipro / TMP-SMX
Outpatient (mild, non-pregnant, tolerating PO) pyelonephritis: oral fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin 7 d or levofloxacin 5-7 d) is preferred. If using an oral beta-lactam or TMP-SMX (14 d), give an initial long-acting IV dose (e.g., ceftriaxone 1 g or an aminoglycoside) because of higher local resistance.
⚠️ If local fluoroquinolone resistance exceeds ~10%, give that one-time IV long-acting dose first — don't rely on oral therapy alone empirically.
11. Pregnancy safe column
Pregnancy-SAFE: nitrofurantoin (avoid near term), amoxicillin/amoxicillin-clavulanate, cephalexin and other cephalosporins, and fosfomycin. AVOID fluoroquinolones (fetal cartilage damage) throughout and TMP-SMX in the 1st trimester (folate antagonist → neural tube defects).
⚠️ 'KERNIC' / 'NEAR TERM' warning: avoid sulfonamides (TMP-SMX) and nitrofurantoin in the 3rd trimester / near delivery — risk of neonatal kernicterus and hemolytic anemia (especially with G6PD deficiency).
12. Recurrent — daily/postcoital
Recurrent UTI = >=2 in 6 months or >=3 in 12 months. Options: continuous low-dose prophylaxis (e.g., nitrofurantoin or TMP-SMX) for 6+ months, post-coital prophylaxis if intercourse-related, and patient-initiated self-treatment. Non-antibiotic measures: methenamine (urinary antiseptic), topical vaginal estrogen in postmenopausal women (restores lactobacilli/lowers pH), and behavioral measures (hydration, post-coital voiding).
⚠️ First evaluate for a correctable cause/structural abnormality before committing to long-term antibiotics; cranberry products have weak/inconsistent evidence and aren't a reliable board answer.
13. Men / Prostate anvil
UTI in a man is by definition complicated — always consider PROSTATITIS as the source. Choose agents that penetrate the prostate: fluoroquinolones or TMP-SMX. Acute bacterial prostatitis is treated 2-4 weeks; chronic bacterial prostatitis 4-6 weeks because of poor antibiotic penetration of the non-inflamed gland.
⚠️ In acute prostatitis AVOID vigorous prostate massage (risk of bacteremia). Don't use nitrofurantoin/fosfomycin for prostatitis (no prostate penetration), and remember a febrile UTI in a man warrants evaluation for prostatic/obstructive source.
14. Vomiting / flank pain
Admission criteria for pyelonephritis: inability to tolerate PO (vomiting), sepsis/hemodynamic instability, pregnancy, immunocompromise, suspected obstruction, or failure of outpatient therapy. If a patient does not improve within 48-72 h, image with CT or ultrasound to exclude an obstructing stone, perinephric abscess, or emphysematous change.
⚠️ Persistent fever past 72 h on appropriate antibiotics means image for a complication (abscess/obstruction) — don't just keep waiting on the same regimen.
