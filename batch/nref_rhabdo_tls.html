📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. IV fluids — 10 L/day possible
Aggressive isotonic crystalloid is the cornerstone of rhabdomyolysis and TLS prevention. Volumes up to ~10 L/day flush obstructing casts and dilute nephrotoxins. Start before/early in the insult — once oliguric ATN is established fluids help far less.
⚠️ Withholding fluids fearing overload — in rhabdo/TLS early aggressive volume is protective; the danger is under-resuscitation.
2. Target urine flow 200–300 mL/h
Titrate IV fluids to a brisk urine output of 200–300 mL/h. High tubular flow prevents myoglobin/uric acid precipitation and cast obstruction. Monitor flow continuously rather than giving fixed volumes.
⚠️ Aiming for normal UOP (>0.5 mL/kg/h) only — prophylaxis requires a supranormal 200–300 mL/h target.
3. Myoglobin lock & Tamm-Horsfall casts
Myoglobin complexes with Tamm-Horsfall protein in acidic urine to form obstructing tubular casts; free iron also generates oxidative tubular injury and vasoconstriction. This is the mechanism of pigment-induced ATN.
⚠️ Thinking myoglobin is filtered harmlessly — it is directly tubulotoxic and forms obstructing casts, especially in acidic urine.
4. Acidic urine worsens casts
Low urine pH promotes myoglobin cast formation and uric acid precipitation. This is the rationale offered for urinary alkalinization, though evidence beyond plain volume expansion is weak.
⚠️ Over-prioritizing alkalinization — saline volume loading is the proven intervention; bicarb is adjunctive at best.
5. NaHCO3 / alkaline urine
Sodium bicarbonate raises urine pH to reduce cast formation (rhabdo) and uric acid crystallization (TLS). Caution: alkalinization can precipitate calcium phosphate in TLS and worsen hypocalcemia — not routinely superior to saline alone.
⚠️ Routinely alkalinizing all TLS — it risks calcium-phosphate deposition; isotonic saline without bicarb is generally preferred.
6. Allopurinol blocks synthesis
Allopurinol inhibits xanthine oxidase, PREVENTING new uric acid formation. Use prophylactically in low/intermediate-risk TLS. It does not lower already-formed uric acid and can cause xanthine nephropathy.
⚠️ Expecting allopurinol to clear existing hyperuricemia — it only blocks synthesis; established high uric acid needs rasburicase.
7. Rasburicase converts to allantoin
Rasburicase (recombinant urate oxidase) enzymatically degrades existing uric acid to highly soluble allantoin, rapidly lowering levels. First-line for high-risk/established TLS with uric acid >8 mg/dL or AKI.
⚠️ Using rasburicase in G6PD deficiency — it causes severe hemolysis (and methemoglobinemia) and is contraindicated.
8. G6PD contraindication
Rasburicase is contraindicated in G6PD deficiency because the H2O2 byproduct triggers oxidative hemolysis and methemoglobinemia. Screen at-risk patients before administering.
⚠️ Giving rasburicase empirically in high-risk ethnicities without G6PD screening — can precipitate life-threatening hemolysis.
9. TLS: uric acid >15, ↑K ↑PO4 ↓Ca
Tumor lysis releases intracellular contents: hyperuricemia, hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, and secondary hypocalcemia. Calcium-phosphate and uric acid crystals obstruct tubules causing AKI. Highest risk in bulky high-turnover tumors (Burkitt, ALL).
⚠️ Treating the hypocalcemia aggressively with calcium — giving calcium in hyperphosphatemic TLS risks metastatic calcification; treat only if symptomatic.
10. Isotonic saline (not hypotonic)
Volume expansion uses isotonic saline to maintain high tubular flow and clear crystals. Hypotonic fluids are avoided. Diuretics/mannitol have NO proven role and may worsen obstruction.
⚠️ Adding loop diuretics or mannitol to 'force diuresis' — no benefit, and they can aggravate cast obstruction and volume issues.
11. 24–48h onset, peak 3–5 days, resolves ~1 wk
Pigment/crystal AKI typically begins 24–48h after the insult, peaks at days 3–5, and recovers over ~1 week with supportive care if tubular integrity is preserved. Timeline mirrors ATN recovery phases.
⚠️ Expecting immediate creatinine rise — the delay to 24–48h onset and 3–5 day peak is characteristic; early normal Cr does not exclude it.
12. Dialysis indications — AEIOU
Refractory cases need dialysis. AEIOU = Acidosis, Electrolytes (refractory hyperK), Ingestions/toxins, Overload (volume), Uremia. In TLS, dialysis clears phosphate/uric acid when medical therapy fails.
⚠️ Delaying dialysis for an 'acceptable' creatinine — the indications are functional (refractory K, acidosis, overload, uremia), not an absolute Cr number.
13. RCT: no survival benefit early vs late
Major trials (e.g., STARRT-AKI, AKIKI) show no survival benefit from early/prophylactic dialysis over a watchful strategy in AKI. Initiate dialysis for true indications rather than empirically.
⚠️ Starting dialysis early 'to be safe' — evidence shows no mortality benefit; reserve it for AEIOU indications.
