📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Skin color clue
PRIMARY AI (Addison, adrenal destroyed): cortisol falls → loss of negative feedback → pituitary ramps up POMC, which is cleaved into BOTH ACTH and alpha-MSH; MSH stimulates melanocytes → diffuse hyperpigmentation. SECONDARY AI (pituitary/hypothalamic, low ACTH): no excess MSH → pale skin, and aldosterone is preserved (RAAS-driven) so NO hyperkalemia and minimal salt-wasting.
⚠️ Hyperpigmentation = primary AI only (high ACTH/MSH). A pale patient with low cortisol AND low/normal ACTH = secondary, and they will NOT be hyperkalemic.
2. Chronic AI clinic
Chronic primary AI is an insidious, nonspecific picture: tired, thin, tan, hypotensive, hyperkalemic. Etiologies — in the developed world #1 is AUTOIMMUNE adrenalitis (21-hydroxylase autoantibodies, often part of APS-1/APS-2); WORLDWIDE the leading cause is TB. Other causes: metastases, adrenoleukodystrophy, drugs (ketoconazole, etomidate).
⚠️ Autoimmune is the #1 cause in the West; TB is #1 worldwide — board picks the answer to fit the geographic/clinical clue.
3. Fatigue & weakness
Nonspecific chronic symptoms: fatigue, weakness, weight loss/anorexia, myalgia/arthralgia, low-grade fever. Because these are vague, Addison is frequently missed until a stressor tips the patient into crisis.
4. Buccal mucosa pigment
ACTH is co-secreted with alpha-MSH (both from POMC); chronically high ACTH/MSH pigments sun-exposed skin, BUCCAL/gingival mucosa, knuckles/extensor surfaces, palmar creases, scars, and the areolae. Mucosal and palmar-crease pigment is the most specific sign because it is not explained by sun exposure.
⚠️ Hyperpigmentation of buccal mucosa/palmar creases is the classic exam clue for chronic primary (not secondary) AI.
5. Postural hypotension
Aldosterone deficiency → renal Na+ and water loss → volume depletion → orthostatic hypotension and SALT CRAVING; cortisol loss adds GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, anorexia, abdominal pain) and impaired vascular tone/pressor responsiveness.
6. Electrolyte panel
Chronic primary AI labs: HYPOnatremia (cortisol loss → impaired free-water excretion/SIADH-like + aldo loss), HYPERkalemia + non-anion-gap metabolic acidosis (aldosterone loss), and HYPOglycemia (cortisol is counter-regulatory). May see mild hypercalcemia and eosinophilia.
⚠️ Hyperkalemia points to PRIMARY AI (aldosterone loss). Secondary AI keeps aldosterone (RAAS-driven) so K+ is normal — hyperkalemia rules secondary OUT.
7. Women's androgen deficiency
The adrenal is the main source of androgens (DHEA/DHEA-S) in women; in primary AI women lose libido, develop loss of axillary/pubic hair and dry skin. Men are unaffected here because testes supply testosterone.
8. Crisis = shock + electrolytes
Adrenal (Addisonian) crisis = the lethal acute form: distributive/hypovolemic SHOCK + hyponatremia + hyperkalemia + hypoglycemia, often with fever and abdominal pain mimicking an acute abdomen. It is an endocrine emergency.
9. Crisis triggers
Precipitants: infection/sepsis, surgery/trauma/major stress, ABRUPT withdrawal of chronic exogenous steroids (suppressed HPA axis can't respond), pituitary apoplexy, and bilateral adrenal hemorrhage. The classic increased cortisol demand of any acute illness in an AI patient unmasks the deficiency.
⚠️ Abruptly stopping long-term glucocorticoids (or failing to stress-dose during illness/surgery) is the single most testable precipitant of crisis.
10. Waterhouse-Friderichsen
Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome = fulminant bilateral adrenal HEMORRHAGE causing acute primary AI, classically from Neisseria meningitidis sepsis (also Pseudomonas, pneumococcus, Haemophilus). Presents with shock, DIC and a petechial/purpuric (purpura fulminans) rash — treat sepsis AND give stress-dose steroids.
⚠️ A child/young adult with meningococcemia, purpura and refractory shock = Waterhouse-Friderichsen; the hypotension won't resolve until you give hydrocortisone.
11. Hypotension / shock
Hallmark = refractory HYPOTENSION/shock that responds poorly to fluids and vasopressors alone (cortisol is permissive for catecholamine vascular tone), plus abdominal pain, fever and confusion. Pressor-resistant shock should prompt empiric hydrocortisone.
⚠️ Vasopressor-refractory shock + suggestive history = give steroids; don't keep escalating pressors.
12. DO NOT WAIT FOR LABS
Treatment is empiric and IMMEDIATE — draw a cortisol/ACTH if convenient but do NOT delay therapy for results. Untreated crisis is rapidly fatal; the consequences of treating an equivocal case are negligible.
⚠️ Waiting for the cortisol/ACTH result (or for a cosyntropin stim test) before treating a suspected crisis is the cardinal board trap — treat first, confirm later.
13. IV hydrocortisone
Give IV HYDROCORTISONE 100 mg bolus immediately, then 50 mg q6h (or 200 mg/24h infusion). Hydrocortisone is preferred because at stress doses it has enough mineralocorticoid activity to cover aldosterone too.
⚠️ If you must preserve a pending cortisol/cosyntropin test, give DEXAMETHASONE instead — it does not cross-react in the cortisol assay; hydrocortisone would falsely elevate it.
14. IV saline + dextrose
Aggressive IV isotonic NORMAL SALINE (often 1–3 L rapidly) corrects volume depletion and hyponatremia; add DEXTROSE for hypoglycemia. Hyperkalemia usually corrects with volume + cortisol/aldosterone effect and rarely needs specific treatment.
⚠️ Correct the volume/Na with normal saline — do not over-rapidly correct hyponatremia, and the hyperkalemia generally self-corrects once steroids/fluids are on board.
15. Treat the trigger
After resuscitation, identify and treat the precipitant (e.g., antibiotics for the infection). High-dose hydrocortisone supplies the mineralocorticoid need acutely; once tapered below ~50 mg/day, add fludrocortisone for ongoing replacement in primary AI.
⚠️ At stress-dose hydrocortisone you do NOT need separate fludrocortisone acutely — its intrinsic mineralocorticoid effect is sufficient; add fludrocortisone only as the dose is tapered.
