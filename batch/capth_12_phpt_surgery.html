⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. Asymptomatic ≠ observe banner — A board case shows an asymptomatic patient with PHPT and a T-score of -2.8, then offers 'observe with annual labs since she has no symptoms' — WRONG. Asymptomatic PHPT still requires surgery if any criterion is met; this T-score below -2.5 alone is an operative indication. The discriminator: screen for ALL objective criteria, not just symptoms.
  2. '50-1-2.5 stones kidneys' mnemonic — A case lists a 24-hr urine calcium of 320 mg and asks if that alone justifies surgery — WRONG, that's below the 400 mg/day threshold. Conversely >400 mg/day with stone risk IS a criterion in many guideline versions. The discriminator: know the exact numbers (50 / 1 / 2.5 / GFR 60 / urine Ca 400); near-threshold values are deliberate distractors.
  3. Any one trigger = operate — A case where the patient meets only ONE criterion (e.g., age 45, otherwise normal) is dismissed as 'not enough indications, needs at least two to operate' — WRONG. A single criterion suffices. The discriminator: the rule is ANY-one, so do not require multiple positives before referring.
  4. Age < 50 — A 45-year-old with mild asymptomatic PHPT and otherwise normal labs is offered 'monitor, she's too young and low-risk for surgery' — WRONG. Young age is itself an indication BECAUSE of the long exposure ahead, not a reason to defer. The discriminator: age <50 = operate, age >50 with no other criteria = can monitor.
  5. Ca >1 mg/dL above ULN — A patient with calcium 11.2 (ULN 10.5) is called 'meets the surgical calcium criterion' — WRONG; that's only 0.7 above ULN, under the >1.0 threshold. The discriminator: it is >1.0 mg/dL ABOVE the upper limit (not just any value above normal); compute the exact difference against that patient's lab ULN.
  6. T-score < -2.5 (DEXA) — A PHPT patient has normal spine and hip DEXA, the forearm is not scanned, and they are labeled 'no bone indication for surgery' — WRONG/incomplete. PHPT hits cortical bone (distal radius) first, so omitting the forearm can miss osteoporosis. The discriminator: always include the distal 1/3 radius on DEXA in PHPT; spine/hip alone can be falsely reassuring.
  7. eGFR < 60 — A PHPT patient with eGFR 55 is told 'kidney function is fine, no renal indication' — WRONG; <60 is below the threshold and is itself an operative criterion. The discriminator: the renal cutoff is eGFR 60 (not 30 or 45); an eGFR in the 50s already qualifies.
  8. Stones / nephrocalcinosis — An asymptomatic PHPT patient with a 4 mm renal stone seen incidentally on CT is dismissed because 'he's never had renal colic' — WRONG. Silent stones or nephrocalcinosis on imaging count as a surgical criterion regardless of symptoms. The discriminator: imaging-detected stones qualify; absence of colic does not exclude the indication.
  9. Parathyroidectomy = definitive (OR) — A surgically eligible PHPT patient is offered 'lifelong cinacalcet' as equivalent definitive treatment — WRONG. Medical therapy only controls calcium or protects bone; it never cures. The discriminator: if criteria are met and the patient can tolerate surgery, parathyroidectomy is the answer; medical therapy is reserved for those who decline or are not operative candidates.
  10. 📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Asymptomatic ≠ observe banner
Most PHPT today is asymptomatic, found incidentally on a high calcium with an inappropriately normal/high PTH. The central teaching point: asymptomatic does NOT equal observe. Per the 2022 (4th International) Workshop guidelines, meeting ANY single objective criterion mandates referral for parathyroidectomy even without symptoms. Symptomatic disease (overt stones, fractures, neuromuscular/psychiatric symptoms) is always a surgical indication.
⚠️ A board case shows an asymptomatic patient with PHPT and a T-score of -2.8, then offers 'observe with annual labs since she has no symptoms' — WRONG. Asymptomatic PHPT still requires surgery if any criterion is met; this T-score below -2.5 alone is an operative indication. The discriminator: screen for ALL objective criteria, not just symptoms.
2. '50-1-2.5 stones kidneys' mnemonic
The surgery-criteria mnemonic for asymptomatic PHPT: AGE <50; calcium >1.0 mg/dL above the upper limit of normal; T-score < -2.5 (or vertebral fracture); plus KIDNEY findings — eGFR <60, nephrolithiasis/nephrocalcinosis, or 24-hr urine calcium >400 mg/day. Any single one triggers operation. Memorize 'less than 50, one above, minus 2.5, and the kidneys.'
⚠️ A case lists a 24-hr urine calcium of 320 mg and asks if that alone justifies surgery — WRONG, that's below the 400 mg/day threshold. Conversely >400 mg/day with stone risk IS a criterion in many guideline versions. The discriminator: know the exact numbers (50 / 1 / 2.5 / GFR 60 / urine Ca 400); near-threshold values are deliberate distractors.
3. Any one trigger = operate
The decision logic is OR, not AND: meeting any single guideline criterion is sufficient to indicate parathyroidectomy. A patient need not have all (or even two) criteria — one is enough. This reflects that even mild asymptomatic disease causes progressive bone loss, renal injury, and stone risk over time, all reversible/preventable with cure.
⚠️ A case where the patient meets only ONE criterion (e.g., age 45, otherwise normal) is dismissed as 'not enough indications, needs at least two to operate' — WRONG. A single criterion suffices. The discriminator: the rule is ANY-one, so do not require multiple positives before referring.
4. Age < 50
Age younger than 50 is an independent surgical criterion in asymptomatic PHPT. Younger patients have many decades of cumulative exposure to elevated calcium/PTH, so the lifetime risk of progressive bone loss, stones, and renal decline is high; cure now prevents these. In the scene the young woman (age 45) gets the green check — operate.
⚠️ A 45-year-old with mild asymptomatic PHPT and otherwise normal labs is offered 'monitor, she's too young and low-risk for surgery' — WRONG. Young age is itself an indication BECAUSE of the long exposure ahead, not a reason to defer. The discriminator: age <50 = operate, age >50 with no other criteria = can monitor.
5. Ca >1 mg/dL above ULN
Serum calcium more than 1.0 mg/dL above the upper limit of normal (e.g., ULN 10.5 → criterion at >11.5 mg/dL) is a surgical indication. Use albumin-corrected or ionized calcium. The threshold is deliberately modest because even mildly elevated calcium in PHPT predicts progression; markedly high calcium also raises crisis risk.
⚠️ A patient with calcium 11.2 (ULN 10.5) is called 'meets the surgical calcium criterion' — WRONG; that's only 0.7 above ULN, under the >1.0 threshold. The discriminator: it is >1.0 mg/dL ABOVE the upper limit (not just any value above normal); compute the exact difference against that patient's lab ULN.
6. T-score < -2.5 (DEXA)
Osteoporosis by DEXA — a T-score < -2.5 at ANY site (lumbar spine, hip, or distal one-third radius) — OR a prior fragility/vertebral fracture (including silent fracture on imaging) is a surgical criterion. Because PHPT preferentially erodes CORTICAL bone, the distal radius is the most sensitive site and DEXA must include the forearm.
⚠️ A PHPT patient has normal spine and hip DEXA, the forearm is not scanned, and they are labeled 'no bone indication for surgery' — WRONG/incomplete. PHPT hits cortical bone (distal radius) first, so omitting the forearm can miss osteoporosis. The discriminator: always include the distal 1/3 radius on DEXA in PHPT; spine/hip alone can be falsely reassuring.
7. eGFR < 60
Renal impairment with eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73m2 (CKD stage 3 or worse) attributable to PHPT is a surgical criterion. Chronic hypercalcemia and hypercalciuria cause nephrocalcinosis and tubulointerstitial damage; operating may halt further decline. eGFR <60 also limits some medical options (bisphosphonates) and raises secondary HPT confusion.
⚠️ A PHPT patient with eGFR 55 is told 'kidney function is fine, no renal indication' — WRONG; <60 is below the threshold and is itself an operative criterion. The discriminator: the renal cutoff is eGFR 60 (not 30 or 45); an eGFR in the 50s already qualifies.
8. Stones / nephrocalcinosis
Nephrolithiasis or nephrocalcinosis (clinical OR detected on imaging), or 24-hr urine calcium >400 mg/day with increased stone risk, is a surgical indication. PHPT classically causes calcium-based stones via hypercalciuria. Even asymptomatic stones found incidentally on CT/ultrasound count — making renal imaging part of the PHPT workup.
⚠️ An asymptomatic PHPT patient with a 4 mm renal stone seen incidentally on CT is dismissed because 'he's never had renal colic' — WRONG. Silent stones or nephrocalcinosis on imaging count as a surgical criterion regardless of symptoms. The discriminator: imaging-detected stones qualify; absence of colic does not exclude the indication.
9. Parathyroidectomy = definitive (OR)
Parathyroidectomy is the ONLY definitive cure for PHPT and is preferred whenever criteria are met and the patient is operable. Preop localization (sestamibi scan, neck ultrasound, 4D-CT) guides minimally invasive single-gland resection; intraoperative PTH should drop >50% within 10 min (Miami criterion). Cure normalizes calcium, improves BMD, and reduces stone recurrence.
⚠️ A surgically eligible PHPT patient is offered 'lifelong cinacalcet' as equivalent definitive treatment — WRONG. Medical therapy only controls calcium or protects bone; it never cures. The discriminator: if criteria are met and the patient can tolerate surgery, parathyroidectomy is the answer; medical therapy is reserved for those who decline or are not operative candidates.
10. Cortical bone sites (radius/hip/spine)
PHPT (excess PTH) preferentially resorbs CORTICAL bone, with the distal third of the radius the earliest and most affected site; trabecular spine is relatively spared early. This is why DEXA in PHPT must assess all three sites including the forearm. Severe long-standing disease causes osteitis fibrosa cystica (subperiosteal resorption, 'salt-and-pepper' skull, brown tumors).
⚠️ A PHPT patient with a normal spine T-score is reassured their bones are protected — WRONG, because PHPT spares trabecular spine relatively while eroding cortical radius. The discriminator: in PHPT the radius (cortical) is the canary, not the spine (trabecular); a normal spine does not rule out PHPT bone disease.
11. No criteria = monitor
If a patient with asymptomatic PHPT meets NO surgical criteria (age >50, Ca <1 above ULN, T-score > -2.5, no fracture, eGFR >60, no stones/normal urine Ca), active surveillance is appropriate. The older man in the scene with calcium 0.3 above ULN, T-score > -2.5, eGFR ok, and no stones gets monitoring rather than the OR.
⚠️ An asymptomatic PHPT patient who meets no criteria is pushed to surgery 'just to be safe' — WRONG/overtreatment. With no criteria met, guideline-concordant care is monitoring, not operating. The discriminator: surgery requires a met criterion (or symptoms); a fully criterion-negative patient is observed.
12. Serial monitoring panel
Surveillance of non-operative PHPT includes serum calcium annually, serum creatinine/eGFR annually, and DEXA (3 sites including forearm) every 1-2 years. Crossing a surgical threshold during follow-up converts the patient to a surgical candidate. Renal imaging is checked if stone symptoms or rising urine calcium arise.
⚠️ A monitored PHPT patient skips DEXA for years because calcium has been 'stable' — WRONG. Calcium can stay flat while bone density silently declines past -2.5, which is itself an operative trigger. The discriminator: monitoring is multi-parameter (Ca + renal + DEXA); stable calcium alone does not confirm stable disease.
13. Medical options (cinacalcet / bisphosphonate)
When surgery is declined or contraindicated: cinacalcet (calcimimetic, activates CaSR) LOWERS serum calcium but does little for bone density; bisphosphonates (e.g., alendronate) improve/protect BMD but do NOT durably lower calcium. They address different problems, so a patient needing both effects may take both. Maintain hydration; ensure adequate (not excessive) vitamin D.
⚠️ A non-operative PHPT patient with hypercalcemia is started on a bisphosphonate to bring the calcium down — WRONG. Bisphosphonates raise BMD but do not correct hypercalcemia; cinacalcet is the agent that lowers calcium. The discriminator: match the drug to the goal — calcium → cinacalcet, bone → bisphosphonate.
14. Avoid thiazides / Li (monitor side)
In PHPT, maintain hydration and AVOID provocateurs of hypercalcemia: thiazide diuretics (increase distal tubular calcium reabsorption → raise serum calcium and can unmask PHPT) and lithium (shifts the parathyroid set-point, raising PTH and calcium). If a patient on lithium/thiazide has borderline hypercalcemia, stop the drug and recheck before diagnosing PHPT.
⚠️ A patient on a thiazide has calcium 10.8 and is immediately diagnosed with PHPT — WRONG/premature. Thiazides (and lithium) themselves raise calcium; you must stop the offending drug and recheck calcium and PTH before committing to a PHPT diagnosis. The discriminator: drug-induced hypercalcemia mimics PHPT — clear the meds first.
