📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Quincke nail-bed pulsations (booth)
Quincke's sign is visible capillary pulsation in the nail bed — the nail blanches and flushes with each heartbeat — driven by the wide pulse pressure of chronic aortic regurgitation. It is one of the eponymous peripheral signs of a hyperdynamic circulation. Seen by lightly compressing the nail.
⚠️ Quincke pulsations reflect WIDE PULSE PRESSURE (chronic AR), not capillary refill or anemia — don't attribute them to perfusion problems.
2. Water-hammer / Corrigan pulse (bell + mallet)
The water-hammer (Corrigan) pulse is a rapidly rising and rapidly collapsing arterial pulse from the large stroke volume and diastolic runoff of AR. It is best appreciated by raising the patient's arm. The bounding-then-collapsing quality is the hallmark peripheral sign.
⚠️ A bounding, collapsing pulse is AR — opposite of the weak, slow-rising parvus et tardus of aortic STENOSIS; don't confuse the two.
3. Corrigan pulse label
Corrigan's pulse is the carotid manifestation of the same water-hammer phenomenon — a visible, abrupt, full upstroke followed by quick collapse. Corrigan and water-hammer name the same finding. It signals significant chronic AR with wide pulse pressure.
⚠️ Corrigan (visible carotid) and water-hammer (palpated radial) are the SAME hemodynamic sign of AR — not two different lesions.
4. De Musset head-bob (clown)
De Musset's sign is rhythmic bobbing of the head with each heartbeat, caused by the forceful systolic ejection and wide pulse pressure of AR transmitted to the carotids. It is a classic visible eponym. Like the other peripheral signs, it reflects a large stroke volume.
⚠️ Head-bobbing is De Musset's sign of AR (wide pulse pressure) — not a neurologic tremor or movement disorder.
5. Pulsus bisferiens (roller-coaster)
Pulsus bisferiens is a pulse with TWO systolic peaks per beat, classically felt in significant AR (and in mixed AS/AR or HOCM). The double hump reflects rapid ejection of a large stroke volume. It is another peripheral marker of the regurgitant load.
⚠️ Bisferiens = two peaks in SYSTOLE (AR or mixed AS/AR); don't confuse it with the dicrotic pulse (a systolic + diastolic peak in low-output states).
6. Pistol-shot femorals (gunslinger)
Traube's sign / pistol-shot femorals are loud, sharp, double sounds auscultated over the femoral arteries in AR. They arise from the abrupt distension and collapse of the artery with the wide pulse pressure. Another auscultatory peripheral sign of severe AR.
⚠️ Pistol-shot femorals are a sign of wide-pulse-pressure AR — not a femoral bruit of arterial stenosis/atherosclerosis.
7. Left sternal border — early diastolic murmur
The AR murmur is an EARLY DIASTOLIC decrescendo murmur heard best at the LEFT sternal border with the patient sitting up, leaning forward, in held EXPIRATION. These maneuvers bring the aortic root closer to the chest wall. Its early-diastolic decrescendo shape and position define it.
⚠️ The AR murmur is best heard sitting forward in EXPIRATION at the left sternal border — not lying flat; missing the maneuver misses the murmur.
8. Austin Flint murmur (apex rumble)
The Austin Flint murmur is a low-pitched mid-to-late DIASTOLIC rumble at the APEX in AR, produced as the regurgitant jet impinges on the anterior mitral leaflet, causing functional mitral inflow obstruction. It can mimic mitral stenosis. There is no opening snap and no loud S1, which helps distinguish it.
⚠️ An apical diastolic rumble in AR is Austin Flint (functional) — don't diagnose true mitral stenosis; there's no opening snap or loud S1.
9. Symptomatic any EF = Class I (top ribbon)
For severe AR, symptoms are an immediate Class I indication for surgery REGARDLESS of EF — exertional dyspnea, angina, or heart failure should not be tolerated. Waiting risks irreversible LV dysfunction. Symptom onset = operate.
⚠️ Operate on symptomatic severe AR at ANY EF — a preserved EF does not justify delaying surgery once the patient is symptomatic.
10. EF 55% or less = Class I (middle ribbon)
In ASYMPTOMATIC severe AR, an LVEF <=55% is a Class I indication for surgery. Declining systolic function signals the ventricle is failing under the volume load even before symptoms appear. The 55% threshold is the asymptomatic EF cutoff.
⚠️ The asymptomatic-AR EF cutoff is <=55% (Class I) — using 50% (the AS number) here is wrong.
11. LVESD over 50 = Class IIa (lower ribbon)
In asymptomatic severe AR with EF >55%, surgery is Class IIa when the LV is dilating: LVESD >50 mm (or indexed >25 mm/m2). Progressive end-systolic enlargement predicts adverse remodeling. This is the dimension-based trigger for earlier intervention.
⚠️ The dimension trigger is LVESD >50 mm (or >25 mm/m2 indexed) — NOT 40 mm; the '>40 mm end-systolic' cutoff is the classic wrong answer.
12. End-systolic over 40 = WRONG CUTOFF (red X)
The 'end-systolic dimension >40 mm' figure is a deliberate distractor — the real surgical dimension cutoff in asymptomatic severe AR is LVESD >50 mm (or >25 mm/m2). Anchoring to 40 mm leads to operating too early on the wrong basis. Reject this number.
⚠️ LVESD >40 mm is the WRONG cutoff for AR surgery — the correct value is >50 mm / >25 mm/m2; this is the planted board trap.
13. LVESD OVER 50 (correct, plain sign)
This restates the CORRECT dimension threshold: LVESD >50 mm (or >25 mm/m2 indexed) drives Class IIa surgery in asymptomatic severe AR with preserved EF. Keep the right number front of mind against the 40 mm distractor. It pairs with the EF <=55% Class I rule.
⚠️ Memorize LVESD >50 mm / >25 mm/m2 as the AR dimension cutoff — the value with the red X (>40) is there to mislead.
14. No surgery for moderate AR (vendor + red X)
These surgical thresholds apply only to SEVERE AR. MODERATE AR is not an indication for valve surgery on its own — it is managed medically with surveillance. Don't be lured into operating on a moderate lesion. Severity must be established before applying the Class I/IIa rules.
⚠️ Don't operate on MODERATE AR — the symptom/EF/LVESD criteria apply to SEVERE AR only; sending a moderate heart to surgery is the trap.
15. Carnival/fairground setting (wide pulse pressure theme)
The whole carnival theme encodes the hyperdynamic, WIDE PULSE PRESSURE circulation of chronic AR — every bouncing, bobbing, and banging eponym (Corrigan, De Musset, Quincke, Traube, bisferiens) stems from a large stroke volume with rapid diastolic runoff. Wide pulse pressure is the unifying mechanism. The peripheral signs are all manifestations of it.
⚠️ All the eponymous peripheral signs share ONE cause — wide pulse pressure of chronic AR; they are not independent findings to memorize in isolation.
