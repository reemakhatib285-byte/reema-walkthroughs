📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. PTH regulation recap
PTH is synthesized as prepro-PTH, cleaved to proPTH, then to the active 84-amino-acid hormone PTH(1-84), which has a short half-life (~2-4 min) for tight minute-to-minute control. Secretion is RAISED by low ionized calcium and low calcitriol, and is also stimulated by high phosphate (partly indirectly, by binding calcium and lowering ionized Ca). Calcitriol feeds back to suppress PTH gene transcription — the rationale for vitamin D analogs in secondary hyperparathyroidism of CKD.
⚠️ A stem says calcitriol STIMULATES PTH secretion — WRONG. Calcitriol provides NEGATIVE feedback, suppressing PTH synthesis. The discriminator: low Ca and low calcitriol drive PTH up; high Ca and high calcitriol turn PTH down.
2. CaSR = Ca thermostat
The calcium-sensing receptor behaves like a thermostat: its SET POINT is the ionized calcium concentration at which PTH secretion is half-maximal. ACTIVATING the CaSR (rising ionized Ca, or agonists like cinacalcet, or hypermagnesemia) SUPPRESSES PTH. The calcimimetic cinacalcet exploits this to lower PTH in secondary/tertiary hyperparathyroidism and in parathyroid carcinoma. Mnemonic: 'sensor sees high Ca → shuts PTH.'
⚠️ A stem implies that high calcium ACTIVATES the parathyroid to release more PTH — WRONG. CaSR activation by high calcium INHIBITS PTH secretion (negative feedback). The discriminator: the CaSR is an off-switch for PTH when calcium is high; it is not a stimulatory sensor.
3. FHH warning sign
Familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia (FHH) is an autosomal dominant INACTIVATING CaSR mutation. The receptor under-senses calcium, so the setpoint shifts up: the body tolerates mild lifelong hypercalcemia with normal-to-mildly-high PTH. The hallmark distinguishing it from primary hyperparathyroidism is INAPPROPRIATELY LOW urinary calcium, with a calcium/creatinine clearance ratio <0.01. It is usually asymptomatic and benign.
⚠️ A young patient with mild hypercalcemia, family history, and normal/high PTH is diagnosed with primary hyperparathyroidism and sent to surgery — WRONG if urine Ca/Cr clearance is <0.01. That ratio indicates FHH. The discriminator: primary HPT typically has urine Ca/Cr ratio >0.02 (normal/high urine calcium); FHH has <0.01 (low urine calcium). Always check urine calcium before parathyroidectomy.
4. No surgery / loss of function
FHH is benign and managed conservatively with reassurance and monitoring — no calcium restriction, no surgery. Parathyroidectomy fails because the defect is a generalized CaSR setpoint problem in every parathyroid cell (and the kidney), not a discrete adenoma; removing glands leaves residual tissue that resets to the same abnormal setpoint and risks hypoparathyroidism.
⚠️ A patient with confirmed FHH (low urine Ca/Cr) undergoes parathyroidectomy for 'hypercalcemia' — WRONG and harmful. Surgery does not cure FHH because the problem is the CaSR setpoint, not a parathyroid tumor. The discriminator: FHH = genetic, lifelong, asymptomatic, low urine Ca → observe; primary HPT with end-organ effects → consider surgery.
5. Kidney hoards Ca / urine Ca low
In FHH the inactivated CaSR in the renal thick ascending limb fails to sense calcium, so the kidney keeps reabsorbing calcium even when serum calcium is high — producing the characteristic INAPPROPRIATELY LOW urinary calcium. (Normally, a high serum calcium activates the renal CaSR to promote calciuria; that brake is broken in FHH.) This renal handle, independent of PTH, is what creates the low Ca/Cr clearance ratio.
⚠️ A stem attributes the low urine calcium of FHH purely to high PTH driving renal reabsorption — WRONG/incomplete. The low urine calcium stems from the defective RENAL CaSR itself (PTH-independent reabsorption), which is why FHH urine calcium stays low even though PTH is only normal/mildly elevated.
6. CaSR dial set point
The setpoint is the calcium concentration at which PTH secretion is half-maximal; the steep sigmoid relationship means small calcium changes near the setpoint produce large PTH swings. Shifting the dial shifts the regulated calcium: inactivating CaSR (FHH) raises the setpoint (tolerates higher Ca), activating CaSR (ADH) lowers it (defends a lower Ca), and lithium nudges it upward pharmacologically.
⚠️ A stem treats the setpoint as fixed and unaffected by drugs — WRONG. Lithium raises the setpoint and calcimimetics (cinacalcet) lower the effective setpoint, both shifting PTH output without any parathyroid pathology. The discriminator: medications can move the CaSR dial, so always review the drug list before invoking primary gland disease.
7. Lithium shifts set point
Lithium raises the CaSR setpoint (the parathyroid needs a higher calcium to shut off), so chronic lithium causes mild hypercalcemia with normal-to-high PTH and reduced urinary calcium — a near-perfect mimic of primary hyperparathyroidism (and of FHH). It can also cause true parathyroid hyperplasia over time. Management is to review/adjust lithium and monitor; surgery is generally avoided unless clearly indicated.
⚠️ A bipolar patient on lithium with mild hypercalcemia and high-normal PTH is rushed to parathyroid surgery — WRONG before reviewing the medication. Lithium-induced hypercalcemia mimics primary HPT. The discriminator: take a drug history; stop/reassess lithium before attributing the picture to an adenoma.
8. Mild up-PTH state
Mild CaSR inactivation (FHH) and lithium effect both produce a deceptively benign picture: mildly elevated calcium with normal-to-mildly-high PTH, closely resembling early/mild primary hyperparathyroidism. Distinguishing them rests on urinary calcium and clinical context (family history, age of onset, medications) rather than the serum calcium/PTH pair alone.
⚠️ A stem concludes 'high-normal PTH with high calcium = primary hyperparathyroidism' without further testing — WRONG. The same labs occur in FHH and lithium use. The discriminator: order a 24-h urinary calcium / Ca-Cr clearance ratio and review medications before committing to primary HPT.
9. Mg Goldilocks panels
Magnesium has a biphasic, 'Goldilocks' effect on PTH. MILD hypomagnesemia stimulates PTH secretion (like low calcium). SEVERE hypomagnesemia paradoxically BLOCKS PTH release and causes end-organ PTH resistance, yielding functional hypoparathyroidism and refractory hypocalcemia. HIGH magnesium activates the CaSR and suppresses PTH. So both too-little and too-much magnesium can lower PTH.
⚠️ A stem assumes 'low magnesium always raises PTH' — WRONG. Only MILD hypomagnesemia raises PTH; SEVERE hypomagnesemia suppresses it. The discriminator is severity: the curve is U-shaped, and severe Mg depletion behaves opposite to mild depletion.
10. Severe low Mg / PTH suppressed
Severe hypomagnesemia (often from PPIs, alcoholism, diarrhea, or aminoglycosides) impairs BOTH PTH secretion and PTH action at bone/kidney, producing hypocalcemia that will not correct with calcium alone. The fix is to replete MAGNESIUM first; once magnesium is restored, PTH secretion and responsiveness recover and calcium normalizes.
⚠️ A patient with hypocalcemia that fails to respond to IV calcium is given ever-larger calcium doses — WRONG. Suspect and correct SEVERE hypomagnesemia first. The discriminator: refractory hypocalcemia (especially with low PTH despite low Ca) points to low magnesium; calcium will not respond until Mg is replaced.
11. High Mg mimics low Ca
Hypermagnesemia activates the CaSR (magnesium is a CaSR agonist), suppressing PTH and lowering calcium. The classic setting is obstetric MgSO4 for preeclampsia/tocolysis, where rising magnesium causes loss of deep tendon reflexes (first sign), then respiratory depression and cardiac effects. Treatment of symptomatic hypermagnesemia is IV calcium gluconate (direct antagonist) plus stopping the source.
⚠️ A stem expects high magnesium to RAISE PTH (treating it like low calcium) — WRONG. High magnesium ACTIVATES the CaSR and SUPPRESSES PTH, lowering calcium. The discriminator: magnesium and calcium act on the same receptor, so hypermagnesemia mimics hypercalcemia at the CaSR (PTH down), even though it lowers serum calcium.
12. ADH gain of function
Autosomal dominant hypocalcemia (ADH, also called type 1 with CaSR gain-of-function) is an ACTIVATING CaSR mutation. The over-sensitive receptor perceives normal calcium as high, so it suppresses PTH and drives renal calcium wasting — yielding hypocalcemia with inappropriately low/normal PTH AND hypercalciuria. It is the mirror image of FHH. Treatment is cautious; calcimimetics worsen it, and recombinant PTH may be used.
⚠️ A patient with ADH and symptomatic hypocalcemia is treated with aggressive calcium and vitamin D to normalize serum calcium — WRONG. Because the renal CaSR is overactive, this dramatically worsens HYPERCALCIURIA, causing nephrocalcinosis, stones, and renal impairment. The discriminator: in ADH, treat only to relieve symptoms (target low-normal calcium); PTH is LOW, not high, so this is NOT ordinary hypoparathyroidism management.
13. Summary dials (FHH/ADH/Li)
Urinary calcium is the key discriminator among CaSR disorders. FHH (inactivating) = hypercalcemia with LOW urine calcium (Ca/Cr clearance <0.01). ADH (activating) = hypocalcemia with HIGH urine calcium. Lithium shifts the setpoint upward, mimicking FHH/primary HPT with variable (often low) urine calcium. Pairing serum calcium with urine calcium and PTH localizes the lesion.
⚠️ A stem pairs an inactivating CaSR phenotype with high urine calcium (or an activating one with low urine calcium) — WRONG; that is backwards. FHH (inactivating) = high serum Ca / LOW urine Ca; ADH (activating) = low serum Ca / HIGH urine Ca. The discriminator: the two are mirror images, and the urine calcium always travels with the direction of CaSR activity (more active receptor → more urinary calcium loss).
