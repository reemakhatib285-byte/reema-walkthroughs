⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. ANK1 ankyrin most common — Spectrin alpha is the most common HS gene. WRONG — ANK1 (ankyrin) is the most common, ~50% of HS. Discriminator: alpha-spectrin (SPTA1) defects are actually autosomal RECESSIVE and rarer; the common dominant HS genes in order are ANK1 > band 3 (SLC4A1) > beta-spectrin (SPTB).
  2. Vertical defect → spherocytosis — WRONG: 'Spherocytes on a smear with a positive direct antiglobulin (Coombs) test confirm hereditary spherocytosis.' Discriminator: HS spherocytes are Coombs-NEGATIVE; a POSITIVE Coombs with spherocytes indicates warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia (acquired), not the inherited vertical-defect membranopathy.
  3. Horizontal defect → elliptocytosis — Horizontal defects cause spherocytosis. WRONG — horizontal (spectrin tetramer / junctional) defects cause ELLIPTOCYTOSIS; vertical (ankyrin/band3/4.2) defects cause spherocytosis. Discriminator: remember 'vertical = sphere (ball), horizontal = ellipse (cigar)'; mixing up the axis is the classic exam trap.
  4. Spectrin/ankyrin lattice — WRONG: 'The biconcave RBC shape is determined primarily by intracellular organelle arrangement.' Discriminator: mature RBCs have no organelles — shape is dictated entirely by the spectrin-ankyrin-band3 membrane skeleton, which is exactly why membrane-protein mutations (not organelle defects) cause the hereditary shape disorders.
  5. SPTB / band 3 / 4.2 — WRONG: 'All hereditary spherocytosis is autosomal dominant, so an affected child always has an affected parent.' Discriminator: most HS (ANK1, SLC4A1, SPTB) is dominant, but SPTA1 and EPB42 are RECESSIVE, and ~25% of dominant cases are de novo — so a negative family history does not exclude HS.
  6. Osmotic fragility test — WRONG: 'A normal osmotic fragility test reliably excludes hereditary spherocytosis.' Discriminator: standard (non-incubated) osmotic fragility misses up to 20% of mild HS and can be normal during reticulocytosis — use incubated osmotic fragility or, better, EMA-binding flow cytometry to confirm/exclude.
  7. EMA binding test — WRONG: 'A reduced EMA-binding result is pathognomonic for hereditary spherocytosis.' Discriminator: reduced EMA binding also occurs in CDA type II, Southeast Asian ovalocytosis, and some other membranopathies — it is highly sensitive for HS but not 100% specific, so interpret alongside smear and clinical picture.
  8. Junctional → elliptocytosis — WRONG: 'Hereditary elliptocytosis always requires splenectomy because of chronic hemolysis.' Discriminator: the vast majority of HE (junctional/horizontal defects) is asymptomatic and transfusion-independent — only the rare severe variants (e.g., HPP or HE with significant hemolysis) warrant splenectomy.
  9. Splenectomy contraindicated sign — WRONG: 'Splenectomy benefits all hereditary membrane hemolytic anemias because the spleen is where the abnormal cells are destroyed.' Discriminator: in stomatocytosis/xerocytosis (PIEZO1) splenectomy is CONTRAINDICATED due to a high post-splenectomy thrombosis rate — confirm the specific diagnosis before recommending splenectomy.
  10. Splenectomy age 4-6 — Splenectomy normalizes RBC shape. WRONG — splenectomy removes the site of destruction and corrects the anemia/hemolysis, but the genetic membrane defect remains, so SPHEROCYTES PERSIST on the post-splenectomy smear (often with Howell-Jolly bodies appearing too). Discriminator: it cures the consequence, not the cause.
  11. Vaccinate first — WRONG: 'Vaccination just before or immediately after splenectomy is equally protective.' Discriminator: vaccines should be given at least 2 weeks BEFORE elective splenectomy to mount an adequate antibody response while the spleen still functions — post-op or last-minute vaccination blunts immunogenicity.
  12. PIEZO1 / Gardos — WRONG: 'Stomatocytes with a high MCHC and mild hemolysis are best managed with early splenectomy, as in spherocytosis.' Discriminator: this picture is dehydrated stomatocytosis (PIEZO1/Gardos), where splenectomy is CONTRAINDICATED because of marked post-splenectomy thrombosis risk — the opposite of HS.
  13. Malaria protection — WRONG: 'Southeast Asian ovalocytosis is a benign trait with no selective significance.' Discriminator: SAO is a malaria-protective balanced polymorphism — heterozygotes are resistant to severe/cerebral malaria, while homozygosity is lethal, which is why the band 3 deletion persists at high frequency in endemic populations.

📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. ANK1 ankyrin most common
ANK1 (ankyrin-1) is the single most common gene defect in hereditary spherocytosis (HS), accounting for ~50% of cases and usually autosomal dominant. Ankyrin is the vertical bridge that links the spectrin cytoskeleton to band 3 (the transmembrane anchor) in the lipid bilayer; losing it un-tethers the membrane, which buds off as microvesicles → progressive surface-area loss → spheroid shape. HS is the most common inherited hemolytic anemia in people of Northern European descent (~1 in 2,000).
⚠️ Spectrin alpha is the most common HS gene. WRONG — ANK1 (ankyrin) is the most common, ~50% of HS. Discriminator: alpha-spectrin (SPTA1) defects are actually autosomal RECESSIVE and rarer; the common dominant HS genes in order are ANK1 > band 3 (SLC4A1) > beta-spectrin (SPTB).
2. Vertical defect → spherocytosis
VERTICAL interactions are the bonds connecting the spectrin lattice DOWN to the membrane (ankyrin, band 3, protein 4.2). When these fail, the bilayer is no longer anchored and sheds, reducing surface-area-to-volume ratio → SPHEROCYTES. Spherocytes are rigid, lose deformability, and get trapped and destroyed in splenic cords (extravascular hemolysis). Labs: high MCHC, negative direct Coombs (distinguishes from autoimmune spherocytes), spherocytes on smear, increased RDW.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Spherocytes on a smear with a positive direct antiglobulin (Coombs) test confirm hereditary spherocytosis.' Discriminator: HS spherocytes are Coombs-NEGATIVE; a POSITIVE Coombs with spherocytes indicates warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia (acquired), not the inherited vertical-defect membranopathy.
3. Horizontal defect → elliptocytosis
HORIZONTAL interactions are the side-to-side bonds within the cytoskeleton — spectrin alpha-beta tetramer self-association and junctional complexes (protein 4.1, adducin). Defects weaken the lattice along the plane of the membrane so cells deform into ellipses under shear → HEREDITARY ELLIPTOCYTOSIS, usually mild/asymptomatic and autosomal dominant. The severe homozygous form is hereditary pyropoikilocytosis (HPP) with bizarre fragmented cells and thermal instability.
⚠️ Horizontal defects cause spherocytosis. WRONG — horizontal (spectrin tetramer / junctional) defects cause ELLIPTOCYTOSIS; vertical (ankyrin/band3/4.2) defects cause spherocytosis. Discriminator: remember 'vertical = sphere (ball), horizontal = ellipse (cigar)'; mixing up the axis is the classic exam trap.
4. Spectrin/ankyrin lattice
The RBC's biconcave shape and deformability are maintained by a 2-D spectrin meshwork tethered to the lipid bilayer: alpha/beta-spectrin tetramers (horizontal) anchored via ankyrin and protein 4.2 to band 3, and via protein 4.1/adducin to glycophorin C at junctional complexes (vertical). This scaffold lets RBCs squeeze through 3-micron splenic sinusoids and capillaries repeatedly over a 120-day lifespan; any break in the lattice shortens survival.
⚠️ WRONG: 'The biconcave RBC shape is determined primarily by intracellular organelle arrangement.' Discriminator: mature RBCs have no organelles — shape is dictated entirely by the spectrin-ankyrin-band3 membrane skeleton, which is exactly why membrane-protein mutations (not organelle defects) cause the hereditary shape disorders.
5. SPTB / band 3 / 4.2
The other dominant HS genes after ANK1: SLC4A1 (band 3, the anion exchanger and vertical anchor, ~25%, autosomal dominant), SPTB (beta-spectrin, ~20%, dominant), and EPB42 (protein 4.2, recessive, more common in Japanese populations). Band 3 loss also reduces eosin-5-maleimide (EMA) dye binding — the basis of the EMA flow test. SPTA1 (alpha-spectrin) is recessive and the odd one out.
⚠️ WRONG: 'All hereditary spherocytosis is autosomal dominant, so an affected child always has an affected parent.' Discriminator: most HS (ANK1, SLC4A1, SPTB) is dominant, but SPTA1 and EPB42 are RECESSIVE, and ~25% of dominant cases are de novo — so a negative family history does not exclude HS.
6. Osmotic fragility test
The osmotic fragility test exploits the spherocyte's low surface-area-to-volume ratio: with little spare membrane to accommodate water influx, spherocytes lyse at HIGHER (less hypotonic) saline concentrations than normal biconcave cells → increased osmotic fragility. Sensitivity improves with 24-hour incubation. It is the classic teaching test but has been largely superseded by EMA-binding flow cytometry because it misses ~10-20% of mild HS and is positive in any condition with spherocytes (e.g., autoimmune).
⚠️ WRONG: 'A normal osmotic fragility test reliably excludes hereditary spherocytosis.' Discriminator: standard (non-incubated) osmotic fragility misses up to 20% of mild HS and can be normal during reticulocytosis — use incubated osmotic fragility or, better, EMA-binding flow cytometry to confirm/exclude.
7. EMA binding test
The eosin-5-maleimide (EMA) binding test is the modern first-line diagnostic for HS: EMA dye binds band 3 and related membrane proteins, and HS cells (which have lost band 3/membrane) show REDUCED fluorescence on flow cytometry. It has high sensitivity (~93%) and specificity (~95%), needs only a small sample, and gives a same-day result. Reduced EMA also occurs in congenital dyserythropoietic anemia type II and Southeast Asian ovalocytosis, so correlate clinically.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A reduced EMA-binding result is pathognomonic for hereditary spherocytosis.' Discriminator: reduced EMA binding also occurs in CDA type II, Southeast Asian ovalocytosis, and some other membranopathies — it is highly sensitive for HS but not 100% specific, so interpret alongside smear and clinical picture.
8. Junctional → elliptocytosis
The junctional complex (protein 4.1R, adducin, dematin, glycophorin C, actin) crosslinks the ends of spectrin tetramers horizontally. Defects here — especially protein 4.1 — weaken side-to-side cytoskeletal cohesion → hereditary elliptocytosis. Protein 4.1 deficiency is a recognized HE cause and is geographically clustered. Most HE is clinically mild and needs no treatment unless a coexisting modifier produces hemolysis.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Hereditary elliptocytosis always requires splenectomy because of chronic hemolysis.' Discriminator: the vast majority of HE (junctional/horizontal defects) is asymptomatic and transfusion-independent — only the rare severe variants (e.g., HPP or HE with significant hemolysis) warrant splenectomy.
9. Splenectomy contraindicated sign
Splenectomy is CONTRAINDICATED in hereditary stomatocytosis / dehydrated stomatocytosis (xerocytosis, PIEZO1) because it markedly increases the risk of life-threatening venous thromboembolism and pulmonary hypertension post-splenectomy. This is a crucial contrast to HS, where splenectomy helps — so you must establish the precise membranopathy before removing the spleen. The red 'forbidden' sign flags this exception.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Splenectomy benefits all hereditary membrane hemolytic anemias because the spleen is where the abnormal cells are destroyed.' Discriminator: in stomatocytosis/xerocytosis (PIEZO1) splenectomy is CONTRAINDICATED due to a high post-splenectomy thrombosis rate — confirm the specific diagnosis before recommending splenectomy.
10. Splenectomy age 4-6
In severe HS, splenectomy (or partial/subtotal splenectomy) dramatically reduces hemolysis and transfusion need, but is DELAYED until age >5-6 years to minimize the risk of overwhelming post-splenectomy sepsis from encapsulated organisms; vaccinate and give penicillin prophylaxis first. Critically, splenectomy stops the destruction but does NOT correct the underlying membrane defect — spherocytes persist on the smear afterward.
⚠️ Splenectomy normalizes RBC shape. WRONG — splenectomy removes the site of destruction and corrects the anemia/hemolysis, but the genetic membrane defect remains, so SPHEROCYTES PERSIST on the post-splenectomy smear (often with Howell-Jolly bodies appearing too). Discriminator: it cures the consequence, not the cause.
11. Vaccinate first
Before any splenectomy, give vaccines against encapsulated organisms — pneumococcus (PCV13/PPSV23), Haemophilus influenzae type b, and meningococcus (ACWY + B) — ideally ≥2 weeks pre-op, plus annual influenza. Post-splenectomy patients also need daily penicillin prophylaxis (especially children for ≥5 years/until adulthood) and a standby antibiotic for febrile illness because of lifelong risk of overwhelming post-splenectomy infection.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Vaccination just before or immediately after splenectomy is equally protective.' Discriminator: vaccines should be given at least 2 weeks BEFORE elective splenectomy to mount an adequate antibody response while the spleen still functions — post-op or last-minute vaccination blunts immunogenicity.
12. PIEZO1 / Gardos
PIEZO1 (mechanosensitive cation channel) gain-of-function and KCNN4 (Gardos channel) mutations cause dehydrated hereditary stomatocytosis (hereditary xerocytosis): excess cation/water efflux dehydrates the cell, raising MCHC and producing stomatocytes/target cells with mild hemolysis. The board-critical pearl is that splenectomy is contraindicated here (high thrombotic risk), distinguishing it from HS management.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Stomatocytes with a high MCHC and mild hemolysis are best managed with early splenectomy, as in spherocytosis.' Discriminator: this picture is dehydrated stomatocytosis (PIEZO1/Gardos), where splenectomy is CONTRAINDICATED because of marked post-splenectomy thrombosis risk — the opposite of HS.
13. Malaria protection
Southeast Asian ovalocytosis (SAO), caused by a SLC4A1/band 3 deletion, produces rigid oval RBCs that resist invasion by malaria parasites — a balanced polymorphism maintained because heterozygotes are protected against cerebral malaria. The homozygous state is embryonically lethal. This parallels other malaria-protective RBC traits (G6PD deficiency, sickle trait, thalassemia) that persist in endemic regions.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Southeast Asian ovalocytosis is a benign trait with no selective significance.' Discriminator: SAO is a malaria-protective balanced polymorphism — heterozygotes are resistant to severe/cerebral malaria, while homozygosity is lethal, which is why the band 3 deletion persists at high frequency in endemic populations.
14. Negative family history fooled
A negative family history does NOT exclude hereditary spherocytosis: roughly 25% of dominant HS cases arise from de novo mutations, and the recessive subtypes (SPTA1/alpha-spectrin, EPB42) skip generations entirely. So an apparently sporadic case with spherocytes, Coombs-negative extravascular hemolysis, high MCHC, and reticulocytosis can still be HS — confirm with EMA-binding flow cytometry.
⚠️ Negative family history rules out HS. WRONG — ~25% of HS is de novo and recessive forms (SPTA1, EPB42) produce no parental phenotype. Discriminator: rely on labs (Coombs-negative spherocytes, high MCHC, positive EMA-binding/osmotic fragility), not the pedigree, to diagnose or exclude HS.
