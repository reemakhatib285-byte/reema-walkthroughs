📖 Read-through — all 16 symbols
1. Vagal maneuvers
Vagal maneuvers are FIRST-LINE for a hemodynamically STABLE, regular, narrow-complex tachycardia (suspected AVNRT/AVRT). They increase parasympathetic tone, which slows AV-nodal conduction and can break the re-entry circuit, terminating the arrhythmia. They are also diagnostic: the response (terminate vs slow vs unmask atrial activity) tells you the mechanism.
⚠️ Vagal maneuvers (and adenosine) are the WRONG first step if the patient is UNSTABLE — unstable narrow-complex tachycardia goes straight to synchronized cardioversion. They also won't terminate AF, flutter, or AT (AV-node-independent rhythms).
2. Valsalva
The Valsalva maneuver (forced expiration against a closed glottis) raises intrathoracic pressure and reflexively boosts vagal tone to slow AV conduction. The MODIFIED Valsalva — strain semi-recumbent, then immediately lay the patient flat and passively raise the legs to 45 degrees — significantly improves conversion rates over standard Valsalva and is the preferred technique.
⚠️ Standard supine Valsalva alone has a fairly low success rate; the modified (leg-lift) Valsalva is the higher-yield, evidence-based version — citing plain Valsalva as equally effective is a trap.
3. Carotid sinus massage
Carotid sinus massage stimulates baroreceptors that augment vagal output to the AV node — both diagnostic and therapeutic for AV-node-dependent SVT. Apply firm pressure to ONE carotid sinus (just below the angle of the jaw) for ~5-10 seconds, never both sides simultaneously.
⚠️ Never massage both carotids at once. CSM is contraindicated with a carotid bruit, known carotid stenosis, or recent (<3 month) TIA/stroke — risk of plaque embolization and stroke.
4. Atherosclerosis caution
Avoid carotid sinus massage in patients with atherosclerotic disease — a carotid bruit, known stenosis, or prior cerebrovascular event — because mechanical pressure can dislodge plaque and cause an embolic stroke. Auscultate for a bruit before attempting CSM, especially in older patients.
⚠️ Performing CSM on an elderly patient with a carotid bruit is a board-favorite contraindication violation — choose Valsalva or adenosine instead.
5. Adenosine syringe
If vagal maneuvers fail, give adenosine: 6 mg rapid IV push followed immediately by a 20 mL saline flush, escalating to 12 mg if no response (a second 12 mg may be repeated). Use a large proximal vein and push fast — its half-life is only ~10 seconds. Adenosine causes transient complete AV block, so it both terminates AV-node-dependent re-entry (AVNRT/AVRT) and serves as a diagnostic by unmasking atrial activity. Warn the patient about a brief, distressing sense of impending doom/flushing/chest pressure.
⚠️ Slow IV administration fails because adenosine is metabolized within seconds — give it as a rapid push with an immediate flush. Reduce the starting dose (e.g., 3 mg) in patients on dipyridamole or carbamazepine (potentiate it) or with a transplanted heart (denervation hypersensitivity); use higher/standard dosing if on theophylline/caffeine (antagonists).
6. Asthma caution
Adenosine can provoke bronchospasm via A2B/A3 receptor activation, so use caution or avoid it in patients with reactive airways — severe asthma or active bronchospasm. A non-dihydropyridine calcium-channel blocker (verapamil or diltiazem) or a vagal maneuver is the safer alternative in significant bronchospastic disease.
⚠️ Reaching for adenosine in a severe/active asthmatic is the trap — bronchospasm can be precipitated. Verapamil/diltiazem is the preferred AV-nodal blocker here (in a stable patient with no WPW).
7. AVOID in WPW-AF
In pre-excited atrial fibrillation (WPW + AF — irregular, rapid, wide/bizarre QRS), AV-nodal blockers including ADENOSINE, verapamil, diltiazem, beta-blockers, and digoxin are CONTRAINDICATED: blocking the AV node shunts conduction down the accessory pathway, accelerating the ventricular rate and risking degeneration to ventricular fibrillation. Use IV PROCAINAMIDE or ibutilide if stable, or synchronized cardioversion if unstable.
⚠️ Giving adenosine (or any AV-nodal blocker) in pre-excited AF is dangerous, not therapeutic — it can trigger VF. The tell is an IRREGULAR, wide-complex, very fast tachycardia with varying QRS morphology.
8. No effect → VT
If there is NO RESPONSE to adequate adenosine, the rhythm is likely NOT AV-node-dependent — reconsider VT (ventricular tachycardia is unaffected by AV-nodal blockade), or an AV-node-independent SVT. A non-responding wide-complex tachycardia should be treated as VT.
⚠️ Interpreting 'no effect from adenosine' as 'the drug failed, give more AV-nodal blockers' is wrong — it is a diagnostic clue pointing toward VT (or atrial flutter/AT). Adenosine can rarely terminate idiopathic RVOT/fascicular VT, but failure to respond should raise VT suspicion, not exclude it.
9. Slows: sinus/junctional
If adenosine only transiently SLOWS the rate and the tachycardia then resumes, the mechanism is AV-node-INDEPENDENT — sinus tachycardia or junctional/automatic rhythm (and sometimes focal AT). The rate is set by the focus, not by an AV-nodal re-entry loop, so blocking the node cannot terminate it.
⚠️ Transient slowing followed by resumption means the AV node was not the engine — do not keep re-dosing adenosine expecting termination; instead identify and treat the underlying driver (e.g., the cause of sinus tach).
10. Terminated: AVNRT/AVRT
ABRUPT termination of the tachycardia with adenosine confirms an AV-node-DEPENDENT re-entry circuit — AVNRT or orthodromic AVRT. Adenosine breaks the loop by transiently blocking the node, which is both curative for the acute episode and diagnostic of the mechanism.
⚠️ After termination, the next step for prevention/definitive therapy depends on the substrate: catheter ablation is first-line definitive therapy for recurrent AVNRT/AVRT, and is strongly preferred in symptomatic WPW (where chronic AV-nodal-blocking drugs may be unsafe if AF develops).
11. AV block reveals atria
When adenosine causes transient AV block WITHOUT terminating the rhythm, it UNMASKS the underlying atrial activity — exposing the sawtooth waves of atrial flutter (atrial rate ~300/min) or the discrete abnormal P waves of focal atrial tachycardia. This is the diagnostic use of adenosine in regular narrow-complex tachycardia.
⚠️ If adenosine reveals flutter/AT rather than terminating, the diagnosis (and therefore the treatment plan — rate control, anticoagulation considerations, possible ablation) changes; don't keep treating it as AVNRT.
12. Look for atrial activity
During the brief adenosine-induced AV pause, run a continuous rhythm strip and inspect for atrial activity — sawtooth flutter waves or atrial-tachy P waves that were hidden within the fast QRS complexes. Always record the strip BEFORE pushing adenosine so you capture the diagnostic moment.
⚠️ Failing to record/monitor during the pause wastes the diagnostic value of adenosine — the unmasking is transient (seconds), so capture it on a printed/continuous strip.
13. Unstable signs
The markers of hemodynamic instability in tachyarrhythmia are: HYPOTENSION/shock, ALTERED MENTAL STATUS, ischemic CHEST PAIN (angina), and acute HEART FAILURE (pulmonary edema). Any one of these in the context of the tachycardia defines an unstable patient.
⚠️ Instability must be attributable to the arrhythmia. Mild palpitations or anxiety alone are NOT instability — but true hypotension/AMS/ischemia/HF mandate immediate electrical therapy regardless of the rhythm subtype.
14. Unstable → cardiovert
Any UNSTABLE tachyarrhythmia with a pulse → immediate SYNCHRONIZED cardioversion. Sedate if time/consciousness allow. Synchronizing the shock to the R wave avoids delivering energy on the T wave (the vulnerable period), which could induce VF.
⚠️ Vagal maneuvers/adenosine first is WRONG when the patient is unstable. Also: use SYNCHRONIZED cardioversion (not unsynchronized defibrillation) for a perfusing rhythm — and pulseless VT/VF or polymorphic VT requires UNSYNCHRONIZED defibrillation instead.
15. Diving reflex / cold water
Cold-water facial immersion (or an ice-water bag to the face) triggers the mammalian diving reflex, a powerful vagal stimulus that slows AV conduction. It is the preferred vagal maneuver in INFANTS and young children, in whom Valsalva and carotid massage are impractical.
⚠️ The diving reflex (ice to the face) is the go-to vagal maneuver in infants — carotid sinus massage is generally avoided in young children. Choosing CSM for a febrile infant with SVT is the wrong technique.
16. Gagging maneuver
Inducing a gag reflex is another vagal maneuver that raises parasympathetic tone to slow AV-nodal conduction. Like the other maneuvers, it is reserved for STABLE, regular narrow-complex SVT and works only on AV-node-dependent rhythms.
⚠️ Like all vagal maneuvers, gagging will not convert atrial fibrillation, flutter, or VT, and is inappropriate as a first step in an unstable patient — who needs synchronized cardioversion.
