📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. HAV ship — fecal-oral
Hepatitis A is a non-enveloped picornavirus (positive-sense RNA) spread fecal-oral via contaminated food/water, shellfish, and during travel to endemic areas. Incubation is ~2-6 weeks (shorter than HBV). It causes an acute, self-limited illness; viremia and fecal shedding peak BEFORE jaundice, so patients are most contagious in the pre-icteric prodrome. Diagnosis = anti-HAV IgM (acute); anti-HAV IgG = past infection or vaccination.
⚠️ HAV never becomes chronic. A question describing a returning traveler with 'chronic hepatitis A and progression to cirrhosis' is wrong — HAV has no carrier state. Pick a different virus (HBV/HCV) for any chronicity stem.
2. HAV good prognosis
HAV recovers fully with lifelong immunity; there is no carrier state and no cirrhosis. Fulminant hepatitis is rare (<1%) and rises with age >50 or pre-existing liver disease (e.g., underlying HCV). A cholestatic or relapsing course can occur over weeks-months but still resolves.
⚠️ Don't choose 'liver transplant for chronic HAV cirrhosis' — HAV does not chronify. The only HAV scenario needing transplant is rare acute fulminant failure, not chronic disease.
3. HBV ship — DNA virus
Hepatitis B is the ONLY DNA hepatitis virus — a partially double-stranded hepadnavirus that replicates via reverse transcriptase (so nucleos(t)ide analogs like tenofovir/entecavir work). It carries surface antigen (HBsAg, the envelope) and core antigen (HBcAg, internal); HBeAg is a secreted marker of replication. Incubation is long (~1-4 months).
⚠️ 'All hepatitis viruses are RNA' is the distractor — HCV, HDV, HAV, and HEV are RNA; only HBV is DNA. Also remember HBsAg-negative does NOT exclude infection during the window period.
4. HBV transmission — blood/sex/birth
HBV spreads parenterally (needlestick, IVDU, transfusion), sexually, and perinatally (vertical) — NOT fecal-oral. It is far more infectious than HIV or HCV per needlestick (~30% for HBeAg+ source vs ~1.8% HCV, ~0.3% HIV). Household/sexual contacts and infants of HBsAg+ mothers are the key exposed groups for prophylaxis.
⚠️ Calling HBV 'fecal-oral' is wrong — that's HAV and HEV. Also, sexual transmission is much more prominent for HBV than for HCV; don't swap those two.
5. HBV chronicity by age
Risk of chronic HBV is inversely age-dependent: ~90% if acquired perinatally/neonatally, ~25-50% in early childhood, but only ~5% if acquired as an immunocompetent adult. This is because neonatal immune tolerance prevents clearance. Hence the urgency of HBIG + vaccine at birth for infants of HBsAg+ mothers.
⚠️ Don't quote the 90% chronicity figure for an adult-acquired infection — adults clear >95% of acute HBV. The 90% number applies to neonatal/perinatal transmission only.
6. HCV yacht — 85% chronic
Hepatitis C is a flavivirus (RNA) that becomes chronic in ~75-85% of those infected — the most chronicity-prone hepatitis virus and the leading cause of chronic viral hepatitis, cirrhosis, and liver transplant in the West. Acute infection is usually asymptomatic, which is why one-time screening of all adults 18-79 is recommended. Cure with direct-acting antivirals (e.g., sofosbuvir-based regimens) achieves SVR >95%.
⚠️ HCV — not HBV — is the leading cause of chronic viral hepatitis and HCC in the United States/West. Don't pick HBV for 'most common cause of chronic hepatitis in the West.'
7. HCV blood-borne
HCV is primarily blood-borne — IVDU is now the dominant route, with pre-1992 transfusion as the classic historical cohort. Sexual transmission (~low, rises with HIV co-infection/MSM) and perinatal transmission (~5%) are uncommon compared with HBV. Extrahepatic associations: mixed cryoglobulinemia, membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis (MPGN), porphyria cutanea tarda.
⚠️ Acute HCV rarely causes fulminant liver failure — its danger is chronic progression, not ALF. If a stem has acute fulminant hepatitis, think HAV/HBV/HEV or acetaminophen, not HCV alone.
8. HDV — coinfection
HDV (delta agent) is a defective circular RNA virus that REQUIRES the HBV surface antigen (HBsAg) to assemble and infect — it cannot replicate alone. COINFECTION = simultaneous acquisition of HBV + HDV; this raises fulminant risk acutely but, because clearing HBV clears HDV, it is usually self-limited with low chronicity.
⚠️ HDV cannot infect an HBsAg-negative person — it needs the HBV envelope. A 'delta hepatitis in a non-HBV patient' stem is impossible. Coinfection (acquired together) is the LESS chronic one; superinfection is the dangerous chronic form.
9. HDV — superinfection
HDV SUPERINFECTION = delta acquired by someone who is already a chronic HBV carrier. Because HBsAg is already abundant, HDV replicates aggressively → high rate of chronic HDV (~80%), accelerated cirrhosis, and increased fulminant/ALF risk. Suspect superinfection when a stable chronic HBV patient suddenly worsens. Diagnose with anti-HDV/HDV RNA.
⚠️ Distinguish superinfection (chronic HBV carrier + new HDV = dangerous, chronic) from coinfection (both at once = mostly self-limited). A chronic HBV patient with abrupt clinical deterioration = HDV superinfection, not a new HAV.
10. HEV ship — fecal-oral
Hepatitis E is an RNA virus (hepevirus) transmitted fecal-oral via contaminated water (epidemic genotypes 1/2 in Asia/Africa) and via undercooked pork/game (zoonotic genotypes 3/4 in developed countries). Like HAV it is usually acute and self-limited with no chronic state in immunocompetent hosts.
⚠️ HEV is fecal-oral like HAV, NOT blood-borne. Exception to remember: HEV CAN become chronic in solid-organ transplant/immunosuppressed patients — treat with ribavirin and reduce immunosuppression.
11. HEV 20% pregnancy mortality
The signature HEV board fact: fulminant hepatitis with ~15-25% mortality in pregnant women, especially the 3rd trimester. A pregnant traveler returning from an endemic area with acute hepatitis and high mortality risk = HEV until proven otherwise.
⚠️ The HEV danger is acute fulminant disease in PREGNANCY, not chronicity. Don't attribute high pregnancy mortality to HAV — it's HEV specifically.
12. Fulminant pirate — INR/enceph
Acute liver failure (fulminant hepatitis) = hepatic encephalopathy PLUS coagulopathy (INR ≥1.5) developing within weeks in a patient without pre-existing liver disease. Among viral causes, HBV (especially with HDV coinfection/superinfection) drives the majority; HEV in pregnancy is another key cause; HCV almost never. Management = ICU + urgent liver transplant evaluation.
⚠️ 'HCV is the top cause of fulminant viral hepatitis' is wrong — HBV (often with HDV) leads, and HCV rarely causes ALF. Remember encephalopathy is REQUIRED to label it ALF; coagulopathy alone is not enough.
