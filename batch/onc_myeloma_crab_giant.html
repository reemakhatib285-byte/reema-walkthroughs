📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. M-spike shield wall (infection)
Infection is the leading cause of death in multiple myeloma. A monoclonal M-protein crowds out normal polyclonal immunoglobulin, producing functional hypogammaglobulinemia and impaired humoral immunity. Patients are especially prone to encapsulated organisms — S. pneumoniae and other respiratory/urinary pathogens.
⚠️ Despite a high total protein/globulin, myeloma patients are immunodeficient — the M-protein is useless; do not be reassured by a 'normal' total immunoglobulin level.
2. Claw B — Bone smashing
The 'B' of CRAB is Bone lesions. Myeloma cells secrete RANKL and DKK1, activating osteoclasts and suppressing osteoblasts, producing purely lytic 'punched-out' lesions and pathologic fractures. Skeletal survey/whole-body low-dose CT or MRI/PET is used — classic bone scan is INSENSITIVE because there is no osteoblastic reaction.
⚠️ Bone (technetium) scan is falsely negative in myeloma because the lesions are osteoclastic/lytic with no osteoblast uptake — use skeletal survey, low-dose whole-body CT, or PET/MRI instead.
3. Bone pain / collapsed spine
Bone pain (back/ribs) is the most common presenting symptom. Vertebral compression fractures cause height loss and can produce spinal cord compression, an oncologic emergency requiring urgent MRI and dexamethasone plus radiation/surgery.
⚠️ New focal back pain with neurologic signs in a myeloma patient is cord compression until proven otherwise — image emergently rather than attributing it to benign mechanical pain.
4. Claw A — Anemia / marrow
The 'A' of CRAB is Anemia, typically normocytic-normochromic from marrow replacement by plasma cells and relative EPO deficiency. Rouleaux formation on smear reflects the high paraprotein. Anemia plus a high total protein/globulin gap is a classic exam trigger to check SPEP.
⚠️ An elevated ESR with anemia and rouleaux points to a paraprotein — order SPEP/UPEP and free light chains, not just an iron panel.
5. Claw C — Calcium >11
The 'C' of CRAB is hyperCalcemia (corrected Ca >11 mg/dL or >1 mg/dL above ULN) from osteoclastic bone resorption. Symptoms: polyuria, constipation, confusion, nausea. Treat with aggressive IV normal saline first, then bisphosphonate (zoledronate) and calcitonin for rapid effect.
⚠️ First-line treatment of myeloma hypercalcemia is IV normal saline volume expansion — bisphosphonates work over days; loop diuretics are NOT first-line and only after euvolemia.
6. Claw R — Renal / cast nephropathy
The 'R' of CRAB is Renal failure. The dominant mechanism is light-chain (cast) nephropathy: filtered free light chains bind Tamm-Horsfall protein in the distal tubule forming obstructing casts. Hypercalcemia and hyperuricemia add insult. Management: hydration, treat hypercalcemia, and rapid anti-myeloma therapy to lower light chains.
⚠️ The classic renal lesion of myeloma is cast nephropathy from FREE LIGHT CHAINS, not the intact M-protein — and AL amyloidosis/MIDD are separate light-chain renal lesions.
7. Dipstick negative for protein
Urine dipstick detects ALBUMIN but NOT immunoglobulin free light chains (Bence Jones protein). A negative dipstick with heavy proteinuria on 24-hour collection or UPEP/immunofixation signals light-chain (Bence Jones) proteinuria — the hallmark of myeloma cast nephropathy.
⚠️ A negative urine dipstick does NOT exclude myeloma proteinuria — dipstick misses light chains; you must order UPEP/urine immunofixation or 24-hour protein.
8. AL amyloidosis (leaking glucose)
AL amyloidosis from misfolded light chains deposits in kidney (nephrotic-range albuminuria), heart (restrictive cardiomyopathy, low-voltage ECG), tongue (macroglossia), and nerves. Apple-green birefringence on Congo red stain confirms it. Proximal tubular light-chain injury can produce Fanconi syndrome with glucosuria.
⚠️ Nephrotic-range ALBUMINURIA in a plasma-cell disorder = AL amyloidosis (glomerular), distinct from cast nephropathy (tubular, light-chain) which spares albumin.
9. Hyperviscosity station
Hyperviscosity syndrome (blurred vision, mucosal bleeding, headache, confusion) is far more common with IgM (Waldenstrom) but can occur with high IgA/IgG myeloma. Funduscopy shows dilated 'sausage-link' retinal veins. Emergent treatment is plasmapheresis to remove the paraprotein, then treat the clone.
⚠️ Hyperviscosity is classically IgM (Waldenstrom macroglobulinemia), not typical IgG myeloma — and the emergency treatment is plasmapheresis, not transfusion (which worsens viscosity).
10. M-component / clotting hazard
The paraprotein can coat platelets and clotting factors causing acquired bleeding (acquired von Willebrand, factor inhibition) — yet myeloma and its IMiD therapy (lenalidomide/thalidomide) also markedly raise VTE risk, so thromboprophylaxis (aspirin or anticoagulation) is standard with IMiD regimens.
⚠️ IMiDs (lenalidomide/thalidomide) carry a high VTE risk requiring prophylaxis — this is a frequently tested management point distinct from the paraprotein's bleeding effect.
11. Neurologic — weak legs / confused
Neurologic features arise from cord compression (weak legs, sensory level), hypercalcemia (confusion), hyperviscosity (CNS), amyloid/light-chain peripheral neuropathy, and POEMS. Bortezomib and thalidomide themselves cause peripheral neuropathy — a key dose-limiting toxicity.
⚠️ Sensory peripheral neuropathy in a treated myeloma patient is often drug-induced (bortezomib > thalidomide); switching to subcutaneous/weekly bortezomib reduces it.
12. Pseudohyponatremia trap
A very high M-protein increases the plasma's non-aqueous (protein) volume, so older indirect ion-selective/flame-photometry sodium assays read a falsely LOW sodium — pseudohyponatremia. Measured serum osmolality is normal; direct ISE on a blood gas gives the true sodium.
⚠️ A 'low' sodium with normal measured osmolality and a huge paraprotein = pseudohyponatremia — do NOT treat with hypertonic saline; confirm with direct ISE (blood gas) sodium.
13. Calcium boulders / lytic skull
Punched-out lytic lesions are most numerous in the skull (pepper-pot/raindrop skull), vertebrae, ribs, and proximal long bones. They reflect uncoupled bone turnover; alkaline phosphatase is often NORMAL because there is no osteoblastic repair.
⚠️ A normal alkaline phosphatase despite extensive lytic bone disease is characteristic of myeloma (no osteoblastic response) and distinguishes it from osteoblastic metastases.
14. >11 mg/dL calcium flag
The CRAB calcium criterion is serum corrected calcium >11 mg/dL (or >1 mg/dL above the upper limit). Meeting any single CRAB feature (plus a clonal plasma-cell disorder) defines symptomatic myeloma requiring treatment, separating it from smoldering myeloma/MGUS.
⚠️ Any one CRAB criterion (or a myeloma-defining biomarker: ≥60% plasma cells, FLC ratio ≥100, >1 focal MRI lesion) upgrades smoldering to active myeloma needing treatment — you do NOT need all of CRAB.
15. Cloggin' drain (light-chain casts)
Mnemonic for the renal mechanism: free light chains + Tamm-Horsfall protein 'clog' the distal tubule. Avoiding nephrotoxins, NSAIDs, and IV contrast, plus volume repletion and rapid light-chain reduction (often bortezomib-based), preserves renal function.
⚠️ Avoid IV contrast and NSAIDs in myeloma renal disease — contrast can precipitate cast nephropathy/AKI in the setting of high light-chain load.
