📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Needle-shaped crystals
Monosodium urate (MSU) crystals are needle-shaped and NEGATIVELY birefringent: yellow when parallel to the slow axis of the red compensator, blue when perpendicular. Synovial fluid polarized microscopy is the gold-standard diagnostic test for gout.
⚠️ CPPD (pseudogout) is rhomboid and POSITIVELY birefringent (blue parallel) — opposite color/shape to MSU.
2. Blue parallel = wrong
For MSU, blue when PARALLEL is incorrect — that pattern defines CPPD. MSU is yellow-parallel. Memorize: yelloW = neWative birefringence = urate.
⚠️ Confusing parallel-axis color is the classic exam trap separating gout from pseudogout.
3. Aspirate / arthrocentesis
Joint aspiration with synovial fluid analysis is mandatory in acute mono-arthritis to confirm crystals AND exclude septic arthritis. WBC is typically 2,000–50,000 (inflammatory) in gout.
⚠️ Never assume gout on clinical grounds in a hot joint — septic arthritis must be excluded by aspiration first.
4. Septic / CPPD / cellulitis DDx
Acute monoarthritis differential: septic arthritis (emergency), CPPD/pseudogout, psoriatic arthritis, cellulitis mimicking joint. Each is excluded by aspiration, crystal exam, and culture.
⚠️ Cellulitis overlying a joint can mimic gout — but cellulitis lacks intra-articular fluid and crystals.
5. Tophi — ear/olecranon
Tophi are chronic MSU deposits in cooler peripheral tissues: helix of ear, olecranon bursa, finger pads, Achilles tendon, first MTP. They signal long-standing untreated hyperuricemia and are an indication for urate-lowering therapy.
⚠️ Rheumatoid nodules also affect olecranon — but tophi are firm, chalky-white, and contain urate, not palisading granuloma.
6. Achilles / toe locations
Classic gout/tophi sites: first MTP (podagra), midfoot, Achilles tendon insertion, toe. Lower-extremity cooler joints favor urate crystallization.
⚠️ Achilles enthesitis suggests spondyloarthropathy — but a chalky tophus there is gout, not enthesitis.
7. Acute attack (red/hot)
Acute gout flare: sudden severe pain, erythema, warmth, swelling peaking in 12–24h. First-line acute therapy: NSAIDs, colchicine, or corticosteroids. Do NOT start or stop urate-lowering therapy during a flare.
⚠️ Starting allopurinol during an acute flare can prolong/worsen it — treat the flare first, then start ULT under prophylaxis.
8. Chalky white tophus
Aspirated tophus material is chalky-white, pasty (toothpaste-like) due to dense MSU crystal aggregates. This gross appearance is pathognomonic of urate.
⚠️ Calcium deposits (CPPD/calcinosis) are also white on X-ray but synovial crystal microscopy distinguishes them.
9. Polarized microscopy
Compensated polarized light microscopy of synovial fluid distinguishes MSU (needle, negative) from CPPD (rhomboid, positive). This is the definitive diagnostic modality.
⚠️ Plain light microscopy cannot determine birefringence — a polarizer with red compensator is required.
10. 5.4 mg/dL urate threshold
MSU saturates plasma around 6.8 mg/dL; urate-lowering therapy targets serum uric acid <6 mg/dL (<5 mg/dL with tophi). Serum urate may be normal/low DURING an acute flare, so a normal level does not exclude gout.
⚠️ A normal serum uric acid during the attack does NOT rule out gout — recheck after the flare resolves.
11. Flare trigger
Flares triggered by purine-rich food, alcohol (beer), dehydration, diuretics, and rapid urate shifts. IL-1β–driven inflammation underlies the attack.
⚠️ Loop/thiazide diuretics RAISE urate and precipitate gout — a common iatrogenic trigger to recognize on the boards.
12. IL-6 / cytokine pickpocket
Gout inflammation is mediated by the NLRP3 inflammasome → IL-1β, with downstream IL-6 and TNF. This rationalizes IL-1 inhibitors (anakinra, canakinumab) for refractory flares.
⚠️ Anti–IL-6 (tocilizumab) is for RA/giant cell arteritis, not standard gout — the key cytokine target in gout is IL-1β.
13. Two crystals (DDx gun)
Two crystal arthropathies: MSU (gout, needle, negative birefringent) vs CPPD/calcium pyrophosphate (pseudogout, rhomboid, positive). Distinguishing them on aspiration directs management.
⚠️ CPPD chondrocalcinosis associates with hemochromatosis, hyperparathyroidism, hypomagnesemia — screen for these in pseudogout.
14. US / DECT / X-ray imaging
Imaging clues: ultrasound 'double-contour sign' (urate on cartilage); dual-energy CT (DECT) color-codes urate deposits; X-ray shows late 'rat-bite' punched-out erosions with overhanging edges.
⚠️ DECT can be falsely positive from artifact; it supports but does not replace synovial crystal confirmation.
15. Culture (exclude septic)
Always send synovial fluid for Gram stain and culture to exclude septic arthritis, which can coexist with crystals. Septic arthritis is a joint-destroying emergency requiring drainage and IV antibiotics.
⚠️ Finding crystals does NOT exclude concurrent infection — a septic joint can also contain crystals; culture is mandatory.
