📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. K+ >= 5.5
Hyperkalemia is defined as serum K+ >= 5.5 mM (normal 3.5-5.0). Severity tiers: mild 5.5-6.0, moderate 6.1-6.9, severe >= 7.0 or any ECG change. The cell stores 98% of body potassium, so serum K+ is a small, tightly regulated window onto a large intracellular pool.
⚠️ WRONG to equate a given serum K+ with total-body potassium status. Discriminator: serum K+ can be high while total body K+ is depleted (DKA) or normal (pseudohyperkalemia) — interpret the number alongside acid-base status, glucose, and how the sample was drawn.
2. Is it REAL?
The first question in any hyperkalemia case is whether the value is real or pseudohyperkalemia (spurious in-vitro K+ release). Reflexively confirm with a properly drawn repeat and review the sample (hemolysis note, platelet/WBC count, draw technique) if the patient is hemodynamically stable. This step prevents unnecessary, potentially harmful treatment.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: rushing to insulin/calcium/kayexalate for a high K+ in a stable, asymptomatic patient with a normal ECG and a hemolyzed sample. Discriminator: if stable with a normal ECG and a suspicious draw, repeat first; if unstable or ECG changes are present, treat empirically and do not wait.
3. Pseudo — fake lane
Pseudohyperkalemia is a spurious elevation from K+ leaking out of cells after the blood is drawn; total body and in-vivo K+ are normal and no treatment is needed. Clues: a hemolysis flag, marked thrombocytosis/leukocytosis, difficult/traumatic draw, or a normal ECG that doesn't fit the number. Confirm with a fresh, atraumatic plasma sample.
⚠️ WRONG to treat pseudohyperkalemia as a true emergency. Discriminator: pseudohyperkalemia has NO ECG changes and a benign clinical picture — recognizing it avoids iatrogenic hypokalemia; treating it can drive the real K+ dangerously low.
4. Hemolysis
In-vitro hemolysis — from a small-gauge needle, excessive aspiration, or delayed/agitated processing — lyses RBCs and leaks K+ into the serum, falsely raising the value. The lab often flags the specimen as hemolyzed. The fix is a clean, atraumatic redraw, not treatment.
⚠️ WRONG to act on a hemolyzed sample's K+. Discriminator: a hemolysis flag plus a normal ECG points to a spurious result — redraw with good technique; do not confuse in-vitro hemolysis with in-vivo hemolysis (which is true hyperkalemia with high LDH, low haptoglobin, elevated indirect bilirubin).
5. Thrombocytosis
Platelets and leukocytes release potassium as blood clots in the tube, so marked thrombocytosis or leukocytosis falsely elevates SERUM K+ while PLASMA K+ stays normal. This is reverse pseudohyperkalemia's classic counterpart. Drawing a heparinized plasma (unclotted) sample reveals the true value.
⚠️ WRONG to diagnose true hyperkalemia from a serum sample in a patient with extreme thrombocytosis/leukocytosis (e.g., myeloproliferative disease). Discriminator: serum (clotted) K+ is high but plasma (unclotted) K+ is normal — order a plasma potassium to unmask the artifact.
6. Fist/tourniquet
Repeated fist clenching or a prolonged/tight tourniquet causes local muscle K+ release and forearm ischemia, spuriously raising the drawn K+. Proper technique (release tourniquet, avoid pumping the fist) prevents it. This is a pure preanalytic artifact.
⚠️ WRONG to treat a mildly elevated K+ from a tourniquet-and-fist-pump draw in a well patient. Discriminator: technique-related pseudohyperkalemia resolves on a properly drawn repeat — fix the draw, not the patient.
7. Door 1: shift
Transcellular shift = potassium moves OUT of cells into the extracellular space without a change in total body K+. Drivers: acidosis (especially mineral acids), insulin deficiency, hyperosmolality, beta-blockade, digoxin (Na/K-ATPase inhibition), succinylcholine, and cell lysis. Therapy that pushes K+ back IN (insulin/glucose, beta-2 agonists) exploits the reverse of these mechanisms.
⚠️ WRONG to assume a shift-driven hyperkalemia reflects potassium excess that must be removed. Discriminator: in pure shift, total body K+ is normal (or low, as in DKA) — treat the underlying driver and shift K+ back into cells; aggressive removal can cause rebound hypokalemia once the shift reverses.
8. DKA shift
In DKA/HHS, insulin deficiency plus hyperosmolality (and solvent drag) drives K+ out of cells, so serum K+ may be high even though total body K+ is markedly DEPLETED from osmotic diuresis. As insulin and fluids are given, K+ shifts back into cells and serum K+ falls fast. Note: DKA's anion-gap (ketoacid) acidosis itself shifts K+ less than mineral-acid acidosis does — the dominant drivers are insulin deficiency and hyperosmolality.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: withholding potassium in DKA because the initial serum K+ looks normal or high. Discriminator: total body K+ is depleted — once serum K+ falls below ~5.2-5.3 mM, add K+ to fluids, and HOLD insulin if K+ < 3.3 mM until repleted to avoid lethal hypokalemia.
9. Door 2: load
Excess potassium load (exogenous: diet, salt substitutes, supplements, KCl, transfusion; endogenous: TLS, rhabdomyolysis, hemolysis) causes hyperkalemia chiefly when excretion is impaired. Healthy kidneys clear large loads, so load alone is rarely sufficient. Endogenous lysis loads are especially dangerous because they are large and ongoing.
⚠️ WRONG to blame dietary/load causes when GFR and aldosterone are normal. Discriminator: with intact kidneys, load alone rarely sustains hyperkalemia — search for a concurrent excretion defect (CKD, hypoaldosteronism, drugs) that converts the load into a problem.
10. Salt sub = KCl
Salt substitutes and 'lite'/low-sodium salts are potassium chloride — a hidden, frequently underreported load. In CKD or on RAAS blockade they readily precipitate severe hyperkalemia. Always ask specifically about salt substitutes, supplements, herbals, and recent transfusions.
⚠️ WRONG to miss the salt substitute because the patient denies taking 'potassium.' Discriminator: patients view salt substitutes as food, not medicine — the classic deadly setup is elderly + DM + CKD on an ACEi who switched to 'lite' salt.
11. Door 3: kidney
Decreased renal excretion is the MOST COMMON cause of true, sustained hyperkalemia: reduced GFR (CKD/AKI), low aldosterone effect (hypoaldosteronism, Type 4 RTA, Addison's), and drugs (ACEi/ARB, MRAs, ENaC blockers, NSAIDs, trimethoprim, heparin, calcineurin inhibitors). Effective secretion needs aldosterone, ENaC, distal Na+ delivery, and urine flow — failure of any limits excretion. This is the highest-yield bucket for chronic/recurrent hyperkalemia.
⚠️ WRONG to anchor on shift or load and overlook an excretion defect in chronic/recurrent hyperkalemia. Discriminator: persistent hyperkalemia almost always involves impaired renal excretion — assess GFR, urine output, aldosterone status, and a medication list before settling on a shift/load cause.
12. Type 4 RTA
Type 4 RTA = hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism, the highest-yield decreased-excretion cause, classically in diabetic nephropathy. It produces a NORMAL-anion-gap (hyperchloremic) metabolic acidosis WITH hyperkalemia and an inappropriately acidic-to-mildly-impaired urine. Treat by removing offending drugs, low-K+ diet, loop/thiazide diuretics, and fludrocortisone in selected patients.
⚠️ WRONG to confuse Type 4 with Type 1 (distal) RTA. Discriminator: Type 4 RTA is the ONLY RTA with HYPERkalemia (Types 1 and 2 cause HYPOkalemia) — high K+ plus NAGMA in a diabetic points straight to hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism.
13. Emergency? ECG
After confirming the value is real, assess for emergency: K+ >= 6.5 mM OR any ECG change triggers immediate IV calcium for membrane stabilization, then shift (insulin/glucose, albuterol) and removal (dialysis, loop diuretics, GI binders). The ECG, not the number alone, governs urgency. Get a STAT ECG and monitor early.
⚠️ WRONG to rely on the absolute K+ value and be reassured by a normal ECG in CKD/ESRD. Discriminator: the ECG can stay NORMAL even at K+ > 7 in chronically adapted CKD/ESRD patients — treat based on the trend, rate of rise, and clinical context, not solely the tracing.
14. Treat if unstable
If the patient is unstable or shows ECG changes, treat first and diagnose later — stabilize the membrane with IV calcium, shift K+ intracellularly, and remove it, before completing the full etiologic workup. Mnemonic 'C BIG K Drop': Calcium, Bicarb (if acidotic), Insulin/glucose, Glucose, Kayexalate/binders, Dialysis/Diuretics. Definitive cause-finding follows stabilization.
⚠️ WRONG to delay treatment to obtain aldosterone levels, TTKG, or other workup in an unstable hyperkalemic patient. Discriminator: hemodynamic instability or ECG changes mandate empiric treatment now — the diagnostic workup is completed after the emergency is controlled, never before.
