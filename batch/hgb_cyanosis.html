📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Carboxyhemoglobin (COHb)
Carbon monoxide binds hemoglobin with ~200–250× the affinity of O2, forming carboxyhemoglobin and also LEFT-shifting the dissociation curve of remaining heme, so O2 is held tightly and not released — a double hit causing severe tissue hypoxia despite a normal PaO2 (dissolved O2 is unaffected). Classic source: smoke inhalation, faulty heaters, car exhaust. Skin is described as 'cherry-red' (often a late/postmortem sign). Symptoms: headache, confusion, syncope; check a COHb level by co-oximetry.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A normal pulse oximetry reading rules out CO poisoning.' Standard 2-wavelength pulse oximetry cannot distinguish COHb from O2Hb, so SpO2 reads FALSELY NORMAL — you must measure COHb with co-oximetry/ABG. PaO2 is also normal and misleading.
2. COHb treatment
Treatment of CO poisoning is high-flow 100% O2 by non-rebreather, which competitively displaces CO and shortens the COHb half-life from ~4–6 hours (room air) to ~60–90 minutes. Hyperbaric O2 (HBO) further shortens it to ~20–30 minutes and is considered for severe cases: loss of consciousness, neurologic deficits, COHb >25% (>15–20% in pregnancy), pregnancy with fetal distress, ischemic ECG/troponin, or severe acidosis — aiming to reduce delayed neurocognitive sequelae.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Methylene blue treats carbon monoxide poisoning.' Methylene blue is for methemoglobinemia, not COHb; CO is treated with 100% O2 ± hyperbaric oxygen.
3. Methemoglobin (MetHb)
Methemoglobin is hemoglobin with iron oxidized to the ferric Fe3+ state, which cannot bind O2; worse, it left-shifts the remaining ferrous heme, impairing O2 release → functional anemia/tissue hypoxia despite normal PaO2. Blood is classically chocolate-brown and does not turn red on exposure to air/O2. The hallmark is a 'saturation gap' (normal PaO2 but low SaO2 by co-oximetry) with SpO2 characteristically stuck around ~85% regardless of supplemental O2. Symptoms appear above ~15–20% MetHb; >50–70% can be lethal.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A low SpO2 that won't rise with 100% O2 means a shunt/severe lung disease.' A pulse ox plateauing near 85% with a NORMAL PaO2 (saturation gap) points to methemoglobinemia — confirm with co-oximetry, not a chest CT.
4. MetHb causes
Acquired methemoglobinemia is driven by oxidant exposures: dapsone (a top board cause), topical/local anesthetics (benzocaine spray, prilocaine/EMLA, lidocaine), nitrites and nitrates (amyl nitrite 'poppers', well-water nitrates in infants, nitroglycerin/nitroprusside), sulfonamides, and aniline dyes. Infants are especially susceptible (lower NADH-cytochrome b5 reductase activity). Congenital forms: cytochrome b5 reductase deficiency (autosomal recessive enzymatic) and Hb M (autosomal dominant globin variant).
⚠️ WRONG: 'Cyanosis in a baby on well water is congenital cyanotic heart disease.' Nitrate-contaminated well water causes acquired methemoglobinemia ('blue baby' from nitrates) — look for chocolate blood and a saturation gap before invoking a cardiac shunt.
5. Methylene blue treatment
Methylene blue (1–2 mg/kg IV) is first-line for symptomatic methemoglobinemia. It is reduced by NADPH-methemoglobin reductase (using NADPH generated by G6PD) to leukomethylene blue, which then non-enzymatically reduces Fe3+ back to functional Fe2+. Onset is within 30–60 minutes; redose if needed. Treat when MetHb is symptomatic or roughly >20–30% (or lower with cardiopulmonary disease).
⚠️ WRONG: 'Methylene blue directly donates electrons without needing NADPH/G6PD.' It works THROUGH the NADPH-dependent pathway (G6PD-supplied) — which is exactly why it fails and is dangerous in G6PD deficiency (next panel).
6. MetHb + G6PD danger
In G6PD deficiency methylene blue is BOTH ineffective and dangerous: without adequate NADPH, MB cannot be reduced to its active leukomethylene-blue form (so it doesn't reverse MetHb), and MB itself acts as an oxidant that can precipitate acute hemolysis. Use alternatives instead: high-dose ascorbic acid (vitamin C), and for severe/refractory cases exchange transfusion or hyperbaric O2.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Methylene blue is the universal antidote for methemoglobinemia in any patient.' In G6PD deficiency it is contraindicated (ineffective + can cause hemolysis) — switch to ascorbic acid or exchange transfusion.
7. Sulfhemoglobin (SulfHb)
Sulfhemoglobin forms when a sulfur atom is incorporated into the heme ring (often from the same oxidant drugs — sulfonamides, dapsone, metoclopramide, phenazopyridine). The bond is IRREVERSIBLE: the affected RBC stays abnormal for its entire ~120-day lifespan. Blood is greenish/dark, cyanosis is out of proportion to the measured level (even small amounts cause visible cyanosis), and co-oximetry may overestimate MetHb unless specifically corrected.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Greenish or dark blood with cyanosis is just methemoglobinemia.' Sulfhemoglobinemia (greenish blood) is a separate, IRREVERSIBLE dyshemoglobin — and unlike MetHb it does not respond to methylene blue.
8. SulfHb management
There is NO antidote for sulfhemoglobinemia. Management is to stop the offending oxidant and WAIT for normal red-cell turnover (~120 days) as new, unaffected RBCs replace the abnormal ones; transfusion can be used if the patient is symptomatic. Methylene blue is useless because the sulfur-heme modification is covalent/irreversible (contrast with MetHb, where MB works).
⚠️ WRONG: 'Methylene blue reverses sulfhemoglobinemia like it does methemoglobinemia.' SulfHb is irreversible — MB does not work; you remove the drug and wait out the RBC lifespan.
9. Hb M (congenital)
Hb M is inherited (autosomal DOMINANT) methemoglobinemia from a globin mutation in the heme pocket that locks iron in the Fe3+ state. It presents as lifelong, well-tolerated cyanosis (grey-blue) with a positive family history (pedigree) and is typically asymptomatic — patients are not in distress despite looking cyanotic. Because the defect is structural (globin), it does NOT respond to methylene blue and generally needs NO treatment.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Congenital cyanosis from Hb M should be treated urgently with methylene blue.' Hb M is a structural globin defect (not enzymatic), so MB is ineffective; it is well tolerated and requires no treatment — contrast with congenital cytochrome b5 reductase deficiency, which CAN respond to MB/ascorbic acid.
10. Master comparison chart
Unifying frame: COHb, MetHb, and SulfHb are dyshemoglobins that cause cyanosis/hypoxia while ABG PaO2 stays NORMAL (PaO2 reflects only dissolved O2, which these don't affect). Co-oximetry (multi-wavelength) is the key test that quantifies and distinguishes each species — ordinary 2-wavelength pulse oximetry is fooled (falsely normal in CO, plateaus ~85% in MetHb). Calculated SaO2 from PaO2 looks fine, producing a saturation gap.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A normal ABG PaO2 means oxygen delivery is adequate.' PaO2 is normal in all dyshemoglobinemias yet tissue O2 delivery is impaired — you must measure functional saturation with co-oximetry, not infer it from PaO2.
11. Trap: brown blood = MetHb
Board pearl: chocolate-brown blood that does NOT turn red on exposure to air/100% O2, plus cyanosis unresponsive to oxygen and a saturation gap, equals methemoglobinemia until proven otherwise. Look for an oxidant trigger (dapsone, benzocaine, nitrites). Confirm with co-oximetry and treat with methylene blue (unless G6PD-deficient).
⚠️ WRONG: 'Brown blood means it's old/venous and clinically meaningless.' Fresh chocolate-brown arterial blood that won't redden with O2 is a specific clue to methemoglobinemia — act on it.
12. Trap: false-normal SpO2 = COHb
Board pearl: a falsely NORMAL pulse-ox reading in a patient with headache, nausea, confusion, or a fire/smoke/heater exposure points to carbon monoxide poisoning — standard pulse oximetry cannot see COHb. Confirm with carboxyhemoglobin level (co-oximetry); treat with 100% O2 ± hyperbaric. Whole families/co-exposed individuals symptomatic together is a tell.
⚠️ WRONG: 'SpO2 of 99% excludes hypoxia, so CO poisoning is off the table.' Pulse oximetry reads COHb as if it were O2Hb (falsely high) — a normal SpO2 does NOT rule out CO; measure COHb.
13. Trap: high-affinity → ↑Hb
Board pearl: unexplained erythrocytosis (high Hb/Hct) with a LOW P50, NORMAL SaO2, and normal/high EPO but NO JAK2 mutation suggests a high-O2-affinity hemoglobin variant. The left-shifted curve withholds O2 from tissues, driving compensatory EPO and red-cell production. It is benign and compensatory — do NOT phlebotomize as you would polycythemia vera.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Erythrocytosis with normal SaO2 must be polycythemia vera — start phlebotomy and check JAK2.' High-affinity Hb (low P50, normal SaO2, no JAK2) is compensatory; phlebotomy worsens tissue O2 delivery.
14. Traps: thal mimics
Two co-inheritance pearls: (1) Iron overload (transfusion-dependent thalassemia, hemochromatosis) plus deferoxamine raises the risk of Yersinia enterocolitica infection (Yersinia is siderophilic and uses deferoxamine as a siderophore) — suspect with febrile diarrhea/sepsis. (2) Co-inherited α-thalassemia in sickle cell disease (HbSS + α-thal) reduces MCHC and lowers MCV, mildly ameliorating the sickling/hemolysis but increasing retinopathy — a microcytic SCD patient may have concurrent α-thal or iron deficiency.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A sickle cell patient with microcytosis just has iron deficiency.' Co-inherited α-thalassemia also lowers MCV in SCD (and tempers hemolysis) — don't assume iron deficiency without checking iron studies and considering α-thal.
