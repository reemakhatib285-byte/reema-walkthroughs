📖 Read-through — all 20 symbols
1. Triad: Uosm>100 + low urate + euvolemic = SIADH
SIADH is euvolemic HYPOtonic hyponatremia from inappropriate ADH: serum osm <275, urine osm >100 (usually >300), urine Na >30–40, clinically euvolemic, with low uric acid (<4) and low BUN from dilution + urate washout. Normal thyroid and adrenal function are required — it's a diagnosis of exclusion. Mnemonic: euvolemic + concentrated urine + low urate = SIADH.
⚠️ Don't call it SIADH before excluding hypothyroidism and adrenal insufficiency — both cause euvolemic hyponatremia that looks identical, and cortisol replacement (not fluid restriction) fixes adrenal cases.
2. SIAD Hospital (setting)
ADH (vasopressin) is released independent of osmolality, so the kidney retains free water, dilutes the serum, and produces concentrated urine. Water is retained but Na is not — total body water rises slightly while the patient stays clinically euvolemic. The result is dilutional hyponatremia, the most common cause of hyponatremia in hospitalized patients.
⚠️ SIADH is a water problem, not a salt-deficit problem — giving salt alone or normal saline can paradoxically worsen the Na if urine osm is high (desalination).
3. Urine osm >100 (often >300)
In SIADH the kidney can't dilute urine despite a hypotonic serum, so urine osm is inappropriately >100 mOsm/kg (commonly >300). This is the single key lab proving ADH is active when it shouldn't be.
⚠️ If urine osm is <100, ADH is appropriately suppressed — that's primary polydipsia or low-solute (beer potomania / tea-and-toast), NOT SIADH.
4. Urine Na >30–40
Because the patient is euvolemic (even mildly volume-expanded), the kidney excretes sodium normally, giving urine Na >30–40 mEq/L. This separates SIADH from hypovolemic hyponatremia.
⚠️ A low urine Na (<20) means the kidney is avidly retaining sodium → think true volume depletion, not SIADH (unless the patient is also salt-restricted).
5. Low uric acid (<4) & low BUN
Mild volume expansion increases urate and urea clearance, so SIADH classically shows hypouricemia (<4 mg/dL) and a low BUN — supportive dilutional markers.
⚠️ A HIGH uric acid / high BUN points to volume depletion (hypovolemic hyponatremia), not SIADH — don't ignore the urate.
6. Euvolemic — normal volume
No edema, no ascites, no orthostasis or dry mucosa — the exam is normal. AVP retains water but ongoing natriuresis prevents frank volume overload, so the patient is clinically euvolemic.
⚠️ Edema/ascites = HYPERvolemic hyponatremia (HF, cirrhosis, nephrotic); dry/orthostatic = HYPOvolemic — both are managed differently from SIADH, so always assess volume first.
7. Copeptin/AVP NOT for diagnosis
SIADH is confirmed by the osmolality/urine/volume pattern, not by measuring AVP. Plasma AVP and copeptin overlap too much with other states to be diagnostic and aren't part of the routine criteria.
⚠️ Ordering an AVP or copeptin level to 'prove' SIADH is the wrong move — the diagnosis is made from serum osm, urine osm, urine Na, volume status, and normal thyroid/adrenal/renal function.
8. SCLC — #1 malignancy cause
Small cell lung cancer is the classic ectopic ADH producer and the most common malignant cause of SIADH (paraneoplastic). New SIADH in a smoker should prompt a chest CT.
⚠️ SQUAMOUS cell lung cancer causes hypercalcemia via PTHrP — not SIADH; don't swap the two paraneoplastic syndromes.
9. Pulmonary — PNA, TB, abscess
Lung infections (pneumonia, TB, lung abscess) and positive-pressure ventilation are common pulmonary triggers of SIADH. Legionella pneumonia in particular is associated with hyponatremia.
⚠️ Hyponatremia with a 'pneumonia' that also has diarrhea and high fever + relative bradycardia points to Legionella — don't treat with a beta-lactam alone (needs a macrolide/fluoroquinolone).
10. CNS — meningitis, SAH, trauma
CNS disease (meningitis, subarachnoid hemorrhage, head trauma, stroke, tumors) is a major SIADH cause. SIADH is treated with fluid restriction.
⚠️ In SAH/CNS injury, distinguish SIADH (euvolemic/hypervolemic → fluid-restrict) from cerebral salt wasting (HYPOvolemic → give salt + saline) — the treatments are opposite, and over-restricting a CSW patient worsens cerebral ischemia.
11. Post-op, pain, nausea
Pain, nausea, and the post-operative state are powerful NON-osmotic stimuli for ADH release. Combined with hypotonic IV fluids, they drive hospital-acquired hyponatremia.
⚠️ Running D5W or half-normal saline in a post-op/nauseated patient is a classic cause of iatrogenic hyponatremia — use isotonic (0.9% saline/LR) for maintenance instead.
12. SSRIs
SSRIs (and SNRIs) are among the most common drug causes of SIADH, especially in the elderly within the first weeks of starting. Check a sodium a few weeks after initiation in older patients.
⚠️ Attributing an elderly patient's confusion/hyponatremia to 'old age' instead of a recently started SSRI misses a reversible cause — stop the drug.
13. Carbamazepine — thirst + AVP
Carbamazepine and oxcarbazepine potentiate ADH action and stimulate thirst, a frequent drug cause of SIADH. Other culprits: antipsychotics, MDMA/ecstasy, and desmopressin.
⚠️ MDMA (ecstasy) hyponatremia is a double hit — SIADH plus large free-water intake; treat the acute severe symptomatic case with hypertonic saline, not just fluid restriction.
14. Chemo — cyclophosphamide
Cyclophosphamide (especially high-dose IV), vincristine, and cisplatin cause SIADH. With IV cyclophosphamide the standard pre-hydration can precipitate dangerous hyponatremia.
⚠️ Pushing extra free water to 'protect the bladder' during IV cyclophosphamide can worsen SIADH hyponatremia — use isotonic fluids and watch the sodium.
15. Step 1: fluid restriction <1 L/day
First-line for chronic, mildly symptomatic SIADH is fluid restriction to <800–1000 mL/day. Add oral salt tabs ± a loop diuretic to raise free-water excretion if restriction alone is insufficient.
⚠️ Fluid restriction FAILS when urine osm is very high (>500) or when urine (Na+K) exceeds serum Na (negative electrolyte-free water clearance) — recognize this and escalate rather than restricting harder.
16. Urea (osmotic free-water loss)
Oral urea 15–30 g/day induces an osmotic diuresis that excretes free water and raises sodium; it's effective, cheap, and low-risk for overcorrection (palatability is the main limit).
⚠️ Don't dismiss urea as obsolete — guidelines favor it (and salt + loop) over demeclocycline, which is nephrotoxic and slow.
17. Tolvaptan / vaptans (V2 antagonist)
Vaptans block the V2 receptor → pure aquaresis (free water out, Na retained), raising serum Na. Start in hospital, do NOT fluid-restrict during initiation, limit tolvaptan to <30 days (hepatotoxicity), and it's expensive.
⚠️ Combining a vaptan with fluid restriction, or using it for chronic mild SIADH, risks rapid OVERcorrection → osmotic demyelination; reserve it and monitor Na closely.
18. Demeclocycline — NOT recommended
Demeclocycline induces nephrogenic DI (blocks ADH at the collecting duct) but is now discouraged: slow onset, nephrotoxic, and unpredictable. Largely replaced by urea and vaptans.
⚠️ Picking demeclocycline as a go-to SIADH drug is the wrong answer on modern exams — choose fluid restriction → salt/urea → vaptan instead.
19. Severe/symptomatic → 3% saline
For severe SYMPTOMATIC hyponatremia (seizures, obtundation, coma), give 3% hypertonic saline 100–150 mL boluses to raise Na ~4–6 mEq/L over the first few hours, then stop. Total correction must stay <8 mEq/L in 24 h.
⚠️ Correcting chronic hyponatremia too fast (>8 mEq/L/24h) causes osmotic demyelination syndrome (central pontine myelinolysis) — if you overshoot, re-lower with D5W ± desmopressin.
20. SGLT2 inhibitor (emerging)
Empagliflozin causes glucosuria and an osmotic diuresis that excretes free water, raising serum Na — an emerging adjunct for SIADH in trials. Watch for euglycemic DKA and genital infections.
⚠️ It's adjunctive/emerging, not first-line — fluid restriction (± salt/urea) remains the initial chronic-SIADH treatment.
