📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Poor dentition / periodontal source
Poor oral hygiene and periodontal disease provide the anaerobic bacterial inoculum for aspiration lung abscess. More teeth/plaque means higher anaerobic burden. Edentulous patients rarely form anaerobic abscess (suspect obstruction/cancer instead).
⚠️ An edentulous patient with a 'lung abscess' should raise suspicion for an obstructing tumor — anaerobic abscess needs a dental source.
2. Aspiration / unconscious
Risk factors are impaired consciousness and impaired swallow: alcohol, seizures, stroke, anesthesia, dysphagia. Aspirated oropharyngeal anaerobes seed dependent lung segments.
⚠️ Loss of consciousness (alcohol/seizure) is the classic setup — primary abscess is the result of aspiration, not hematogenous spread.
3. Dependent segments (RUL post, RLL sup)
Aspiration in the supine position seeds gravity-dependent segments: posterior segment of the right upper lobe and superior segment of the right lower lobe. The right bronchus is more vertical, so the right lung is favored.
⚠️ Right-sided, dependent-segment location is classic for aspiration abscess — left-sided or atypical location should prompt other diagnoses.
4. Anaerobes / polymicrobial
Primary lung abscess is typically polymicrobial anaerobic (Peptostreptococcus, Prevotella, Bacteroides, Fusobacterium) with synergy. Tissue necrosis produces the cavity over 7–14 days.
⚠️ Anaerobic abscess produces foul/putrid sputum; cultures are often unhelpful because of contamination — treat empirically for anaerobes.
5. Putrid sputum / no abscess clue
Foul-smelling (putrid) sputum strongly suggests anaerobic lung abscess. Conversely, a toothless/edentulous patient lacks the anaerobic source, so abscess is unlikely — look for obstruction or another cause.
⚠️ Edentulous + cavity = think malignancy/obstruction, NOT anaerobic abscess. The board trap is assuming all cavities are infectious.
6. CXR — air-fluid level
Chest X-ray shows a thick-walled cavity with an air-fluid level. A round opacity that cavitates over days is characteristic. Air-fluid level indicates communication with the airway.
⚠️ An air-fluid level on CXR indicates a cavity communicating with bronchus (abscess) — distinguish from a fluid-filled empyema, which needs drainage not just antibiotics.
7. CT — abscess vs empyema
CT differentiates lung abscess (round, thick irregular wall, acute angle with pleura) from empyema (lenticular, smooth wall, 'split pleura' sign). This distinction changes management: drain empyema, usually don't drain abscess.
⚠️ Empyema needs chest-tube drainage; an uncomplicated abscess usually drains spontaneously via the airway and is managed with antibiotics alone — the CT split-pleura sign separates them.
8. Amphoric breath / clubbing
Examination may reveal amphoric/cavernous breath sounds over the cavity and digital clubbing with chronicity. Chronic suppuration drives clubbing.
⚠️ Clubbing implies chronicity (weeks); an acute presentation without clubbing does not exclude abscess but suggests a shorter course.
9. Clindamycin > penicillin
Historically clindamycin outperformed penicillin for anaerobic lung abscess due to beta-lactamase–producing anaerobes; ampicillin-sulbactam or amoxicillin-clavulanate are now first-line. Metronidazole alone fails (misses microaerophilic streptococci).
⚠️ Metronidazole monotherapy fails lung abscess — it doesn't cover aerobic/microaerophilic streptococci; always combine or use a beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitor.
10. Ampicillin-sulbactam / moxifloxacin
Preferred regimens: ampicillin-sulbactam, amoxicillin-clavulanate, or moxifloxacin. These cover the polymicrobial anaerobic-aerobic mix. Carbapenems for resistant/complicated cases.
⚠️ Choose a beta-lactamase inhibitor combination or moxifloxacin — a plain beta-lactam may be inactivated by beta-lactamase–producing anaerobes.
11. Prolonged duration / defervesce <7d
Therapy is prolonged — typically several weeks until cavity closure (often 3–8+ weeks), guided by radiographic resolution. Fever should resolve within ~7 days; persistent fever suggests inadequate drainage or wrong diagnosis.
⚠️ Stopping antibiotics when the patient feels better (a few days) causes relapse — treat for weeks until imaging shows resolution.
12. Drainage if >6 cm / no response
Most abscesses drain via the bronchus and respond to antibiotics. Percutaneous or bronchoscopic drainage is reserved for failure of medical therapy, very large cavities (>6 cm), or sepsis. Surgery is rarely needed.
⚠️ Routine drainage is NOT first-line for primary lung abscess — most resolve with antibiotics; intervene only for failure, large size, or hemorrhage.
13. Right-sided endocarditis source
Septic emboli from right-sided (tricuspid) infective endocarditis — classically in IV drug users with S. aureus — produce multiple peripheral cavitary lung lesions, distinct from primary aspiration abscess.
⚠️ Multiple bilateral peripheral cavities suggest septic emboli from tricuspid endocarditis (IVDU), not single aspiration abscess — get blood cultures and echo.
14. Lemierre / jugular vein septic emboli
Lemierre syndrome: Fusobacterium necrophorum oropharyngeal infection causes septic internal jugular vein thrombophlebitis, showering septic emboli to the lungs. Suspect with recent pharyngitis and neck tenderness.
⚠️ Lemierre (Fusobacterium, jugular thrombophlebitis) is the trap for septic pulmonary emboli after a sore throat — not simple aspiration.
15. Primary vs secondary mortality
Primary (aspiration) abscess has low mortality (~2%) and responds well to antibiotics. Secondary abscess (obstruction, immunocompromise, resistant organisms) carries far higher mortality (up to ~75%).
⚠️ The key prognostic split is primary vs secondary — secondary abscess (tumor/immunosuppression) is the deadly one and demands a search for the underlying cause.
