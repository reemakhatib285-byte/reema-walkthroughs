📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Swaddled neonate (gold star)
Neonatal meningitis (<1 month) is caused by Group B Strep (S. agalactiae), E. coli, and Listeria. Empiric therapy is ampicillin PLUS cefotaxime (or ampicillin + gentamicin) — NOT ceftriaxone, which displaces bilirubin and risks kernicterus. GBS is acquired from maternal vaginal colonization; intrapartum penicillin after 35-37wk screening prevents early-onset disease.
2. Sausage baby in bassinet (GBS)
Group B Streptococcus (S. agalactiae) is the leading cause of NEONATAL meningitis (early-onset from maternal colonization) and is rising in older adults with diabetes/cirrhosis. It is a gram-positive coccus in chains, beta-hemolytic, CAMP-test and hippurate positive. Intrapartum GBS prophylaxis prevents early- but not late-onset neonatal disease.
3. Crossed-out teddy bear (Hib, vaccine)
Hib was historically a top cause of childhood meningitis but is now RARE in vaccinated children due to the conjugate vaccine. Consider it in unvaccinated/under-vaccinated kids, and in adults with CSF leak, sinusitis/otitis, or asplenia. It is a small gram-negative coccobacillus needing factors X and V.
⚠️ "complete vaccine sticker rules out Hib" -> wrong; verify TRUE immunization status — unvaccinated/partially vaccinated kids still get Hib, and non-type-b strains aren't vaccine-covered.
4. Nurse with crossed-out syringe
Ceftriaxone is AVOIDED in neonates: it displaces bilirubin (kernicterus) and precipitates with calcium-containing IV fluids. Use cefotaxime, or ampicillin + gentamicin, for the newborn instead. The neonatal pathogen trio (GBS, E. coli, Listeria) all need ampicillin on board.
⚠️ "ceftriaxone for the neonate" -> wrong; causes kernicterus and calcium precipitation — substitute cefotaxime or ampicillin + gentamicin.
5. Pneumococcus suspect (rash teen + diplococci)
Streptococcus pneumoniae is the #1 cause of bacterial meningitis in adults/elderly — gram-positive, lancet-shaped diplococci, alpha-hemolytic, optochin-sensitive, bile-soluble. It carries the HIGHEST case-fatality (~20-30%) with frequent sequelae. Empiric coverage is ceftriaxone PLUS vancomycin (resistant strains); dexamethasone has its strongest benefit here.
⚠️ "N. meningitidis is the most common adult cause" -> wrong; meningococcus dominates outbreaks/young adults, but pneumococcus is the single most common cause overall in adults.
6. Pneumococcus risk: alcohol & asplenia
Pneumococcal risk factors: basilar skull fracture/CSF leak (recurrent meningitis), alcoholism, ASPLENIA/sickle cell, diabetes, multiple myeloma/hypogammaglobulinemia, cochlear implants. Asplenic/sickle patients are vulnerable to ALL encapsulated bugs (pneumococcus, meningococcus, Hib) and must be vaccinated.
⚠️ "recurrent pneumococcal meningitis = just re-treat" -> wrong; recurrence signals an anatomic CSF leak (basilar fracture, cribriform defect) or complement/Ig deficiency — hunt for it.
7. Detective in fedora (N. meningitidis)
Neisseria meningitidis leads in ADOLESCENTS/young adults, especially crowded settings (dorms, barracks) — gram-negative diplococci that ferment maltose AND glucose, grow on Thayer-Martin. Serogroups B/C/W/Y predominate; MenACWY and MenB are recommended for adolescents, college freshmen in dorms, recruits, and asplenic/complement-deficient patients. Endotoxin drives DIC and Waterhouse-Friderichsen (adrenal hemorrhage, shock).
⚠️ "petechial rash + fever = benign viral exanthem" -> wrong; a non-blanching petechial/purpuric rash with fever and meningismus is meningococcemia until proven otherwise — immediate antibiotics, droplet isolation, contact prophylaxis.
8. Complement deficiency (skull/MAC)
Terminal complement deficiency (C5-C9, the MAC) and complement inhibitors (eculizumab, ravulizumab) markedly predispose to recurrent/invasive Neisseria, because MAC is required to lyse Neisseria. These patients need meningococcal vaccination and sometimes prophylactic antibiotics.
⚠️ "don't work up immune deficiency after recurrent Neisseria" -> wrong; recurrent meningococcal disease should trigger a CH50/complement workup for terminal complement deficiency.
9. Listeria suspect (purple rod villain)
Listeria monocytogenes is a gram-positive ROD with tumbling motility and actin-based 'rocket' propulsion, causing meningitis in neonates, adults >50, pregnant women, and the immunocompromised; it favors the brainstem (rhombencephalitis). It is intrinsically resistant to ALL cephalosporins. Treat with ampicillin (+ gentamicin for synergy), or TMP-SMX if penicillin-allergic.
⚠️ "ceftriaxone + vancomycin covers everything" -> wrong; cephalosporins have ZERO Listeria activity — failing to ADD ampicillin in a Listeria-risk host is a fatal omission.
10. Listeria food: deli meat & soft cheese
Listeria is foodborne via deli/processed meats, unpasteurized soft cheeses, hot dogs, pate, smoked seafood, and contaminated coleslaw/produce. It uniquely multiplies at refrigeration temperatures (cold enrichment, 4°C). Pregnant women avoid these foods due to transplacental infection, miscarriage, and neonatal listeriosis (granulomatosis infantiseptica).
⚠️ "it was refrigerated, so not Listeria" -> wrong; Listeria grows at fridge temperatures — 'kept cold' is no reassurance, the cold-deli history is a positive clue.
11. Listeria host: elderly + immunocompromised
Add ampicillin for Listeria in the classic hosts: <1 month (neonate), >50 years, pregnancy, and impaired cell-mediated immunity (transplant, chronic steroids, TNF inhibitors, HIV, malignancy). Listeria is intracellular and controlled by T cells, so any cell-mediated defect raises risk.
⚠️ "only neonates and the very elderly need Listeria coverage" -> wrong; pregnant women and any cell-mediated-immunity defect (steroids, transplant, HIV) are equally at risk regardless of age.
12. Cooler / lunchbox (refrigeration cue)
The cooler reinforces that Listeria survives and grows at 4°C, which is why refrigeration does not exclude it and why outbreaks trace to chilled ready-to-eat foods. Empiric ampicillin must accompany ceftriaxone + vancomycin whenever the CSF Gram stain shows a gram-positive ROD or the host is high-risk.
13. Mortality ranking (height chart)
Approximate case-fatality ranking: S. pneumoniae ~20-30% (highest), Listeria ~15-20%, H. influenzae ~5%, N. meningitidis ~3-7% (lowest when treated). Mortality reflects host and virulence; pneumococcal survivors often have lasting neurologic deficits.
⚠️ "meningococcus is the deadliest because of its rash" -> wrong; treated meningococcal meningitis has comparatively LOW case fatality — pneumococcus and Listeria are deadlier per case.
14. Empiric regimen by age (bottom rule)
Age-based empiric therapy: NEONATES → ampicillin + cefotaxime (or + gentamicin); 1mo-50yr → ceftriaxone + vancomycin; >50yr or immunocompromised → ceftriaxone + vancomycin + AMPICILLIN. Bottom line: gram-positive RODS or a Listeria-risk host means ADD AMPICILLIN — cephalosporins miss Listeria entirely.
⚠️ "ceftriaxone + vancomycin alone for an elderly/immunocompromised patient" -> wrong; this pair misses Listeria — omitting ampicillin in neonates, >50, pregnant, alcoholic, or immunocompromised is a classic fatal error.
