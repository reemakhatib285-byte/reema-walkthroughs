📖 Read-through — all 17 symbols
1. Child-Pugh vs MELD board
The two cirrhosis severity scores are Child-Pugh (CTP) and MELD. Child-Pugh ranges 5-15 points and contains SUBJECTIVE clinical items (ascites, encephalopathy graded by the clinician). MELD is fully OBJECTIVE (laboratory-only) and ranges ~6-40, which is why it (as MELD-Na) is used for organ ALLOCATION/waitlist prioritization, while Child-Pugh is a bedside prognostic estimate.
⚠️ WRONG answer: using Child-Pugh to prioritize the transplant waiting list. Discriminator: organ allocation uses the objective MELD-Na (creatinine, bilirubin, INR, sodium); Child-Pugh's subjective ascites/encephalopathy make it unsuitable for ranking candidates fairly.
2. Child-Pugh subjective items
Child-Pugh's two SUBJECTIVE components are ascites and hepatic encephalopathy, each graded none/mild/moderate-severe. These clinician-dependent items are precisely what MELD deliberately leaves out to keep allocation objective.
⚠️ WRONG answer: thinking MELD includes ascites or encephalopathy. Discriminator: ascites and encephalopathy belong to CHILD-PUGH only; MELD uses creatinine, bilirubin, INR (+Na for MELD-Na) — no clinical/subjective variables.
3. MELD objective items
MELD is calculated from three objective labs — creatinine (renal function), total bilirubin, and INR — and MELD-Na adds serum sodium because hyponatremia independently predicts mortality in cirrhosis. The inclusion of creatinine is why hepatorenal syndrome sharply raises the MELD score.
⚠️ WRONG answer: omitting sodium or adding albumin to MELD. Discriminator: modern allocation uses MELD-Na (Cr, bilirubin, INR, Na); albumin is a Child-Pugh component, NOT a MELD variable.
4. Child classes A/B/C
Child-Pugh classes: A = 5-6 points (well-compensated, ~1-year survival ~100%), B = 7-9 (significant compromise, ~80%), C = 10-15 (decompensated, ~45%). The class guides surgical risk and broad prognosis.
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming a Child-Pugh A patient is safe for major elective abdominal/hepatic surgery without further thought. Discriminator: even Child A carries elevated perioperative mortality, and Child C is often a contraindication to elective surgery — use the class to assess operative risk, and MELD for waitlist priority.
5. Transplant criterion ≥7
A Child-Pugh score >=7 (class B) is the traditional threshold to begin transplant EVALUATION/referral, marking the transition from compensated to clinically significant disease. It flags who should be referred, not who is immediately listed.
⚠️ WRONG answer: treating Child-Pugh >=7 as the score that determines actual list priority/timing. Discriminator: >=7 triggers referral for evaluation, but listing and ranking are by MELD-Na — once referred, the objective MELD score (esp. >=15) drives timing.
6. Left judge: Child-Pugh
The Child-Pugh 'judge' combines five components: prothrombin time/INR, albumin, bilirubin, ascites, and encephalopathy. Two are objective labs (PT, albumin, bilirubin) and two are subjective (ascites, encephalopathy) — the blend is why it is a prognostic, not an allocation, tool.
⚠️ WRONG answer: leaving out bilirubin or albumin when listing Child-Pugh components. Discriminator: remember all five via PABASE (PT, Albumin, Bilirubin, Ascites, Encephalopathy); dropping bilirubin/albumin is the common partial-recall error.
7. PABASE mnemonic
PABASE = PT (INR), Albumin, Bilirubin, Ascites, Encephalopathy — the five Child-Pugh components. Note bilirubin and INR are SHARED with MELD, while albumin/ascites/encephalopathy are unique to Child-Pugh.
⚠️ WRONG answer: adding creatinine or sodium to PABASE. Discriminator: creatinine and sodium are MELD-Na variables, NOT Child-Pugh — Child-Pugh has no renal term, which is one reason MELD (with creatinine) better captures the sickest, often hepatorenal, patients.
8. Center judge: MELD-Na
The MELD-Na 'judge' prioritizes deceased-donor organ ALLOCATION using objective labs (creatinine, bilirubin, INR, sodium). Because it is purely quantitative it is harder to game and ranks candidates by predicted short-term mortality — the sickest objective scores go first.
⚠️ WRONG answer: thinking the patient with the worst symptoms (massive ascites, encephalopathy) automatically gets priority. Discriminator: allocation is by MELD-Na numbers, not symptom burden; some severely symptomatic Child-Pugh patients can still have a relatively low MELD, and 'exception points' (e.g., for HCC) are added separately.
9. MELD predicts mortality
MELD predicts 90-day mortality on the waitlist and is the basis for the transplant-benefit threshold: survival benefit from transplantation generally emerges when MELD >15, whereas transplanting at MELD <15 tends to incur EXCESS mortality compared with waiting (the operative/immunosuppression risk outweighs the disease risk).
⚠️ WRONG answer: listing/transplanting a low-MELD (<15) patient on the assumption that earlier transplant is always better. Discriminator: below MELD 15 the surgical/immunosuppression risk exceeds the benefit — these patients do better waiting; benefit accrues above 15.
10. MELD <15 = WAIT
A MELD <15 means outcomes are better by continuing to WAIT and monitor rather than transplanting now — the predicted post-transplant risk outweighs the benefit. These patients are managed medically with ongoing surveillance and re-scoring.
⚠️ WRONG answer: placing a MELD <15 patient on the active transplant list based on the score alone. Discriminator: a low MELD argues for waiting; the exception is HCC or other 'MELD exception' indications, where standardized exception points override the raw lab MELD.
11. MELD 12 example
Worked example: a MELD of 12 is below the 15 threshold, so the disposition is to continue to wait, optimize medically, and re-score periodically rather than transplant.
⚠️ WRONG answer: prioritizing the MELD-12 patient over someone with a higher MELD or an HCC exception. Discriminator: 12 is below the benefit threshold — they wait; a MELD-12 with no exception does not outrank higher-MELD or exception-point candidates.
12. Encephalopathy crossed (MELD)
This crossed-out item reinforces that MELD deliberately EXCLUDES the subjective encephalopathy and ascites that Child-Pugh relies on, keeping allocation objective and reproducible across centers.
⚠️ WRONG answer: expecting a patient's encephalopathy/ascites to raise their MELD score. Discriminator: those are Child-Pugh-only; to capture sickness MELD instead uses creatinine, bilirubin, INR and sodium — clinical decompensation may not move the MELD number.
13. Right judge: Factor V
Factor V is the BEST marker of synthetic function in ACUTE liver failure: it is liver-synthesized, has a short half-life (~12-36 h) so it tracks rapid changes, and is NOT vitamin-K-dependent so it isn't confounded by vitamin K status. In fulminant failure a very low Factor V predicts poor prognosis (a component of the King's College / Clichy criteria).
⚠️ WRONG answer: using albumin or even raw PT/INR as the best acute synthetic marker. Discriminator: albumin is too slow (~20-day half-life) and PT/INR is confounded by vitamin K/transfusion — Factor V is the cleanest real-time readout of acute hepatic synthesis.
14. Factor V chained
Factor V reflects TRUE liver synthesis and is independent of vitamin K — unlike the vitamin-K-dependent factors (II, VII, IX, X) that prolong PT/INR. So if a prolonged PT corrects after vitamin K, the problem was deficiency/malabsorption; if Factor V is low, the liver itself is failing to synthesize.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing a low Factor V to vitamin K deficiency. Discriminator: Factor V does NOT depend on vitamin K and will NOT correct with it — a low Factor V means genuine hepatic synthetic failure, the key distinction from cholestatic vitamin-K malabsorption.
15. PT/INR useless acute
In ACUTE liver failure, PT/INR is confounded — vitamin K administration, fresh frozen plasma/transfusion, and concurrent coagulopathy all distort it — so it is unreliable for tracking synthetic function moment-to-moment. Factor V is preferred in that setting.
⚠️ WRONG answer: trusting a 'corrected' INR after FFP/vitamin K as evidence the liver is recovering. Discriminator: FFP and vitamin K artificially normalize INR without improving hepatic synthesis — follow Factor V instead to judge true recovery in acute failure.
16. Albumin too slow
Albumin has a long serum half-life (~18-21 days), so it changes too slowly to reflect ACUTE liver failure — it is a useful marker of CHRONIC synthetic function (and a Child-Pugh component) but lags behind acute events by weeks.
⚠️ WRONG answer: using albumin to assess or trend acute/fulminant liver failure. Discriminator: albumin's ~20-day half-life makes it useless acutely (and it is also lowered by nephrotic syndrome, malnutrition, inflammation) — use Factor V or PT/INR-context for acute synthetic assessment, reserve albumin for chronic.
17. Child-Pugh juror cards
The jurors holding 'Child-Pugh 7' represent class B (>=7), the chronic-disease threshold that prompts transplant EVALUATION/referral. It anchors the chronic side of the court (Child-Pugh for chronic decompensated cirrhosis) against the acute side (Factor V for acute liver failure).
⚠️ WRONG answer: confusing the indications — applying Child-Pugh/MELD logic to acute liver failure or Factor V/King's College logic to chronic cirrhosis. Discriminator: chronic decompensated cirrhosis is staged/referred by Child-Pugh and listed by MELD-Na; ACUTE liver failure is assessed by Factor V and King's College criteria — and recall transplant indications (decompensated cirrhosis, acute liver failure, HCC within Milan criteria: single tumor <=5 cm or up to 3 tumors each <=3 cm, no vascular invasion) versus contraindications (active extrahepatic malignancy, uncontrolled sepsis, severe cardiopulmonary disease, active substance use).
