📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. IgM monoclonal protein
WM = lymphoplasmacytic lymphoma secreting monoclonal IgM. Large pentameric IgM stays intravascular and drives hyperviscosity. Defined by IgM M-spike + bone-marrow lymphoplasmacytic infiltration.
⚠️ Don't call it multiple myeloma. MM secretes IgG/IgA/light-chain and causes lytic bone lesions; WM secretes IgM with NO lytic lesions and NO hypercalcemia.
2. Hyperviscosity syndrome
High serum IgM raises viscosity: headache, dizziness, ataxia, drowsiness, blurred vision. Symptoms typically appear once serum viscosity >4 centipoise. The classic mucosal bleeding plus neuro triad.
⚠️ Hyperviscosity is the WM emergency, not tumor bulk. Symptoms correlate with IgM/viscosity level, not marrow plasma-cell percentage.
3. Retinal 'sausage' veins
Fundoscopy shows dilated, tortuous, segmented ('sausage-link' / boxcar) retinal veins, flame hemorrhages, papilledema — the hallmark sign of hyperviscosity.
⚠️ Sausage-link retinal veins point to IgM hyperviscosity (WM), not diabetic or hypertensive retinopathy.
4. MYD88 L265P mutation
MYD88 L265P is present in >90% of WM and activates NF-κB via BTK signaling. It is the rationale for BTK-inhibitor therapy (ibrutinib) and helps distinguish WM from other IgM gammopathies.
⚠️ MYD88 mutant + CXCR4 WT predicts the best ibrutinib response; CXCR4 mutation blunts and delays ibrutinib response.
5. CXCR4 mutation
CXCR4 WHIM-like mutations occur in ~30-40% of WM. They confer drug resistance and predict delayed/blunted response to ibrutinib, and higher IgM/hyperviscosity burden.
⚠️ CXCR4 mutation = worse, slower ibrutinib response — the opposite of MYD88-mutant/CXCR4-WT patients.
6. Ibrutinib (BTK inhibitor)
Ibrutinib blocks BTK downstream of mutant MYD88; ~91% response in WM. First-line oral option, especially for symptomatic disease or hyperviscosity not needing immediate plasmapheresis.
⚠️ Response depends on genotype: best in MYD88-mut/CXCR4-WT, weakest in MYD88-WT.
7. Plasmapheresis
Plasmapheresis rapidly removes intravascular IgM and is FIRST-LINE for acute symptomatic hyperviscosity. It is a bridge — it does not treat the clone, so definitive therapy must follow.
⚠️ For acute hyperviscosity, plasmapheresis comes BEFORE/with starting chemo — don't wait for rituximab to work.
8. Avoid single-agent rituximab (IgM flare)
Rituximab can trigger a transient IgM 'flare' (rise) that worsens hyperviscosity. In patients with high IgM, hold rituximab or plasmapherese first / use combination regimens.
⚠️ Giving rituximab alone to a high-IgM WM patient can precipitate hyperviscosity crisis — a classic exam pitfall.
9. Bendamustine–rituximab / DRC
Chemoimmunotherapy (bendamustine-rituximab, or dexamethasone-rituximab-cyclophosphamide) is standard for symptomatic WM, especially with bulky disease. Alkylator-based regimens give durable responses.
⚠️ Asymptomatic WM is observed; treat only for symptoms (hyperviscosity, anemia, B-symptoms), not for IgM level alone.
10. Cold agglutinins / cryoglobulins
Monoclonal IgM can act as a cold agglutinin (Coombs positive, cold-reactive) causing hemolysis, or as type I cryoglobulin causing acrocyanosis. Warm the patient/keep samples warm.
⚠️ WM hemolysis is cold-agglutinin mediated (IgM, C3+ DAT, worse in cold) — not warm AIHA.
11. Anemia (most common cytopenia)
Anemia is the most common indication to treat WM, from marrow infiltration plus dilutional effect of high IgM expanding plasma volume. Often with fatigue and constitutional symptoms.
⚠️ Anemia in WM is multifactorial (marrow + dilution from plasma volume expansion), so Hb can look falsely low.
12. Hepatosplenomegaly / lymphadenopathy
Because WM is a lymphoplasmacytic LYMPHOMA, organomegaly and lymphadenopathy occur — features myeloma lacks. Reflects tissue infiltration by the lymphoplasmacytic clone.
⚠️ Lymphadenopathy/HSM favor WM over myeloma; their presence argues against pure plasma-cell myeloma.
13. Peripheral neuropathy (anti-MAG)
IgM can target myelin-associated glycoprotein (anti-MAG), producing a distal demyelinating sensory neuropathy. Distinct mechanism from amyloid or compressive neuropathy.
⚠️ WM neuropathy is often anti-MAG demyelinating — test for it; it differs from POEMS or amyloid neuropathy.
14. BM lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate
Diagnosis requires ≥10% clonal lymphoplasmacytic cells in marrow plus IgM monoclonal protein of any level. Cells show CD19/CD20+ with plasmacytoid differentiation.
⚠️ WM marrow is lymphoplasmacytic (small B-cells + plasma cells), not sheets of pure plasma cells as in myeloma.
15. No lytic bone lesions
Unlike myeloma, WM characteristically spares bone — no lytic lesions, no hypercalcemia, and renal involvement is rare. A key MM-vs-WM discriminator.
⚠️ If the IgM gammopathy patient has lytic lesions/hypercalcemia, reconsider IgM myeloma (rare) rather than WM.
