📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Fibrofatty RV replacement
ARVC pathology: desmosomal gene mutations cause myocyte detachment, death, and progressive replacement of RV myocardium by adipose tissue and fibrous scar. The fibrofatty scar is an arrhythmogenic substrate. The right side of the car crumbling = the RV free wall degenerating.
⚠️ It is fibroFATTY replacement, not simple dilation — distinguishing ARVC from dilated cardiomyopathy, where the problem is global pump failure rather than a scar-based arrhythmic substrate.
2. Arrhythmia before EF drop
Key board concept: in ARVC, ventricular arrhythmias appear BEFORE EF drops. Arrhythmia (palpitations, syncope, SCD) is the presenting manifestation, not heart failure. By the time RV dysfunction is overt, disease is already advanced. This sequence drives risk stratification.
⚠️ Do not wait for a low EF to act — unlike DCM, a normal/preserved EF does NOT reassure in ARVC because lethal VT can occur with structurally early disease.
3. Exertional SCD in the young
ARVC is a leading cause of exertional sudden cardiac death in young athletes. Exercise promotes myocyte detachment at desmosomes under mechanical stress, accelerating disease and triggering VT. This is why exercise restriction is a cornerstone of management.
⚠️ A young athlete with exertional syncope/SCD and RV findings is ARVC — not benign 'athlete's heart'; the discriminator is arrhythmia + RV structural/ECG abnormality, not deconditioning.
4. LBBB-morphology VT
VT originating in the RV produces LBBB morphology on ECG, because the impulse travels left across the interventricular septum. LBBB-pattern VT localizes the focus to the RV and is highly suggestive of ARVC in the right clinical context.
⚠️ RBBB-morphology VT points to an LV origin instead — so LBBB-morphology VT is the discriminator that flags the RV (ARVC), not an LV cardiomyopathy.
5. Epsilon wave / T-inversions V1–V3
Resting-ECG clues for ARVC: an epsilon wave (small terminal deflection after QRS in V1–V3) and T-wave inversions in V1–V3 (right precordial leads). These reflect delayed RV depolarization and repolarization over fibrofatty scar and are diagnostic-criteria features.
⚠️ T-inversions V1–V3 in an adult are abnormal and suggest ARVC — do not dismiss them as the persistent juvenile pattern, which should have resolved by adulthood.
6. ICD even if EF >35%
Critical board rule: in ARVC an ICD may be indicated even with preserved EF (>35%) when arrhythmic risk is high (sustained VT, prior cardiac arrest, high-risk features). Arrhythmic risk — not EF alone — drives the decision, unlike DCM.
⚠️ Applying the DCM threshold (ICD only if EF ≤35%) to ARVC is the trap — in ARVC you can implant an ICD with normal EF because the risk is arrhythmic, not pump-failure, death.
7. Family history of SCD
Family history of sudden cardiac death is a major risk factor. ARVC desmosomal variants follow autosomal dominant inheritance with incomplete penetrance and variable expression. A positive family history both supports the diagnosis and raises arrhythmic risk.
⚠️ Incomplete penetrance means an unaffected-appearing parent can still carry and transmit the mutation — a negative-seeming family exam does not exclude inherited ARVC.
8. Mimics: sarcoidosis & Chagas
Cardiac sarcoidosis and Chagas disease can produce identical RV dysfunction and arrhythmias. Both are treatable but with different approaches (immunosuppression for sarcoid; antiparasitic/HF care for Chagas). When ARVC is suspected, exclude these mimics — CMR and appropriate testing are required.
⚠️ Diagnosing ARVC without excluding sarcoidosis/Chagas is the trap — sarcoid responds to steroids and Chagas has its own management, so missing them means withholding the correct, treatment-altering diagnosis.
9. Desmosomal genes DSP/PKP2/DSC2
Key ARVC genes are desmosomal: DSP (desmoplakin), PKP2 (plakophilin-2), DSC2 (desmocollin-2). PKP2 is the most commonly identified mutation. Desmosomes are cell-to-cell adhesion junctions; when mutated, myocytes detach under mechanical stress, die, and are replaced by fat and fibrosis.
⚠️ DSP mutations cause a LEFT-dominant ACM variant where the LV is primarily affected — so 'ARVC genetics' can present with LV (not RV) disease, an underdiagnosed phenotype.
10. Myocyte detachment at desmosomes
Desmosomes anchor adjacent cardiac myocytes. Mutated desmosomal proteins fail under mechanical load, so myocytes detach, die, and are replaced by fibrofatty tissue. This is the molecular basis linking the gene defect to the arrhythmogenic scar — and why exercise (high mechanical stress) accelerates disease.
⚠️ Because mechanical stress drives detachment, ENDURANCE EXERCISE worsens ARVC — exercise restriction is therapeutic, the opposite of advice you'd give most patients.
11. ICI myocarditis = EMERGENCY
Immune checkpoint inhibitor (anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4) myocarditis is an emergency. Presentation: troponin elevation + new arrhythmia or AV conduction block, often fulminant. Management: STOP the immunotherapy immediately + high-dose corticosteroids. Mortality is substantial; early recognition is critical.
⚠️ Do not 'continue ICI and observe' or treat as routine NSTEMI — the answer is urgent drug cessation plus high-dose steroids; delay is the lethal trap.
12. ICI checkpoint mechanism
Checkpoint inhibitors release T-cell inhibition; the resulting autoimmune attack can target myocardium. The broken checkpoint gate encodes loss of immune tolerance. Troponin rise with new conduction block in a patient on anti-PD-1/PD-L1/CTLA-4 therapy is the classic vignette.
⚠️ New high-grade AV block in a cancer patient on immunotherapy is ICI myocarditis until proven otherwise — pacing alone without stopping the ICI and giving steroids misses the driver.
13. Steroids — urgent
High-dose corticosteroids are the immediate treatment for ICI myocarditis, given urgently alongside stopping the offending checkpoint inhibitor. The 'urgent' label encodes that minutes-to-hours matter given the high mortality. Refractory cases may need additional immunosuppression.
⚠️ Low-dose or delayed steroids are inadequate — ICI myocarditis requires high-dose corticosteroids urgently; under-treating is as dangerous as not treating.
14. Cobalt CM: prosthesis + polycythemia
Cobalt cardiomyopathy: metal-on-metal hip prostheses release cobalt ions causing a dilated or restrictive cardiomyopathy, plus polycythemia (cobalt has an erythropoietin-like effect), hypothyroidism, and goiter. Vignette: hip prosthesis + new CM + polycythemia + thyroid abnormalities = cobalt toxicity.
⚠️ The tetrad (CM + polycythemia + hypothyroidism + goiter in a hip-replacement patient) is the discriminator — attributing the cardiomyopathy to idiopathic DCM misses a reversible toxic cause (remove the source).
15. Cobalt confirmation: EM
Confirmation of cobalt cardiomyopathy is dense intramitochondrial particles on electron microscopy of myocardial biopsy. Goiter and hypothyroidism accompany the picture because cobalt impairs thyroid function. Identifying the failing metal-on-metal prosthesis is key to management.
⚠️ Serum cobalt and the clinical tetrad point to it, but the definitive histologic clue is dense intramitochondrial particles on EM — not amyloid or iron, which have their own stains/findings.
