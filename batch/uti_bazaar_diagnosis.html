📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Nitrite
Urine nitrite is produced when Enterobacteriaceae (E. coli, Klebsiella, Proteus) reduce dietary nitrate to nitrite. A POSITIVE nitrite is fairly specific for a gram-negative UTI. It requires adequate bladder dwell time, so a first-morning specimen is best; very dilute or briefly-held urine can read falsely negative.
⚠️ Nitrite is specific but NOT sensitive — a negative nitrite does not exclude UTI, and organisms that don't reduce nitrate (Enterococcus, Staph saprophyticus, Pseudomonas) give negative nitrite despite true infection.
2. Leukocyte esterase
Leukocyte esterase detects pyuria (WBCs/neutrophils in urine) and is the sensitive partner to nitrite. LE positive indicates inflammation/pyuria. Combined dipstick: nitrite + LE both positive strongly supports UTI; both negative makes it unlikely. Microscopic pyuria is >10 WBC/hpf.
⚠️ Sterile pyuria (LE+/pyuria but negative routine culture) should trigger thought of Chlamydia/gonorrhea urethritis, TB, or interstitial nephritis — not just 'treat as UTI.'
3. Dilute = false negative
Very dilute urine (high fluid intake, low specific gravity) or short bladder incubation lowers nitrite concentration below the detection threshold, producing false-negative dipstick results. This is why dipstick is a screen, not a rule-out, and clinical symptoms plus culture guide management.
⚠️ A negative dipstick in a symptomatic patient does not rule out UTI — dilution and non-nitrate-reducers cause false negatives; send a culture when suspicion is high.
4. E. coli — #1 uropathogen
E. coli causes ~75–95% of uncomplicated UTIs — the dominant uropathogen across cystitis and pyelonephritis. Uropathogenic E. coli uses P-fimbriae/adhesins to ascend. Knowing E. coli is #1 anchors empiric therapy choices (nitrofurantoin, TMP-SMX, fosfomycin) before culture returns.
⚠️ E. coli is #1 overall, but in a young sexually active woman with nitrite-NEGATIVE UTI think Staphylococcus saprophyticus (the #2 cause in this group); Proteus suggests stones/alkaline urea-splitting infection.
5. Staph saprophyticus
Staphylococcus saprophyticus is the second most common cause of acute uncomplicated cystitis in young, sexually active women. It is coagulase-negative, novobiocin-RESISTANT, and is nitrite-negative (doesn't reduce nitrate), so dipstick may mislead.
⚠️ A young woman with cystitis but NEGATIVE nitrite — don't dismiss UTI. Staph saprophyticus is nitrite-negative; coagulase-negative + novobiocin resistance distinguishes it from S. epidermidis (novobiocin-sensitive).
6. Asymptomatic bacteriuria — TREAT ONLY arch
Asymptomatic bacteriuria (positive culture, NO symptoms) is generally NOT treated — treatment doesn't help and breeds resistance. The major EXCEPTIONS to treat are: (1) pregnancy and (2) before urologic procedures with expected mucosal bleeding (e.g., TURP). The 'TREAT ONLY' arch flags these two carve-outs.
⚠️ Do NOT treat asymptomatic bacteriuria in the elderly, diabetics, catheterized patients, or pre-non-urologic surgery — only pregnancy and invasive urologic procedures justify treatment. Treating others is a classic wrong answer.
7. Pyuria alone ≠ treat
Pyuria frequently accompanies asymptomatic bacteriuria, particularly in elderly and catheterized patients, and by itself is NOT an indication to treat. Decisions hinge on SYMPTOMS, not on pyuria or a positive culture in an asymptomatic host.
⚠️ Pyuria in an asymptomatic catheterized or elderly patient is not a reason for antibiotics — pyuria does not upgrade asymptomatic bacteriuria into a treatable UTI.
8. Pregnancy — treat asymptomatic bacteriuria
In pregnancy, asymptomatic bacteriuria IS screened for and treated because untreated it progresses to pyelonephritis (~30–40%) and is linked to preterm birth and low birth weight. Safe agents: nitrofurantoin (avoid at term/G6PD), amoxicillin, cephalexin, fosfomycin. Avoid fluoroquinolones and TMP-SMX (esp. 1st and 3rd trimesters).
⚠️ Pregnancy is THE classic exception — always treat asymptomatic bacteriuria in pregnancy. And avoid TMP-SMX near term (kernicterus) and fluoroquinolones throughout.
9. Symptomatic cystitis
Symptomatic UTI = dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain (cystitis), or fever/flank pain/CVA tenderness (pyelonephritis). Symptoms — not culture colony counts alone — drive the decision to treat. Acute uncomplicated cystitis in a healthy non-pregnant woman can be treated empirically.
⚠️ Treat based on symptoms + urinalysis, not on a positive culture in isolation. Fever and flank pain push you from simple cystitis (short oral course) to pyelonephritis (longer, broader therapy).
10. Catheter / transplant host
Catheter-associated bacteriuria is usually NOT treated unless symptomatic; replace/remove the catheter first ('replace first'). Renal-transplant and other immunosuppressed hosts are an important nuance — some guidelines screen/treat early post-transplant — but routine asymptomatic catheter bacteriuria is still generally left alone.
⚠️ For catheter-associated bacteriuria, REPLACE the catheter and treat only if symptomatic — don't reflexively give antibiotics for a positive catheter-urine culture in an asymptomatic patient.
11. Replace catheter first / 7–14 d
For catheter-associated UTI that IS symptomatic, exchange the indwelling catheter before/at the start of therapy to remove the biofilm reservoir, then treat 7–14 days depending on severity and response. Source control (catheter exchange/removal) is as important as antibiotic choice.
⚠️ Antibiotics without removing/exchanging the colonized catheter leads to relapse — biofilm shields organisms. Always pair therapy with catheter exchange or removal.
12. Fluconazole — candiduria
Candiduria in an asymptomatic patient is usually managed by removing the catheter (often clears spontaneously) — not by antifungals. Treat (fluconazole) only if symptomatic, neutropenic, undergoing urologic manipulation, or in a renal-transplant recipient. Fluconazole is first-line for susceptible Candida albicans urinary infection.
⚠️ Asymptomatic candiduria generally needs catheter removal, NOT fluconazole — antifungals are reserved for symptomatic disease or high-risk hosts (neutropenia, urologic surgery, transplant).
13. Candida / fungus
Funguria is usually Candida, seen in catheterized, diabetic, broad-spectrum-antibiotic-exposed, or immunosuppressed patients. Most represents colonization. Pyelonephritis or fungus ball (bezoar) is the concerning invasive form, especially in diabetics, and can cause obstruction.
⚠️ Most candiduria is colonization, not invasive infection — the leap to systemic antifungals or amphotericin bladder irrigation is rarely warranted and is a classic over-treatment trap.
14. Emphysematous pyelonephritis
Emphysematous pyelonephritis is a necrotizing, gas-forming renal infection (CT shows gas in the renal parenchyma), occurring almost exclusively in DIABETICS, usually from E. coli or Klebsiella. It is life-threatening: management is aggressive — IV antibiotics + percutaneous drainage, and nephrectomy if it fails.
⚠️ Gas in the kidney on imaging in a diabetic = emphysematous pyelonephritis, a surgical/drainage emergency — not a simple pyelonephritis you can treat with oral antibiotics. E. coli is the usual culprit.
