📖 Read-through — all 10 symbols
1. Entecavir knight (gold star)
GOLD STAR first-line: entecavir, a nucleoside analog with a HIGH barrier to resistance. Potent HBV DNA suppression, well tolerated. Dose-adjust in renal impairment; avoid as monotherapy if prior lamivudine resistance (cross-resistance).
2. Tenofovir knight (gold star)
GOLD STAR first-line: tenofovir (TDF/TAF), high barrier to resistance. PREFERRED in pregnancy (lowers vertical transmission) and in HIV co-infection (active against both, part of ART). TDF: monitor renal function and bone density; TAF is gentler on kidney/bone.
3. PEG-interferon archer
PEG-interferon = a FINITE course (~48 weeks) aiming at HBeAg seroconversion and HBsAg loss; immunomodulatory, no resistance. CONTRAINDICATED in decompensated cirrhosis, autoimmune disease, pregnancy, severe psychiatric disease. Flu-like effects, cytopenias, depression.
4. Goblins escaping castle (decompensated)
The breached castle = decompensated cirrhosis, where interferon is contraindicated (can precipitate liver failure/sepsis). Use a nucleos(t)ide analog (entecavir/tenofovir) instead, which is safe and indicated in decompensated disease.
5. Reactivation prophylaxis scientist
Before chemotherapy, immunosuppression, rituximab, or high-dose steroids, screen HBsAg AND anti-HBc; if positive, give prophylactic entecavir/tenofovir to prevent reactivation. Start before and continue 6–12 months after immunosuppression. Rituximab/anthracyclines are the highest-risk.
6. Caged virus suppressed
Prophylaxis keeps the virus 'caged' — suppressed HBV DNA so immunosuppression doesn't unleash a flare/fulminant reactivation. Skipping prophylaxis in an anti-HBc-positive patient on rituximab can cause fatal hepatitis.
⚠️ 'Start chemo/rituximab without HBV prophylaxis in an anti-HBc-positive patient' → wrong; omitting entecavir/tenofovir prophylaxis risks fatal HBV reactivation.
7. HBeAg / HBV DNA balance chart
Treatment goals: suppress HBV DNA, achieve HBeAg→anti-HBe seroconversion, normalize ALT, and ideally HBsAg loss (functional cure, uncommon). Falling DNA on the scale = successful nucleos(t)ide suppression.
8. Ribavirin barrel (red X)
Ribavirin is an HCV/RSV antiviral with NO role in HBV. Selecting it for HBV is a wrong-disease error. HBV = entecavir/tenofovir (± PEG-IFN); HCV = direct-acting antivirals (ribavirin only as a legacy add-on there).
⚠️ 'Treat chronic HBV with ribavirin' → wrong; ribavirin is for HCV — HBV first-line is entecavir or tenofovir.
9. Old low-barrier swords (red X)
Lamivudine, adefovir, and telbivudine are OLD low-barrier agents → high rates of resistance with prolonged use; no longer first-line. Entecavir/tenofovir replaced them. Lamivudine resistance also compromises later entecavir.
⚠️ 'Use lamivudine (or adefovir/telbivudine) first-line for chronic HBV' → wrong; low genetic barrier breeds resistance — use entecavir or tenofovir.
10. HCC surveillance (US ± AFP)
Chronic HBV needs HCC surveillance — abdominal ultrasound ± AFP every 6 months in cirrhotics and high-risk non-cirrhotics (Asian men >40, Asian women >50, Africans, family history of HCC). HBV causes HCC even without cirrhosis via direct integration.
