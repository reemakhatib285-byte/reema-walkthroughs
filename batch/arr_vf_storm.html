📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Electrical storm = ≥3 in 24h
Electrical storm is defined as 3 or more separate episodes of sustained VT/VF (or 3 or more appropriate ICD shocks) within 24 hours — it is a true medical emergency with high short-term mortality. It reflects a hyperadrenergic, electrically unstable myocardium. Management is layered: defibrillate, suppress with antiarrhythmics (amiodarone) PLUS beta-blockade, deeply sedate, fix reversible triggers, and escalate to ablation/mechanical support if refractory.
⚠️ WRONG: defining electrical storm as one long VT episode — it requires 3 or more discrete sustained episodes (or ICD shocks) in 24 hours. A single sustained VT, however prolonged, is incessant VT, not storm.
2. Burning heart = VF
Ventricular fibrillation is chaotic, disorganized ventricular electrical activity with no organized QRS and no effective cardiac output, producing immediate pulseless cardiac arrest. The ECG shows irregular, fibrillatory waves of varying amplitude (coarse early, fine later). Survival depends on the time to defibrillation — every minute of delay drops survival ~7–10%.
⚠️ WRONG: attempting synchronized cardioversion for VF — there is no R wave to synchronize to, so the defibrillator may withhold the shock. VF is always treated with immediate UNSYNCHRONIZED defibrillation.
3. Defibrillation first
VF and pulseless VT are treated with immediate unsynchronized defibrillation (200 J biphasic) plus high-quality CPR — electrical therapy precedes drugs. In ACLS, after the first/second shock and CPR cycles, give epinephrine 1 mg IV every 3–5 min and an antiarrhythmic (amiodarone 300 mg then 150 mg, or lidocaine) for shock-refractory VF. Mnemonic: 'Shock, CPR, drugs' — in that order.
⚠️ WRONG: giving amiodarone or epinephrine before the first shock in VF arrest — defibrillation is the only intervention that restores a perfusing rhythm in VF and must come first. Drugs are adjuncts for shock-refractory VF, not the initial step.
4. Most common cause = acute MI
Acute myocardial ischemia/infarction is the most common cause of VF — ischemic polymorphic VT during an acute MI degenerates into VF, the leading mechanism of pre-hospital sudden cardiac death. Definitive treatment is emergent revascularization plus beta-blockade. VF in the first 48 hours of an MI is from acute ischemia and does NOT by itself mandate an ICD if it is fully revascularized.
⚠️ WRONG: implanting an ICD for VF that occurred within 48 hours of an acute MI that was revascularized — this is reversible ischemic VF, not a primary arrhythmic substrate, so it does not meet secondary-prevention ICD criteria. Reassess EF ≥40 days post-MI for primary prevention.
5. Amiodarone in storm
IV amiodarone (300 mg bolus, then 150 mg repeats and a continuous infusion ~1 mg/min) is the antiarrhythmic of choice to suppress refractory VF and electrical storm after defibrillation. Procainamide or lidocaine are alternatives. Amiodarone works best when COMBINED with beta-blockade, which targets the catecholamine surge that amiodarone alone does not.
⚠️ WRONG: relying on amiodarone alone for electrical storm — the synergistic, often outcome-changing addition is sympathetic blockade (beta-blocker). Amiodarone monotherapy frequently fails in storm without concurrent beta-blockade and sedation.
6. Beta-blocker (sympathetic block)
Beta-blockade is a cornerstone of electrical storm: it blunts the catecholamine surge that drives recurrent VT/VF. Non-selective propranolol or IV esmolol infusion is preferred; in refractory storm, left stellate ganglion / cardiac sympathetic denervation is the next step. This is the single most under-used yet highest-yield therapy in storm.
⚠️ WRONG: avoiding beta-blockers in electrical storm out of fear of hypotension — sympathetic blockade is the key therapy that reduces recurrent shocks and mortality. Use IV esmolol (titratable) or propranolol; do not withhold beta-blockade in storm.
7. Sedation / intubation
Deep sedation (and intubation if needed) reduces endogenous sympathetic drive and pain/anxiety from repeated shocks, directly lowering the catecholamine load that fuels storm. Agents that also dampen sympathetic tone (e.g., propofol, dexmedetomidine) are favored. Sedation is an active antiarrhythmic measure in storm, not just comfort care.
⚠️ WRONG: treating sedation as purely supportive comfort in storm — reducing sympathetic drive through deep sedation is part of the antiarrhythmic strategy and is recommended alongside beta-blockade and amiodarone.
8. Ablation for refractory storm
Catheter ablation of the triggering PVC focus or the reentrant VT circuit/scar is used for electrical storm refractory to drugs and beta-blockade, and reduces recurrent shocks. In hemodynamically unsupportable storm, mechanical circulatory support (ECMO/Impella) may bridge to ablation. Ablation targets the substrate that drugs cannot quiet.
⚠️ WRONG: escalating antiarrhythmic drugs indefinitely in drug-refractory storm — guideline-favored next step after failed drugs/beta-blockade is catheter ablation, not stacking more antiarrhythmics.
9. ICD terminates, doesn't prevent
An ICD terminates VT/VF by delivering a shock but does NOT prevent the arrhythmia from recurring — it is a safety net, not a cure. Recurrent appropriate shocks define storm, cause myocardial injury and PTSD-like distress, and independently worsen prognosis. Adjunctive drugs/ablation are needed to reduce the burden of episodes.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming an ICD alone is sufficient management once placed — it does not reduce arrhythmia frequency, and recurrent shocks signal storm requiring antiarrhythmics, beta-blockade, and often ablation.
10. Reversible causes (ischemia, K/Mg)
Always hunt for and treat reversible drivers of storm: acute ischemia (revascularize), electrolyte derangements (correct K+ to ~4.5–5.0 and replete Mg2+), QT-prolonging or proarrhythmic drugs, decompensated heart failure, and hyperthyroidism. Beta-blockade lowers VF risk across these settings. Reversible-cause correction can terminate storm by itself.
⚠️ WRONG: jumping to ablation or escalating antiarrhythmics before checking electrolytes and ischemia — uncorrected hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia or ongoing ischemia will defeat any antiarrhythmic. Fix reversible triggers first.
11. HCM — leading SCD in young
Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy is a leading cause of sudden cardiac death in young people and athletes, from VT/VF on a hypertrophied, fibrotic, disarrayed myocardium (annual SCD risk often <1%/yr but concentrated in high-risk patients). ICD is indicated for high-risk features: prior cardiac arrest/sustained VT, unexplained syncope, massive LVH (≥30 mm), family history of SCD, or nonsustained VT/abnormal BP response.
⚠️ WRONG: clearing an HCM patient on EF alone — HCM patients usually have preserved/hyperdynamic EF yet remain at high SCD risk. Risk stratify by the specific HCM risk markers, not ejection fraction.
12. Lamin A/C — high VT/SCD
Lamin A/C (LMNA) cardiomyopathy is a malignant genetic dilated cardiomyopathy carrying a high risk of VT/SCD and progressive conduction disease (AV block) — it warrants a LOW ICD threshold even when EF is relatively preserved. Other arrhythmogenic mimics: SCN5A, PLN, desmoplakin. Suspect it in DCM with early conduction disease or family history of SCD.
⚠️ WRONG: applying the standard EF ≤35% ICD cutoff to LMNA cardiomyopathy — its high arrhythmic risk justifies ICD at higher EF and earlier than typical nonischemic cardiomyopathy. Genotype changes the ICD threshold.
13. ICD driving restriction
After an ICD is placed (or after a sustained VA event), patients face temporary driving restrictions because of syncope/shock risk while driving. Typical guidance: after secondary-prevention ICD or an arrhythmic event, no private driving for ~3–6 months and a permanent ban on commercial driving; after primary-prevention ICD, ~1 week (post-implant). Counseling on driving is a commonly tested practical point.
⚠️ WRONG: telling an ICD patient they may resume commercial/heavy-vehicle driving — secondary-prevention ICD recipients are permanently disqualified from commercial driving, and private driving is restricted for a defined recovery period.
14. In-hospital recovery / monitoring
After resuscitated VF/storm, patients need continuous telemetry and ICU-level monitoring (the '48h' window) to catch early recurrence, manage post-arrest care, and stabilize the substrate (revascularization, electrolytes, beta-blockade). Targeted temperature management and neuroprognostication follow out-of-hospital arrest. Early recurrence is common, so monitored recovery is essential.
⚠️ WRONG: discharging or down-grading a resuscitated VF patient quickly because they look well — early arrhythmia recurrence and post-arrest complications require continuous monitored recovery and full reversible-cause workup before any de-escalation.
