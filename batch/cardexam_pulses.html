📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Parvus et tardus = severe AS
Pulsus parvus et tardus is a SLOW-RISING (tardus), LOW-AMPLITUDE (parvus) carotid pulse — the hallmark of SEVERE AORTIC STENOSIS. The stenotic valve blunts and delays ejection, weakening and slowing the upstroke.
⚠️ Parvus et tardus is AORTIC STENOSIS, NOT aortic regurgitation — AR gives the opposite (sharp, bounding, collapsing) pulse.
2. Slow low pulse tracing
The blunted upstroke and reduced peak reflect the fixed obstruction of AS — flow is throttled across the narrowed valve, so the arterial pressure rises slowly and never reaches a normal peak.
⚠️ A weak, delayed upstroke is obstruction (AS); don't read low amplitude alone as hypovolemia without the slow rise.
3. Heavy calcified valve
Severe calcific AORTIC STENOSIS is the lesion behind parvus et tardus — a heavily calcified, narrowed valve raises the resistance to LV ejection and produces the slow, small pulse.
⚠️ The calcified obstructing valve is the cause of the weak slow pulse — the underlying valve, not a vascular tone problem.
4. NOT AR
This explicitly rules out aortic regurgitation for the parvus-et-tardus pulse. AS = weak/slow; AR = bounding/collapsing — the two are bedside opposites and a classic distractor pairing.
⚠️ The tested wrong answer is AR — a slow, weak, delayed pulse is AS, never the bounding water-hammer of AR.
5. Water-hammer = severe AR
The water-hammer (Corrigan) pulse has a SHARP, brisk RISE and a RAPID COLLAPSE — the classic finding of SEVERE AORTIC REGURGITATION. Regurgitant runoff into the LV in diastole drops aortic diastolic pressure abruptly.
⚠️ A bounding, collapsing pulse is AR, NOT AS — pairing it with AS is the trap; AS is slow and weak.
6. Mallet / hammer blow
The hammer blow encodes the abrupt, forceful, percussive upstroke of the Corrigan pulse — a sudden hit followed by an equally sudden collapse, felt strikingly at the wrist with the arm raised.
⚠️ The forceful collapsing quality is AR runoff, not a hyperdynamic AS pulse — AS cannot produce a collapsing pulse.
7. Wide pulse pressure
Severe AORTIC REGURGITATION causes a WIDE PULSE PRESSURE — high systolic and low diastolic pressure — driving the peripheral signs (water-hammer pulse, Quincke's, de Musset's, Traube's).
⚠️ Wide pulse pressure goes with AR; severe AS narrows the pulse pressure — don't swap them.
8. Bisferiens = AR / HOCM
Pulsus bisferiens has TWO SYSTOLIC PEAKS in one pulse. It is seen in AORTIC REGURGITATION, HOCM (dynamic outflow obstruction), or MIXED AS + AR. The mid-systolic dip separates the two percussion/tidal peaks.
⚠️ Bisferiens is NOT pure aortic stenosis — it points to AR, HOCM, or mixed AS+AR; pure AS gives parvus et tardus.
9. Two systolic peaks tracing
The two peaks reflect a percussion wave then a tidal wave within a single systole — produced by the rapid ejection of AR/mixed disease or the mid-systolic obstruction dynamics of HOCM.
⚠️ Two peaks ≠ two beats — bisferiens is one pulse with twin peaks, not an arrhythmia or pulsus alternans.
10. NOT AS only
This rules out isolated aortic stenosis as the cause of bisferiens. Pure AS produces a single slow small peak; twin peaks require AR, HOCM, or combined AS+AR physiology.
⚠️ The trap answer is 'AS only' — bisferiens specifically implies more than isolated AS.
11. Alternans = LV failure
Pulsus alternans is a regular rhythm with beats of ALTERNATING STRONG and WEAK amplitude. It signals SEVERE LEFT VENTRICULAR SYSTOLIC FAILURE — the failing LV cannot generate equal stroke volume each beat.
⚠️ Pulsus alternans is LV systolic failure, NOT an arrhythmia — the rhythm is regular; only the amplitude alternates.
12. Strong/weak alternating beats
The big-small-big-small amplitude pattern at a constant rate reflects beat-to-beat variation in LV stroke volume from a severely depressed, failing ventricle.
⚠️ Don't call it bigeminy — the timing is regular, so this is alternans (pump failure), not an ectopic rhythm.
13. NOT arrhythmia
This rules out an arrhythmic explanation for alternans. The pulse alternates in strength at a regular rate — the problem is contractile (severe LV systolic dysfunction), not electrical.
⚠️ The trap answer is 'arrhythmia' — alternans has a normal regular rhythm; only amplitude varies.
14. Paradoxus = tamponade
Pulsus paradoxus is an EXAGGERATED FALL in systolic BP (>10 mmHg) during INSPIRATION. Classic in CARDIAC TAMPONADE; also constrictive pericarditis, severe ASTHMA, and COPD. Inspiratory RV filling bows the septum and limits LV stroke volume.
⚠️ It is a quantified inspiratory DROP (>10 mmHg), not just any respiratory variation — and it is exaggeration of a normal phenomenon, not a true 'paradox'.
15. Inspiration drops SBP
On inspiration, increased venous return fills the RV, the interventricular septum shifts left in the constrained pericardial space, LV filling and stroke volume fall, and systolic BP drops more than 10 mmHg — measured with the cuff.
⚠️ Pulsus paradoxus is detected by BP cuff (>10 mmHg inspiratory fall), not by palpation alone — and tamponade is the headline cause.
