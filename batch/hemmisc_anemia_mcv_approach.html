⚠️ Board traps 14

  3. Thalassemia low RDW — WRONG answer: diagnosing iron deficiency in a microcytic patient with a NORMAL RDW and high RBC count. Discriminator: normal RDW + high RBC + normal ferritin = thalassemia trait, not IDA. A normal HbA2 does NOT exclude alpha-thalassemia (need genetic studies).
  4. Anemia of chronic dz — WRONG answer: calling it iron deficiency because serum iron is low. Discriminator: in ACD the TIBC is LOW and ferritin is HIGH/normal, whereas IDA has HIGH TIBC and LOW ferritin. When ACD and IDA coexist, a low transferrin saturation with soluble transferrin receptor or a bone marrow iron stain settles it.
  5. Normocytic 85-95 — WRONG answer: ordering iron studies/B12 first for a normocytic anemia. Discriminator: the retic count is the branch point in normocytic anemia — low retic points to marrow/renal/chronic-disease causes; high retic points to bleeding or hemolysis.
  6. EPO/BUN/SPEP — WRONG answer: ignoring renal function in a normocytic anemia with low retic. Discriminator: CKD-related EPO deficiency is a leading, easily missed cause — check creatinine; rouleaux/elevated total protein should trigger SPEP for myeloma.
  7. Aplastic / marrow — WRONG answer: attributing pancytopenia with a 'dry tap' purely to aplastic anemia. Discriminator: a dry/blood tap with teardrop cells and nucleated RBCs suggests marrow infiltration or fibrosis (myelophthisis), which needs a biopsy — aplastic anemia is hypocellular but aspirable, not fibrotic.
  8. Macrocytic >95 — WRONG answer: treating all macrocytosis as B12/folate deficiency. Discriminator: the smear and degree of macrocytosis separate them — hypersegmented neutrophils + oval macrocytes + very high MCV = megaloblastic; round macrocytes without hypersegmentation = liver/alcohol/thyroid or reticulocytosis.

Discriminator: always check/replace B12 before folate; elevated MMA confirms B12 (not folate) deficiency.

📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Microcytic <80
MCV <80 fL = microcytic anemia, reflecting defective hemoglobin synthesis. The classic differential is captured by 'TAILS': Thalassemia, Anemia of chronic disease (can be normo- or microcytic), Iron deficiency, Lead poisoning, Sideroblastic anemia. First-line workup is iron studies (ferritin, serum iron, TIBC, transferrin saturation) plus RDW.
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming every microcytic anemia is iron deficiency and starting iron empirically. Discriminator: check ferritin first — thalassemia, lead, and sideroblastic anemia have normal/high iron stores, and giving iron to a thalassemia trait patient risks iron overload.
2. IDA most common
Iron deficiency is the most common anemia worldwide. Pattern: LOW ferritin (most specific test), low serum iron, HIGH TIBC/transferrin, low transferrin saturation (<15%), and HIGH RDW (anisocytosis as new microcytes mix with older normocytes). In adult men or postmenopausal women, IDA mandates a GI source workup (colonoscopy) for occult bleeding.
⚠️ WRONG answer: stopping at 'low ferritin = treat with iron' without finding the cause in an at-risk adult. Discriminator: unexplained IDA in a man or postmenopausal woman is GI malignancy until proven otherwise — pursue endoscopy/colonoscopy, don't just replace iron.
3. Thalassemia low RDW
Thalassemia trait shows a NORMAL or HIGH RBC count with disproportionately low MCV, a NORMAL/low RDW (uniform small cells), and normal/high ferritin. The Mentzer index (MCV/RBC) <13 favors thalassemia; >13 favors IDA. Confirm beta-thalassemia with Hb electrophoresis (↑HbA2); alpha-thal often needs genetic testing.
⚠️ WRONG answer: diagnosing iron deficiency in a microcytic patient with a NORMAL RDW and high RBC count. Discriminator: normal RDW + high RBC + normal ferritin = thalassemia trait, not IDA. A normal HbA2 does NOT exclude alpha-thalassemia (need genetic studies).
4. Anemia of chronic dz
Anemia of chronic disease/inflammation is usually normocytic but can be mildly microcytic. Hepcidin (up-regulated by inflammation) traps iron in macrophages: LOW serum iron, LOW TIBC, and HIGH/normal ferritin (acute-phase reactant). It sits on the micro/normocytic border.
⚠️ WRONG answer: calling it iron deficiency because serum iron is low. Discriminator: in ACD the TIBC is LOW and ferritin is HIGH/normal, whereas IDA has HIGH TIBC and LOW ferritin. When ACD and IDA coexist, a low transferrin saturation with soluble transferrin receptor or a bone marrow iron stain settles it.
5. Normocytic 85-95
MCV 80–100 fL = normocytic anemia. The pivotal next step is the RETICULOCYTE count/index, which splits causes into hypoproliferative (low retic = marrow not responding) vs hyperproliferative (high retic = blood loss or hemolysis).
⚠️ WRONG answer: ordering iron studies/B12 first for a normocytic anemia. Discriminator: the retic count is the branch point in normocytic anemia — low retic points to marrow/renal/chronic-disease causes; high retic points to bleeding or hemolysis.
6. EPO/BUN/SPEP
Hypoproliferative (low-retic) normocytic workup: BUN/creatinine and EPO level (CKD → inadequate erythropoietin), SPEP/free light chains (multiple myeloma), TSH (hypothyroidism), and consider marrow exam. Anemia of chronic disease and renal anemia are the most common causes.
⚠️ WRONG answer: ignoring renal function in a normocytic anemia with low retic. Discriminator: CKD-related EPO deficiency is a leading, easily missed cause — check creatinine; rouleaux/elevated total protein should trigger SPEP for myeloma.
7. Aplastic / marrow
Low-retic normocytic causes from the marrow itself: aplastic anemia (pancytopenia, hypocellular marrow), marrow infiltration/myelophthisis (fibrosis, mets, leukemia — gives leukoerythroblastic smear with teardrops and nucleated RBCs), multiple myeloma, and pure red cell aplasia (e.g., parvovirus B19, especially in immunocompromised/sickle cell aplastic crisis).
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing pancytopenia with a 'dry tap' purely to aplastic anemia. Discriminator: a dry/blood tap with teardrop cells and nucleated RBCs suggests marrow infiltration or fibrosis (myelophthisis), which needs a biopsy — aplastic anemia is hypocellular but aspirable, not fibrotic.
8. Macrocytic >95
MCV >100 fL = macrocytic anemia. The first split is megaloblastic (impaired DNA synthesis → oval macrocytes + hypersegmented neutrophils) vs non-megaloblastic (round macrocytes, normal neutrophils). MCV >110–120 strongly favors megaloblastic.
⚠️ WRONG answer: treating all macrocytosis as B12/folate deficiency. Discriminator: the smear and degree of macrocytosis separate them — hypersegmented neutrophils + oval macrocytes + very high MCV = megaloblastic; round macrocytes without hypersegmentation = liver/alcohol/thyroid or reticulocytosis.
9. Oval / DNA defect
Megaloblastic macrocytosis (oval macrocytes, hypersegmented neutrophils, MCV often >110): B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, MDS, and drugs blocking DNA synthesis (methotrexate, hydroxyurea, zidovudine, phenytoin, trimethoprim). Both B12 and folate deficiency raise homocysteine, but only B12 deficiency raises methylmalonic acid (MMA).
⚠️ WRONG answer: giving folate to a patient who actually has B12 deficiency. Folate corrects the anemia/MCV but the neurologic damage (subacute combined degeneration — dorsal columns, paresthesias, ataxia) progresses irreversibly. Discriminator: always check/replace B12 before folate; elevated MMA confirms B12 (not folate) deficiency.
10. Round / membrane
Non-megaloblastic (round) macrocytosis, usually mild (MCV ~100–110): liver disease, alcohol (direct marrow toxicity, can occur without B12/folate deficiency), hypothyroidism, MDS, and reticulocytosis (large young RBCs) from hemolysis/bleeding. No hypersegmented neutrophils.
⚠️ WRONG answer: working up B12/folate exhaustively while missing a high reticulocyte count. Discriminator: brisk reticulocytosis (hemolysis or recent bleed) raises MCV because reticulocytes are large — check the retic count before assuming a nutritional megaloblastic cause.
11. Hemolysis ↑retic
Hemolysis labs: HIGH LDH, HIGH reticulocytes, HIGH indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin, and LOW haptoglobin (best single marker for intravascular hemolysis). Then split immune vs non-immune with the direct antiglobulin (Coombs) test, and read the smear (schistocytes → MAHA; spherocytes → HS/AIHA; bite cells → G6PD).
⚠️ WRONG answer: calling a Coombs-negative hemolytic anemia 'autoimmune.' Discriminator: a NEGATIVE direct Coombs points away from AIHA toward intrinsic/mechanical causes (G6PD, hereditary spherocytosis, PNH, MAHA, prosthetic valve) — let the smear and Coombs direct the workup.
12. Bleeding ↑retic
Hyperproliferative (high-retic) normocytic anemia = acute blood loss OR hemolysis. Acute hemorrhage may show a NORMAL Hb/Hct in the first hours before plasma refill, then a falling hematocrit with reticulocytosis appearing at 2–3 days.
⚠️ WRONG answer: excluding significant acute hemorrhage because the initial hemoglobin is normal. Discriminator: in early acute blood loss the Hb/Hct lags (whole-blood loss, no time to hemodilute) — trend the hematocrit and watch vitals/orthostatics rather than relying on a single early value.
13. Ferritin low = IDA
Ferritin is the most useful and most SPECIFIC test for iron deficiency: a low ferritin (<15–30 ng/mL) essentially confirms IDA. Caveat: ferritin is an acute-phase reactant, so in inflammation it can be falsely normal/high even with true iron deficiency (then look at transferrin saturation/soluble transferrin receptor).
⚠️ WRONG answer: ruling out iron deficiency because ferritin is 'normal' in an inflamed/hospitalized patient. Discriminator: with coexisting inflammation, a ferritin up to ~100 ng/mL can still represent iron deficiency — use transferrin saturation, sTfR, or marrow iron when ACD and IDA may coexist.
14. Master algorithm
The unifying approach: (1) confirm anemia and bin by MCV (micro <80 / normo 80–100 / macro >100); (2) microcytic → iron studies + RDW (+ Mentzer index, Hb electrophoresis); (3) normocytic → reticulocyte count (low = marrow/renal/ACD; high = bleed/hemolysis); (4) macrocytic → smear for megaloblastic features (B12/MMA, folate) vs non-megaloblastic (liver/alcohol/thyroid/retic).
⚠️ WRONG answer: relying on MCV bins alone and ignoring RDW and reticulocytes. Discriminator: MCV starts the algorithm but RDW (anisocytosis) and the retic count are what actually separate IDA vs thalassemia and hypo- vs hyper-proliferative causes — mixed deficiencies (e.g., IDA + B12) can give a deceptively normal MCV with a high RDW.
