📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. T-cell soldiers
Central mechanism of acquired AA: autoreactive cytotoxic CD8+ T LYMPHOCYTES (oligoclonal, often expanded) recognize antigens on hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells and destroy them, emptying the marrow. This is an immune attack, not a primary stem-cell defect — the rationale for immunosuppressive therapy.
⚠️ Boards contrast this with a stem-cell-intrinsic disorder: acquired AA is predominantly IMMUNE-mediated (responds to ATG/cyclosporine). Inherited marrow failure (Fanconi, dyskeratosis congenita) IS an intrinsic stem-cell/DNA-repair/telomere defect — different management.
2. IFN-gamma fire
Activated T cells secrete the inhibitory cytokines IFN-γ and TNF-α, which suppress proliferation of CD34+ progenitors, upregulate Fas on target cells, and trigger apoptosis — the molecular 'firepower' behind marrow destruction.
⚠️ IFN-γ/TNF-α-driven Th1 immunity is the lever immunosuppression pulls. This cytokine milieu also explains why eltrombopag (TPO-receptor agonist) can stimulate the surviving stem cells despite the suppressive environment.
3. Fas target
Stem/progenitor cells are killed largely by FAS–FAS-ligand–mediated APOPTOSIS, producing the profoundly hypocellular (fatty) marrow that defines aplastic anemia.
⚠️ The marrow is empty because of increased APOPTOSIS/immune killing, not because cells are maturing and being consumed peripherally — hence a LOW reticulocyte count (underproduction), the opposite of hemolysis.
4. HLA flags
There are HLA associations (classically HLA-DR15/DR2), and immune-escape biology: clones that lose or alter HLA expression (6p copy-neutral loss of heterozygosity) evade the T-cell attack and can expand — one route to clonal hematopoiesis in AA.
⚠️ HLA-DR15 positivity and a PNH/HLA-loss clone predict better response to immunosuppression, but clonal escape (PNH, HLA-LOH, or MDS-type clones) also flags risk of later clonal EVOLUTION.
5. PNH escape boat
GPI-anchor-deficient PNH clones (PIGA-mutant, lacking CD55/CD59) 'escape' the immune attack because they lack the GPI-anchored target antigen, so they survive and expand within the failing marrow — explaining the strong AA–PNH overlap.
⚠️ Screen all AA patients with flow cytometry for a PNH clone at diagnosis and on follow-up. A PNH clone supports immune pathogenesis but also requires monitoring for hemolysis and thrombosis.
6. Pancytopenia man
Clinical presentation = consequences of PANCYTOPENIA: anemia → fatigue, pallor, dyspnea; thrombocytopenia → petechiae, mucosal bleeding, epistaxis, easy bruising; neutropenia → infection risk. Onset is usually insidious; the marrow does not infiltrate, so there is typically NO hepatosplenomegaly or lymphadenopathy.
⚠️ Absence of organomegaly/lymphadenopathy helps separate AA from leukemia/lymphoma. Hepatosplenomegaly or blasts on smear argue AGAINST aplastic anemia and toward an infiltrative/clonal process.
7. Infection NO
At presentation patients are often afebrile/without active infection despite leukopenia, but NEUTROPENIA (especially ANC <500) makes them highly vulnerable; febrile neutropenia is a medical emergency requiring urgent broad-spectrum antibiotics.
⚠️ Don't be reassured by an initially well-appearing patient — severe neutropenia means a fever must be treated as life-threatening sepsis immediately, even without a localizing source.
8. Empty marrow biopsy
DIAGNOSIS requires peripheral PANCYTOPENIA plus a HYPOCELLULAR marrow on BIOPSY (cellularity <25%, fatty replacement) with NO fibrosis, NO infiltration, and NO dysplasia/blast excess. A core biopsy (not just aspirate) is essential to judge cellularity.
⚠️ The classic exam pitfall: AA marrow is HYPOcellular. A HYPERcellular or fibrotic/infiltrated marrow excludes AA and points to MDS, leukemia, myelofibrosis, or marrow infiltration.
9. Severity meter
CAMITTA severity criteria (need ≥2 of 3 in a hypocellular marrow): ANC <500/µL, platelets <20,000/µL, reticulocytes <20,000/µL (or <1%). SEVERE AA = meets criteria with ANC <500; VERY SEVERE AA = ANC <200.
⚠️ Severity (especially ANC <500 = severe, <200 = very severe) drives treatment urgency and choice: severe/very-severe disease in a young patient → urgent allo-HSCT; non-severe disease may be observed or treated with IST.
10. Reticulocyte low
The reticulocyte count is LOW (corrected retic/RPI low; absolute retic <20–25,000), confirming marrow UNDERPRODUCTION rather than peripheral destruction or loss.
⚠️ A LOW reticulocyte count is the hallmark. A HIGH reticulocyte count would indicate hemolysis or blood loss (appropriate marrow response) and argues against aplastic anemia.
11. Exclude MDS (ASXL1)
Must EXCLUDE hypoplastic MDS — look for dysplasia, abnormal cytogenetics (e.g., monosomy 7, del(5q)), and somatic mutations. Mutations like ASXL1, DNMT3A, and RUNX1 suggest MDS-type clonal hematopoiesis and predict higher risk of progression, whereas BCOR/BCORL1 and PIGA mutations favor immune AA.
⚠️ Hypocellular MDS is the great mimic of AA. Cytogenetics + a myeloid mutation panel are needed; finding monosomy 7 or high-risk mutations (ASXL1/RUNX1) reclassifies the case as MDS and changes prognosis/therapy.
12. Frayed rope telomere
SHORT/FRAYED TELOMERES flag an inherited telomere-biology disorder (dyskeratosis congenita; TERT/TERC mutations) presenting as 'apparently acquired' AA. Telomere length testing is part of the young-patient workup.
⚠️ Telomeropathies do NOT respond well to immunosuppression and have higher transplant toxicity (lung/liver) — missing them is a real management error. Look for the mucocutaneous triad (nail dystrophy, oral leukoplakia, reticular skin pigmentation) and pulmonary fibrosis.
13. Hairy cell exclusion
Also exclude infiltrative/clonal causes of a deceptively 'empty-looking' marrow: HAIRY CELL LEUKEMIA (pancytopenia, monocytopenia, splenomegaly, dry tap from fibrosis), large granular lymphocytic leukemia, lymphoma, and hypoplastic leukemia.
⚠️ Hairy cell leukemia classically causes pancytopenia with MONOCYTOPENIA and splenomegaly and a fibrotic 'dry-tap' marrow — flow cytometry (CD11c/CD25/CD103, BRAF V600E) distinguishes it from true aplastic anemia.
