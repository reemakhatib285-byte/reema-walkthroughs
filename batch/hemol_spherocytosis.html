⚠️ Board traps 13
  1. Membrane assembly line — WRONG: HS is caused by an enzyme deficiency like G6PD or pyruvate kinase. Discriminator — HS is a MEMBRANE/cytoskeleton structural defect (vertical interactions), not a metabolic/enzymatic one; smear shows spherocytes (no central pallor), not bite cells/Heinz bodies (G6PD) or echinocytes (PK).
  2. Spectrin tetramer (vertical) — WRONG: HS is a horizontal spectrin self-association defect. Discriminator — horizontal defects produce hereditary ELLIPTOCYTOSIS (cigar-shaped cells), not spherocytes; HS is the vertical (ankyrin/band 3/spectrin-to-membrane) defect.
  3. ANK1 / SLC4A1 / SPTB — WRONG: HS is X-linked, so it affects mainly males like hemophilia or G6PD. Discriminator — HS is autosomal (most often dominant); it affects both sexes equally and you typically see an affected parent. X-linked recessive is G6PD deficiency.
  4. Spherocytes on smear — WRONG: an elevated MCHC on the analyzer is just a lab artifact and can be ignored. Discriminator — a high MCHC (>36) is a real and characteristic clue to spherocytosis (HS or warm AIHA); pairing high MCHC + spherocytes + reticulocytosis should prompt HS vs autoimmune workup, not dismissal.
  5. Osmotic fragility / EMA — WRONG: HS shows DECREASED osmotic fragility. Discriminator — fragility is INCREASED in HS (cells lyse in only mildly hypotonic saline because they have no spare membrane); DECREASED osmotic fragility is the picture in thalassemia/iron deficiency (target cells with excess membrane).
  6. DAT negative — WRONG: HS is Coombs-positive. Discriminator — HS is Coombs/DAT NEGATIVE (it is an inherited membrane defect, not antibody-mediated); a POSITIVE DAT with spherocytes means warm AIHA instead. Confusing the two is the classic board pitfall.
  7. Spleen gauntlet phagocytosis — WRONG: HS causes intravascular hemolysis with hemoglobinuria and hemosiderinuria. Discriminator — HS is EXTRAVASCULAR (splenic phagocytosis); you do NOT see hemoglobinuria or schistocytes. Intravascular hemolysis (hemoglobinuria, schistocytes, very low haptoglobin) points to PNH, MAHA/TTP, or mechanical valve, not HS.
  8. Gallstones — WRONG: the gallstones in HS are cholesterol stones from the metabolic syndrome. Discriminator — chronic hemolysis produces PIGMENT (bilirubinate) stones, not cholesterol stones; pigment stones in a young patient with jaundice and splenomegaly should trigger a hemolysis workup.
  9. Splenectomy cures hemolysis — WRONG: after splenectomy the spherocytes disappear because the defect is corrected. Discriminator — splenectomy removes the GRAVEYARD, not the genetic membrane defect; spherocytes persist (now you also see Howell-Jolly bodies and target cells), but hemolysis/anemia resolve because there is no spleen to trap them.
  10. Vaccinate / wait by age — WRONG: vaccines can be given right after splenectomy whenever convenient. Discriminator — vaccinate ≥2 weeks BEFORE elective splenectomy for the best response; an asplenic, unvaccinated patient is at high risk of fatal pneumococcal sepsis (OPSI).

  12. Pregnant woman / aplastic crisis — WRONG: in parvovirus B19 aplastic crisis you expect a high reticulocyte count from the hemolysis. Discriminator — the hallmark of an APLASTIC crisis is a LOW reticulocyte count (parvovirus arrests erythropoiesis) despite worsening anemia; a HIGH retic count points to a hemolytic, not aplastic, crisis.

Discriminator — HS patients are NOT iron deficient (hemolysis recycles iron); routine iron risks iron OVERLOAD, especially if transfused — give FOLATE, not iron, unless documented deficiency. Monitor ferritin in chronically transfused patients.
📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Membrane assembly line
Hereditary spherocytosis (HS) is an intrinsic RBC defect: the proteins that anchor the lipid bilayer to the underlying spectrin cytoskeleton are deficient, so the membrane is poorly tethered. Unsupported regions of membrane bleb off and are pinched away each time the cell squeezes through the spleen. With each pass the cell loses surface area but keeps its volume, so the surface-area-to-volume ratio falls and the biconcave disc collapses into a sphere. The sphere is rigid and fragile — the central lesion that explains every downstream finding (anemia, jaundice, splenomegaly, gallstones).
⚠️ WRONG: HS is caused by an enzyme deficiency like G6PD or pyruvate kinase. Discriminator — HS is a MEMBRANE/cytoskeleton structural defect (vertical interactions), not a metabolic/enzymatic one; smear shows spherocytes (no central pallor), not bite cells/Heinz bodies (G6PD) or echinocytes (PK).
2. Spectrin tetramer (vertical)
The HS lesion is a VERTICAL-interaction defect — the linkage that runs perpendicular from the spectrin lattice up to the bilayer (spectrin ↔ ankyrin ↔ band 3, and spectrin ↔ protein 4.2). Break a vertical strut and a patch of membrane lifts off → spherocyte. Contrast with HORIZONTAL defects (spectrin self-association, α/β-spectrin or protein 4.1), which produce hereditary ELLIPTOCYTOSIS, where cells stay intact but elongate. Mnemonic: 'Vertical → spheres (HS), horizontal → ellipses (HE).'
⚠️ WRONG: HS is a horizontal spectrin self-association defect. Discriminator — horizontal defects produce hereditary ELLIPTOCYTOSIS (cigar-shaped cells), not spherocytes; HS is the vertical (ankyrin/band 3/spectrin-to-membrane) defect.
3. ANK1 / SLC4A1 / SPTB
Genes: ANK1 (ankyrin, the single most common, ~50%), SLC4A1 (band 3, ~25%), SPTB (β-spectrin), SPTA1 (α-spectrin), EPB42 (protein 4.2). Inheritance is autosomal DOMINANT in ~75% (family history of anemia/jaundice/gallstones/splenectomy is the classic clue); the remaining cases are recessive or de novo. Northern European ancestry is the textbook association. A positive family history with chronic Coombs-negative hemolysis essentially makes the diagnosis.
⚠️ WRONG: HS is X-linked, so it affects mainly males like hemophilia or G6PD. Discriminator — HS is autosomal (most often dominant); it affects both sexes equally and you typically see an affected parent. X-linked recessive is G6PD deficiency.
4. Spherocytes on smear
Peripheral smear shows small, round, hyperdense spherocytes that LACK central pallor — the hallmark. Because volume is preserved in a smaller cell, the mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration (MCHC) is INCREASED, often >36 g/dL — a near-unique finding (also seen in homozygous SC/SS disease and severe burns). Labs: normal-to-high MCHC, normal/low MCV, reticulocytosis, increased RDW, elevated indirect bilirubin and LDH, low haptoglobin.
⚠️ WRONG: an elevated MCHC on the analyzer is just a lab artifact and can be ignored. Discriminator — a high MCHC (>36) is a real and characteristic clue to spherocytosis (HS or warm AIHA); pairing high MCHC + spherocytes + reticulocytosis should prompt HS vs autoimmune workup, not dismissal.
5. Osmotic fragility / EMA
Confirmatory tests: the osmotic fragility test shows INCREASED fragility — spherocytes have minimal extra membrane, so they burst (hemolyze) at higher (less hypotonic) saline concentrations than normal biconcave discs; incubated osmotic fragility raises sensitivity. The modern test of choice is the EMA (eosin-5-maleimide) binding flow cytometry assay, which binds band 3 — REDUCED fluorescence indicates band 3/membrane protein deficiency (high sensitivity and specificity, fast). The acidified glycerol lysis test is an alternative.
⚠️ WRONG: HS shows DECREASED osmotic fragility. Discriminator — fragility is INCREASED in HS (cells lyse in only mildly hypotonic saline because they have no spare membrane); DECREASED osmotic fragility is the picture in thalassemia/iron deficiency (target cells with excess membrane).
6. DAT negative
HS is direct antiglobulin test (DAT/Coombs) NEGATIVE — this is the single most important discriminator from warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia, because BOTH diseases show spherocytes and extravascular splenic hemolysis. Always send a DAT in any patient with spherocytic hemolytic anemia: negative + family history → HS (intrinsic); positive (IgG ± C3) → warm AIHA (acquired/immune). This one test changes management entirely (splenectomy/supportive vs steroids).
⚠️ WRONG: HS is Coombs-positive. Discriminator — HS is Coombs/DAT NEGATIVE (it is an inherited membrane defect, not antibody-mediated); a POSITIVE DAT with spherocytes means warm AIHA instead. Confusing the two is the classic board pitfall.
7. Spleen gauntlet phagocytosis
Hemolysis is EXTRAVASCULAR — rigid spherocytes cannot deform to pass through the narrow splenic cord slits (fenestrations of the cords of Billroth), so they are trapped, conditioned (losing more membrane), and ultimately engulfed by splenic macrophages. This explains the splenomegaly, the extravascular labs (high indirect bilirubin, high LDH, low haptoglobin, NO hemoglobinuria), and why splenectomy is curative of the anemia. Hemoglobin is recycled, not dumped into urine.
⚠️ WRONG: HS causes intravascular hemolysis with hemoglobinuria and hemosiderinuria. Discriminator — HS is EXTRAVASCULAR (splenic phagocytosis); you do NOT see hemoglobinuria or schistocytes. Intravascular hemolysis (hemoglobinuria, schistocytes, very low haptoglobin) points to PNH, MAHA/TTP, or mechanical valve, not HS.
8. Gallstones
Chronic hemolysis raises unconjugated bilirubin turnover, supersaturating bile and producing pigment (calcium bilirubinate, black) gallstones — a leading complication, often presenting in adolescence/young adulthood as biliary colic or cholecystitis. Stones may be the first clue to undiagnosed HS, and risk is higher in patients who co-inherit Gilbert syndrome (UGT1A1). Screen with RUQ ultrasound; concurrent cholecystectomy is often considered if splenectomy is planned.
⚠️ WRONG: the gallstones in HS are cholesterol stones from the metabolic syndrome. Discriminator — chronic hemolysis produces PIGMENT (bilirubinate) stones, not cholesterol stones; pigment stones in a young patient with jaundice and splenomegaly should trigger a hemolysis workup.
9. Splenectomy cures hemolysis
Splenectomy (or partial/subtotal splenectomy) removes the site of destruction, normalizing the hemoglobin and stopping the anemia — but the spherocytes REMAIN on smear because the underlying membrane defect is untouched. Reserve for moderate-to-severe or transfusion-dependent disease; defer until age >5–6 years (ideally ≥6) to preserve immune competence against encapsulated organisms. Post-splenectomy smear shows Howell-Jolly bodies. Lifelong overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI) risk persists.
⚠️ WRONG: after splenectomy the spherocytes disappear because the defect is corrected. Discriminator — splenectomy removes the GRAVEYARD, not the genetic membrane defect; spherocytes persist (now you also see Howell-Jolly bodies and target cells), but hemolysis/anemia resolve because there is no spleen to trap them.
10. Vaccinate / wait by age
Before elective splenectomy, vaccinate against the encapsulated organisms — pneumococcus (PCV13/PPSV23), Haemophilus influenzae type b, and meningococcus (MenACWY + MenB) — given ≥2 weeks pre-op. After splenectomy give daily penicillin prophylaxis (especially in young children) and counsel on urgent fever evaluation (OPSI). In children, delay surgery to age ≥6 to let the immune response to encapsulated bacteria mature.
⚠️ WRONG: vaccines can be given right after splenectomy whenever convenient. Discriminator — vaccinate ≥2 weeks BEFORE elective splenectomy for the best response; an asplenic, unvaccinated patient is at high risk of fatal pneumococcal sepsis (OPSI).
11. Severity lanes (green/yellow/red)
HS spans a spectrum graded by hemoglobin, reticulocyte count, and bilirubin: mild/compensated (~20–30%, near-normal Hgb, sometimes only diagnosed on family screening), moderate (most patients, mild anemia + intermittent jaundice), and severe (transfusion-dependent, growth failure). Severity guides therapy — mild cases need only folate and monitoring; severe cases warrant splenectomy. A spuriously 'normal' reticulocyte count during an aplastic crisis can mask severity.
⚠️ WRONG: all HS patients need splenectomy at diagnosis. Discriminator — most HS is mild/moderate and managed supportively (folate, surveillance); splenectomy is reserved for moderate-to-severe/transfusion-dependent disease and is deferred in young children.
12. Pregnant woman / aplastic crisis
Stressors that decompensate HS: PARVOVIRUS B19 causes an APLASTIC crisis — it infects erythroid progenitors and shuts off marrow red-cell production, so an already hemolytic patient (who depends on brisk reticulocytosis) drops the hemoglobin sharply with an INAPPROPRIATELY LOW reticulocyte count (the key clue); classic in a child with a preceding 'slapped-cheek' illness. Other triggers: pregnancy (increased demand) and infection. A HEMOLYTIC crisis (rising retics, falling Hgb) is different and usually infection-driven. Megaloblastic crisis occurs with folate depletion.
⚠️ WRONG: in parvovirus B19 aplastic crisis you expect a high reticulocyte count from the hemolysis. Discriminator — the hallmark of an APLASTIC crisis is a LOW reticulocyte count (parvovirus arrests erythropoiesis) despite worsening anemia; a HIGH retic count points to a hemolytic, not aplastic, crisis.
13. Folate / EPO support
Supportive care: daily FOLIC ACID (1 mg/day) to meet the high demand of chronic erythropoiesis and prevent megaloblastic crises; transfusion for severe anemia or crises; erythropoietin in neonates/young infants (whose marrow under-responds) to reduce transfusion needs in the first months of life. These measures support — they do NOT cure — only splenectomy abolishes the hemolysis.
⚠️ WRONG: iron supplementation is a routine part of HS care. Discriminator — HS patients are NOT iron deficient (hemolysis recycles iron); routine iron risks iron OVERLOAD, especially if transfused — give FOLATE, not iron, unless documented deficiency. Monitor ferritin in chronically transfused patients.
