📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Reduced vitamin K is active
Vitamin K cycles between two forms: the REDUCED (hydroquinone) form is the ACTIVE cofactor, and it is oxidized to the inactive vitamin K EPOXIDE during each carboxylation reaction. The enzyme VKORC1 then regenerates the reduced form so the cycle can continue. Mnemonic: 'reduced = ready (charged battery); epoxide = empty.'
⚠️ The oxidized vitamin K EPOXIDE is INACTIVE until VKORC1 recycles it back to the reduced form — and VKORC1 is exactly where warfarin acts.
2. GGCX gamma-carboxylase
Gamma-glutamyl carboxylase (GGCX) is the vitamin K–dependent enzyme that adds a carboxyl (-COOH) group to specific glutamate (Glu) residues, converting them to gamma-carboxyglutamate (Gla) on factors II, VII, IX, X and proteins C/S. This post-translational modification is what makes the factors functional. Mnemonic: 'GGCX is the press that stamps Glu → Gla.'
⚠️ Without carboxylation the factor PROTEINS are still synthesized and secreted — they are just NONFUNCTIONAL (these are the PIVKAs). So factor antigen can be normal while activity is low.
3. Factors II, VII, IX, X
The vitamin K–dependent PROCOAGULANTS are factors II (prothrombin), VII, IX, and X — all require gamma-carboxylation so their Gla domains can chelate Ca²⁺ and dock onto phospholipid membranes for cascade assembly. Mnemonic: '1972 — the years vitamin K depends on (factors 10, 9, 7, 2).'
⚠️ Factor V is NOT vitamin K–dependent (it's made by the liver but needs no carboxylation) — this is the key fact that separates vitamin K deficiency (FV normal) from liver failure (FV low).
4. Protein C & S also VK-dependent
The natural ANTICOAGULANTS protein C and protein S are ALSO vitamin K–dependent. Because protein C has a SHORT half-life (~8 h, similar to factor VII), it is depleted FIRST when warfarin starts — transiently before the procoagulants fall — creating an early hypercoagulable window. Mnemonic: 'C falls first → clots first.'
⚠️ Warfarin's transient drop in protein C is the mechanism of warfarin-induced SKIN NECROSIS (and is worsened in underlying protein C deficiency). This is why warfarin is BRIDGED with heparin at initiation and never started alone in acute thrombosis.
5. Carboxylation needs Ca²⁺
The gamma-carboxyglutamate (Gla) residues created by GGCX form negatively charged pockets that bind Ca²⁺, which in turn tethers the factor to negatively charged phospholipid membranes (e.g., activated platelet surface). This membrane localization is essential for tenase and prothrombinase complex assembly. Mnemonic: 'Gla grabs calcium, calcium glues to membrane.'
⚠️ UNcarboxylated factors cannot bind Ca²⁺ or membranes — these des-gamma-carboxy proteins (PIVKAs) circulate but cannot participate in the cascade.
6. Warfarin blocks VKORC1
Warfarin (a vitamin K antagonist) inhibits VKORC1 (vitamin K epoxide reductase complex 1), blocking regeneration of reduced vitamin K. With no recycled active cofactor, GGCX cannot carboxylate new factors → functional vitamin K deficiency. Mnemonic: 'warfarin bombs the recycler.'
⚠️ Warfarin does NOT destroy factors already in circulation — it only prevents synthesis of new FUNCTIONAL ones. That's why the anticoagulant effect is DELAYED (~2–3 days, until existing factors decay) rather than immediate.
7. Vitamin K reversal bypasses block
Administering vitamin K replenishes the REDUCED form directly, BYPASSING the VKORC1 block so GGCX can resume carboxylation. This is the rationale for vitamin K reversal of warfarin / treatment of deficiency. Mnemonic: 'flood the system with charged batteries to bypass the broken charger.'
⚠️ Vitamin K reversal is SLOW — it takes ~8–10 h (often 12–24 h for full effect) to synthesize new functional factors. For EMERGENT reversal you must add 4F-PCC (or FFP) for immediate factor replacement; vitamin K alone is not enough for life-threatening bleeds.
8. PIVKAs — nonfunctional factors
PIVKAs (Proteins Induced by Vitamin K Absence/Antagonism) are the des-gamma-carboxy factors produced when vitamin K is deficient or blocked — present in plasma but unable to bind Ca²⁺/membranes, so functionally inert. (PIVKA-II / des-gamma-carboxy prothrombin is also a tumor marker in hepatocellular carcinoma.) Mnemonic: 'PIVKA = Proteins In a Vitamin-K-less state.'
⚠️ Factor antigen/protein may be PRESENT and normal by immunoassay while factor ACTIVITY is low — measuring antigen instead of clotting activity will miss the defect.
9. PT prolongs first
PT/INR rises FIRST in vitamin K deficiency and on starting warfarin because it depends on factor VII, which has the SHORTEST half-life (~4–6 h) of all the factors — so VII falls fastest. PT is therefore the earliest, most sensitive screen. Mnemonic: 'Seven is the shortest → PT moves first.'
⚠️ It is PT (not aPTT) that is the earliest and most sensitive marker. Expecting the aPTT to move first is the classic distractor.
10. aPTT prolongs later
As deficiency persists, the longer-half-life factors II, IX, and X also fall, so the aPTT prolongs LATER; eventually BOTH PT and aPTT are abnormal. Sequence: PT first → then aPTT → then both. Mnemonic: 'VII leaves first (PT), the rest follow (aPTT).'
⚠️ A NORMAL aPTT early does NOT exclude vitamin K deficiency — only the PT may be prolonged at first. Don't rule it out on a normal aPTT.
11. TT stays normal
The thrombin time (TT) stays NORMAL in vitamin K deficiency because fibrinogen (factor I) and the thrombin-to-fibrin step are NOT vitamin K–dependent. A normal TT with prolonged PT/aPTT helps point to a factor-synthesis/carboxylation problem rather than a fibrinogen problem. Mnemonic: 'K doesn't touch fibrinogen, so TT is fine.'
⚠️ An ABNORMAL TT points AWAY from pure vitamin K deficiency — toward DIC, dysfibrinogenemia (liver disease), heparin contamination, or hypofibrinogenemia.
12. Factor V normal in VK def
Factor V is NORMAL in vitamin K deficiency because FV is not vitamin K–dependent. So the deficiency pattern is: low II/VII/IX/X (and protein C/S) but NORMAL factor V and normal fibrinogen. This normal FV is the key fingerprint. Mnemonic: 'V is fine when it's just K.'
⚠️ A LOW factor V means the problem is NOT isolated vitamin K deficiency — it indicates liver synthetic failure (where FV also drops). Use FV to triage between the two.
13. Liver failure: FV also low
In LIVER failure factor V (non-VK-dependent, hepatically synthesized) is ALSO low — the critical lab that distinguishes hepatic synthetic failure from pure vitamin K deficiency. Pairing: low FV + low FVIII → think DIC; low FV + normal/high FVIII → think liver; normal FV → vitamin K deficiency. Mnemonic: 'Five low = liver; Five fine = vitamin K.'
⚠️ Don't blame liver disease when factor V is NORMAL — that's vitamin K deficiency, which responds to vitamin K. Conversely a low FV won't correct with vitamin K.
