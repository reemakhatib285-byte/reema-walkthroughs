📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Glycolysis pipeline
Mature RBCs lack mitochondria, so glycolysis (Embden-Meyerhof) is their ONLY ATP source. Enzyme defects along this pipeline cause chronic nonspherocytic hemolytic anemia; severity depends on which step and residual activity.
⚠️ RBC enzymopathies cause Coombs-NEGATIVE, nonspherocytic hemolysis — distinguishing them from autoimmune (Coombs+) and membrane disorders (spherocytes).
2. Pyruvate kinase (PK) deficiency — last step
PK deficiency is the MOST COMMON glycolytic enzymopathy and the second most common enzymatic cause of hemolysis after G6PD. Autosomal recessive; ATP depletion causes chronic nonspherocytic hemolytic anemia, often from birth.
⚠️ PK deficiency is autosomal RECESSIVE and Coombs-negative — contrast with G6PD (X-linked, episodic, bite cells/Heinz bodies).
3. 2,3-BPG accumulation — right-shift
In PK deficiency, the upstream block raises 2,3-BPG, which right-shifts the O2-hemoglobin curve and ENHANCES O2 delivery to tissues. Patients tolerate anemia better than the Hb level predicts.
⚠️ PK patients are often less symptomatic than their Hb suggests because high 2,3-BPG improves tissue oxygen unloading.
4. Reticulocytosis after splenectomy — paradox
PK deficiency relies partly on residual mitochondrial ATP in reticulocytes; the spleen culls them. After SPLENECTOMY the reticulocyte count paradoxically RISES (more survive) even as hemolysis improves.
⚠️ A rising reticulocyte count after splenectomy in PK deficiency is expected and not treatment failure — a classic paradox.
5. Mitapivat (Pyrukynd)
Mitapivat is an oral PK ACTIVATOR that increases enzyme activity, raises hemoglobin, and reduces hemolysis/transfusion need in PK deficiency. It is the targeted therapy for missense (residual-activity) genotypes.
⚠️ Mitapivat works only when there's some residual enzyme to activate (missense alleles) — null/null genotypes respond poorly.
6. PFK deficiency — Tarui disease (GSD VII)
Phosphofructokinase deficiency (Tarui, glycogen storage disease type VII) combines a myopathy with exertional myoglobinuria AND a compensated hemolytic anemia, because PFK is shared by muscle and RBC glycolysis.
⚠️ Exercise intolerance + myoglobinuria + hemolysis points to PFK (Tarui), distinct from McArdle (GSD V, no hemolysis).
7. TPI deficiency — severe CNS + neuromuscular
Triosephosphate isomerase deficiency is the MOST SEVERE glycolytic enzymopathy: hemolytic anemia PLUS progressive neuromuscular and CNS degeneration, usually fatal in early childhood.
⚠️ TPI deficiency is unique among enzymopathies for severe neurologic involvement — not just hematologic.
8. PGK1 deficiency — X-linked
Phosphoglycerate kinase deficiency is the ONLY X-linked glycolytic enzymopathy (besides G6PD), causing hemolysis with variable myopathy and CNS dysfunction.
⚠️ Among glycolytic enzymes PGK is the X-linked one; most others (PK, TPI, PFK) are autosomal recessive.
9. Basophilic stippling — lead poisoning
Lead inhibits pyrimidine-5'-nucleotidase (and ferrochelatase/ALAD), leaving ribosomal RNA fragments that appear as coarse basophilic stippling. Causes hemolysis plus neurologic and GI symptoms.
⚠️ Coarse basophilic stippling = lead poisoning (P5'N inhibition); fine stippling is nonspecific. Don't confuse with reticulocyte polychromasia.
10. P5'N deficiency — same blood picture as lead
Inherited pyrimidine-5'-nucleotidase deficiency mimics lead poisoning: RNA fragments accumulate causing basophilic stippling and hemolysis, but WITHOUT elevated lead.
⚠️ Basophilic stippling with normal lead level = hereditary P5'N deficiency; both share the mechanism (failure to clear RNA).
11. Iron overload without transfusion
Chronic hemolytic enzymopathies (esp. PK deficiency) cause iron overload from increased absorption even WITHOUT transfusions; chelation may be needed.
⚠️ Don't assume iron overload in hemolysis requires transfusions — ineffective erythropoiesis drives absorption independently.
12. Coombs-negative — diagnostic sign
Enzymopathies and membrane defects are Coombs (direct antiglobulin) NEGATIVE, separating them from autoimmune hemolytic anemia. A negative DAT in chronic hemolysis redirects work-up to enzyme assays.
⚠️ A negative Coombs in chronic hemolysis rules OUT autoimmune cause and points to enzyme/membrane/hemoglobin disorders.
13. Pressure accumulation side tank
Upstream blocks in glycolysis cause buildup of intermediates (e.g., 2,3-BPG in PK deficiency) which both modulate O2 affinity and indicate the level of the enzymatic lesion.
⚠️ The pattern of accumulated intermediates localizes the defective enzyme — useful when interpreting metabolic studies.
14. Young pregnant woman — first presentation
Mild PK deficiency can stay subclinical for years and first declare itself when hemolysis is unmasked by pregnancy or another stressor, with delayed diagnosis.
⚠️ A chronic well-compensated enzymopathy may present late under physiologic stress — absence of childhood anemia doesn't exclude it.
