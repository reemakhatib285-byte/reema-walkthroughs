📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Suspect 1: S. pneumoniae
Streptococcus pneumoniae is the #1 cause of bacterial meningitis in adults and the elderly, appearing as gram-positive, lancet-shaped diplococci that are alpha-hemolytic, optochin-sensitive, and bile-soluble. It is the predominant pathogen across most age groups beyond the neonatal period. Empiric coverage is ceftriaxone PLUS vancomycin (for resistant strains), and dexamethasone has its strongest mortality benefit here.
⚠️ WRONG: naming N. meningitidis as the most common adult cause. Although meningococcus dominates outbreak/young-adult settings, S. pneumoniae is the single most common cause overall in adults — picking meningococcus is the classic age-group trap.
2. Pneumococcus risk factors
Pneumococcal meningitis risk factors: basilar skull fracture / CSF leak (recurrent meningitis), alcoholism, ASPLENIA/sickle cell (loss of encapsulated-organism clearance), diabetes, multiple myeloma/hypogammaglobulinemia, and cochlear implants. Asplenic/sickle patients are vulnerable to all encapsulated bugs (pneumococcus, meningococcus, Hib) and must be vaccinated.
⚠️ WRONG: forgetting recurrent meningitis points to an anatomic defect. Recurrent pneumococcal meningitis should prompt a search for a CSF leak (basilar skull fracture, cribriform plate defect) or a complement/immunoglobulin deficiency — not simply re-treating each episode.
3. 50% / 20% mortality
S. pneumoniae accounts for roughly half of adult bacterial meningitis cases and carries the HIGHEST mortality (~20-30%), with frequent neurologic sequelae (hearing loss, cognitive deficits, seizures). Its high lethality is why empiric vancomycin and adjunctive dexamethasone are emphasized for suspected pneumococcal disease.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming meningococcal disease is the deadliest because of its dramatic rash. Per-case mortality is actually highest for pneumococcus (~20-30%) and Listeria, while treated meningococcal meningitis runs lower (~3-7%); the rash signals severity but not the highest overall case fatality.
4. Suspect 2: N. meningitidis
Neisseria meningitidis is the leading cause in ADOLESCENTS and YOUNG ADULTS, especially in crowded settings (college dorms, military barracks), seen as gram-negative diplococci that ferment maltose and glucose and grow on Thayer-Martin agar. Serogroups B, C, W, and Y predominate; vaccination (MenACWY and MenB) is recommended for adolescents, college freshmen in dorms, military recruits, and asplenic/complement-deficient patients.
⚠️ WRONG: choosing pneumococcus for a college freshman in a dorm with a petechial rash and rapid sepsis. The age, crowded-living exposure, and purpuric rash point to N. meningitidis — context overrides the 'pneumococcus is most common overall' default.
5. Petechial/purpuric rash
Meningococcemia begins as a maculopapular rash that rapidly becomes PETECHIAL/PURPURIC and can progress to purpura fulminans with skin necrosis. Endotoxin-driven DIC and bilateral adrenal hemorrhage produce Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome (shock, hypotension, adrenal insufficiency). This is a true emergency requiring immediate antibiotics and droplet isolation.
⚠️ WRONG: attributing a non-blanching petechial rash with fever to a benign viral exanthem. A petechial/purpuric rash with fever and meningismus is meningococcemia until proven otherwise — delay in antibiotics is fatal, and the patient needs droplet precautions plus contact prophylaxis.
6. Complement deficiency
Terminal complement deficiency (C5-C9, the membrane attack complex) and use of complement inhibitors (eculizumab, ravulizumab) markedly predispose to recurrent/invasive Neisseria infections, because MAC is needed to lyse Neisseria. Such patients (and those on eculizumab) must receive meningococcal vaccination and sometimes prophylactic antibiotics.
⚠️ WRONG: not investigating immune deficiency after recurrent meningococcal (Neisseria) infections. Recurrent Neisseria disease should trigger a CH50/complement workup for terminal complement deficiency — missing this leaves the patient at risk for repeated life-threatening episodes.
7. Suspect 3: Listeria
Listeria monocytogenes is a gram-positive ROD with characteristic tumbling motility (and intracellular actin-based 'rocket' propulsion) that causes meningitis in neonates, adults >50, pregnant women, and the immunocompromised. It has a predilection for the brainstem (rhombencephalitis) and is intrinsically resistant to all cephalosporins. Treat with ampicillin (add gentamicin for synergy) or TMP-SMX if penicillin-allergic.
⚠️ WRONG: covering an elderly or immunocompromised patient with ceftriaxone + vancomycin alone. Cephalosporins have ZERO Listeria activity, so failing to add ampicillin in a Listeria-risk host is a fatal omission — the cue is a gram-positive rod or a high-risk host.
8. Listeria host: >50 / transplant
Add ampicillin for Listeria coverage in the classic high-risk hosts: age <1 month (neonates), age >50, pregnancy, and impaired cell-mediated immunity (transplant, chronic steroids, TNF inhibitors, HIV, malignancy). Listeria is an intracellular pathogen controlled by T cells, so any T-cell/cell-mediated defect raises risk.
⚠️ WRONG: thinking only neonates and the very elderly need Listeria coverage. Pregnant women and any patient with impaired cell-mediated immunity (steroids, transplant, HIV) are equally at risk regardless of age — all require empiric ampicillin.
9. Listeria food sources
Listeria is foodborne, transmitted via deli/processed meats, unpasteurized soft cheeses, hot dogs, pâté, smoked seafood, and contaminated coleslaw/produce. It uniquely grows at refrigeration temperatures (cold enrichment, 4°C). Pregnant women are counseled to avoid these foods because of the risk of transplacental infection, miscarriage, and neonatal listeriosis (granulomatosis infantiseptica).
⚠️ WRONG: dismissing listeriosis because food was refrigerated. Listeria multiplies at refrigerator temperatures, so 'it was kept cold' does not exclude it — the cold-tolerant deli-meat/soft-cheese history is a positive clue, not reassurance.
10. Suspect 4: Group B Strep
Group B Streptococcus (Streptococcus agalactiae) is the leading cause of NEONATAL meningitis (early-onset from maternal vaginal colonization; prevented by intrapartum penicillin after 35-37 week screening) and is increasingly seen in older adults with diabetes, cirrhosis, or other comorbidities. It is a gram-positive coccus in chains, beta-hemolytic, CAMP-test and hippurate positive.
⚠️ WRONG: limiting GBS to neonates only. GBS meningitis is rising in elderly and immunocompromised adults — anchoring it solely to newborns misses the at-risk older diabetic, and intrapartum GBS prophylaxis prevents early- but not late-onset neonatal disease.
11. Fifth suspect: Hib
Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) was historically a top cause of childhood meningitis but is now RARE in vaccinated populations thanks to the conjugate vaccine. Consider it in unvaccinated or under-vaccinated children, and in adults with CSF leak, sinusitis/otitis, or asplenia. It is a small gram-negative coccobacillus requiring factors X and V for growth.
⚠️ WRONG: excluding Hib in any child with a 'complete' vaccine sticker. In unvaccinated or partially vaccinated children, Hib remains a real cause — verify true immunization status rather than assuming protection, and recall non-type-b strains are not vaccine-covered.
12. Mortality ranking
Approximate case-fatality ranking: S. pneumoniae ~20-30% (highest), Listeria ~15-20%, H. influenzae ~5%, N. meningitidis ~3-7% (lowest of these when treated). Mortality reflects host factors and organism virulence, and survivors of pneumococcal disease often have lasting neurologic deficits.
⚠️ WRONG: ranking meningococcus as most lethal because of its fulminant presentation. Despite its dramatic septic course, treated meningococcal meningitis has comparatively LOW case fatality; pneumococcus and Listeria are deadlier per case.
13. Bottom rule: add ampicillin
Bottom-line rule: gram-positive RODS on CSF Gram stain, or a Listeria-risk host (neonate, >50, pregnant, alcoholic, transplant/immunosuppressed), means ADD AMPICILLIN to the ceftriaxone + vancomycin backbone. Cephalosporins have no Listeria coverage, so ampicillin (or TMP-SMX if PCN-allergic) is the dedicated agent.
⚠️ WRONG: trusting ceftriaxone + vancomycin alone to cover all comers. This pair misses Listeria entirely — in neonates, >50, pregnant, alcoholic, or immunocompromised patients, omitting ampicillin is a board-classic fatal error.
14. Empiric regimen by age summary
Added age-based empiric summary: NEONATES (GBS, E. coli, Listeria) → ampicillin + cefotaxime (or + gentamicin); 1 month-50 yr (pneumococcus, meningococcus) → ceftriaxone + vancomycin; >50 yr or immunocompromised → ceftriaxone + vancomycin + AMPICILLIN. Match the empiric drugs to the age-specific pathogen spectrum.
⚠️ WRONG: giving ceftriaxone to a neonate. Ceftriaxone is generally avoided in neonates (displaces bilirubin → kernicterus; precipitates with calcium-containing fluids) — use cefotaxime (or ampicillin + gentamicin) instead in the newborn.
