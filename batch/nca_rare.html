📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Rare outliers worth knowing
The board-edge causes beyond the common primary-hyperPTH-vs-malignancy split. These show up as 'second-order' questions: the labs don't fit the usual pattern, or a syndrome/context is the giveaway. The discriminator is almost always the combination of PTH, phosphate, urine calcium, and the clinical setting (CKD, pregnancy, autoimmune, mitochondrial, metastatic).
⚠️ WRONG: forcing every hypercalcemia into 'hyperPTH or PTHrP.' When labs are discordant (e.g., high PTH after a transplant, low Ca with prostate cancer), recognize a rare cause rather than over-anchoring on the common two.
2. Tertiary hyperPTH
Tertiary hyperparathyroidism: after years of CKD-driven SECONDARY hyperPTH, the glands become AUTONOMOUS (hyperplastic) and keep over-secreting PTH even after the stimulus is gone — classically post renal transplant → high Ca, HIGH PTH, often low/normal phosphate after transplant. Treat with cinacalcet or parathyroidectomy.
⚠️ WRONG: calling persistent high PTH + high calcium after a kidney transplant 'primary' hyperPTH. The history of long-standing CKD/secondary hyperPTH that became autonomous defines TERTIARY disease.
3. Ectopic PTH secretion
TRUE ectopic PTH secretion (intact PTH made by a non-parathyroid tumor — e.g., ovarian, lung, neuroendocrine) is exceedingly rare: high Ca with HIGH PTH but no parathyroid source on imaging. Far rarer than PTHrP-mediated humoral hypercalcemia.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming high PTH in a cancer patient means ectopic PTH. The overwhelmingly common malignancy mediator is PTHrP (which SUPPRESSES native PTH) — true ectopic PTH is a curiosity, not the default.
4. Paget / immobilization
Paget disease of bone (disordered high-turnover remodeling, very high alk phos) is usually normocalcemic but causes hypercalcemia when high bone turnover meets IMMOBILIZATION → skeletal calcium release. Bisphosphonates treat both the Paget activity and the hypercalcemia.
⚠️ WRONG: expecting hypercalcemia in active ambulatory Paget. Paget patients are typically normocalcemic; it is IMMOBILIZATION (or a fracture) superimposed on high turnover that liberates calcium.
5. Williams / elfin
Williams syndrome (7q11.23 deletion, elastin/ELN): elfin facies, supravalvular AORTIC STENOSIS, intellectual disability with a notably friendly/'cocktail-party' personality, and IDIOPATHIC INFANTILE HYPERCALCEMIA. A pediatric/genetic vignette cause of high calcium.
⚠️ WRONG: missing the cardiac association. A friendly child with elfin facies and hypercalcemia has Williams — and the key complication to screen for is supravalvular aortic stenosis.
6. Rhabdomyolysis recovery
Rhabdomyolysis: EARLY hypocalcemia (calcium deposits into damaged muscle and binds liberated phosphate), then REBOUND hypercalcemia during recovery as that calcium is mobilized back out of healing tissue. The phase determines the direction of the calcium derangement.
⚠️ WRONG: aggressively repleting calcium during the early hypocalcemic phase of rhabdo. Unless symptomatic, hold off — the calcium will rebound (sometimes to hypercalcemia) as muscle recovers; over-repletion worsens the rebound.
7. Pregnancy/lactation PTHrP
During pregnancy/lactation the breast and placenta produce PTHrP to mobilize calcium for the fetus/milk → can cause maternal hypercalcemia. Usually mild; consider it before attributing hypercalcemia in a lactating woman to a tumor.
⚠️ WRONG: launching a malignancy workup for mild hypercalcemia in a lactating woman. Physiologic breast/placental PTHrP is the benign explanation — context first.
8. Pseudohypercalcemia
Pseudohypercalcemia: a high paraprotein (multiple MYELOMA, Waldenström) binds calcium, raising TOTAL calcium while IONIZED calcium is normal — the patient is not truly hypercalcemic. Hemoconcentration/dehydration with high albumin does the same.
⚠️ WRONG: treating an elevated total calcium in a myeloma patient as an emergency without checking ionized Ca. If ionized is normal, it's pseudohypercalcemia — but note myeloma ALSO causes TRUE hypercalcemia via lytic bone disease, so verify with ionized calcium.
9. APS-1
Autoimmune polyglandular syndrome type 1 (APECED, AIRE gene): the triad of chronic mucocutaneous CANDIDIASIS, autoimmune HYPOPARATHYROIDISM (→ hypocalcemia, low PTH), and ADRENAL insufficiency (Addison). A genetic cause of acquired hypoparathyroidism in the young.
⚠️ WRONG: working up isolated hypocalcemia without the syndrome lens. Hypoparathyroidism plus candidiasis plus adrenal insufficiency = APS-1 — and missing the adrenal component risks fatal adrenal crisis.
10. Kearns-Sayre / mito
Mitochondrial disorders (Kearns-Sayre: progressive external ophthalmoplegia, pigmentary retinopathy, cardiac conduction block before age 20) can include HYPOPARATHYROIDISM → hypocalcemia. A rare multisystem cause of low PTH/low Ca.
⚠️ WRONG: overlooking the cardiac conduction defect in Kearns-Sayre. The hypocalcemia is a feature, but the life-threatening association is heart BLOCK (may need a pacemaker).
11. Wilson / hemochromatosis infiltrate
Infiltrative/deposition diseases (hemochromatosis and transfusional iron overload in thalassemia, Wilson copper, sarcoid, metastases) can DESTROY the parathyroid glands → hypoparathyroidism with hypocalcemia and low PTH. Consider in patients with iron/copper overload syndromes.
⚠️ WRONG: attributing endocrine failure in hemochromatosis only to diabetes/hypogonadism. Iron deposition can also wreck the parathyroids — check calcium/PTH in the multi-organ infiltrative picture.
12. HyperPTH obstetric
Maternal PRIMARY hyperparathyroidism in pregnancy (rare): maternal hypercalcemia crosses the placenta and suppresses fetal parathyroids → NEONATAL HYPOCALCEMIA/tetany after birth. Maternal disease may also cause miscarriage/pre-eclampsia; surgery is sometimes done in the 2nd trimester.
⚠️ WRONG: ignoring the newborn. A neonate with hypocalcemic tetany should prompt evaluation of the MOTHER for hyperparathyroidism — the fetal parathyroids were chronically suppressed in utero.
13. Acute kidney injury
AKI shifts calcium by phase: OLIGURIC phase → hyperphosphatemia and hypocalcemia (phosphate retention, reduced 1,25-D); RECOVERY/diuretic phase or rhabdo-AKI → rebound HYPERcalcemia as deposited calcium re-mobilizes. Direction depends on the stage of injury.
⚠️ WRONG: over-treating early AKI hypocalcemia. As in rhabdo, calcium often rebounds in recovery — replete only for symptoms/severe levels to avoid recovery-phase hypercalcemia.
14. Prostate cancer osteoblastic
OSTEOBLASTIC metastases (classically prostate cancer, also some breast and small-cell lung) consume calcium to build (sclerotic) bone → HYPOcalcemia, the opposite of osteolytic mets. Look for sclerotic lesions on imaging and a high alk phos with low calcium.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming all bone metastases raise calcium. OSTEOLYTIC mets (breast, myeloma) cause HYPERcalcemia; OSTEOBLASTIC mets (prostate) cause HYPOcalcemia — the lesion type flips the direction.
15. Severe hyper/hypo banners
The extremes to recognize instantly: SEVERE HYPERcalcemia → bradyarrhythmia, SHORT QT, lethargy/coma, profound dehydration; SEVERE HYPOcalcemia → carpopedal spasm, LARYNGOSPASM, seizures, LONG QT/torsades. Both are emergencies needing immediate, opposite interventions.
⚠️ WRONG: swapping the QT findings. SHORT QT = HYPER-calcemia; LONG QT = HYPO-calcemia. This single mix-up is one of the most common ECG distractors on calcium questions.
