📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. a wave
The a wave is the venous pulsation produced by RIGHT ATRIAL CONTRACTION in late diastole, just before S1. It corresponds to the P wave on ECG and is the first positive deflection of the JVP.
⚠️ Don't time it to systole — the a wave is PRESYSTOLIC (atrial kick), losing it points to loss of organized atrial activity.
2. c wave
The c wave is the small bulging of the closed TRICUSPID VALVE into the right atrium at the very start of ventricular systole (isovolumetric contraction). It immediately follows S1.
⚠️ The c wave is a tricuspid bulge, not an atrial event — don't confuse it with the a wave's atrial contraction.
3. x descent
The x descent reflects ATRIAL RELAXATION plus downward pull of the closed tricuspid valve as the ventricle contracts. It is the dominant downward movement of normal JVP in systole.
⚠️ A blunted/absent x descent suggests tricuspid regurgitation — it gets replaced by the regurgitant systolic wave.
4. v wave
The v wave is passive RIGHT ATRIAL FILLING against a CLOSED tricuspid valve during ventricular systole. It peaks at end-systole, just before the tricuspid opens.
⚠️ The v wave is atrial FILLING against a shut valve — not ventricular filling; a giant v wave means tricuspid regurgitation.
5. y descent
The y descent is the fall in RA pressure as the TRICUSPID VALVE OPENS and the right atrium empties rapidly into the ventricle in early diastole.
⚠️ Slow/absent y descent = obstructed RA emptying (TS, tamponade, myxoma); a steep y descent = TR or constriction.
6. Giant a wave
A GIANT a wave means the atrium contracts against high resistance to RA emptying: TRICUSPID STENOSIS, PULMONARY HYPERTENSION, or PULMONIC STENOSIS. The hypertrophied RA generates an exaggerated presystolic wave.
⚠️ Giant a is resistance to emptying (TS / pulm HTN / PS) — it still requires organized atrial contraction, so it cannot occur in atrial fibrillation.
7. Absent a wave = AFib
An ABSENT a wave indicates loss of organized atrial contraction — the hallmark is ATRIAL FIBRILLATION. With no coordinated atrial systole, the presystolic a wave disappears entirely.
⚠️ Absent a wave is pathologic (AFib), NOT a normal variant — recognizing its loss is the tested point.
8. Cannon a waves
CANNON a waves occur in AV DISSOCIATION — the atrium contracts against a CLOSED tricuspid valve (ventricle already contracting). Causes: COMPLETE (third-degree) HEART BLOCK and VENTRICULAR TACHYCARDIA.
⚠️ Cannon a waves are AV dissociation, NOT a rhythm artifact — intermittent cannons are a bedside clue to complete heart block / VT.
9. Slammed-shut tricuspid
The cannon fires because the atrium contracts when the tricuspid valve is already SHUT — atrial blood has nowhere to go but back up the jugular vein, producing the explosive wave.
⚠️ It is the closed valve at the wrong moment (dissociation), not a strong atrium per se, that makes the cannon.
10. Broken conduction wire
The broken wire encodes AV CONDUCTION FAILURE — the electrical link between atria and ventricles is severed, so they beat independently (AV dissociation), which is the mechanism behind cannon a waves.
⚠️ AV dissociation is an electrical conduction problem; cannon a waves are its visible jugular consequence, not the cause.
11. Tall v + fast y = TR
A TALL v wave with a RAPID y descent is the signature of TRICUSPID REGURGITATION — blood regurgitates into the RA during systole (giant v), then empties fast when the valve opens (steep y).
⚠️ This is TR, not TS — TR gives a TALL v with a STEEP/fast y, the opposite of TS's slow y.
12. Burst waterfall chute
The gushing waterfall depicts the rapid RA emptying of TR — the regurgitant volume drains quickly into the RV in early diastole, producing the brisk y descent.
⚠️ A fast, gushing y descent points to TR (or constriction), the opposite picture from the trickling slow-y of obstruction.
13. Slow y = TS / tamponade
A SLOW or ABSENT y descent means impaired RA emptying: TRICUSPID STENOSIS, CARDIAC TAMPONADE, or RA MYXOMA. The RA cannot empty quickly, so the descent is sluggish.
⚠️ Slow y = obstruction to RA emptying (TS/tamponade/myxoma); don't confuse with the fast y of TR/constriction.
14. Kussmaul sign
Kussmaul sign is a PARADOXICAL RISE in JVP on INSPIRATION (normally JVP falls). It signals impaired RV filling: CONSTRICTIVE PERICARDITIS, RV INFARCTION, or RESTRICTIVE CARDIOMYOPATHY.
⚠️ Kussmaul is a paradoxical RISE on inspiration — normal JVP FALLS with inspiration; the rise is the abnormal, tested finding.
15. JVP vs carotid characteristics
JVP is NON-PALPABLE, BIPHASIC (two peaks per cycle), FALLS with inspiration and with sitting up, and is measured at 30–45°. The CAROTID is PALPABLE and monophasic — the key bedside distinction.
⚠️ Palpable = carotid, not JVP — a palpable, single-peak, non-respiratory pulsation is arterial, so don't read it as venous waveforms.
