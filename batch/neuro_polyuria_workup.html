📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Sequence: urine, Na, copeptin, MRI
The modern polyuria workup proceeds from confirming polyuria, to serum sodium/osmolality, to copeptin-based testing, then MRI. Copeptin has largely replaced the classic water deprivation test for accuracy. The order localizes the lesion efficiently.
⚠️ Hypertonic-saline-stimulated copeptin outperforms the water deprivation test, which often misclassifies partial central DI and polydipsia.
2. Confirm true polyuria >3 L
True polyuria is urine output >3 L/day or >50 mL/kg/day on a 24-hour collection. This separates genuine polyuria from urinary frequency. Osmotic causes (glucose) are excluded at this step.
⚠️ Frequency without high 24h volume is not polyuria; and glucose/mannitol osmotic diuresis must be excluded before DI workup.
3. Primary polydipsia — low Na
A low-normal serum sodium suggests primary polydipsia, where excess intake dilutes plasma and suppresses AVP. Baseline copeptin is low and rises appropriately with stimulation. Water excess, not water loss, drives symptoms.
⚠️ Low serum sodium favors polydipsia (water excess); DI typically presents with high-normal sodium from free water loss.
4. High Na (>147) branch
A high serum sodium with polyuria points toward AVP deficiency or resistance, since water loss concentrates plasma. This branch directs toward copeptin testing to separate central from nephrogenic. Implies inadequate water replacement.
⚠️ High-normal/high sodium with dilute urine = DI (deficiency or resistance); the sodium direction is the first fork.
5. Station 1 — baseline copeptin
Baseline copeptin is drawn first. A baseline copeptin >21.4 pmol/L diagnoses nephrogenic DI (AVP resistance) without further deprivation. High AVP secretion with renal non-response defines this.
⚠️ Baseline copeptin >21.4 pmol/L = nephrogenic DI; no water deprivation needed at this cutoff.
6. Copeptin >21.4 = AVP resistance
A markedly elevated baseline copeptin reflects intense endogenous AVP that the kidney ignores. This confirms nephrogenic DI and shortcuts the protocol. DDAVP will fail to concentrate urine.
⚠️ Very high copeptin means the pituitary is working — the lesion is renal (nephrogenic), so DDAVP is not the treatment.
7. Station 2/3 — stimulated copeptin
If baseline is intermediate, copeptin is stimulated with hypertonic saline (or arginine) to provoke AVP. A stimulated copeptin >4.9 pmol/L excludes central DI with ~97% accuracy. This separates partial central DI from primary polydipsia.
⚠️ Stimulated copeptin >4.9 pmol/L rules out central DI; below it confirms AVP deficiency — the key polydipsia-vs-central-DI discriminator.
8. Stimulated >4.9 at Na 150
During hypertonic saline infusion sodium is driven toward 150, and copeptin response is measured. A robust rise excludes central DI; a blunted rise confirms it. Requires close sodium monitoring during infusion.
⚠️ Hypertonic saline testing needs careful sodium monitoring; the copeptin response at target Na is what discriminates, not symptoms.
9. Station 4 — DDAVP/desmopressin trial
A desmopressin trial assesses renal responsiveness: urine concentrates in central DI but not nephrogenic DI. A urine osmolality rise >50% after DDAVP indicates central DI. A rise <50% indicates AVP resistance.
⚠️ DDAVP response >50% urine osmolality rise = central DI; <50% = nephrogenic DI — the functional confirmation step.
10. Urine Uosm rise >50% = central
Concentrating the urine after exogenous DDAVP confirms the kidney can respond, localizing the defect to AVP production (central). This complements copeptin testing. Treatment is then DDAVP replacement.
⚠️ A good DDAVP response localizes the lesion centrally — replace with DDAVP; a poor response means manage as nephrogenic DI.
11. MRI bright spot
MRI of the hypothalamic-pituitary region looks for the posterior pituitary bright spot, absent in central DI, plus stalk lesions or masses. Imaging follows biochemical confirmation. Identifies treatable structural causes.
⚠️ Absent bright spot supports central DI but is nonspecific with age — confirm biochemically with copeptin before attributing to a lesion.
12. Gestational AVP deficiency
Gestational DI arises from placental vasopressinase clearing native AVP and presents in the third trimester. It responds to DDAVP, which resists the enzyme. Resolves after delivery.
⚠️ In pregnancy, copeptin assays must account for vasopressinase; gestational DI responds to DDAVP, not native vasopressin.
13. New names same diseases
Nomenclature updated: central DI = AVP deficiency, nephrogenic DI = AVP resistance, with primary polydipsia unchanged. The renaming reduces confusion with diabetes mellitus. The underlying pathophysiology is identical.
⚠️ Know both terms: AVP deficiency = central DI, AVP resistance = nephrogenic DI — exams may use either nomenclature.
