📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Warfarin throne (first-line)
Warfarin (a vitamin-K antagonist) is first-line for thrombotic APS because it provides durable anticoagulation that DOACs cannot reliably match in this antibody-driven hypercoagulable state. Standard target INR is 2-3 after a venous event; warfarin inhibits VKORC1, depleting factors II, VII, IX, X and proteins C and S. Always bridge with heparin/LMWH at initiation to cover the transient protein-C-driven procoagulant window.
⚠️ DOACs (rivaroxaban, apixaban) are the wrong answer for thrombotic APS — the TRAPS trial showed significantly higher arterial recurrence with rivaroxaban, especially in triple-positive (lupus anticoagulant + anticardiolipin + anti-β2GP1) patients.
2. Lifelong banner
Anticoagulation after a thrombotic APS event is indefinite/lifelong, unlike a provoked VTE which stops at 3-6 months. The persistent antiphospholipid antibodies represent an ongoing prothrombotic driver, so recurrence risk stays high if therapy is withdrawn. Mnemonic: APS clot = 'A Permanent Script' for anticoagulation.
⚠️ Stopping anticoagulation at a fixed 3-6 months (the rule for a provoked clot) is the classic trap — in confirmed APS the antibodies persist, so the clot is effectively 'unprovoked' and treatment continues indefinitely.
3. Venous target (INR 2-3)
After a venous thrombosis, target INR is 2-3 with warfarin — the same intensity used for standard VTE. Higher-intensity warfarin (INR 3-4) was studied and showed NO benefit over INR 2-3 for venous disease while increasing bleeding. So for venous APS, conventional intensity wins.
⚠️ Reflexively pushing INR to 3-4 for any APS clot is wrong for venous events — trials (Crowther 2003) found high-intensity warfarin gave no extra protection and more bleeding for venous disease.
4. Arterial / higher-intensity risk shelf
Arterial APS events (stroke, MI) often warrant either higher-intensity warfarin (target INR ~3, range 3-4) or warfarin (INR 2-3) PLUS low-dose aspirin. Triple-positivity and recurrent thrombosis push toward more aggressive regimens. The arterial bed is more prone to recurrence, so single-agent low-intensity therapy is frequently inadequate.
⚠️ Treating an arterial APS stroke with antiplatelet monotherapy (aspirin alone) is a common wrong answer — anticoagulation, often intensified or combined with aspirin, is needed; aspirin alone underprotects against recurrent arterial thrombosis.
5. DOAC graveyard (banned)
DOACs (rivaroxaban, apixaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) are avoided/contraindicated in high-risk thrombotic APS. The 2019 EULAR guidance recommends against DOACs in triple-positive patients and after arterial events. Mechanism rationale: factor Xa/IIa inhibition appears insufficient against the multi-hit antibody-mediated thrombogenesis.
⚠️ Switching a stable APS patient to a DOAC for 'convenience' or to avoid INR monitoring is the signature board trap — especially in triple-positive or arterial disease, where DOACs raise recurrence.
6. Asymptomatic shelf
Asymptomatic aPL carriers (positive antibodies, no clot) may receive low-dose aspirin 81 mg for primary prophylaxis, especially with a high-risk profile (triple-positive, persistent lupus anticoagulant) or coexisting SLE, where hydroxychloroquine adds protection. Risk-factor modification (avoid estrogen, treat HTN/dyslipidemia) is essential.
⚠️ Putting an asymptomatic aPL carrier on full therapeutic anticoagulation is over-treatment — they have never clotted, so the benefit-to-bleed ratio favors at most low-dose aspirin, not warfarin.
7. Warfarin teratogenic (no in pregnancy)
Warfarin crosses the placenta and is teratogenic — first-trimester exposure causes warfarin embryopathy (nasal hypoplasia, stippled epiphyses/chondrodysplasia punctata) and later CNS/bleeding complications. It must be stopped before conception or as soon as pregnancy is confirmed and replaced with LMWH, which does not cross the placenta. Mnemonic: 'Warfarin Wrecks the Womb.'
⚠️ Continuing warfarin into pregnancy is the trap — it causes fetal warfarin syndrome; the correct move is to switch to heparin/LMWH (heparins are large and do not cross the placenta).
8. Obstetric bed — no prior clot
Obstetric APS without prior thrombosis (recurrent miscarriage/late fetal loss/severe pre-eclampsia) is treated with PROPHYLACTIC-dose LMWH (e.g., enoxaparin 40 mg daily) PLUS low-dose aspirin 81 mg, ideally started preconception/early pregnancy. This combination improves live-birth rates by preventing placental microthrombosis.
⚠️ Aspirin alone is insufficient for obstetric APS — the heparin-plus-aspirin combination is what raises live-birth rates; choosing aspirin monotherapy is the undertreatment trap.
9. Thrombotic bed — prior clot
A pregnant APS patient with a prior thrombosis needs THERAPEUTIC (full-dose) LMWH (e.g., enoxaparin 1 mg/kg BID) plus low-dose aspirin throughout pregnancy, then transition back to warfarin postpartum (warfarin is safe in breastfeeding). They were on lifelong anticoagulation already, so pregnancy converts warfarin to full-dose LMWH, not prophylactic dosing.
⚠️ Dropping a prior-clot patient to prophylactic LMWH during pregnancy is the trap — a history of thrombosis demands therapeutic dosing; prophylactic dosing leaves them under-anticoagulated.
10. Refractory bed — escalate
Refractory obstetric APS (pregnancy loss despite LMWH + aspirin) is escalated by adding hydroxychloroquine (immunomodulatory, lowers antibody titers and stabilizes placental function) and/or low-dose prednisone in the first trimester; IVIG/plasmapheresis are last-resort. HCQ is increasingly favored adjunctively in APS pregnancies.
⚠️ Abandoning anticoagulation or jumping straight to high-dose chronic steroids is wrong — escalation builds ON the LMWH/aspirin backbone with HCQ first, not instead of it.
11. CAPS definition board
Catastrophic APS (CAPS, 'Asherson syndrome') = involvement of ≥3 organs/systems developing simultaneously over <1 week, with histopathologic confirmation of small-vessel occlusion and laboratory aPL positivity. It is rare (<1% of APS) but carries ~30-50% mortality. Kidneys, lungs, brain, heart, and skin are commonly hit.
⚠️ CAPS is a microvascular small-vessel thrombotic storm — confusing it with the usual single large-vessel APS event (one DVT/stroke) misses the diagnosis; CAPS is the 'all organs at once' catastrophe.
12. CAPS DDx flags (TTP/HUS/DIC/HIT)
CAPS must be distinguished from other thrombotic microangiopathies: TTP (ADAMTS13 <10%, treat with PLEX), HUS (Shiga toxin/complement, schistocytes), DIC (consumptive coagulopathy with low fibrinogen and prolonged PT/PTT), and HIT (platelet drop 5-10 days after heparin, positive PF4 antibody). aPL positivity plus multi-organ small-vessel thrombi points to CAPS.
⚠️ Mislabeling CAPS as DIC is a key trap — in DIC fibrinogen falls and bleeding dominates, whereas CAPS is primarily a thrombotic storm with aPL positivity; the wrong label delays immunosuppression and plasma exchange.
13. Triple therapy station
First-line CAPS therapy is TRIPLE therapy: therapeutic anticoagulation (IV heparin) + high-dose glucocorticoids (e.g., methylprednisolone 1 g IV pulse x3 days) + plasma exchange and/or IVIG. Refractory cases add rituximab or eculizumab (complement blockade). The combination targets clot, inflammation, and circulating antibodies simultaneously.
⚠️ Anticoagulation alone is inadequate in CAPS — the trap is treating it like a routine clot; you must add immunosuppression (steroids) plus PLEX/IVIG to halt the cytokine-and-antibody-driven storm.
14. Treat triggers
CAPS is precipitated in ~half of cases by an identifiable trigger — most commonly infection, but also surgery/trauma, anticoagulation withdrawal, malignancy, and pregnancy/puerperium. Aggressively identifying and treating the precipitant (e.g., antibiotics for infection) is part of management alongside triple therapy.
⚠️ Treating only the thrombosis while ignoring an underlying infection or subtherapeutic/withdrawn anticoagulation misses the driver — failure to remove the trigger lets the storm continue despite anticoagulation.
