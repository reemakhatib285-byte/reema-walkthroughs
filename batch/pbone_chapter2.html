⚠️ Board traps 13
  1. Osteoclast = macrophage lineage — WRONG answer: 'osteoclasts and osteoblasts share a common mesenchymal precursor.' Only osteoBLASTS come from mesenchyme; osteoCLASTS come from the macrophage/monocyte (hematopoietic) lineage. Discriminator: any cell that resorbs bone and is multinucleated is hematopoietic-derived. The other classic error is thinking PTH binds the osteoclast directly — it does not; calcitonin is the only hormone with a direct osteoclast receptor.
  2. Osteoclast (multinucleated) — WRONG answer: confusing the osteoCLAST (resorbs, multinucleated, macrophage origin) with the osteoBLAST (builds matrix, single nucleus, mesenchymal) or the osteoCYTE (mature osteoblast trapped in matrix that senses mechanical load and secretes sclerostin/FGF23). Discriminator: multinucleated + ruffled border + cathepsin K = osteoclast.
  3. Ruffled border / H+ pump — WRONG answer: carbonic anhydrase II deficiency causes a purely renal problem. In fact it causes OSTEOPETROSIS with renal tubular acidosis and cerebral calcifications — osteoclasts can't acidify the lacuna, so bone is dense but brittle (the 'marble bone' that paradoxically fractures). Discriminator: dense sclerotic bone + RTA + cerebral Ca = CA-II deficiency.
  4. Cathepsin K — WRONG answer: cathepsin K dissolves the mineral. It does NOT touch mineral — protons/acid dissolve hydroxyapatite first, and cathepsin K then cleaves the collagen. Discriminator: pycnodysostosis (cathepsin K) gives osteosclerosis WITHOUT the RTA seen in carbonic anhydrase II deficiency.
  5. RANKL — WRONG answer: estrogen stimulates osteoclasts. Estrogen actually SUPPRESSES RANKL and promotes osteoclast apoptosis; its withdrawal at menopause is why postmenopausal osteoporosis is resorption-driven. Discriminator: a high RANKL:OPG ratio = net bone loss; a low ratio = net preservation.
  6. OPG (decoy) — WRONG answer: OPG turns osteoclasts ON. It is a DECOY that sequesters RANKL and turns them OFF, shifting balance toward bone formation/preservation. Discriminator: OPG mops up RANKL (anti-resorptive); RANKL activates RANK (pro-resorptive).
  7. Denosumab (anti-RANKL) — WRONG answer: 'you can simply stop denosumab when therapy is done.' Abrupt discontinuation causes a REBOUND surge in osteoclast activity with multiple vertebral fractures — never interrupt without transitioning to a bisphosphonate. Discriminator vs bisphosphonates: denosumab is SAFE in renal failure and is NOT incorporated into bone (effect is fully reversible/non-cumulative), which is exactly why the rebound occurs.
  8. PTH (via OB) — WRONG answer: 'PTH is always bad for bone, so PTH analogs can't treat osteoporosis.' Intermittent teriparatide (PTH 1-34, 20 mcg SC daily) is a potent ANABOLIC agent that builds bone. Discriminator: continuous PTH = bone loss (osteitis fibrosa cystica); pulsatile/intermittent PTH = bone gain.
  9. 1,25(OH)2D (via OB) — WRONG answer: 'low vitamin D causes hypercalcemia from bone.' Vitamin D DEFICIENCY causes hypocalcemia -> secondary hyperparathyroidism and osteomalacia; it is EXCESS 1,25-D that drives resorption and hypercalcemia. Discriminator: deficiency = low Ca + high PTH + soft bones; excess = high Ca + low PTH.
  10. Calcitonin (direct) — WRONG answer: 'calcitonin works through the osteoblast like PTH and vitamin D.' Calcitonin is the ONLY hormone here acting DIRECTLY on the osteoclast (PTH, 1,25-D, estrogen all act via the osteoblast/RANKL). Discriminator: also note calcitonin is nearly dispensable — thyroidectomy (no calcitonin) does NOT cause hypercalcemia, so it is a minor player in human Ca homeostasis.
  11. Formation markers (P1NP/BSAP) — WRONG answer: P1NP and BSAP go DOWN on anabolic teriparatide. They RISE — they are formation signals confirming the osteoblast is building bone. Discriminator: formation markers up with anabolics; resorption markers (CTX) down with antiresorptives.
  12. Resorption markers (CTX/NTX) — WRONG answer: a rising CTX confirms a bisphosphonate is working. Effective antiresorptives DROP CTX/NTX; a CTX that fails to fall suggests non-adherence or malabsorption of an oral bisphosphonate. Discriminator: resorption markers (CTX/NTX/pyridinoline) track osteoclast/breakdown; formation markers (P1NP/BSAP/osteocalcin) track osteoblast/buildup.
  13. Bisphosphonate mechanism — WRONG answer: 'use a bisphosphonate in a patient with eGFR 25.' Bisphosphonates are renally cleared and CONTRAINDICATED at eGFR <30–35 — use denosumab instead. Other tested adverse effects: osteonecrosis of the jaw and atypical (subtrochanteric) femoral fractures with long-term use, plus an acute-phase flu-like reaction after IV zoledronate. Discriminator: renal failure -> denosumab, not bisphosphonate.
📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Osteoclast = macrophage lineage
Osteoclasts are bone-resorbing cells derived from the hematopoietic monocyte/macrophage lineage, NOT from mesenchymal osteoblasts. M-CSF drives precursor proliferation and survival, and RANKL (RANK ligand) drives their fusion into multinucleated giant cells and their activation. The unifying boards concept: nearly every pro-resorptive hormone (PTH, 1,25-D, estrogen withdrawal, glucocorticoids, thyroid hormone) acts INDIRECTLY by signaling the osteoblast/stromal cell to make RANKL — the osteoclast itself has no receptors for these.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'osteoclasts and osteoblasts share a common mesenchymal precursor.' Only osteoBLASTS come from mesenchyme; osteoCLASTS come from the macrophage/monocyte (hematopoietic) lineage. Discriminator: any cell that resorbs bone and is multinucleated is hematopoietic-derived. The other classic error is thinking PTH binds the osteoclast directly — it does not; calcitonin is the only hormone with a direct osteoclast receptor.
2. Osteoclast (multinucleated)
The osteoclast is a large multinucleated cell (often 5–20 nuclei) formed by fusion of macrophage precursors, and it is the ONLY cell that resorbs bone. It attaches to bone via the alpha-v-beta-3 integrin sealing zone, creating a sealed acidic 'Howship lacuna' beneath its ruffled border. Its activity is read out clinically by resorption markers (CTX/NTX).
⚠️ WRONG answer: confusing the osteoCLAST (resorbs, multinucleated, macrophage origin) with the osteoBLAST (builds matrix, single nucleus, mesenchymal) or the osteoCYTE (mature osteoblast trapped in matrix that senses mechanical load and secretes sclerostin/FGF23). Discriminator: multinucleated + ruffled border + cathepsin K = osteoclast.
3. Ruffled border / H+ pump
Once the osteoclast seals the resorption pit with its alpha-v-beta-3 integrin, the ruffled border pumps protons (via a vacuolar H+-ATPase) into the lacuna to dissolve the hydroxyapatite mineral. Carbonic anhydrase II generates those protons (CO2 + H2O -> H+ + HCO3-). Acidification first removes mineral; cathepsin K then digests the exposed collagen.
⚠️ WRONG answer: carbonic anhydrase II deficiency causes a purely renal problem. In fact it causes OSTEOPETROSIS with renal tubular acidosis and cerebral calcifications — osteoclasts can't acidify the lacuna, so bone is dense but brittle (the 'marble bone' that paradoxically fractures). Discriminator: dense sclerotic bone + RTA + cerebral Ca = CA-II deficiency.
4. Cathepsin K
Cathepsin K is the lysosomal cysteine protease that digests the type I collagen matrix AFTER acid has dissolved the mineral. It is the molecular target of odanacatib (a developmental antiresorptive). Loss-of-function cathepsin K mutations cause pycnodysostosis (dense brittle bones, short stature, acro-osteolysis — the disease attributed to the painter Toulouse-Lautrec).
⚠️ WRONG answer: cathepsin K dissolves the mineral. It does NOT touch mineral — protons/acid dissolve hydroxyapatite first, and cathepsin K then cleaves the collagen. Discriminator: pycnodysostosis (cathepsin K) gives osteosclerosis WITHOUT the RTA seen in carbonic anhydrase II deficiency.
5. RANKL
RANKL (receptor activator of NF-kB ligand) is expressed on osteoblasts/stromal cells; it binds RANK on osteoclast precursors to drive their fusion, activation, and survival — the master 'ON switch' for resorption. PTH, 1,25-D, glucocorticoids, and inflammatory cytokines all converge on raising the RANKL:OPG ratio. Estrogen RESTRAINS RANKL, so menopause/estrogen loss unleashes resorption.
⚠️ WRONG answer: estrogen stimulates osteoclasts. Estrogen actually SUPPRESSES RANKL and promotes osteoclast apoptosis; its withdrawal at menopause is why postmenopausal osteoporosis is resorption-driven. Discriminator: a high RANKL:OPG ratio = net bone loss; a low ratio = net preservation.
6. OPG (decoy)
Osteoprotegerin (OPG) is a soluble decoy receptor secreted by osteoblasts that binds and neutralizes RANKL before it can reach RANK — the natural brake on resorption. The RANKL:OPG ratio is the central determinant of bone balance: high ratio = resorption (postmenopausal loss, malignancy), low ratio = preservation. Denosumab is essentially a pharmacologic OPG.
⚠️ WRONG answer: OPG turns osteoclasts ON. It is a DECOY that sequesters RANKL and turns them OFF, shifting balance toward bone formation/preservation. Discriminator: OPG mops up RANKL (anti-resorptive); RANKL activates RANK (pro-resorptive).
7. Denosumab (anti-RANKL)
Denosumab is a fully human monoclonal antibody against RANKL (a pharmacologic OPG mimic), given 60 mg SC every 6 months for osteoporosis and 120 mg SC every 4 weeks for hypercalcemia of malignancy / bone metastases. Because it is cleared by the reticuloendothelial system (not the kidney), it is the antiresorptive of choice in CKD/eGFR <30 where bisphosphonates are contraindicated. Watch for hypocalcemia (replete Ca/vit D first), osteonecrosis of the jaw, and atypical femoral fractures.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'you can simply stop denosumab when therapy is done.' Abrupt discontinuation causes a REBOUND surge in osteoclast activity with multiple vertebral fractures — never interrupt without transitioning to a bisphosphonate. Discriminator vs bisphosphonates: denosumab is SAFE in renal failure and is NOT incorporated into bone (effect is fully reversible/non-cumulative), which is exactly why the rebound occurs.
8. PTH (via OB)
PTH acts on the osteoBLAST (PTH1R receptor) to raise RANKL and lower OPG, indirectly activating osteoclasts. The key boards twist is dose-pattern dependence: CONTINUOUS high PTH (primary hyperparathyroidism, continuous infusion) is net catabolic/resorptive, whereas INTERMITTENT once-daily PTH (teriparatide) is net ANABOLIC, favoring osteoblastic formation.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'PTH is always bad for bone, so PTH analogs can't treat osteoporosis.' Intermittent teriparatide (PTH 1-34, 20 mcg SC daily) is a potent ANABOLIC agent that builds bone. Discriminator: continuous PTH = bone loss (osteitis fibrosa cystica); pulsatile/intermittent PTH = bone gain.
9. 1,25(OH)2D (via OB)
Active vitamin D (1,25(OH)2D / calcitriol) also raises RANKL through the osteoblast, mobilizing calcium from bone when needed. Its dominant physiologic role, however, is increasing intestinal calcium and phosphate absorption; the bone resorptive effect appears mainly when dietary Ca is inadequate. At supraphysiologic levels (vitamin D toxicity, granulomatous disease) it drives hypercalcemia.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'low vitamin D causes hypercalcemia from bone.' Vitamin D DEFICIENCY causes hypocalcemia -> secondary hyperparathyroidism and osteomalacia; it is EXCESS 1,25-D that drives resorption and hypercalcemia. Discriminator: deficiency = low Ca + high PTH + soft bones; excess = high Ca + low PTH.
10. Calcitonin (direct)
Calcitonin (from thyroid parafollicular C-cells) is the lone exception: it binds a receptor ON the osteoclast itself and directly inhibits resorption, lowering serum calcium. Clinically it is weak and tachyphylactic, used short-term for acute hypercalcemia (4 IU/kg, works within hours but escapes in 48 h) and for acute painful vertebral fractures. Calcitonin's main exam role is as a tumor marker for medullary thyroid carcinoma.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'calcitonin works through the osteoblast like PTH and vitamin D.' Calcitonin is the ONLY hormone here acting DIRECTLY on the osteoclast (PTH, 1,25-D, estrogen all act via the osteoblast/RANKL). Discriminator: also note calcitonin is nearly dispensable — thyroidectomy (no calcitonin) does NOT cause hypercalcemia, so it is a minor player in human Ca homeostasis.
11. Formation markers (P1NP/BSAP)
Bone FORMATION markers reflect osteoblast activity: P1NP (procollagen type I N-terminal propeptide, cleaved off collagen as it is laid down), bone-specific alkaline phosphatase (BSAP), and osteocalcin. They RISE with anabolic therapy (teriparatide/abaloparatide) and with high-turnover states like Paget disease. Total ALP elevation prompts checking BSAP to confirm bony (vs hepatic) origin.
⚠️ WRONG answer: P1NP and BSAP go DOWN on anabolic teriparatide. They RISE — they are formation signals confirming the osteoblast is building bone. Discriminator: formation markers up with anabolics; resorption markers (CTX) down with antiresorptives.
12. Resorption markers (CTX/NTX)
Bone RESORPTION markers are collagen breakdown fragments released by osteoclasts: serum CTX (C-telopeptide), urine NTX (N-telopeptide), and pyridinoline/deoxypyridinoline crosslinks. CTX FALLS sharply with antiresorptive therapy (bisphosphonates, denosumab) and is used to confirm adherence/response and, in dentistry, to gauge ONJ risk before procedures.
⚠️ WRONG answer: a rising CTX confirms a bisphosphonate is working. Effective antiresorptives DROP CTX/NTX; a CTX that fails to fall suggests non-adherence or malabsorption of an oral bisphosphonate. Discriminator: resorption markers (CTX/NTX/pyridinoline) track osteoclast/breakdown; formation markers (P1NP/BSAP/osteocalcin) track osteoblast/buildup.
13. Bisphosphonate mechanism
Bisphosphonates (alendronate 70 mg weekly, risedronate, IV zoledronate 5 mg yearly) are pyrophosphate analogs that bind hydroxyapatite at sites of active resorption; when the osteoclast ingests them, nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates inhibit farnesyl pyrophosphate synthase (the mevalonate pathway), disrupting the cytoskeleton and triggering osteoclast apoptosis. First-line for postmenopausal osteoporosis, Paget disease, and hypercalcemia of malignancy; oral dosing requires taking on empty stomach with water and staying upright 30–60 min to avoid esophagitis.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'use a bisphosphonate in a patient with eGFR 25.' Bisphosphonates are renally cleared and CONTRAINDICATED at eGFR <30–35 — use denosumab instead. Other tested adverse effects: osteonecrosis of the jaw and atypical (subtrochanteric) femoral fractures with long-term use, plus an acute-phase flu-like reaction after IV zoledronate. Discriminator: renal failure -> denosumab, not bisphosphonate.
