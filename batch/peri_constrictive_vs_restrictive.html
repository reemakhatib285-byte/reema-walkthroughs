⚠️ Board traps 13
  1. Tamponade booth — WRONG to wait for a confirmatory CT or to give a large fluid bolus as definitive therapy. Tamponade is a clinical/echo diagnosis; the discriminator from constriction is that tamponade has an ABSENT y descent (only x), whereas constriction has a prominent y descent (rapid). Definitive treatment is pericardiocentesis/drainage, not imaging.
  2. Tamponade = small/fast — WRONG to assume a small effusion 'can't be tamponade' — acute small effusions tamponade most readily. The pressure-volume curve of the pericardium is the discriminator: acute = steep curve (small volume, big pressure); chronic = shifted right (large volume tolerated).
  3. Constrictive booth — WRONG to label this restrictive cardiomyopathy. The discriminator is respiratory VARIATION/interdependence: constriction = DISCORDANT ventricular pressures + respiratory variation in mitral/tricuspid inflow (>25% mitral E variation), thickened/calcified pericardium on imaging, and NORMAL BNP. Treatment is pericardiectomy, not heart-failure medical therapy.
  4. Pericardial calcification — WRONG to require calcification before diagnosing constriction. Calcification is specific but insensitive; a substantial minority of surgically proven constriction shows no calcification. The presence of calcification rules IN pericardial disease, but its absence does not rule it out.
  5. Pericardiectomy = only definitive — WRONG to offer pericardiectomy for restrictive cardiomyopathy — stripping a normal pericardium off a stiff myocardium gives no benefit. The discriminator before sending to surgery: confirm the pericardium (not the myocardium) is the constraining structure via CT/MRI thickness and discordant cath hemodynamics.
  6. Restrictive CM booth — Restrictive CM is NOT cured by pericardiectomy. WRONG to send these patients to surgery. Discriminators from constriction: HIGH BNP (vs normal in constriction), CONCORDANT ventricular pressures with respiration (vs discordant), tissue Doppler e' REDUCED/septal e' <8 (vs preserved or 'annulus paradoxus' increased in constriction), and a normal-thickness pericardium on MRI. Treat the underlying infiltrative disease.
  7. Myocardium vs pericardium — WRONG to use the square-root sign or prominent x/y descents to separate the two — BOTH share these. The actual discriminators are respiratory ventricular interdependence (discordant = constriction), BNP (normal = constriction, high = restrictive), tissue Doppler, and pericardial imaging.
  8. MRI differentiates — WRONG to rely on echo alone when constriction vs restriction is unclear — echo can be ambiguous. The discriminating tools are MRI/CT pericardial thickness, simultaneous LV/RV cath showing discordance (constriction) vs concordance (restriction), and biopsy for infiltrative myocardial disease.
  9. RV MI booth — WRONG to give nitrates or diuretics for the hypotension/elevated JVP — RV MI is preload-dependent, so these precipitate shock. The discriminator from pericardial constriction is the elevated troponin plus V4R ST elevation; treat with fluids and revascularization, not pericardiectomy.
  10. Effusive-constrictive booth — WRONG to assume drainage will normalize hemodynamics. The discriminator: persistently elevated RA/diastolic pressure after the effusion is removed signals effusive-constrictive disease, which may ultimately need visceral pericardiectomy rather than drainage alone.
  11. Equalization of pressures — WRONG to read equalized diastolic pressures as proof of constriction specifically — tamponade also equalizes pressures. Use the respiratory pattern (discordance) and the y-descent morphology to distinguish; and remember restrictive CM typically does NOT fully equalize (LV>RV by >5 mmHg).
  12. Pulsus paradoxus pattern — WRONG to exclude tamponade because pulsus is absent — it can be absent in low-pressure tamponade (hypovolemia), severe LV dysfunction, ASD, or aortic regurgitation. Conversely, pulsus also occurs in severe asthma/COPD and massive PE, so it is not specific to pericardial disease.
  13. Drains shift to constriction — WRONG to discharge the patient as 'tamponade resolved' once fluid is drained. The discriminator is the persistent elevation of RA pressure and emergence of a rapid y descent after drainage — that unmasked constriction drives ongoing symptoms and may require surgery.
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Differential table
Master differential of conditions causing right-heart failure physiology with clear lungs: tamponade, constrictive pericarditis, restrictive cardiomyopathy, RV MI, and effusive-constrictive disease. All five share elevated JVP and near-equalized diastolic pressures; the discriminators are the respiratory variation pattern, the JVP descents (x vs y), imaging (pericardial thickness/calcification), BNP, and biopsy. Board strategy: anchor on whether the problem is the PERICARDIUM (fixable surgically) or the MYOCARDIUM (not surgically fixable).
2. Tamponade booth
Beck's triad = hypotension + muffled heart sounds + distended neck veins (JVP). Pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg drop in systolic BP on inspiration is the key bedside sign; ECG shows low voltage and electrical alternans. Echo shows RA systolic collapse (sensitive) and RV early-diastolic collapse (specific) plus IVC plethora. JVP shows a blunted/absent y descent (X present, Y absent) because the chamber cannot fill in diastole against the surrounding fluid.
⚠️ WRONG to wait for a confirmatory CT or to give a large fluid bolus as definitive therapy. Tamponade is a clinical/echo diagnosis; the discriminator from constriction is that tamponade has an ABSENT y descent (only x), whereas constriction has a prominent y descent (rapid). Definitive treatment is pericardiocentesis/drainage, not imaging.
3. Tamponade = small/fast
Rate of accumulation matters more than absolute volume: a rapidly accumulating effusion of only 150–200 mL (e.g., trauma, dissection, post-procedure) can tamponade because the stiff pericardium cannot stretch acutely. Drain via subxiphoid pericardiocentesis (echo-guided) without delay; surgical window if recurrent or loculated.
⚠️ WRONG to assume a small effusion 'can't be tamponade' — acute small effusions tamponade most readily. The pressure-volume curve of the pericardium is the discriminator: acute = steep curve (small volume, big pressure); chronic = shifted right (large volume tolerated).
4. Constrictive booth
A rigid, scarred pericardium prevents diastolic filling. Hallmarks: Kussmaul sign (JVP fails to fall, or rises, with inspiration), pericardial knock (early high-pitched S3-like sound from abrupt cessation of filling), prominent rapid x AND y descents, and a 'dip-and-plateau'/square-root sign on ventricular pressure tracing. Catheterization shows DISCORDANT LV/RV systolic pressures with respiration (ventricular interdependence: inspiration → LV pressure falls, RV rises). BNP is normal or only mildly elevated. Common causes: prior viral/idiopathic pericarditis, cardiac surgery, mediastinal radiation, TB (worldwide).
⚠️ WRONG to label this restrictive cardiomyopathy. The discriminator is respiratory VARIATION/interdependence: constriction = DISCORDANT ventricular pressures + respiratory variation in mitral/tricuspid inflow (>25% mitral E variation), thickened/calcified pericardium on imaging, and NORMAL BNP. Treatment is pericardiectomy, not heart-failure medical therapy.
5. Pericardial calcification
Pericardial thickening >3–4 mm (best seen on cardiac CT/MRI) or eggshell calcification on lateral CXR strongly favors constriction over restrictive cardiomyopathy. Calcification is classic for chronic TB or remote viral pericarditis. Note: ~20% of constriction has a normal-thickness pericardium, so absence of thickening does not exclude it — use hemodynamics/MRI tagging if doubt persists.
⚠️ WRONG to require calcification before diagnosing constriction. Calcification is specific but insensitive; a substantial minority of surgically proven constriction shows no calcification. The presence of calcification rules IN pericardial disease, but its absence does not rule it out.
6. Pericardiectomy = only definitive
Complete pericardiectomy (surgical stripping of the rigid pericardium) is the only definitive cure for chronic constriction; diuretics give only temporary symptom relief. Operative mortality is significant (≈6–12%), worst with radiation-induced constriction (because the myocardium is often co-involved). Transient/early post-viral constriction may resolve with 2–3 months of anti-inflammatory therapy before committing to surgery.
⚠️ WRONG to offer pericardiectomy for restrictive cardiomyopathy — stripping a normal pericardium off a stiff myocardium gives no benefit. The discriminator before sending to surgery: confirm the pericardium (not the myocardium) is the constraining structure via CT/MRI thickness and discordant cath hemodynamics.
7. Restrictive CM booth
A stiff, infiltrated MYOCARDIUM impairs diastolic filling while the pericardium is normal. Causes: amyloidosis (most common; look for low-voltage ECG with pseudo-infarct Q waves but THICK walls on echo — the 'voltage-mass mismatch'), sarcoidosis, hemochromatosis, endomyocardial fibrosis, Loeffler endocarditis. BNP is HIGH (myocardium is diseased), filling pressures show CONCORDANT LV/RV changes with respiration, and septal motion lacks the constrictive 'bounce'. Endomyocardial biopsy confirms the infiltrate (e.g., Congo-red apple-green birefringence in amyloid).
⚠️ Restrictive CM is NOT cured by pericardiectomy. WRONG to send these patients to surgery. Discriminators from constriction: HIGH BNP (vs normal in constriction), CONCORDANT ventricular pressures with respiration (vs discordant), tissue Doppler e' REDUCED/septal e' <8 (vs preserved or 'annulus paradoxus' increased in constriction), and a normal-thickness pericardium on MRI. Treat the underlying infiltrative disease.
8. Myocardium vs pericardium
The single most important board distinction: constriction = diseased PERICARDIUM (surrounding shell is the problem; myocardium is intrinsically normal → surgically fixable). Restrictive CM = diseased MYOCARDIUM (the muscle itself is stiff/infiltrated → not surgically fixable). Mnemonic: 'Constriction Constrains from outside; Restriction is Rigid muscle inside.' Both produce a prominent y descent and a square-root sign, so those shared features cannot tell them apart — use BNP, respiratory concordance, and imaging.
⚠️ WRONG to use the square-root sign or prominent x/y descents to separate the two — BOTH share these. The actual discriminators are respiratory ventricular interdependence (discordant = constriction), BNP (normal = constriction, high = restrictive), tissue Doppler, and pericardial imaging.
9. MRI differentiates
Cardiac MRI/CT measures pericardial thickness (>3–4 mm favors constriction) and shows calcification; real-time cine MRI demonstrates the septal bounce and ventricular interdependence. MRI also characterizes myocardial infiltration (late gadolinium enhancement in amyloid/sarcoid). If imaging and echo remain equivocal, simultaneous LV/RV catheterization (looking for discordance) or endomyocardial biopsy settles it.
⚠️ WRONG to rely on echo alone when constriction vs restriction is unclear — echo can be ambiguous. The discriminating tools are MRI/CT pericardial thickness, simultaneous LV/RV cath showing discordance (constriction) vs concordance (restriction), and biopsy for infiltrative myocardial disease.
10. RV MI booth
RV infarction (usually with inferior STEMI from proximal RCA occlusion) mimics tamponade/constriction: elevated JVP, clear lungs, hypotension, Kussmaul sign, and a slow y descent. The discriminators are ELEVATED TROPONIN, ST elevation in right-sided lead V4R (most sensitive), and inferior ST elevation. Management is preload-DEPENDENT: give IV fluids, AVOID nitrates/diuretics/morphine (which drop preload and cause profound hypotension), and reperfuse.
⚠️ WRONG to give nitrates or diuretics for the hypotension/elevated JVP — RV MI is preload-dependent, so these precipitate shock. The discriminator from pericardial constriction is the elevated troponin plus V4R ST elevation; treat with fluids and revascularization, not pericardiectomy.
11. Effusive-constrictive booth
A hybrid: a pericardial effusion (tamponade-like) overlying a constricting visceral pericardium. The clue is that after pericardiocentesis removes the fluid and intrapericardial pressure falls to zero, the RIGHT ATRIAL pressure stays ELEVATED — unmasking the underlying constriction. Often TB or radiation/neoplastic etiology. Pulsus paradoxus is prominent before drainage.
⚠️ WRONG to assume drainage will normalize hemodynamics. The discriminator: persistently elevated RA/diastolic pressure after the effusion is removed signals effusive-constrictive disease, which may ultimately need visceral pericardiectomy rather than drainage alone.
12. Equalization of pressures
Near-equalization of end-diastolic pressures across all four chambers (RA ≈ RV ≈ PA diastolic ≈ PCWP, typically within 5 mmHg) is seen in tamponade and constriction because an external constraint dominates filling. In restrictive CM the LV diastolic pressure usually exceeds RV by >5 mmHg, helping separate it.
⚠️ WRONG to read equalized diastolic pressures as proof of constriction specifically — tamponade also equalizes pressures. Use the respiratory pattern (discordance) and the y-descent morphology to distinguish; and remember restrictive CM typically does NOT fully equalize (LV>RV by >5 mmHg).
13. Pulsus paradoxus pattern
Pulsus paradoxus = inspiratory fall in systolic BP >10 mmHg, from exaggerated ventricular interdependence within a fixed total cardiac volume. Most prominent in tamponade and effusive-constrictive disease; often absent or mild in pure (non-effusive) constriction, where Kussmaul sign predominates instead.
⚠️ WRONG to exclude tamponade because pulsus is absent — it can be absent in low-pressure tamponade (hypovolemia), severe LV dysfunction, ASD, or aortic regurgitation. Conversely, pulsus also occurs in severe asthma/COPD and massive PE, so it is not specific to pericardial disease.
14. Drains shift to constriction
In effusive-constrictive disease, draining the effusion converts the hemodynamic picture from a tamponade pattern to a pure constrictive pattern (RA pressure stays high, y descent becomes prominent). This is the diagnostic 'reveal' and identifies patients who may need pericardiectomy of the visceral layer.
⚠️ WRONG to discharge the patient as 'tamponade resolved' once fluid is drained. The discriminator is the persistent elevation of RA pressure and emergence of a rapid y descent after drainage — that unmasked constriction drives ongoing symptoms and may require surgery.
