⚠️ Board traps 12
  1. FeSO4 — first-line oral — WRONG: starting an expensive ferric salt or IV iron first-line for simple, uncomplicated IDA. Ferrous sulfate is the proven, low-cost standard; reserve newer ferric formulations and IV iron for intolerance, malabsorption, or failure of oral therapy.
  2. Take before meals — WRONG: routinely advising patients to take iron WITH meals or antacids to reduce nausea. Food and acid-suppressants markedly cut absorption; if GI upset is limiting, lower the dose or switch to alternate-day dosing rather than pairing it with food/PPIs that defeat the therapy.
  3. Alternate-day dosing — WRONG: thinking three-times-daily dosing absorbs more iron and works faster. The dose-induced hepcidin spike means TID is NOT superior — alternate-day/once-daily dosing actually absorbs more per milligram given and is better tolerated. More frequent ≠ more absorbed.
  4. Lower-elemental salts — WRONG: comparing tablets by total milligrams rather than ELEMENTAL iron. A 325 mg fumarate tablet (~106 mg elemental) is not equivalent to a 325 mg sulfate tablet (~65 mg elemental) — always dose by elemental iron, or you may under- or over-treat.
  5. 3 months to refill stores — WRONG: stopping iron as soon as the hemoglobin normalizes. Hb corrects before stores refill; discontinuing then leaves ferritin low and guarantees recurrence. Treat to a target ferritin, not just a normal Hb.
  6. Hepcidin/DMT1 absorption block — WRONG: assuming absorption is simply proportional to how often you dose. The hepcidin feedback caps short-interval absorption, so doubling frequency does not double uptake — it can lower fractional absorption and add side effects.
  7. Unabsorbed iron / GI side effects — WRONG: mistaking benign iron-induced black stool for melena (GI bleeding). Iron causes greenish-black, NON-tarry, guaiac-negative stool; true melena from upper GI bleed is sticky, tarry, and occult-blood positive. Don't trigger an unnecessary bleed workup — but also don't ignore real melena attributing it to iron.
  8. IV iron indications checklist — WRONG: defaulting to IV iron for simple uncomplicated IDA with a working gut. Oral iron is first-line; IV is costlier and carries infusion-reaction risk. Reserve IV for the specific indications above — choosing IV when oral would work is a board pitfall.
  9. IV safety / anaphylaxis — WRONG: avoiding IV iron entirely out of fear of anaphylaxis, or routinely giving a test dose for modern agents. Serious reactions are rare with current preparations; the test-dose requirement applied to old high-molecular-weight dextran, not to ferric carboxymaltose/iron sucrose.
  10. FCM — largest single dose — WRONG: forgetting hypophosphatemia as the characteristic FCM adverse effect. Repeated or high-dose FCM can cause symptomatic hypophosphatemia (bone pain, osteomalacia, weakness) — monitor phosphate, especially with recurrent infusions, rather than attributing symptoms elsewhere.
  11. 1-2 rule reticulocyte response — WRONG: declaring oral iron a failure after only a few days because Hb hasn't moved. Hb rises slowly (~1 g/dL per 2–3 weeks); the first thing to check is the reticulocyte response at ~1 week. Re-evaluating Hb too early causes premature, unnecessary switches to IV iron.
  12. No-response detective board — WRONG: assuming nonresponse automatically means the patient needs IV iron. First confirm adherence, exclude ongoing bleeding and malabsorption (check anti-tTG for celiac), and re-verify the diagnosis — switching a misdiagnosed thalassemia or ACD patient to IV iron just adds iron they don't need.
📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. FeSO4 — first-line oral
Ferrous SULFATE is first-line oral therapy: a 325 mg tablet provides ~65 mg ELEMENTAL iron (ferrous sulfate is ~20% elemental). It is the cheapest, most studied option; total daily target is ~100–200 mg elemental iron, increasingly given as a single daily or alternate-day dose.
⚠️ WRONG: starting an expensive ferric salt or IV iron first-line for simple, uncomplicated IDA. Ferrous sulfate is the proven, low-cost standard; reserve newer ferric formulations and IV iron for intolerance, malabsorption, or failure of oral therapy.
2. Take before meals
Take oral iron on an EMPTY stomach (1 hr before or 2 hr after meals), ideally with vitamin C / orange juice to enhance Fe3+→Fe2+ reduction and absorption. Separate it from calcium/dairy, antacids, PPIs, and tea/coffee, which all impair uptake.
⚠️ WRONG: routinely advising patients to take iron WITH meals or antacids to reduce nausea. Food and acid-suppressants markedly cut absorption; if GI upset is limiting, lower the dose or switch to alternate-day dosing rather than pairing it with food/PPIs that defeat the therapy.
3. Alternate-day dosing
Newer evidence: ONCE-daily or ALTERNATE-day dosing achieves greater fractional absorption than divided multiple-daily doses, because each dose triggers a transient hepcidin rise that blunts absorption of subsequent doses for ~24 hr. Spacing doses also reduces GI side effects and improves adherence.
⚠️ WRONG: thinking three-times-daily dosing absorbs more iron and works faster. The dose-induced hepcidin spike means TID is NOT superior — alternate-day/once-daily dosing actually absorbs more per milligram given and is better tolerated. More frequent ≠ more absorbed.
4. Lower-elemental salts
Alternative oral salts differ in elemental content and tolerability: ferrous GLUCONATE (~27–36 mg elemental/tab, ~12%), ferrous FUMARATE (~33% elemental, ~106 mg/325 mg tab), plus sucrosomial iron, ferric maltol, and ferric citrate (also lowers phosphate in CKD). Lower elemental dose can mean fewer GI symptoms.
⚠️ WRONG: comparing tablets by total milligrams rather than ELEMENTAL iron. A 325 mg fumarate tablet (~106 mg elemental) is not equivalent to a 325 mg sulfate tablet (~65 mg elemental) — always dose by elemental iron, or you may under- or over-treat.
5. 3 months to refill stores
Continue oral iron for AT LEAST 3 months AFTER the hemoglobin normalizes to rebuild ferritin/storage iron — total course is often 4–6 months. Stopping when Hb corrects leaves stores empty and the anemia relapses. Recheck ferritin/CBC to confirm repletion.
⚠️ WRONG: stopping iron as soon as the hemoglobin normalizes. Hb corrects before stores refill; discontinuing then leaves ferritin low and guarantees recurrence. Treat to a target ferritin, not just a normal Hb.
6. Hepcidin/DMT1 absorption block
Each oral iron dose transiently raises serum hepcidin, which degrades enterocyte ferroportin and blunts absorption of the next dose for roughly 24 hours. This pharmacology is exactly why ALTERNATE-day/once-daily dosing maximizes net absorption per dose taken.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming absorption is simply proportional to how often you dose. The hepcidin feedback caps short-interval absorption, so doubling frequency does not double uptake — it can lower fractional absorption and add side effects.
7. Unabsorbed iron / GI side effects
Unabsorbed luminal iron causes the classic GI side effects — constipation, nausea, epigastric pain, metallic taste, and harmless dark/black stools — which are the leading reason for nonadherence. Manage by dose reduction, alternate-day dosing, or switching salts; add a stool softener for constipation.
⚠️ WRONG: mistaking benign iron-induced black stool for melena (GI bleeding). Iron causes greenish-black, NON-tarry, guaiac-negative stool; true melena from upper GI bleed is sticky, tarry, and occult-blood positive. Don't trigger an unnecessary bleed workup — but also don't ignore real melena attributing it to iron.
8. IV iron indications checklist
Use IV iron when: oral iron fails or isn't tolerated; MALABSORPTION (celiac, gastric bypass, IBD); ongoing/severe blood loss outpacing oral repletion; chronic kidney disease (especially on dialysis/ESAs); heart failure (functional iron deficiency); 2nd/3rd-trimester pregnancy; and pre-operative correction when surgery is imminent.
⚠️ WRONG: defaulting to IV iron for simple uncomplicated IDA with a working gut. Oral iron is first-line; IV is costlier and carries infusion-reaction risk. Reserve IV for the specific indications above — choosing IV when oral would work is a board pitfall.
9. IV safety / anaphylaxis
Modern IV iron formulations (ferric carboxymaltose, iron sucrose, ferric derisomaltose, ferumoxytol) are SAFE with low reaction rates; have anaphylaxis precautions available. The dangerous high-molecular-weight iron DEXTRAN (which required a test dose) is largely obsolete. Mild self-limited 'Fishbane' flushing reactions don't require stopping permanently.
⚠️ WRONG: avoiding IV iron entirely out of fear of anaphylaxis, or routinely giving a test dose for modern agents. Serious reactions are rare with current preparations; the test-dose requirement applied to old high-molecular-weight dextran, not to ferric carboxymaltose/iron sucrose.
10. FCM — largest single dose
Ferric carboxymaltose (FCM) and ferric derisomaltose allow large single-visit doses (up to ~1000+ mg), correcting deficits in 1–2 visits. A notable FCM side effect is transient, usually asymptomatic HYPOPHOSPHATEMIA (from increased FGF23); check phosphate with repeated dosing.
⚠️ WRONG: forgetting hypophosphatemia as the characteristic FCM adverse effect. Repeated or high-dose FCM can cause symptomatic hypophosphatemia (bone pain, osteomalacia, weakness) — monitor phosphate, especially with recurrent infusions, rather than attributing symptoms elsewhere.
11. 1-2 rule reticulocyte response
Monitoring of effective iron therapy: RETICULOCYTOSIS appears at ~5–7 days (~1 week), and HEMOGLOBIN rises ~1 g/dL every 2–3 weeks, with full correction over ~6–8 weeks. A reticulocyte bump confirms the marrow is responding and the patient is adherent.
⚠️ WRONG: declaring oral iron a failure after only a few days because Hb hasn't moved. Hb rises slowly (~1 g/dL per 2–3 weeks); the first thing to check is the reticulocyte response at ~1 week. Re-evaluating Hb too early causes premature, unnecessary switches to IV iron.
12. No-response detective board
When iron therapy fails, work the differential: (1) NONADHERENCE (most common — GI side effects), (2) ongoing blood loss exceeding intake, (3) MALABSORPTION (undiagnosed celiac, gastric bypass, PPI, H. pylori), and (4) WRONG DIAGNOSIS (thalassemia, ACD, sideroblastic — these don't respond to iron).
⚠️ WRONG: assuming nonresponse automatically means the patient needs IV iron. First confirm adherence, exclude ongoing bleeding and malabsorption (check anti-tTG for celiac), and re-verify the diagnosis — switching a misdiagnosed thalassemia or ACD patient to IV iron just adds iron they don't need.
