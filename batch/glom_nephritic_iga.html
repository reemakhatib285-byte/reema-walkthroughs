📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Berger's Café
IgA nephropathy (Berger's disease) is the most common primary glomerulonephritis worldwide, with highest prevalence in East Asians and whites and notably lower rates in those of African descent. It typically presents in the second-to-third decade, more often in males. Mnemonic: 'IgA = I Got Asia,' and 'Berger = the burger you eat right when you get a cold' (synpharyngitic).
⚠️ PSGN is the most common GN worldwide — IgA nephropathy holds that title; PSGN is instead the classic post-infectious GN of children weeks after strep
2. Poorly galactosylated IgA1
The core defect is overproduction of galactose-deficient IgA1, which the immune system recognizes as an autoantigen, generating anti-glycan IgG/IgA antibodies that form circulating immune complexes. These complexes deposit in the mesangium and trigger inflammation. This 'multi-hit' model (abnormal IgA1 → autoantibody → immune complex → mesangial deposition → injury) is the high-yield mechanism.
3. Mesangial IgA deposits
The diagnostic hallmark on immunofluorescence is dominant/co-dominant IgA deposition in the MESANGIUM (often with C3 and IgG), and EM shows mesangial electron-dense deposits. Light microscopy reveals mesangial hypercellularity. Diagnosis ultimately requires biopsy because serum IgA levels are unreliable (elevated in only ~50%). Mesangial IgA is the single best discriminator.
⚠️ Subepithelial 'humps' on EM — those are PSGN; IgA nephropathy deposits are MESANGIAL, not subepithelial
4. Immune complex / alt + lectin pathway
The mesangial immune complexes activate complement via the ALTERNATIVE and LECTIN pathways (not the classical pathway), yet serum complement remains NORMAL because consumption is localized rather than systemic. This is why IgA is a NORMAL-complement nephritic disease, unlike PSGN, lupus, MPGN, and endocarditis. Normal complement + IgA on biopsy is the combination to remember.
⚠️ Serum C3 is low in IgA nephropathy — it is NORMAL; low complement instead points to PSGN, lupus, MPGN, or endocarditis
5. Mesangial hypercellularity
Light microscopy shows mesangial proliferation/hypercellularity. The Oxford MEST-C classification (Mesangial hypercellularity, Endocapillary hypercellularity, Segmental sclerosis, Tubular atrophy/interstitial fibrosis, Crescents) scores biopsy features and predicts prognosis and treatment response. Tubular atrophy/fibrosis (T) and crescents (C) are the strongest predictors of progression.
6. Synpharyngitic hematuria
The classic presentation is gross (cola-colored) hematuria appearing WITHIN 1–2 DAYS of a mucosal infection — 'synpharyngitic,' meaning concurrent with the pharyngitis. This tight temporal link is the key timing discriminator from PSGN, which lags the infection by weeks. A young adult with recurrent visible hematuria during URIs is the prototypical vignette.
⚠️ Hematuria appearing 2–3 weeks AFTER a sore throat — that delayed pattern is PSGN; IgA hematuria is synpharyngitic (1–2 days, concurrent)
7. URI appetizer
A mucosal infection — upper respiratory or gastrointestinal — provokes the IgA response and triggers the flare, because IgA is the predominant mucosal immunoglobulin. This mucosal trigger explains the synpharyngitic timing and the recurrent, relapsing-with-infections course. Tonsillitis and GI infections are common precipitants.
8. Asymptomatic microhematuria
Beyond the dramatic gross-hematuria episodes, the most common presentation overall is incidentally discovered persistent microscopic hematuria with or without mild (sub-nephrotic) proteinuria and preserved renal function. Many such patients are found on routine urinalysis. Dysmorphic RBCs and RBC casts indicate the glomerular (nephritic) origin.
9. RPGN only (rare crescents)
A crescentic, rapidly progressive course is RARE in IgA nephropathy; only this severe subset warrants aggressive immunosuppression (high-dose corticosteroids ± cyclophosphamide, occasionally plasmapheresis). Routine IgA does NOT get cytotoxic therapy. Reserve immunosuppression for crescentic RPGN on biopsy or rapidly declining GFR despite supportive care.
⚠️ All IgA patients need cyclophosphamide — most do not; cytotoxic immunosuppression is reserved for the rare crescentic/RPGN subset
10. Treatment shelf — ACEi/ARB + fish oil
First-line therapy for the typical patient is supportive: an ACE inhibitor or ARB to control blood pressure and reduce proteinuria (target proteinuria below ~1 g/day), with fish oil as an adjunct. Corticosteroids are considered when proteinuria persists above ~1 g/day despite optimized RAAS blockade. SGLT2 inhibitors are increasingly used to slow CKD progression. Escalate to immunosuppression only for high-risk/progressive disease.
11. IgA vasculitis (HSP) link
Henoch-Schönlein purpura, now called IgA vasculitis, is the SYSTEMIC form of the same disease — identical IgA-dominant immune deposits but with extrarenal involvement: palpable purpura on the lower extremities/buttocks, arthralgias, abdominal pain (GI bleeding/intussusception), and nephritis. It is the most common childhood vasculitis and usually self-limited. Same renal histology as Berger's, broader clinical syndrome.
12. ESKD long-term
IgA nephropathy is NOT uniformly benign: roughly 20–40% of patients progress to end-stage kidney disease over 10–20 years. Poor prognostic markers include hypertension, persistent proteinuria above ~1 g/day, reduced GFR at diagnosis, and adverse MEST-C features (T and C). It can also recur in a transplanted kidney, though graft loss from recurrence is uncommon.
