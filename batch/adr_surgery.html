📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Surgery = only cure
Adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC) is a rare, aggressive tumor with a bimodal age distribution (young children and adults 40–50). Complete surgical RESECTION with negative (R0) margins is the ONLY potentially curative therapy; unresectable or metastatic disease has a poor prognosis. Functional tumors most often hypersecrete cortisol (rapid-onset Cushing) ± androgens (virilization).
⚠️ A large (>4–6 cm), heterogeneous adrenal mass with high unenhanced CT density (>10 HU) and slow washout = suspicious for ACC, not a benign adenoma — go to surgery, don't just observe.
2. Open adrenalectomy for ACC
Suspected ACC is removed by OPEN adrenalectomy performed by an experienced surgeon, with en-bloc resection of the tumor and any locally invaded structures. The open approach protects the capsule from rupture and prevents peritoneal tumor seeding that would convert a curable tumor into incurable carcinomatosis.
⚠️ Even for a large mass, the open approach is chosen specifically to keep the capsule intact — capsule violation worsens prognosis.
3. Laparoscopic = X for ACC
Laparoscopic adrenalectomy is appropriate for small benign tumors but is CONTRAINDICATED when ACC is suspected because of the high risk of capsule rupture, tumor spillage and port-site/peritoneal recurrence.
⚠️ Choosing laparoscopic resection for a suspected ACC is the classic wrong answer — it risks spillage and recurrence; do an open procedure.
4. En-bloc + negative margins
Cure depends on a complete EN-BLOC R0 resection with NEGATIVE margins, including resection of locally invaded adjacent organs and regional lymph node dissection. Incomplete (R1/R2) resection or capsular rupture markedly raises recurrence risk and is an indication for adjuvant mitotane.
⚠️ Positive margins or intraoperative tumor spillage convert a 'curative' operation into high-risk disease requiring adjuvant therapy.
5. Post-op steroid replacement
A cortisol-secreting ACC chronically suppresses ACTH and the contralateral adrenal; after resection the patient is acutely adrenally INSUFFICIENT and needs glucocorticoid replacement (with stress-dosing perioperatively), tapered slowly over months as the HPA axis recovers.
⚠️ Post-resection fatigue, hypotension, nausea and hypoglycemia = expected adrenal insufficiency from a previously cortisol-secreting tumor — give steroids, don't miss it.
6. Mitotane potion
Mitotane (an o,p'-DDD/insecticide derivative) is an ADRENOLYTIC drug that is selectively toxic to adrenocortical cells and inhibits steroidogenesis; it is the mainstay of medical and adjuvant therapy for ACC. Because it destroys cortical cells, it itself CAUSES adrenal insufficiency that must be covered with glucocorticoid replacement.
⚠️ Mitotane creates iatrogenic adrenal insufficiency — every patient on it needs concurrent glucocorticoid replacement.
7. Mitotane therapeutic level
Mitotane has a NARROW therapeutic window requiring drug-level monitoring: target 14–20 mg/L. Below 14 mg/L it is ineffective; above 20 mg/L it causes dose-limiting NEUROTOXICITY (ataxia, confusion, lethargy). GI upset is the most common early side effect.
⚠️ Both under- and over-dosing are tested: <14 = no antitumor effect, >20 = neurotoxic — dose to the level, not by symptoms alone.
8. CYP3A4 induction → double hydrocortisone
Mitotane is a potent CYP3A4 INDUCER and raises cortisol-binding globulin, so it accelerates hydrocortisone metabolism on top of causing adrenal insufficiency — patients typically need roughly DOUBLE the usual hydrocortisone replacement dose. The CYP3A4 induction also lowers levels of many co-administered drugs (e.g., warfarin, some chemo).
⚠️ A standard hydrocortisone dose is insufficient on mitotane — failure to double it (and account for CYP3A4 drug interactions) leads to under-replacement and crisis.
9. Mitotane indications
Adjuvant/therapeutic mitotane is reserved for HIGH-RISK or advanced ACC: stage III/IV disease, high proliferation (Ki-67 >10%), incomplete (R1/R2) resection or capsular rupture, and recurrent disease. Low-risk completely resected tumors may just be surveilled.
⚠️ Risk stratification (Ki-67, margins, stage) decides who gets adjuvant mitotane — not every resected ACC needs it.
10. EDP-M metastatic chemo
For advanced/metastatic ACC, first-line systemic therapy is the EDP-M regimen: Etoposide + Doxorubicin + cisPlatin combined with Mitotane (the FIRM-ACT trial standard). Lung is the most common site of metastasis.
⚠️ EDP-M is the go-to combination for metastatic ACC — mitotane is continued alongside the cytotoxic backbone.
11. Surveillance every 3-6 months
ACC recurs early and often, so surveillance is intensive: cross-sectional imaging (and any tumor markers/hormones the tumor secreted) every 3–6 months for at least the first 2 years, then less frequently up to ~5–10 years. Most recurrences appear within the first 2 years.
⚠️ Because recurrence is early, surveillance intervals are short (3–6 mo) initially — don't space them out too soon.
12. Clinical pearl
One-line synthesis: OPEN en-bloc R0 adrenalectomy is the only cure for ACC; mitotane (target 14–20 mg/L) is the adrenolytic adjuvant/medical therapy and EDP-M is added for metastatic disease; mitotane's CYP3A4 induction + adrenolysis means DOUBLE the hydrocortisone; post-op or on-mitotane fatigue/hypotension = adrenal insufficiency; surveil every 3–6 months.
⚠️ The high-yield triad: don't go laparoscopic for ACC, don't under-replace steroids on mitotane, and don't wait to treat the resulting adrenal insufficiency.
