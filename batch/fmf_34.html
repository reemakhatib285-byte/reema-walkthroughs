📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Mediterranean flags
FMF is the most common monogenic autoinflammatory (hereditary periodic fever) disease and clusters in eastern Mediterranean ancestries — Sephardic/North African and Iraqi Jews, Armenians, Turks, and Arabs — with carrier frequencies up to 1 in 5 in some groups. It is highly relevant in Israel and is a favorite board setup: a young patient of Mediterranean descent with recurrent self-limited fevers. Onset is usually in childhood/adolescence, and >90% present before age 20.
⚠️ A board stem describing recurrent fevers in a Northern European / Scandinavian child is steering you AWAY from FMF — pick TRAPS (which is autosomal dominant and common in those populations) or another periodic fever. Ancestry is a deliberate discriminator on the exam.
2. MEFV / pyrin pot
The MEFV gene (chromosome 16) encodes pyrin (marenostrin), expressed in neutrophils/monocytes. Loss-of-function pyrin mutations (M694V is the most common and most severe, associated with higher amyloidosis risk) remove pyrin's normal brake on the inflammasome, leading to unregulated assembly and uncontrolled IL-1β. Most pathogenic mutations sit in exon 10.
⚠️ The defect is in MEFV/pyrin, NOT in colchicine metabolism, NOT in NLRP3 (that is the cryopyrinopathies FCAS/MWS/NOMID), and NOT in mevalonate kinase (that is HIDS). Genetic testing confirms but is not required: FMF is a clinical diagnosis, and a negative MEFV panel does not exclude it.
3. Autosomal recessive
Classic FMF is autosomal recessive (two MEFV mutations), with very high carrier frequencies (~1:5 to 1:10) in at-risk groups so an affected sibling without affected parents is typical. A minority of single-mutation carriers can show milder/variable phenotypes, but boards expect recessive inheritance.
⚠️ Don't call it autosomal dominant — that is the trap meant to confuse it with TRAPS (TNFRSF1A, dominant). Likewise X-linked points to VEXAS, not FMF.
4. Caspase-1 / IL-1β
Mutant pyrin permits unchecked inflammasome activation → caspase-1 (the cysteine protease) cleaves pro-IL-1β into active IL-1β, driving neutrophilic serosal inflammation and the febrile attacks. This IL-1 dependence is exactly why IL-1 inhibitors (anakinra, canakinumab) work in colchicine-resistant disease.
⚠️ The dominant cytokine is IL-1β, NOT TNF-α. A stem suggesting a TNF inhibitor (etanercept/infliximab) as the rational mechanism is wrong for FMF — that targeting belongs to TRAPS/DADA2, not FMF.
5. Recurrent short fever
Attacks are recurrent, stereotyped, self-limited febrile episodes lasting 1–3 days (12–72h) that resolve spontaneously, separated by symptom-free intervals of weeks to months. During attacks inflammatory markers spike (ESR, CRP, leukocytosis, SAA) and then normalize. Triggers include stress, exertion, menses, and cold.
⚠️ Fever persisting >7 days argues AGAINST FMF and toward TRAPS (attacks last >1 week, often weeks). HIDS attacks run 3–7 days. If you anchor on duration, you can sort the periodic fevers correctly — FMF is the shortest of the classic three.
6. Peritonitis (acute abdomen)
Sterile peritonitis is the most common serositis (~90% of patients) — severe diffuse abdominal pain with guarding, rebound, and even free fluid that mimics a surgical acute abdomen, so patients classically undergo unnecessary appendectomies/laparotomies. Pain is self-limited and resolves within 1–3 days; recurrent attacks can leave peritoneal adhesions.
⚠️ The WRONG move is to take the patient to the OR for appendicitis. The discriminator: recurrent, stereotyped, self-resolving episodes with prior identical attacks and Mediterranean ancestry point to FMF serositis, not a surgical abdomen — diagnostic colchicine response supports it.
7. Pleuritis
Pleuritis produces acute unilateral pleuritic chest pain from serosal inflammation, sometimes with a small exudative effusion and decreased breath sounds, resolving in 1–3 days. Pericarditis can occur but is much less common.
⚠️ Unilateral pleuritic chest pain in a young Mediterranean patient with recurrent self-limited attacks is FMF serositis — NOT pulmonary embolism (no Wells-criteria risk, no hypoxia/tachycardia pattern) and NOT pneumonia (no infiltrate/sputum, sterile and self-limited).
8. Erysipeloid rash
Erysipeloid erythema — a sharply demarcated, hot, tender red plaque over the ankle, dorsum of the foot, or shin — is the pathognomonic FMF skin sign during attacks and clears with the attack in days. It reflects the underlying serosal/neutrophilic inflammation rather than infection.
⚠️ The WRONG answer is bacterial cellulitis/erysipelas treated with antibiotics. Discriminator: the FMF lesion is sterile, recurs in the same distribution, accompanies fever and serositis, and self-resolves in 1–3 days — antibiotics do nothing and cultures are negative.
9. Monoarthritis
Acute self-limited monoarthritis of a large weight-bearing joint (knee, ankle, hip) is the typical articular attack — markedly inflamed, sterile synovial fluid, resolving over days to a couple of weeks without joint destruction. A protracted (chronic) arthritis variant exists but is uncommon.
⚠️ FMF arthritis is monoarticular, non-erosive, and self-limited — do NOT mistake it for rheumatoid arthritis (chronic symmetric small-joint polyarthritis with erosions/RF/anti-CCP) or septic arthritis (positive cultures, single episode that does not spontaneously remit and recur).
10. Elevated SAA
Serum amyloid A (SAA) is the acute-phase precursor protein driven up by chronic IL-1-mediated inflammation. Persistently elevated SAA — even subclinically between attacks — is what fuels misfolded AA fibril deposition; the goal of therapy is to keep SAA/CRP normalized, not merely to abort attacks.
⚠️ SAA is the AA-amyloid precursor. The danger is sustained SAA elevation BETWEEN attacks (ongoing subclinical inflammation), not just the peak during an attack — which is why colchicine is taken continuously and dosed to normalize markers.
11. AA amyloidosis (renal)
AA (secondary) amyloidosis is the most feared complication: SAA-derived fibrils deposit in the kidney first, causing proteinuria → nephrotic syndrome → progressive renal failure/ESRD, and can also involve gut, liver, and spleen. Biopsy shows Congo-red-positive, apple-green birefringent deposits. M694V homozygosity and untreated disease raise the risk.
⚠️ The feared complication is AA amyloidosis, NOT AL (light-chain) amyloidosis — AL is the plasma-cell/myeloma type and is the wrong choice here. New proteinuria, especially persisting between attacks, is the red-flag screen for amyloid renal involvement and an indication to confirm and escalate therapy.
12. Colchicine — daily lifelong
Colchicine is first-line, taken DAILY and lifelong (typical adult dose ~1.2–1.8 mg/day, titrated; pediatric dosing weight-based). It inhibits microtubule polymerization and neutrophil chemotaxis, reducing both attack frequency AND amyloidosis risk. It is continued in pregnancy and lactation. GI upset is the main side effect; reduce dose in renal/hepatic impairment and beware interaction with CYP3A4/P-gp inhibitors (clarithromycin, statins, cyclosporine) which raises toxicity risk.
⚠️ Colchicine is PROPHYLACTIC and continuous — it is NOT taken only during an attack (not PRN) and does not reliably abort an attack in progress. Stopping it because the patient feels well is the wrong move; lifelong adherence is what prevents amyloidosis.
13. Colchicine prevents amyloid
Beyond symptom control, colchicine's key disease-modifying benefit is preventing AA amyloidosis and preserving renal function by suppressing SAA. Adherent patients have dramatically lower rates of amyloid/ESRD, which is why it is given even to patients with few or mild attacks.
⚠️ Even when attacks are infrequent or mild, lifelong colchicine is still indicated — to prevent amyloidosis. Withholding it in 'mild' disease is a board trap; the renal protection, not just symptom relief, drives the recommendation.
14. IL-1 inhibitors 2nd line
For colchicine-resistant (~5–10% of patients) or colchicine-intolerant FMF, IL-1 inhibitors are second-line: anakinra (daily subcutaneous IL-1 receptor antagonist) or canakinumab (monthly anti-IL-1β monoclonal). They reduce attacks and SAA in refractory disease; colchicine is generally continued alongside them.
⚠️ IL-1 blockers are SECOND-line — colchicine stays first-line and remains the amyloid-preventer. Reaching for a biologic before an adequate colchicine trial (or stopping colchicine once a biologic is started) is the wrong sequence on the exam.
