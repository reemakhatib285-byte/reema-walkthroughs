📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Nitrite strip (specific)
Positive nitrites are highly SPECIFIC for Enterobacteriaceae (gram-negatives like E. coli) that reduce dietary nitrate to nitrite via nitrate reductase. Leukocyte esterase is the SENSITIVE partner marking pyuria (WBCs). A NEGATIVE nitrite does NOT exclude UTI: Staph saprophyticus, Enterococcus, and Pseudomonas lack nitrate reductase. Nitrite also needs ~4 h bladder dwell time, so frequent voiding gives false negatives.
2. Dilute = false negative
Very dilute urine (high fluid intake, recent diuretic) drops bacteria, nitrites, and esterase below the dipstick threshold, producing false negatives. A first-morning concentrated specimen is most sensitive. Classic board distractor: a symptomatic patient with a 'negative' dipstick on dilute urine.
3. E. coli king (#1 cause)
Uropathogenic E. coli is the #1 cause of UTI overall — ~80% of uncomplicated cystitis and pyelonephritis. Virulence comes from P-fimbriae/type-1 pili binding uroepithelial receptors to drive ascending infection. Empiric therapy is built around expected E. coli susceptibility, so rising fluoroquinolone and TMP-SMX resistance reshapes first-line choices.
4. Red-X contaminants
The red-X trio flags non-classic urinary isolates that are usually contaminants or not the primary answer in uncomplicated UTI. Skin/genital flora and viral findings should not anchor the diagnosis when E. coli or Staph saprophyticus fit the clinical picture better. Always interpret a urine isolate against symptoms and proper collection.
⚠️ "spiky virus / barrel contaminant" -> wrong; a single coag-negative or environmental isolate in an asymptomatic or improperly collected sample is contamination, not the cause — don't treat it.
5. Staph saprophyticus (silver)
Staphylococcus saprophyticus is the 2nd most common cause of acute cystitis in young, sexually active women ('honeymoon cystitis'). It is coagulase-NEGATIVE, novobiocin-RESISTANT (vs novobiocin-sensitive S. epidermidis), urease-positive, and nitrite-negative, so it can be missed on dipstick.
⚠️ "contaminant coag-neg Staph" -> wrong; in a young woman with cystitis symptoms S. saprophyticus is a true pathogen. Novobiocin RESISTANCE is the buzzword separating it from S. epidermidis.
6. Sterile flag (sterile pyuria)
Pyuria = WBCs in urine (>=10 WBC/hpf or positive leukocyte esterase), signaling inflammation but nonspecific. STERILE pyuria (pyuria with a NEGATIVE standard culture) is a classic board flag pointing AWAY from routine UTI: think Chlamydia/gonorrhea urethritis, renal TB, interstitial nephritis, nephrolithiasis, or recently treated UTI.
7. TREAT green arch (pregnancy/pre-procedure)
Asymptomatic bacteriuria is treated ONLY in (1) pregnancy and (2) before an invasive urologic procedure with anticipated mucosal trauma/bleeding (e.g., TURP). Mnemonic: 'Pregnant women and pre-procedure — everyone else, leave it alone.' In pregnancy, screen at the first prenatal visit (~12-16 wks) and treat positives 4-7 days with a test-of-cure, since untreated cases progress to pyelonephritis in ~20-30% and raise preterm/low-birth-weight risk.
8. NO TREAT red-X arch (elderly/catheter)
Do NOT treat asymptomatic bacteriuria in the elderly, diabetics, spinal-cord-injury, or chronically catheterized patients — even with pyuria. Antibiotics don't help and breed resistance. In a confused elderly patient with a positive urine culture, hunt for another cause before blaming the urine.
⚠️ "treat the positive urine culture in confused elder" -> wrong; 'treating the culture' instead of finding the real cause is the classic distractor — asymptomatic bacteriuria here is left alone.
9. Castle = upper-tract / complicated
Anatomy drives severity: lower tract (cystitis) = local dysuria/frequency/urgency without fever; upper tract (pyelonephritis, the castle) adds fever, rigors, flank/CVA tenderness, and vomiting. Fever and flank pain are the clinical hinge that mandate culture, longer therapy, and tissue-penetrating agents (so NOT nitrofurantoin or fosfomycin).
10. FEVER knight + burning kidney
Pyelonephritis = systemic infection of the renal parenchyma: fever, rigors, flank/CVA tenderness, nausea/vomiting. Culture is mandatory and therapy is longer with tissue-penetrating drugs. Failure to improve within 48-72 h should trigger imaging to exclude obstruction or abscess.
11. STONE / obstruction
An obstructing stone turns pyelonephritis into an emergency: infected hydronephrosis (pyonephrosis) needs urgent decompression (ureteral stent or percutaneous nephrostomy) plus antibiotics, because antibiotics alone can't sterilize obstructed urine. Obstruction also reclassifies any UTI as complicated.
12. GAS man = emphysematous pyelo
Emphysematous pyelonephritis is a necrotizing, gas-forming renal infection almost always in DIABETICS (gas from glucose fermentation by E. coli or Klebsiella). CT showing gas within the parenchyma/collecting system is diagnostic. It is a urologic emergency: IV antibiotics, glycemic control, drainage, and nephrectomy if no response.
⚠️ "keep giving oral antibiotics" -> wrong; in a diabetic with severe pyelonephritis not improving, get an urgent CT for gas/abscess — high-mortality emphysematous pyelonephritis often needs drainage or nephrectomy.
