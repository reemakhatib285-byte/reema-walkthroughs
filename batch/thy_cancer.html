📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. PTC most common
Papillary thyroid carcinoma is the most common thyroid malignancy (~80–85%) and a differentiated (DTC) cancer with an excellent prognosis (>95% 10-yr survival). Risk factors: childhood/neck radiation and the BRAF V600E mutation. It concentrates iodine, so it responds to TSH suppression and radioiodine.
⚠️ Assuming the most common thyroid cancer carries a poor prognosis — wrong; PTC is the most common AND the most curable.
2. PTC nuclear features
PTC is a nuclear (cytologic) diagnosis: Orphan-Annie-eye nuclei (optically clear with intranuclear pseudoinclusions), nuclear grooves, and psammoma bodies (laminated calcifications). Because these are nuclear features, FNA diagnoses PTC directly — unlike follicular cancer.
⚠️ Confusing psammoma bodies as unique to thyroid — they also appear in serous ovarian, meningioma, and mesothelioma; here the combination with Orphan-Annie nuclei is what nails PTC.
3. PTC = lymphatic spread
Papillary cancer spreads via LYMPHATICS to cervical lymph nodes. Nodal metastases are common yet have relatively little impact on survival in young patients — the cancer remains highly curable. Central/lateral neck node disease guides the extent of lymph node dissection.
⚠️ Saying PTC spreads hematogenously — wrong; that is follicular. PTC = lymphatic (paPillary → lymPh node). Mixing these up is a classic board distractor.
4. FTC ~5%, hematogenous
Follicular thyroid carcinoma (~5–10%) is a differentiated cancer that spreads HEMATOGENOUSLY to lung and bone (distant mets), unlike papillary's nodal spread. It typically presents as a solitary nodule and, being differentiated, still takes up radioiodine.
⚠️ Saying FTC spreads via lymphatics or commonly to nodes — wrong; follicular = blood-borne (lung/bone) spread. (Follicular → 'Flows' through blood.)
5. FTC needs histology
FTC is diagnosed only by histology showing capsular and/or vascular invasion — FNA cannot distinguish a benign follicular adenoma from carcinoma (the follicular paradox). Indeterminate follicular cytology (Bethesda IV) therefore goes to diagnostic lobectomy or molecular testing.
⚠️ Diagnosing or excluding follicular carcinoma on FNA cytology — impossible; invasion is an architectural finding requiring the resected specimen.
6. FTC in iodine-deficient areas
Follicular carcinoma is relatively more common in iodine-DEFICIENT regions, whereas papillary predominates in iodine-SUFFICIENT (or iodine-supplemented) areas. Iodine repletion of a population shifts the cancer mix toward papillary.
⚠️ Linking iodine deficiency to papillary cancer — reversed; iodine deficiency favors follicular, iodine sufficiency favors papillary.
7. MTC — calcitonin + CEA
Medullary thyroid carcinoma arises from parafollicular C cells (neural-crest derived), so it secretes CALCITONIN (and CEA) — these are its tumor markers for diagnosis and post-op monitoring. MTC does NOT take up iodine (C cells are not follicular), so RAI and TSH suppression are ineffective; treatment is surgical ± targeted kinase inhibitors (vandetanib/cabozantinib).
⚠️ Treating MTC with radioiodine or TSH suppression — wrong; C cells don't concentrate iodine or depend on TSH, so DTC therapy fails. Also, calcitonin (not thyroglobulin) is the MTC marker.
8. MTC — screen pheo first (MEN2)
MTC is the thyroid cancer of MEN2 (germline RET proto-oncogene mutation): MEN2A = MTC + pheochromocytoma + primary hyperparathyroidism; MEN2B = MTC + pheo + mucosal neuromas/marfanoid habitus (no hyperparathyroidism). Before any MTC surgery, SCREEN FOR PHEOCHROMOCYTOMA (plasma/urine metanephrines) — an unrecognized pheo can trigger fatal intra-operative hypertensive crisis. Test relatives for RET; prophylactic thyroidectomy is offered to RET carriers.
⚠️ Operating on MTC without first excluding pheochromocytoma — wrong and dangerous; you must resect/block the pheo first. Forgetting the germline RET work-up of family members is the second trap.
9. Anaplastic — rapidly fatal
Anaplastic (undifferentiated) carcinoma occurs in the ELDERLY as a rapidly enlarging, hard, fixed neck mass with compressive symptoms (dysphagia, stridor, hoarseness); prognosis is dismal (survival in months). Being undifferentiated, it does NOT take up iodine, so RAI is useless; management is airway protection plus chemoradiation, with BRAF/MEK inhibitors (dabrafenib+trametinib) for BRAF-mutated tumors.
⚠️ Expecting radioiodine to work or pursuing slow elective work-up — wrong; anaplastic cancer is undifferentiated (no iodine uptake) and is an airway emergency requiring urgent referral.
10. Lymphoma from Hashimoto
Primary thyroid lymphoma (usually diffuse large B-cell or MALT type) arises on a background of long-standing Hashimoto's thyroiditis, presenting as a rapidly enlarging mass in an older patient (mimics anaplastic — biopsy distinguishes them). Treatment is chemotherapy (R-CHOP) and/or radiation, NOT surgery.
⚠️ Taking thyroid lymphoma to surgery as first-line — wrong; lymphoma is treated medically (chemo/radiation). Also do not assume a fast-growing mass in the elderly is anaplastic — biopsy to exclude lymphoma, which is far more treatable.
11. Surgery decision (lobectomy vs total)
Extent of surgery in DTC is risk- and size-based: lobectomy for small (<1 cm, up to ~4 cm) low-risk unifocal PTC with no extrathyroidal extension or nodes; total thyroidectomy for tumors >4 cm, extrathyroidal extension, bilateral/multifocal disease, nodal or distant metastases, or prior radiation (total thyroidectomy is also required if RAI is planned). Key surgical complications: recurrent laryngeal nerve injury (hoarseness) and hypoparathyroidism (hypocalcemia).
⚠️ Defaulting every DTC to total thyroidectomy — wrong; small low-risk papillary cancers are adequately treated by lobectomy (or even active surveillance), avoiding lifelong replacement and surgical risks.
12. Active surveillance micropapillary
Papillary microcarcinoma (<1 cm), low-risk and without nodal/extrathyroidal disease, may be managed with active surveillance (serial ultrasound) rather than immediate surgery, given its indolent behavior and excellent outcomes.
⚠️ Insisting all thyroid cancers need immediate resection — wrong; low-risk micropapillary PTC can be safely observed.
13. TSH suppression ladder
After surgery for DTC, levothyroxine is dosed to SUPPRESS TSH because TSH is a growth factor for differentiated thyroid cancer cells. Target by recurrence risk: high-risk TSH <0.1, intermediate ~0.1–0.5, low-risk ~0.5–2 mIU/L. Suppression is relaxed once the patient is disease-free.
⚠️ Over-suppressing TSH long-term in low-risk patients — wrong; excessive suppression causes atrial fibrillation and osteoporosis, so the target is risk-stratified and de-escalated over time. (Note: TSH suppression only helps DTC — it does nothing for medullary or anaplastic cancer.)
14. RAI + Tg/anti-Tg follow-up
Radioactive iodine (I-131) ablates residual/microscopic differentiated cancer after total thyroidectomy in higher-risk DTC, and enables surveillance. Follow-up uses serum THYROGLOBULIN (a tumor marker after the normal gland is removed — a rising Tg signals recurrence) plus neck ultrasound; whole-body RAI scans for higher-risk cases. Recombinant TSH or thyroid hormone withdrawal raises TSH to stimulate iodine uptake before RAI/scanning.
⚠️ Trusting a 'normal' thyroglobulin when anti-thyroglobulin antibodies are present — wrong; anti-Tg antibodies falsely LOWER measured Tg, masking recurrence, so anti-Tg must always be measured alongside Tg. Also: RAI is useless in medullary/anaplastic cancer (no iodine uptake).
