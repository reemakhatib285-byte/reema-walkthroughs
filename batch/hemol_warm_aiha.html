📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Warm AIHA = 37C
Warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia (warm AIHA) is the most common AIHA. The autoantibody is IgG with maximal binding at body/'warm' temperature (37°C) — hence the courtroom thermometer at 37. IgG-coated cells are not lysed in the bloodstream; instead the spleen removes them (extravascular). It is idiopathic in ~half; the rest are secondary (CLL/lymphoma, SLE, drugs, immunodeficiency). Diagnosis rests on Coombs-positive hemolysis at 37°C.
⚠️ WRONG: warm AIHA is mediated by IgM. Discriminator — WARM = IgG (active at 37°C, extravascular/splenic); IgM is the COLD agglutinin antibody (active at 0–4°C, complement-fixing). Mnemonic: warm = IgG (Greatest at body temp), cold = IgM (Most active in the cold).
2. IgG coats RBC
IgG opsonizes the RBC surface; splenic macrophages bear Fc-gamma receptors that recognize the IgG Fc tail, so the cell is destroyed EXTRAVASCULARLY in the spleen. Because IgG is a poor complement fixer, intravascular lysis is uncommon — labs show high indirect bilirubin, high LDH, low haptoglobin, reticulocytosis, but typically NO hemoglobinuria. This Fc-mediated mechanism is exactly why steroids, rituximab, and splenectomy work.
⚠️ WRONG: warm AIHA destroys RBCs mainly inside blood vessels via the membrane attack complex. Discriminator — IgG-mediated clearance is EXTRAVASCULAR (Fc-receptor phagocytosis in the spleen); complement/MAC-driven intravascular hemolysis is the cold-agglutinin/PNH pattern, not warm AIHA.
3. Spherocytes formed
Splenic macrophages take 'bites' of the IgG-coated membrane rather than swallowing the whole cell; each bite removes surface area, so the cell rounds up into a SPHEROCYTE (same physics as hereditary spherocytosis). Spherocytes on the smear of an acquired hemolytic anemia are a strong clue to warm AIHA — and the DAT separates it from HS. Look also for polychromasia/nucleated RBCs from brisk reticulocytosis.
⚠️ WRONG: warm AIHA shows schistocytes on the smear. Discriminator — warm AIHA shows SPHEROCYTES (membrane nibbled in the spleen); SCHISTOCYTES (fragmented RBCs) indicate a microangiopathic process (TTP/HUS, DIC, mechanical valve), which is Coombs-NEGATIVE — a completely different workup.
4. DAT (Coombs) positive
The DIRECT antiglobulin test (DAT/direct Coombs) is the diagnostic anchor: in warm AIHA it is POSITIVE for IgG, with or without C3d. (The indirect Coombs detects free serum antibody.) A positive DAT for IgG with spherocytic hemolysis = warm AIHA. Note: rare DAT-negative AIHA exists (antibody below detection threshold) — diagnosed clinically. The DAT pattern also subtypes AIHA: IgG ± C3 = warm; C3 only = cold agglutinin.
⚠️ WRONG: warm AIHA is Coombs/DAT negative. Discriminator — warm AIHA is DAT POSITIVE (IgG ± C3); a Coombs-NEGATIVE spherocytic hemolytic anemia points to hereditary spherocytosis instead. The DAT is THE test that separates the two look-alikes.
5. Secondary: CLL/SLE/drugs
Always hunt for a secondary cause of warm AIHA: lymphoproliferative disease (CLL is classic, also non-Hodgkin lymphoma), autoimmune disease (SLE foremost), drugs, primary immunodeficiency (CVID), and immune-checkpoint inhibitors (an increasingly tested immunotherapy toxicity). New warm AIHA in an older adult mandates evaluation for CLL/lymphoma (peripheral flow cytometry, exam for lymphadenopathy). Treating the underlying disease often controls the hemolysis.
⚠️ WRONG: warm AIHA is always idiopathic, so no malignancy workup is needed. Discriminator — roughly half are SECONDARY; new warm AIHA (especially in an older patient) requires a workup for CLL/lymphoma and SLE — missing an underlying lymphoproliferative disorder is the trap.
6. Treatment: steroids first
First-line for warm AIHA = CORTICOSTEROIDS (prednisone ~1 mg/kg/day), which reduce autoantibody production and dampen splenic Fc-receptor phagocytosis; most respond in days to weeks, then taper. Second-line = RITUXIMAB (anti-CD20) and/or SPLENECTOMY (removes the site of destruction). IVIG gives a transient boost in urgent settings. Add folate; transfuse cautiously (least-incompatible units) for life-threatening anemia — never withhold a needed transfusion over cross-match difficulty.
⚠️ WRONG: first-line warm AIHA management is keep-warm measures/avoiding cold. Discriminator — keep-warm is the treatment for COLD agglutinin disease; warm AIHA (IgG, 37°C) responds to STEROIDS first, then rituximab/splenectomy. Steroids are notably INEFFECTIVE in cold agglutinin disease — swapping them is the classic mistake.
7. Cold agglutinin 4-30C
Cold agglutinin disease (CAD): the autoantibody is IgM, maximally active in the cold (thermal amplitude ~0–4°C, agglutinating up to ~30°C in peripheral/acral cool tissues). Clinically — acrocyanosis/Raynaud-like changes of ears, nose, fingers in the cold, plus chronic hemolysis. The high cold titer agglutinates cells in the lab (clumping on the smear, spuriously high MCV) and on cooling of the patient's extremities.
⚠️ WRONG: cold agglutinin disease is mediated by IgG. Discriminator — CAD = IgM (the large pentamer that agglutinates and fixes complement at cold temperatures); IgG is the WARM antibody. Reversing them flips the entire diagnosis and treatment.
8. IgM + complement
IgM is a strong complement activator: in cool peripheral blood IgM binds RBCs and fixes C1 → C3b deposition; as cells return to the warm core the IgM falls off but C3b remains, so cells are cleared by hepatic Kupffer cells (C3b receptors) — and brisk complement activation can drive some INTRAVASCULAR hemolysis (hemoglobinuria). The DAT is positive for C3d ONLY (the IgM has eluted), distinguishing CAD from warm AIHA.
⚠️ WRONG: in cold agglutinin disease the DAT is IgG-positive. Discriminator — CAD DAT is C3d-positive, IgG-NEGATIVE (the IgM dissociates at 37°C, leaving only complement on the cell); an IgG-positive DAT points to warm AIHA instead.
9. Mycoplasma / EBV
Cold agglutinin causes: acute/secondary forms follow infection — Mycoplasma pneumoniae (anti-I specificity) and EBV/infectious mononucleosis (anti-i specificity); these are usually self-limited. Chronic/primary CAD is a clonal B-cell (lymphoproliferative) disorder, classically Waldenström macroglobulinemia/IgM monoclonal gammopathy producing a monoclonal cold agglutinin. Mnemonic: 'Mycoplasma = anti-I (capital I, adult cells), infectious Mono/EBV = anti-i (little i, fetal/cord cells).'
⚠️ WRONG: post-Mycoplasma cold agglutinin hemolysis needs lifelong rituximab. Discriminator — INFECTION-associated cold agglutinins (Mycoplasma, EBV) are typically transient/self-limited needing only supportive keep-warm care; chronic/clonal (Waldenström) CAD is what may warrant rituximab-based therapy.
10. Keep warm + rituximab
Cold agglutinin disease management: AVOID COLD (keep the patient and infused fluids/blood warm) is foundational. For significant chronic hemolysis, RITUXIMAB-based therapy (± bendamustine) targets the clonal B cells; sutimlimab (anti-C1s complement inhibitor) is a newer option; plasmapheresis is a temporizing measure (IgM is mostly intravascular) for severe acute hemolysis or pre-op. Treat any underlying lymphoma/infection.
⚠️ WRONG: steroids and splenectomy are effective for cold agglutinin disease just like warm AIHA. Discriminator — steroids and SPLENECTOMY DO NOT work in CAD (hemolysis is complement-mediated and clearance is HEPATIC, not splenic); the mainstays are keep-warm, rituximab, and complement inhibition (sutimlimab).
11. Spleen not the site (cold)
In CAD the extravascular clearance occurs in the LIVER (Kupffer cells via C3b receptors), not the spleen — this is precisely why splenectomy fails in cold agglutinin disease. (Contrast warm AIHA: IgG-coated cells cleared in the SPLEEN, so splenectomy helps.) The site of destruction maps directly onto which therapies work.
⚠️ WRONG: splenectomy benefits cold agglutinin disease because that's where the cells are removed. Discriminator — cold agglutinin clearance is HEPATIC (Kupffer cells); the spleen is bypassed, so splenectomy is INEFFECTIVE — a reliably tested contrast with warm (splenic) AIHA.
12. PCH Donath-Landsteiner
Paroxysmal cold hemoglobinuria (PCH): a BIPHASIC IgG autoantibody with anti-P specificity (the Donath-Landsteiner antibody) binds RBCs in the COLD periphery and then fixes complement on REWARMING in the core → acute intravascular hemolysis with hemoglobinuria. Classically follows a viral illness in young CHILDREN and is usually self-limited. The Donath-Landsteiner test (cold-incubate then warm) is diagnostic.
⚠️ WRONG: PCH is caused by a cold IgM, like cold agglutinin disease. Discriminator — PCH is a BIPHASIC IgG anti-P (binds cold, lyses on warming) — the only AIHA where IgG fixes complement intravascularly; cold agglutinin disease is IgM. Different antibody, different test (Donath-Landsteiner).
13. Drug-induced immune HA
Drug-induced immune hemolytic anemia by mechanism: (1) HAPTEN/drug-adsorption — penicillins, cephalosporins (drug coats RBC, antibody attacks the drug; DAT IgG+); (2) autoantibody induction — methyldopa (and fludarabine) trigger a true warm-type autoantibody indistinguishable from idiopathic warm AIHA; (3) immune-complex/'innocent bystander' — classically cefotetan, ceftriaxone, quinidine (can cause brisk intravascular hemolysis, DAT C3+). Management: STOP the offending drug; most resolve.
⚠️ WRONG: drug-induced immune hemolytic anemia needs lifelong immunosuppression. Discriminator — the cornerstone is STOPPING the culprit drug; hemolysis usually resolves once it's withdrawn (steroids are only for severe cases). Continuing the drug while escalating immunosuppression is the error.
14. Evans syndrome
Evans syndrome = warm AIHA combined with immune thrombocytopenia (ITP), ± immune neutropenia — simultaneous or sequential autoimmune cytopenias. It is frequently SECONDARY (SLE, CLL/lymphoma, common variable immunodeficiency, ALPS in children), so a secondary-cause workup is mandatory. It tends to be chronic and relapsing; treatment mirrors warm AIHA (steroids, then rituximab) and ITP.
⚠️ WRONG: anemia plus thrombocytopenia with schistocytes and a Coombs-negative smear is Evans syndrome. Discriminator — Evans is Coombs/DAT POSITIVE autoimmune (AIHA + ITP); a Coombs-NEGATIVE anemia + thrombocytopenia with SCHISTOCYTES is a microangiopathy (TTP/HUS/DIC) — a medical emergency needing plasma exchange, not steroids.
