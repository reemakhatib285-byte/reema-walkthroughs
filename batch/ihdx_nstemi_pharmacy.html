📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Enoxaparin vs UFH
Anticoagulation is mandatory in ACS. Enoxaparin (LMWH) is preferred over UFH for medically managed NSTEMI; UFH is preferred when early PCI is planned (titratable, reversible). Both bridge until revascularization.
⚠️ Using LMWH in a patient going straight to cath where UFH is easier to monitor with ACT, or in severe renal failure where UFH is safer.
2. Bivalirudin
Bivalirudin is a direct thrombin inhibitor used during PCI, notably in heparin-induced thrombocytopenia where heparins are contraindicated. Lower bleeding than UFH+GPIIb/IIIa in some trials.
⚠️ Reaching for heparin in a patient with prior HIT — use bivalirudin or argatroban instead.
3. Fondaparinux — less bleeding
Fondaparinux (factor Xa inhibitor) gives the lowest bleeding risk for medically managed NSTEMI but carries catheter-thrombosis risk during PCI — adjunctive UFH must be given if the patient goes to cath.
⚠️ Using fondaparinux alone during PCI — it causes catheter thrombosis; add UFH at the time of the procedure.
4. P2Y12 × 12 months
DAPT = aspirin + a P2Y12 inhibitor (clopidogrel, prasugrel, or ticagrelor) for 12 months after ACS regardless of stent type, then aspirin indefinitely. Ticagrelor/prasugrel are preferred over clopidogrel post-PCI.
⚠️ Stopping the P2Y12 inhibitor early after ACS — premature DAPT cessation is a major risk for stent thrombosis.
5. Aspirin 75–162 mg
Aspirin loading 162–325 mg then lifelong maintenance 75–100 mg/day (often quoted 75–162). It irreversibly inhibits COX-1/TXA2 for the platelet's lifespan (~7–10 days).
⚠️ High maintenance aspirin doses add bleeding without efficacy benefit — low-dose (81 mg) is standard for chronic therapy.
6. Prasugrel dose & contraindication
Prasugrel (irreversible P2Y12) is contraindicated with prior stroke/TIA (net harm), and dose-reduced to 5 mg if <60 kg or age >75. Most potent; avoid before coronary anatomy is known.
⚠️ Giving prasugrel to a patient with prior CVA/TIA — it is absolutely contraindicated due to intracranial bleeding risk.
7. Clopidogrel CYP2C19 — 1 in 3 poor responders
Clopidogrel is a prodrug needing CYP2C19 activation; loss-of-function alleles → poor response and higher stent thrombosis. Ticagrelor/prasugrel are not affected and are preferred post-ACS PCI.
⚠️ Assuming clopidogrel is working in a poor-metabolizer — consider ticagrelor/prasugrel after stent thrombosis on clopidogrel.
8. DES = platelet-mediated → antiplatelet
Stent (DES) thrombosis is a platelet-driven white clot — prevented by DAPT (antiplatelets), not by anticoagulants. This is why P2Y12 + aspirin, not warfarin, protect the stent.
⚠️ Adding warfarin to prevent stent thrombosis — arterial stent clot is platelet-mediated and needs antiplatelet therapy.
9. Dabigatran — fibrin, not platelet
Anticoagulants (dabigatran DTI, warfarin) prevent fibrin-rich red clots (venous/AF stroke), not the platelet plug of arterial stent thrombosis. Right drug, right clot mechanism.
⚠️ Using an oral anticoagulant as monotherapy to protect a coronary stent — it targets fibrin, not platelets.
10. Vasospastic angina — spasm resolves
Prinzmetal/vasospastic angina: transient resting ST elevation that resolves spontaneously, normal/non-obstructive coronaries, often negative troponin. Smoking is the #1 risk factor; triggered by ergot, cocaine, hyperventilation.
⚠️ Calling transient resting ST elevation with clean coronaries a STEMI — it's vasospasm; treat with CCB/nitrates, not thrombolysis.
11. Beta-blocker worsens spasm
In vasospastic angina, non-selective beta-blockade leaves alpha-mediated vasoconstriction unopposed and worsens spasm. Avoid beta-blockers; same logic as cocaine chest pain.
⚠️ Giving a beta-blocker for vasospastic/Prinzmetal angina (or cocaine MI) — unopposed alpha worsens coronary spasm.
12. CCB first-line; avoid ASA/NSAID
Treat vasospasm with calcium channel blockers (first-line) ± nitrates. High-dose aspirin/NSAIDs inhibit protective prostacyclin (PGI2) and can worsen spasm. Smoking cessation is key.
⚠️ High-dose aspirin in vasospastic angina — it blocks vasoprotective prostacyclin; CCBs are the cornerstone therapy.
