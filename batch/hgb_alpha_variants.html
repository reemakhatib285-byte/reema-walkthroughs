📖 Read-through — all 11 symbols
1. 4 genes deleted — Bart/hydrops
There are 4 α-globin genes (two on each chromosome 16). Deletion of ALL 4 (--/--) leaves no α chains, so fetal γ chains self-associate into Hb Barts (γ4), which has extremely high O2 affinity and cannot release O2 to tissues. Result: severe in-utero hypoxia → hydrops fetalis (high-output failure, anasarca), death in utero or shortly after birth. The mother is at risk of severe preeclampsia / mirror syndrome and difficult/hemorrhagic delivery.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Hb Barts (γ4) carries oxygen well because it has four heme groups.' Its O2 affinity is so high it never unloads O2 — tissues are profoundly hypoxic, hence fatal hydrops.
2. 3 genes deleted — HbH
Deletion of 3 of 4 α genes (--/-α) leaves one functioning gene → marked α-chain deficit. Excess β chains form HbH (β4), an unstable tetramer that precipitates into Heinz-like inclusions, causing a moderate-to-severe chronic hemolytic anemia with splenomegaly, jaundice, and microcytosis. HbH (5–30%) is detectable on hemoglobin electrophoresis/HPLC and with supravital (brilliant cresyl blue) staining showing a golf-ball/pitted inclusion pattern. Like HbH, Hb Barts also fails to deliver O2.
⚠️ WRONG: 'HbH disease is asymptomatic like α-thal trait.' Three-gene deletion produces a clinically significant transfusion-occasional hemolytic anemia with splenomegaly — not a silent state.
3. 2 genes — α-thal trait
Loss of 2 α genes = α-thalassemia trait (minor): mild or absent anemia with disproportionate microcytosis (MCV ~65–80) and a normal-to-high RBC count (Mentzer index <13). Crucially, electrophoresis/HPLC is NORMAL — HbA2 and HbF are NOT elevated — so α-thal trait is a diagnosis of exclusion (normal iron studies, normal HbA2) confirmed by genetic testing.
⚠️ WRONG: 'α-thal trait shows an elevated HbA2.' HbA2 is normal in α-thal trait; elevated HbA2 (>3.5%) is the signature of β-thalassemia trait — that mix-up is the classic board trap.
4. Cis vs trans / ethnicity
The two missing genes can be in cis (both on the SAME chromosome, --/αα — common in Southeast Asians) or trans (one on EACH chromosome, -α/-α — common in those of African descent). This matters for offspring: a cis carrier can pass -- to a child, so cis × cis risks HbH disease or Hb Barts/hydrops. Trans carriers cannot produce a -- chromosome, so their offspring are not at risk for the severe forms — hence genetic counseling hinges on ethnicity/configuration.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Any couple who are both α-thal trait can have a hydrops fetalis baby.' Only the cis (--/αα, Asian) configuration can transmit a --; trans (-α/-α, African) carriers do not produce hydrops/HbH offspring.
5. 1 gene — silent carrier
Deletion of a single α gene (-α/αα) is the silent carrier state: completely normal CBC, normal MCV, normal electrophoresis, no anemia. It is detectable only by DNA analysis and is clinically irrelevant except for genetic counseling. This is the bottom rung of the ladder — the mildest end of the α-thalassemia spectrum.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A mild microcytic anemia with normal HbA2 must be one-gene silent carrier.' Microcytosis requires loss of TWO genes (trait); the one-gene silent carrier has entirely normal indices.
6. HbC (β6 Glu→Lys)
HbC results from a β6 Glutamate→LYSINE substitution (same codon as sickle, different amino acid). Homozygous HbCC causes a mild chronic hemolytic anemia with abundant target cells and pathognomonic intracellular HbC crystals (and microspherocytes). Heterozygous HbC trait is asymptomatic. Compound HbSC disease (one S + one C gene) is a MODERATE sickling disorder — milder than HbSS but with more retinopathy, avascular necrosis, and renal papillary necrosis.
⚠️ WRONG: 'HbC is a Glu→Val substitution at β6.' Glu→VAL at β6 is HbS (sickle); HbC is Glu→LYSINE at the same position — the discriminator is the substituted amino acid (Val vs Lys).
7. HbE (β26 Glu→Lys)
HbE is a β26 Glu→Lys mutation that also activates a cryptic splice site, reducing β-chain output (so it behaves partly like a mild β-thalassemia). Homozygous HbEE causes mild microcytosis with target cells and minimal/no anemia. It is the most prevalent Hb variant in Southeast Asia. The board catch: HbE/β-thalassemia (one HbE + one β-thal allele) can be SEVERE — a major transfusion-dependent thalassemia syndrome.
⚠️ WRONG: 'HbE is always a benign trait.' HbEE is mild, but co-inheritance as HbE/β-thalassemia produces a clinically severe, often transfusion-dependent thalassemia.
8. High-O2-affinity Hb
High-affinity hemoglobin variants have a LEFT-shifted O2 dissociation curve (low P50): they grip O2 and won't release it to tissues. Tissue hypoxia drives appropriate (secondary) erythrocytosis via EPO — but here SaO2 is normal and the polycythemia is COMPENSATORY. Diagnose with a low measured P50 and normal/high EPO; there is no JAK2 mutation and no splenomegaly. Usually no treatment is needed.
⚠️ WRONG: 'High-affinity Hb erythrocytosis should be treated like polycythemia vera with phlebotomy.' This erythrocytosis is compensatory for tissue hypoxia; phlebotomy worsens O2 delivery. There is no JAK2 mutation — distinguish via low P50 and absent splenomegaly.
9. Low-O2-affinity Hb
Low-affinity hemoglobin variants have a RIGHT-shifted curve (high P50): they release O2 readily, so venous and tissue O2 are high and adequate. Because more deoxyhemoglobin is present, patients can appear cyanotic despite a NORMAL PaO2, and EPO drive is low, giving a mild anemia. The condition is generally benign and requires no therapy — the cyanosis is a red herring.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Cyanosis with a normal PaO2 in a well patient means methemoglobinemia.' Low-affinity (high-P50) Hb also causes cyanosis with normal PaO2 — but blood is not chocolate-brown and co-oximetry is normal; tissue oxygenation is actually good.
10. Unstable Hb
Unstable hemoglobins (e.g., Hb Köln, Hb Hasharon, Hb Zürich) carry mutations that destabilize the globin fold, so the Hb denatures and precipitates as Heinz bodies, producing a Heinz-body hemolytic anemia. Hemolysis is worsened by oxidant stress, drugs, and febrile illness. Diagnose with the heat-stability (50°C) test or isopropanol precipitation test; supravital staining shows Heinz bodies. Management is supportive — avoid oxidant triggers; splenectomy in severe cases.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Heinz bodies on a smear automatically mean G6PD deficiency.' Heinz bodies also appear in unstable Hb variants (and HbH); the discriminating test is heat/isopropanol stability for unstable Hb vs G6PD enzyme assay.
11. HbM / methemoglobinemia
Hb M denotes globin-chain mutations (usually in the heme pocket, often a proximal/distal histidine substitution) that stabilize iron in the ferric Fe3+ state, producing CONGENITAL methemoglobinemia. Patients have lifelong cyanosis that does NOT respond to supplemental O2 and is generally well tolerated. Because the defect is in globin (not the reductase enzyme), methylene blue does NOT work and no treatment is needed. Inheritance is autosomal dominant.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Hb M methemoglobinemia is reversed with methylene blue.' Methylene blue works only for enzymatic (cytochrome b5 reductase / acquired oxidant) MetHb; Hb M is a structural globin defect — methylene blue is ineffective and the condition needs no treatment.
