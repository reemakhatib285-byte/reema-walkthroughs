📖 Read-through — all 16 symbols
1. Neg. of pulmonary edema
CEP's classic radiographic sign is the 'photographic negative of pulmonary edema': peripheral (outer-zone) consolidations/opacities that SPARE the central perihilar region — the mirror image of cardiogenic pulmonary edema, which is central/perihilar ('bat-wing'). This pattern in a subacute cougher with eosinophilia is nearly diagnostic. Mnemonic: 'Chronic Eosinophilic = edges, not center.'
⚠️ WRONG answer: reading central perihilar 'bat-wing' opacities and calling it CEP. CEP is the photographic NEGATIVE — peripheral opacities sparing the center. Central bat-wing edema = cardiogenic pulmonary edema, not CEP.
2. Subpleural upper-mid zones
CEP opacities are subpleural and upper/mid-lung predominant — the classic CEP distribution. This contrasts with IPF and most fibrotic ILDs, which are basal/subpleural and lower-zone predominant. Upper-zone peripheral consolidation in an asthmatic woman with eosinophilia points to CEP.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing upper-lobe peripheral consolidations to IPF or postprimary TB. IPF is lower-zone fibrotic and steroid-unresponsive; CEP is upper/mid peripheral, eosinophilic, and melts away with steroids.
3. Migratory opacities
CEP infiltrates can be migratory, shifting location over serial films — overlapping with the migratory infiltrates of Loffler/simple pulmonary eosinophilia and organizing pneumonia. In CEP they are more persistent and peripheral, and accompanied by blood eosinophilia and systemic symptoms.
⚠️ WRONG answer: calling transient, rapidly migrating infiltrates with minimal symptoms CEP. Fleeting migratory infiltrates that clear on their own suggest Loffler (simple pulmonary eosinophilia); CEP infiltrates are more fixed, peripheral, symptomatic, and steroid-requiring.
4. Middle-aged woman
Classic CEP patient: a middle-aged woman (40s-50s), frequently with asthma/atopy. Female predominance (~2:1) and a background of asthma are high-yield demographic clues that separate CEP from AEP (young male, new smoker, no asthma).
⚠️ WRONG answer: expecting a young male new-smoker — that is the AEP profile. CEP favors middle-aged atopic/asthmatic women with a subacute course.
5. Asthma / atopy history
Most CEP patients (about half or more) have a preceding history of asthma or atopy; asthma may worsen as CEP develops. This atopic background is a key distinguishing feature from AEP and aligns CEP on the eosinophilic-asthma/allergic spectrum that also includes EGPA and ABPA.
⚠️ WRONG answer: using asthma history to diagnose EGPA instead of CEP. Asthma occurs in both, but EGPA adds systemic vasculitis (neuropathy, sinus, cardiac, ANCA); isolated lung disease with peripheral eosinophilia and the photographic-negative pattern is CEP.
6. Subacute symptoms
CEP is subacute-to-chronic: weeks to months of cough, dyspnea, wheeze, low-grade fever, weight loss, and night sweats. The 'B-symptom' constitutional picture plus weight loss often triggers a TB or malignancy workup before eosinophilia and the imaging pattern reveal CEP. Duration (weeks-months) is the key contrast with AEP (<1 month, acute).
⚠️ WRONG answer: working the chronic-cough/weight-loss/night-sweats picture as TB or lymphoma and missing CEP. The peripheral blood eosinophilia plus peripheral upper-zone (photographic-negative) opacities reorient the diagnosis to CEP.
7. Peripheral blood eos
CEP has peripheral blood eosinophilia in >90% of patients (peripheral eosinophils commonly making up a large fraction of the differential, mean >30%) — a defining laboratory feature and a major contrast with AEP, where early peripheral eosinophilia is absent. Blood eosinophilia plus the imaging pattern often makes the diagnosis without biopsy.
⚠️ WRONG answer: thinking the absence of peripheral eosinophilia is typical (that is AEP). In CEP, peripheral blood eosinophilia is present in >90% and is one of the most reliable clues — a normal eosinophil count argues against CEP.
8. BAL eos >40%
BAL in CEP shows marked eosinophilia (commonly >40%, sometimes >60%) — even higher than the >25% threshold used for AEP. With the typical clinical/imaging picture, this degree of BAL eosinophilia confirms CEP and obviates lung biopsy.
⚠️ WRONG answer: requiring a surgical lung biopsy to diagnose CEP. The combination of peripheral eosinophilia, photographic-negative imaging, and high BAL eosinophilia is sufficient — biopsy is usually unnecessary.
9. <25% pathognomonic
A high BAL eosinophil percentage in the correct clinical setting is essentially pathognomonic for eosinophilic pneumonia. The threshold matters: >25% defines the eosinophilic pneumonias, and the very high percentages seen in CEP (often >40%) virtually exclude other ILDs in the differential.
⚠️ WRONG answer: dismissing eosinophilic pneumonia because BAL eosinophils are 'only' mildly elevated. Mild BAL eosinophilia is nonspecific; it is the >25% (and in CEP often >40%) threshold that is diagnostically powerful.
10. High relapse on taper
CEP relapses in >50% of patients as steroids are tapered or stopped, so most patients need a prolonged course (often many months) and some need long-term low-dose maintenance. This high relapse rate is the single best feature distinguishing CEP from AEP, which does not recur.
⚠️ WRONG answer: stopping steroids after a couple of weeks expecting a one-and-done cure (that is AEP behavior). CEP relapses in >50% on taper — premature discontinuation predictably brings the disease back.
11. Steroids — rapid recovery
CEP responds dramatically and rapidly to oral corticosteroids — prednisone ~0.5 mg/kg/day (roughly 40-60 mg/day) with symptom and imaging improvement within days, sometimes hours. The catch is the slow taper over months because of the high relapse risk; rapid initial response does not mean short treatment.
⚠️ WRONG answer: equating rapid response with a short course. The brisk response is real, but unlike AEP the taper must be prolonged (months) to prevent relapse.
12. Anti-IL-5 sparing
For relapsing or steroid-dependent CEP, anti-IL-5/IL-5-receptor biologics (mepolizumab, reslizumab, benralizumab) act as steroid-sparing agents — IL-5 is the key cytokine driving eosinophil maturation and survival. This mirrors their use in severe eosinophilic asthma and EGPA.
⚠️ WRONG answer: reaching for cyclophosphamide or a broad immunosuppressant for steroid-dependent CEP. CEP is not a vasculitis; the targeted steroid-sparing approach is anti-IL-5 biologic therapy, not cytotoxic immunosuppression.
13. No acute resp failure
Unlike AEP, CEP rarely causes acute hypoxemic respiratory failure or an ARDS picture — its course is subacute/insidious, and patients are usually not ventilator-dependent at presentation. The slow tempo plus peripheral eosinophilia frames CEP as a chronic, steroid-managed disease rather than an ICU emergency.
⚠️ WRONG answer: expecting fulminant respiratory failure to confirm CEP. Acute respiratory failure/ARDS picture points to AEP; CEP presents subacutely and seldom needs ventilation.
14. Elevated ESR/CRP/IgE
Supportive (nonspecific) labs in CEP: elevated ESR and CRP, elevated serum IgE (reflecting the atopic/allergic background), and thrombocytosis, in addition to the peripheral eosinophilia. These reinforce the inflammatory eosinophilic picture but are not specific on their own.
⚠️ WRONG answer: using elevated inflammatory markers to call this a generic infection or to exclude an eosinophilic process. In CEP, raised ESR/CRP/IgE are expected and supportive; the eosinophilia and imaging pattern are what specify the diagnosis.
15. EGPA mimic
Differential: EGPA (eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis, formerly Churg-Strauss) — an eosinophilic ANCA-associated vasculitis with asthma plus multisystem features (mononeuritis multiplex, sinusitis, cardiac, GI, skin purpura, glomerulonephritis); p-ANCA/MPO is positive in ~40-60%. CEP overlaps (asthma + eosinophilia + lung infiltrates) but is lung-limited without vasculitis.
⚠️ WRONG answer: labeling lung-limited eosinophilic pneumonia as EGPA. The discriminator is systemic vasculitis: neuropathy, sinus disease, cardiac/renal involvement, or ANCA positivity indicate EGPA; their absence with isolated peripheral lung disease indicates CEP.
16. IPF mimic
Differential: idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) — but IPF affects older patients (typically >60), shows lower-zone/basal subpleural reticulation with honeycombing (UIP pattern), has NO eosinophilia, and is steroid-UNRESPONSIVE (treated with antifibrotics pirfenidone/nintedanib). CEP is younger, upper/mid peripheral, eosinophilic, and steroid-responsive.
⚠️ WRONG answer: starting antifibrotics (pirfenidone/nintedanib) or withholding steroids because you assumed IPF. IPF is basal/fibrotic, non-eosinophilic, and steroid-unresponsive; CEP's peripheral upper-lobe eosinophilic infiltrates resolve with corticosteroids — completely different management.
