⚠️ Board traps 12
  1. FGF23 saboteur guild — WRONG ANSWER: 'FGF23 excess raises calcitriol because it's a hormone responding to low phosphate.' Discriminator: FGF23 LOWERS phosphate AND LOWERS calcitriol — the opposite of the appropriate physiologic response (low PO4 should normally stimulate 1-alpha-hydroxylase). This dissociation (low PO4 with non-elevated calcitriol) is the fingerprint of an FGF23-mediated disorder versus dietary phosphate loss.
  2. XLH child with rickets — WRONG ANSWER: 'Give high-dose vitamin D / cholecalciferol to cure the rickets.' Discriminator: XLH rickets is driven by phosphate wasting, NOT vitamin D deficiency — 25-OH-D is normal and calcitriol is low/normal as a CONSEQUENCE of FGF23, not the primary defect. Plain vitamin D won't fix it; you need phosphate repletion or burosumab to neutralize FGF23.
  3. TIO tumor — WRONG ANSWER: 'Treat the chronic hypophosphatemic osteomalacia with lifelong phosphate/calcitriol and stop looking.' Discriminator: TIO is potentially CURABLE by finding and resecting the FGF23-secreting tumor — an adult with new unexplained hypophosphatemic osteomalacia warrants a tumor hunt (DOTATATE PET), not just symptomatic repletion. Missing the tumor is the tested error.
  4. ADHR resistant to cleavage — WRONG ANSWER: 'ADHR is due to overproduction of FGF23 from a tumor.' Discriminator: ADHR is a germline FGF23 mutation that blocks DEGRADATION (cleavage-resistant), not increased synthesis or a tumor (that's TIO). Iron deficiency unmasking/worsening symptoms is a uniquely ADHR clue.
  5. FGF23 pattern scroll — WRONG ANSWER: 'Low phosphate always means dietary insufficiency or a GI cause — calcitriol should be high.' Discriminator: in FGF23 excess, calcitriol does NOT rise the way it should with low PO4, and urinary phosphate is inappropriately HIGH (renal wasting). Checking FEPO4 and seeing renal loss distinguishes FGF23/renal causes from dietary/GI loss (where urine PO4 is appropriately low).
  6. PTH vs FGF23 scroll — WRONG ANSWER: 'PTH and FGF23 do the same thing, so calcitriol behaves identically in hyperparathyroidism and FGF23 excess.' Discriminator: the calcitriol direction splits them — high PTH → high calcitriol (low PO4, high Ca, high calcitriol = primary HPT picture), while high FGF23 → low calcitriol (low PO4, normal Ca, low calcitriol). Calcitriol level is the single best discriminator.

Discriminator: chronic aluminum binders cause aluminum accumulation → adynamic bone disease/osteomalacia, microcytic anemia, and dialysis encephalopathy. Non-calcium binders (sevelamer) are preferred long-term; aluminum is only for short-term severe hyperphosphatemia.

  10. Tumor lysis / rhabdo — WRONG ANSWER: 'Give calcium aggressively for the hypocalcemia of tumor lysis.' Only treat hypocalcemia if symptomatic — first lower phosphate. Replacing PO4 here would be the opposite error since PO4 is already high.
  11. Cell breakdown bed — WRONG ANSWER: 'Soft-tissue/vascular calcification reflects high calcium alone.' Discriminator: it's the Ca × PO4 PRODUCT, not calcium alone — hyperphosphatemia with even normal calcium can drive calcification (calciphylaxis in dialysis patients is a feared example). 📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. FGF23 saboteur guild
FGF23 (fibroblast growth factor 23) is a bone-derived phosphatonin secreted by osteocytes/osteoblasts; it acts on the proximal tubule via the FGFR1–Klotho co-receptor to downregulate the NaPi-2a/2c sodium-phosphate cotransporters, producing renal phosphate wasting and hypophosphatemia. Critically, it also inhibits renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase (CYP27B1) and stimulates 24-hydroxylase (CYP24A1), so it LOWERS calcitriol (1,25-vitamin D). The net 'FGF23 syndrome' signature is low PO4 + inappropriately low/normal calcitriol. Mnemonic: 'FGF23 = Phosphate Goes, Free of vitamin D' — it wastes phosphate and suppresses active D.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'FGF23 excess raises calcitriol because it's a hormone responding to low phosphate.' Discriminator: FGF23 LOWERS phosphate AND LOWERS calcitriol — the opposite of the appropriate physiologic response (low PO4 should normally stimulate 1-alpha-hydroxylase). This dissociation (low PO4 with non-elevated calcitriol) is the fingerprint of an FGF23-mediated disorder versus dietary phosphate loss.
2. XLH child with rickets
X-linked hypophosphatemia (XLH) is the most common heritable form of rickets, caused by a loss-of-function PHEX gene mutation (X-linked dominant). Loss of PHEX leads to increased circulating intact FGF23, driving renal phosphate wasting, hypophosphatemia, and impaired bone mineralization with bowing, short stature, and dental abscesses. Labs: low PO4, normal/low calcitriol, normal calcium and PTH, elevated ALP. Treatment has shifted from oral phosphate + calcitriol to burosumab (anti-FGF23 monoclonal antibody), which is now first-line for XLH.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Give high-dose vitamin D / cholecalciferol to cure the rickets.' Discriminator: XLH rickets is driven by phosphate wasting, NOT vitamin D deficiency — 25-OH-D is normal and calcitriol is low/normal as a CONSEQUENCE of FGF23, not the primary defect. Plain vitamin D won't fix it; you need phosphate repletion or burosumab to neutralize FGF23.
3. TIO tumor
Tumor-induced osteomalacia (TIO, oncogenic osteomalacia) is an acquired paraneoplastic syndrome in which a small, often occult mesenchymal tumor (phosphaturic mesenchymal tumor) secretes FGF23, producing the same low-PO4, low-calcitriol picture as XLH but in an adult. Classic presentation: insidious bone pain, proximal muscle weakness, and fractures with persistent unexplained hypophosphatemia. Tumors are typically tiny and hide in bone or soft tissue (feet, head/neck); localization often requires 68-Ga-DOTATATE PET or octreotide scanning. Resection is curative.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Treat the chronic hypophosphatemic osteomalacia with lifelong phosphate/calcitriol and stop looking.' Discriminator: TIO is potentially CURABLE by finding and resecting the FGF23-secreting tumor — an adult with new unexplained hypophosphatemic osteomalacia warrants a tumor hunt (DOTATATE PET), not just symptomatic repletion. Missing the tumor is the tested error.
4. ADHR resistant to cleavage
Autosomal dominant hypophosphatemic rickets (ADHR) is caused by a gain-of-function mutation in the FGF23 gene itself at the RXXR furin cleavage site, rendering FGF23 resistant to proteolytic inactivation so it accumulates. Phenotype overlaps XLH (low PO4, low/normal calcitriol, phosphaturia) but inheritance is autosomal dominant and onset is variable — penetrance and severity fluctuate with iron status (iron deficiency raises FGF23, worsening disease). Mnemonic: ADHR = FGF23 that 'won't die' (uncleavable).
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'ADHR is due to overproduction of FGF23 from a tumor.' Discriminator: ADHR is a germline FGF23 mutation that blocks DEGRADATION (cleavage-resistant), not increased synthesis or a tumor (that's TIO). Iron deficiency unmasking/worsening symptoms is a uniquely ADHR clue.
5. FGF23 pattern scroll
The unifying FGF23-excess lab pattern: LOW serum phosphate with INAPPROPRIATELY low or normal calcitriol (1,25-D), normal serum calcium, normal/normal-ish PTH, and elevated urinary phosphate (high fractional excretion of phosphate, FEPO4 >5–20%). The 'tell' is that calcitriol fails to rise despite hypophosphatemia, because FGF23 directly suppresses 1-alpha-hydroxylase. ALP is often elevated reflecting active osteomalacia.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Low phosphate always means dietary insufficiency or a GI cause — calcitriol should be high.' Discriminator: in FGF23 excess, calcitriol does NOT rise the way it should with low PO4, and urinary phosphate is inappropriately HIGH (renal wasting). Checking FEPO4 and seeing renal loss distinguishes FGF23/renal causes from dietary/GI loss (where urine PO4 is appropriately low).
6. PTH vs FGF23 scroll
Both PTH and FGF23 are phosphaturic (both downregulate proximal-tubule NaPi cotransporters and lower serum phosphate), but they have OPPOSITE effects on vitamin D: PTH STIMULATES 1-alpha-hydroxylase (raises calcitriol), whereas FGF23 INHIBITS it (lowers calcitriol). They also differ on calcium: PTH raises serum Ca; FGF23 has little direct Ca effect. FGF23 and PTH form a feedback loop — phosphate and calcitriol stimulate FGF23, and FGF23 in turn suppresses PTH. Mnemonic: 'PTH pumps up D, FGF23 fights D.'
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'PTH and FGF23 do the same thing, so calcitriol behaves identically in hyperparathyroidism and FGF23 excess.' Discriminator: the calcitriol direction splits them — high PTH → high calcitriol (low PO4, high Ca, high calcitriol = primary HPT picture), while high FGF23 → low calcitriol (low PO4, normal Ca, low calcitriol). Calcitriol level is the single best discriminator.
7. Phosphate binders treatment
Two opposite problems, two opposite treatments. HYPERphosphatemia (CKD-MBD): dietary phosphate restriction plus oral phosphate binders taken WITH meals — sevelamer (non-calcium, preferred to avoid Ca load/vascular calcification), calcium acetate/carbonate (also treats hypocalcemia but risks calcification), lanthanum, and short-term aluminum hydroxide (avoid chronic use due to aluminum toxicity/encephalopathy/osteomalacia). FGF23-driven HYPOphosphatemia (XLH/TIO): burosumab, an anti-FGF23 monoclonal antibody, now first-line.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Aluminum hydroxide is a fine long-term phosphate binder in CKD.' Discriminator: chronic aluminum binders cause aluminum accumulation → adynamic bone disease/osteomalacia, microcytic anemia, and dialysis encephalopathy. Non-calcium binders (sevelamer) are preferred long-term; aluminum is only for short-term severe hyperphosphatemia.
8. Hyperphosphatemia 4 mechanisms
Four buckets for hyperphosphatemia: (1) DECREASED renal excretion — chronic/acute kidney disease is by far the most common cause (kidney can't dump PO4 once GFR falls below ~25–30); (2) LOW PTH action — hypoparathyroidism or pseudohypoparathyroidism removes the phosphaturic drive; (3) CELLULAR RELEASE — tumor lysis syndrome, rhabdomyolysis, hemolysis dump intracellular phosphate; (4) EXCESS LOAD — phosphate-containing enemas/laxatives (sodium phosphate), IV phosphate, or massive vitamin D intake. Spurious hyperphosphatemia (pseudohyperphosphatemia) can occur with hemolyzed samples or paraproteinemia.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'A sodium-phosphate bowel prep is harmless regarding phosphate.' Discriminator: oral sodium phosphate purgatives can cause acute severe hyperphosphatemia and acute phosphate nephropathy (nephrocalcinosis), especially in elderly/CKD patients — an 'excess load' cause that's a classic exam vignette.
9. CKD-MBD bed
CKD–mineral and bone disorder (CKD-MBD): as GFR falls, the kidney can neither excrete phosphate nor make calcitriol. Early, FGF23 rises (first compensatory change) to promote phosphaturia; eventually phosphate retention wins. Low calcitriol + hyperphosphatemia + the resulting hypocalcemia all drive secondary hyperparathyroidism (high PTH). Sequence to remember: ↑FGF23 → ↓calcitriol → ↓Ca + ↑PO4 → ↑PTH. Long-standing stimulation can become autonomous (tertiary HPT with high Ca). Management targets phosphate (binders), calcitriol/analogs, and calcimimetics (cinacalcet).
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'CKD-MBD looks like an FGF23-wasting syndrome with low phosphate.' Discriminator: in CKD phosphate is HIGH (retention from low GFR) and calcitriol is LOW — the REVERSE of XLH/TIO where phosphate is low. The shared feature is low calcitriol; the splitter is the phosphate direction (high in CKD, low in FGF23-wasting).
10. Tumor lysis / rhabdo
Massive cell breakdown releases intracellular phosphate (cells are phosphate-rich), causing acute hyperphosphatemia. Tumor lysis syndrome (after chemotherapy of bulky/high-turnover tumors — Burkitt lymphoma, ALL) gives the tetrad: ↑K, ↑PO4, ↑uric acid, ↓Ca, often with AKI. Rhabdomyolysis (crush injury, statins, exertion) releases PO4, K, myoglobin, and CK. Prevention/treatment: aggressive IV hydration, rasburicase/allopurinol for urate, and managing electrolytes.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Give calcium aggressively for the hypocalcemia of tumor lysis.' Discriminator: in tumor lysis the hypocalcemia is driven by HIGH phosphate (Ca-PO4 precipitation); giving calcium when phosphate is high risks metastatic calcification. Only treat hypocalcemia if symptomatic — first lower phosphate. Replacing PO4 here would be the opposite error since PO4 is already high.
11. Cell breakdown bed
When the calcium × phosphate product rises (classically a product >55 mg2/dL2), calcium-phosphate precipitates in soft tissues, causing metastatic/ectopic calcification — vascular calcification, calciphylaxis (in CKD/dialysis), conjunctival/corneal deposits, and nephrocalcinosis. This is the unifying danger of sustained hyperphosphatemia and a major driver of cardiovascular mortality in CKD. It's why phosphate control (binders, dialysis) matters so much.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Soft-tissue/vascular calcification reflects high calcium alone.' Discriminator: it's the Ca × PO4 PRODUCT, not calcium alone — hyperphosphatemia with even normal calcium can drive calcification (calciphylaxis in dialysis patients is a feared example). Controlling phosphate, not just calcium, is the key intervention.
12. Hypoparathyroidism bed
Hypoparathyroidism removes PTH's phosphaturic action, so phosphate is retained → hyperphosphatemia, alongside hypocalcemia (no PTH to mobilize Ca or activate calcitriol) and a LOW/inappropriately normal PTH. Causes: post-surgical (thyroidectomy/parathyroidectomy — most common), autoimmune (APS-1), DiGeorge (22q11 deletion), and hypomagnesemia (impairs PTH secretion/action). Symptoms are those of hypocalcemia: perioral numbness, Chvostek/Trousseau signs, tetany, QT prolongation. Pseudohypoparathyroidism gives the same Ca/PO4 picture but with HIGH PTH (end-organ resistance).
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'High phosphate + low calcium means CKD/secondary hyperparathyroidism.' Discriminator: check PTH. Hypoparathyroidism = high PO4, low Ca, LOW PTH. CKD secondary HPT = high PO4, low Ca, but HIGH PTH. Pseudohypoparathyroidism = high PO4, low Ca, HIGH PTH (resistance, often with Albright osteodystrophy). The PTH level splits these three.
