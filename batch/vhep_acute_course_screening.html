📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. HBV screen — all adults >18
CDC 2023 recommends one-time hepatitis B screening for ALL adults aged 18 and older, using the triple panel: HBsAg, anti-HBs, and total anti-HBc. This replaced older risk-based-only screening. Pregnant women are screened with HBsAg at every pregnancy regardless of prior testing. Periodic re-screening is for those with ongoing risk (IVDU, HIV, multiple partners, household/sexual contacts of HBV).
⚠️ Don't pick 'screen only high-risk groups' — that is the outdated approach. Current guidance is universal one-time adult screening for HBV with the 3-test panel, not HBsAg alone.
2. HCV screen — all adults 18–79
USPSTF/CDC recommend one-time hepatitis C screening with anti-HCV antibody for ALL adults aged 18–79, plus screening in pregnancy and ongoing testing for those with continued risk. The historic-cohort man captures the legacy 1945–1965 'baby boomer' birth-cohort recommendation, now folded into universal adult screening. A reactive anti-HCV requires confirmatory HCV RNA to distinguish active from cleared/past infection.
⚠️ A positive anti-HCV alone does NOT mean active infection — ~25% spontaneously clear. You must confirm with HCV RNA before diagnosing chronic HCV or treating.
3. Sexual / multiple partners
HBV is efficiently transmitted sexually and is the most sexually-transmissible of the hepatitis viruses; multiple partners is a major risk factor. HBV is ~50–100x more infectious than HIV per exposure. This is why HBV vaccination and screening are emphasized in those with multiple sexual partners or STIs.
⚠️ HCV is poorly transmitted sexually in monogamous heterosexual couples — high sexual transmission points to HBV (or HCV in HIV+ MSM), not routine HCV.
4. IVDU — both HBV and HCV
Injection drug use is a shared risk factor: it is the LEADING risk factor for HCV in the developed world ('IVDU #1') and a significant route for HBV. Needle-sharing transmits blood-borne virus efficiently. Anyone with current or past IVDU warrants screening for HBV, HCV, and HIV.
⚠️ For HCV the single most common transmission route now is IVDU — not transfusion (which is now rare due to blood-supply screening since ~1992) and not perinatal.
5. Transfusion before 1992 / blood products
Blood transfusion before universal anti-HCV donor screening (US ~July 1992) is a classic HCV risk factor — the older patient who got a transfusion or clotting factors decades ago. After 1992, transfusion-transmitted HCV became extremely rare. Hemophiliacs treated before factor-concentrate viral inactivation are a high-risk historic cohort.
⚠️ A transfusion in 2010 is essentially zero HCV risk — the board's transfusion clue points to HCV only when it predates ~1992.
6. Perinatal / HBV mom
Perinatal (vertical) transmission is the dominant global route for HBV, especially in endemic Asia/Africa — risk is highest when mother is HBeAg-positive/high viral load. Infants of HBsAg+ mothers get HBIG + birth-dose vaccine. Perinatal HCV transmission occurs but is much less efficient (~5%), higher with maternal HIV co-infection.
⚠️ Vertical transmission causing chronic infection is mainly an HBV story (neonatal infection → ~90% chronicity). HCV vertical transmission is uncommon and there is no HCV vaccine or neonatal prophylaxis.
7. Prodrome / pre-icteric
The pre-icteric prodrome of acute viral hepatitis: 1–2 weeks of malaise, fatigue, anorexia, nausea, low-grade fever, and right-upper-quadrant discomfort. HBV characteristically can cause a serum-sickness-like syndrome (arthralgias, urticaria/rash) from circulating immune complexes BEFORE jaundice. RUQ pain and aversion to smoking/food are classic.
⚠️ Serum-sickness (arthritis + urticaria) preceding jaundice is a hallmark of acute HBV (immune-complex mediated) — not typically HAV or HCV.
8. Icteric phase
As bilirubin rises the patient becomes jaundiced with dark urine (conjugated bilirubinuria), pale/clay-colored stool, pruritus, and tender hepatomegaly. ALT/AST are markedly elevated (often >1000, ALT>AST in viral hepatitis). The icteric phase lasts days to weeks; in many adults (especially HCV and many HBV) infection is anicteric/subclinical.
⚠️ In viral hepatitis ALT > AST. An AST:ALT ratio >2:1 points instead to ALCOHOLIC hepatitis, not acute viral hepatitis.
9. Convalescent phase
Recovery phase: jaundice resolves, appetite and energy return, and aminotransferases trend down toward normal over weeks. The downsloping LFT curve marks resolution. Most acute HAV and adult HBV resolve fully; the key prognostic branch points are progression to chronicity (HBV/HCV) or, rarely, to fulminant failure.
⚠️ Symptom resolution does NOT equal viral clearance in HBV/HCV — you must document HBsAg loss / undetectable HCV RNA. Clinical recovery alone can hide progression to chronic infection.
10. Serology timing — anti-HBc
Diagnostic windows: in acute HBV, IgM anti-HBc is the marker during the window period when HBsAg may have fallen and anti-HBs not yet risen. For HCV, antibody appears weeks after exposure, so in very early/acute HCV the Ab can be NEGATIVE while RNA is already POSITIVE (Ab−/RNA+ = early acute). Ab+/RNA+ = current infection; Ab+/RNA− = cleared/resolved.
⚠️ In suspected acute HCV, a negative anti-HCV antibody does not rule it out — order HCV RNA, which turns positive within ~1–2 weeks, well before antibody.
11. >20,000 IU/mL threshold
HBV DNA level guides chronic-HBV treatment decisions: a DNA >20,000 IU/mL (HBeAg+) or >2,000 IU/mL (HBeAg−) with elevated ALT and/or fibrosis generally warrants antiviral therapy. High viral load also predicts long-term cirrhosis/HCC risk (REVEAL study) and perinatal transmission risk (treat mothers >200,000 IU/mL in third trimester).
⚠️ Don't treat every HBV-DNA-positive patient — the immune-tolerant phase (very high DNA but normal ALT, no fibrosis) is typically monitored, not treated. Treatment hinges on DNA level + ALT + fibrosis together.
12. Prognostic lab — PT/INR
The single best prognostic test in acute hepatitis/liver failure is the PROTHROMBIN TIME / INR — it reflects hepatic synthetic function (clotting-factor production) and rises early as the liver fails. A prolonged INR (≥1.5) plus encephalopathy defines acute liver failure. INR, not transaminase level, predicts outcome.
⚠️ The absolute height of ALT/AST does NOT predict prognosis — falling transaminases with a RISING INR/bilirubin and new encephalopathy signals massive hepatocyte loss (worsening), not improvement.
13. Acute liver failure — King's criteria markers
Fulminant hepatic failure = encephalopathy + coagulopathy (INR≥1.5) within ~8 weeks of onset, no prior liver disease. Red flags: hypoglycemia, rising bilirubin and creatinine, lactic acidosis, and worsening encephalopathy/cerebral edema. These feed the King's College criteria that trigger urgent transplant evaluation. HBV (esp. with HDV superinfection) and HAV/HEV can cause this.
⚠️ HEV in PREGNANT women carries a strikingly high fulminant-failure mortality (~20%); and acetaminophen is the leading drug cause — King's criteria differ for acetaminophen (pH, lactate) vs non-acetaminophen (INR, bilirubin, age) failure.
14. Creatinine — hepatorenal
Rising creatinine in acute liver failure signals hepatorenal syndrome or acute tubular necrosis and is a poor-prognostic marker incorporated into MELD and King's (non-acetaminophen) criteria. Renal dysfunction markedly worsens survival and strengthens the case for transplant listing.
⚠️ New AKI in fulminant hepatitis is an ominous prognostic sign, not an incidental finding — it raises transplant urgency rather than being managed expectantly.
