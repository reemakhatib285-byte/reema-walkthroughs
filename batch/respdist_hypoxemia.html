📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Alveolar gas equation
PAO2 = FiO2 × (Patm − PH2O) − PaCO2/R. On room air at sea level this simplifies to PAO2 ≈ 150 − PaCO2/0.8 (since 0.21 × (760 − 47) ≈ 150 and R ≈ 0.8). PAO2 is the calculated ALVEOLAR oxygen you subtract the measured arterial PaO2 from to get the A–a gradient.
⚠️ Forgetting to divide PaCO2 by R (≈0.8), or forgetting to subtract water vapor (47 mmHg), undercalculates expected PAO2 and misclassifies the A–a gradient.
2. PiO2 = 150
Inspired/alveolar PO2 ≈ 150 mmHg on room air at sea level. At ALTITUDE the barometric pressure falls, so inspired PO2 falls → hypoxemia with a NORMAL A–a gradient (the lung is fine; the air is thin). Supplemental O2 corrects it readily.
⚠️ High-altitude (and any low-FiO2) hypoxemia is NOT a shunt or diffusion problem — the A–a gradient stays NORMAL because both alveolar and arterial O2 fall together.
3. NM disease / clean filter
Neuromuscular weakness causes HYPOVENTILATION — the muscular 'pump' fails but the gas-exchange 'filter' (alveolar membrane) is intact, so the A–a gradient is NORMAL. PaCO2 rises and, because PAO2 = 150 − PaCO2/0.8, the rising CO2 mathematically lowers PAO2 and thus PaO2.
⚠️ Pure hypoventilation has a NORMAL A–a gradient — do not call it a shunt or fibrosis. The two normal-gradient causes of hypoxemia are hypoventilation and low FiO2/altitude.
4. Myasthenia gravis
Myasthenia gravis causes a restrictive/hypoventilatory pattern from respiratory muscle weakness; diagnose with anti-acetylcholine-receptor (anti-AChR) antibodies (and anti-MuSK if seronegative), plus repetitive nerve stimulation/single-fiber EMG.
⚠️ Methacholine challenge tests for ASTHMA (airway hyperresponsiveness), NOT for neuromuscular weakness — don't pick it to work up MG.
5. Supine FVC drop >25%
A fall in FVC of >25% (often cited >10–25%) from upright to supine indicates BILATERAL diaphragm weakness (the abdominal contents push the weak diaphragm up when lying flat). Seen in ALS, bilateral phrenic palsy, and advanced neuromuscular disease; presents as orthopnea.
⚠️ A large supine FVC drop points to diaphragmatic/neuromuscular disease, not parenchymal lung disease — it's the classic clue to bilateral diaphragm paralysis.
6. ALS patient
ALS leads to progressive respiratory muscle failure → pure hypoventilation: rising PaCO2 with a NORMAL A–a gradient. Respiratory failure (often nocturnal hypoventilation first) is the leading cause of death; treat with noninvasive ventilation (BiPAP).
⚠️ Hypercapnia + a NORMAL A–a gradient = a VENTILATION problem, not an oxygenation problem — the fix is supporting ventilation (NIV), and supplemental O2 alone can worsen hypercapnia.
7. A–a gradient calculator
A–a gradient = PAO2 − PaO2. Normal estimate ≈ (age/4) + 4, and it widens physiologically with age. It is the central tool that separates the NORMAL-gradient causes (hypoventilation, low FiO2/altitude) from the WIDENED-gradient causes (V/Q mismatch, shunt, diffusion limitation).
⚠️ Using a single fixed cutoff (e.g., '10') for every patient is wrong — the normal A–a gradient rises with age, so a value of 19 may be normal in an older adult.
8. A–a gradient gauges
Interpretation: a WIDENED A–a gradient = V/Q mismatch, right-to-left shunt, or diffusion defect (a problem in the lung between alveolus and capillary). A NORMAL A–a gradient = hypoventilation or low FiO2/altitude (the lung itself is fine).
⚠️ A widened gradient EXCLUDES pure hypoventilation/low-FiO2 as the sole cause — there must be intrinsic lung pathology contributing.
9. Normal A–a / pure hypoventilation
Hypoxemia + elevated PaCO2 + NORMAL A–a gradient = pure hypoventilation: CNS depression (opioids, sedatives, central hypoventilation), neuromuscular disease, or chest-wall disorders. Correcting ventilation corrects the hypoxemia.
⚠️ Don't attribute pure-hypoventilation hypoxemia to lung parenchyma — if the gradient is normal, the alveolar-capillary unit is working and the problem is ventilatory drive/mechanics.
10. Refractory shunt
A right-to-left SHUNT (blood bypasses ventilated alveoli) is the V/Q extreme of zero ventilation. Causes: ARDS, lobar pneumonia, atelectasis, pulmonary edema, AVMs, and intracardiac R→L shunts. It is REFRACTORY to supplemental O2 because shunted blood never contacts alveolar gas.
⚠️ Failure to correct hypoxemia with 100% O2 = SHUNT, NOT V/Q mismatch. This 100%-O2 test is the classic way to distinguish the two on the boards.
11. Responds to V:Q / O2
V/Q mismatch is the MOST COMMON cause of hypoxemia (COPD, asthma, PE, pneumonia, atelectasis). Because some ventilation reaches the affected units, raising FiO2 boosts the alveolar O2 in low-V/Q regions and CORRECTS the hypoxemia.
⚠️ If hypoxemia improves with supplemental O2, it is V/Q mismatch — true shunt would NOT correct. (V/Q mismatch is the default/most-common answer for unexplained hypoxemia.)
12. Surface area ↑→ DLCO ↑
DLCO is governed by alveolar-capillary SURFACE AREA, MEMBRANE thickness, and HEMOGLOBIN/pulmonary blood volume. More functioning surface area or more Hgb → higher DLCO; destroyed/thickened membrane or less Hgb → lower DLCO.
⚠️ Anemia falsely LOWERS DLCO; polycythemia and alveolar hemorrhage falsely RAISE it — always interpret DLCO after correcting for hemoglobin.
13. Fibrosis (membrane thick ↓DLCO)
ILD/fibrosis thickens and destroys the alveolar-capillary membrane → DIFFUSION limitation → low DLCO and a WIDENED A–a gradient. Diffusion-limited hypoxemia classically appears or worsens with exercise (less transit time for equilibration) and does respond to supplemental O2.
⚠️ Low DLCO + restriction = ILD/fibrosis, NOT obesity or neuromuscular disease (those keep a normal DLCO).
14. Vascular ↓DLCO / Pulm HTN
Isolated LOW DLCO with NORMAL spirometry and NORMAL lung volumes points to pulmonary VASCULAR disease — pulmonary arterial hypertension or chronic thromboembolic disease (loss of the capillary bed for CO uptake).
⚠️ Normal volumes + normal FEV1/FVC but an ISOLATED low DLCO = pulmonary vascular disease (PAH/CTEPH), not parenchymal or airway disease — a very high-yield pattern.
15. High DLCO causes
High DLCO causes: diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, polycythemia, left-to-right shunt / early CHF (increased pulmonary blood volume), asthma, exercise, and the supine position.
⚠️ A HIGH DLCO is a clue to alveolar hemorrhage or polycythemia — not a normal incidental finding to ignore; pair it with hemoptysis/anemia (hemorrhage) or elevated Hct (polycythemia).
