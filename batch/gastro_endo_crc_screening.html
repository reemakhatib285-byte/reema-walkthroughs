📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. AGE 45 gold-star scope
GOLD STAR: average-risk screening now starts at age 45 (lowered from 50), colonoscopy every 10 years if normal. Continue through 75; individualize 76–85; stop at 85. Colonoscopy is the gold standard because it visualizes the entire colon and removes polyps in one step.
2. FIT yearly
Fecal immunochemical test detects human globin, done yearly; any positive FIT mandates a full colonoscopy. Non-invasive but only a screen — does not remove polyps and misses lesions that aren't bleeding.
3. FIT-DNA 1–3 yr
Multitarget stool DNA (Cologuard) every 1–3 years: higher sensitivity than FIT but lower specificity (more false positives). A positive result requires colonoscopy.
4. CT colonography 5 yr
CT colonography every 5 years; non-invasive but requires bowel prep, and any significant finding still needs colonoscopy. Does not allow biopsy or polypectomy.
5. Sigmoidoscope LEFT ONLY (red X)
Flexible sigmoidoscopy reaches only the rectum and left colon (to splenic flexure) — it MISSES the right colon where many lesions and most serrated polyps occur. It is an acceptable screening modality but never 'clears the whole colon'.
⚠️ 'A flexible sigmoidoscopy clears/screens the whole colon' → wrong; it only reaches the left colon and misses right-sided cancers.
6. FAP grandmother — APC, start 10
Familial adenomatous polyposis (autosomal dominant, APC gene, hundreds–thousands of polyps): annual sigmoidoscopy/colonoscopy from age ~10–12. Near-100% cancer risk untreated → prophylactic colectomy. Genetic testing of at-risk relatives.
7. Lynch uncle — HNPCC, start 20–25
Lynch/HNPCC (MMR genes MLH1/MSH2/MSH6/PMS2, microsatellite instability): colonoscopy every 1–2 years starting age 20–25 (or 2–5 yr before youngest case). Genetic testing first. Right-sided cancers, plus endometrial/ovarian/urothelial risk.
8. One relative with CRC — start 40, q5
One first-degree relative with CRC (or advanced adenoma) → begin at age 40, OR 10 years before the relative's age at diagnosis, whichever is earlier; repeat every 5 years (not 10).
9. 1–2 small tubular adenomas — 7–10 yr
Post-polypectomy surveillance: 1–2 small (<10 mm) tubular adenomas, low-grade → repeat colonoscopy in 7–10 years. Normal-equivalent low-risk finding.
10. 3+ / advanced adenoma — 3 yr
High-risk polyps → repeat in 3 years: 3–10 adenomas, any adenoma ≥10 mm, villous histology, or high-grade dysplasia. Piecemeal resection of large sessile lesions or HGD warrants short-interval recheck at ~6 months.
11. Hyperplastic — 10 yr
Small distal hyperplastic polyps → 10-year interval (treated like a normal exam). Caveat: large (≥10 mm), proximal, or sessile serrated lesions carry malignant potential and follow adenoma-like shorter intervals.
12. IBD colitis surveillance — 8–10 yr
Chronic colitis (ulcerative colitis or Crohn colitis) → start dysplasia surveillance colonoscopy 8–10 years after symptom onset, then every 1–3 years with chromoendoscopy / multiple biopsies. PSC + IBD → begin surveillance at diagnosis, annually.
13. Stool test for surveillance (red X)
After a polyp is removed, surveillance must be COLONOSCOPY, not FIT/stool DNA. Stool tests only screen for bleeding lesions and cannot follow a known polyp-prone colon.
⚠️ 'Use a yearly FIT to follow a patient after polypectomy' → wrong; post-polypectomy surveillance requires colonoscopy, not a stool test.
