📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Antibiotics locked
Most asthma exacerbations are triggered by viral URIs, allergens, or non-adherence — not bacterial infection — so antibiotics are NOT routine. Reserve them for clear bacterial pneumonia (focal infiltrate + fever + purulent sputum) or proven bacterial sinusitis. Routine CXR is not needed for a typical exacerbation either; obtain it only if you suspect pneumonia, pneumothorax, or the patient fails to respond.
⚠️ Do not give empiric antibiotics for an asthma exacerbation without evidence of bacterial pneumonia — purulent-looking sputum in asthma is usually eosinophil-laden, not bacterial.
2. Silent chest / PEF <60% admit
A silent chest means airflow is too low to generate wheeze — a sign of life-threatening obstruction and impending arrest. Severity is graded by PEF: ≥50-75% predicted = moderate, 33-50% = acute severe, <33% (or silent chest, cyanosis, bradycardia, exhaustion, SpO2 <92%) = life-threatening. PEF/FEV1 <60% predicted that fails to improve warrants admission.
⚠️ Absence of wheeze is NOT reassuring — a quiet chest in a distressed asthmatic signals too little air movement to wheeze, i.e., near-fatal obstruction.
3. Firefighters at flames (inflamed lungs)
The acute attack is driven by bronchial smooth-muscle spasm, mucosal edema, and mucus plugging on a background of type-2 (eosinophilic) airway inflammation. The therapeutic strategy mirrors the firefight: bronchodilators (SABA ± ipratropium) relax muscle immediately while systemic steroids extinguish the underlying inflammation.
4. Normal/rising PCO2 = danger
Early in an attack the tachypneic patient blows off CO2 → respiratory ALKALOSIS with low PaCO2 (the expected ABG). As fatigue sets in, the PaCO2 climbs back toward 'normal' (40) and then high — this normalization/rise signals the patient can no longer sustain the work of breathing and is heading for respiratory failure. A rising PaCO2 with falling pH is an indication to consider intubation.
⚠️ A 'normal' PaCO2 in a tachypneic, distressed asthmatic is a red flag, not reassurance — it means fatigue and impending failure, not improvement.
5. Mg2+ sulfate cannon
IV magnesium sulfate (typically 2 g over 20 min) is a smooth-muscle relaxant used as adjunctive rescue for severe/life-threatening exacerbations not responding to first-line nebulized SABA + ipratropium + steroids. It modestly reduces hospital admissions in severe attacks.
⚠️ Magnesium is adjunct/rescue, not first-line — SABA, ipratropium, and systemic steroids come first; reaching for Mg before them is the wrong sequence.
6. Albuterol firehose (SABA)
Inhaled SABA (albuterol/salbutamol) is the first-line acute bronchodilator for every exacerbation — give early, repeated, and via nebulizer or MDI-spacer with continuous dosing in severe cases. It acts within minutes on β2 receptors to relax airway smooth muscle.
⚠️ Side effects of high-dose β2-agonist: tachycardia, tremor, hypokalemia, and lactic acidosis — don't misread the lactate or low K as a separate disease process.
7. Ipratropium hose (SAMA)
Add nebulized ipratropium (a SAMA/anticholinergic) to SABA in moderate-to-severe exacerbations — the combination produces greater bronchodilation and fewer admissions than SABA alone. It is an ED adjunct, not a maintenance controller.
⚠️ Ipratropium is for the acute attack only — it has no role in chronic asthma maintenance (that's tiotropium/LAMA at higher steps).
8. Intubation prevention
Maximal pharmacotherapy (continuous SABA, ipratropium, steroids, IV Mg ± heliox) aims to avoid intubation, which is high-risk in asthma. If ventilation is required, use a lung-protective, low-rate, low-tidal-volume strategy with prolonged expiratory time to allow full exhalation and permissive hypercapnia.
⚠️ Do not hyperventilate the intubated asthmatic — high respiratory rates prevent complete exhalation, causing breath-stacking, dynamic hyperinflation (auto-PEEP), hypotension, and barotrauma/pneumothorax.
9. Monitor / ventilator settings
Ventilator settings in severe asthma maximize expiratory time (low rate ~8-12, low tidal volume ~6 mL/kg, high inspiratory flow, minimal/zero applied PEEP) to combat air-trapping. Tolerate hypercapnia (pH down to ~7.2) rather than raising the rate. Sudden hypotension on the vent = check for auto-PEEP/tension pneumothorax (disconnect to exhale).
⚠️ If a ventilated asthmatic suddenly drops their blood pressure, the first move is to DISCONNECT the circuit to let trapped air escape (auto-PEEP) and rule out tension pneumothorax — not to give more fluids blindly.
10. Steroids 'I arrive late' (4-6 hrs)
Systemic corticosteroids (oral prednisone ~40-50 mg or IV methylprednisolone) reduce airway inflammation and relapse but take 4-6 hours to act — so give them EARLY in every moderate-to-severe attack rather than waiting. Oral and IV are equivalent in efficacy; a typical course is 5-7 days with no taper needed for short courses.
⚠️ Steroids are not bronchodilators and won't relieve symptoms immediately — but the delay is exactly why you start them at the first hour, not after bronchodilators fail.
11. Epinephrine in trash
IM epinephrine is the treatment for ANAPHYLAXIS, not routine asthma — for an ordinary exacerbation, inhaled SABA delivers selective β2 bronchodilation without epinephrine's systemic α/β1 effects. Epinephrine is reserved for asthma only when anaphylaxis is the trigger or as a last resort in peri-arrest.
⚠️ Do not reach for epinephrine in a routine asthma attack — but DO give it immediately if the attack is part of anaphylaxis (urticaria, angioedema, hypotension after an allergen).
12. Theophylline crossed out
Theophylline/aminophylline is largely abandoned in acute asthma — it adds no benefit over standard therapy and has a narrow therapeutic window with dangerous toxicity (arrhythmias, seizures, vomiting), worsened by interactions (macrolides, ciprofloxacin, cimetidine inhibit its metabolism).
⚠️ Theophylline is NOT recommended in acute exacerbations — and watch for toxicity precipitated by adding a macrolide or fluoroquinolone, which raises theophylline levels.
