📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Glucocorticoid excess king
Cushing SYNDROME = the clinical state of chronic glucocorticoid excess from ANY cause — this throned king is the unifying endpoint every branch in the court feeds into. The buffalo hump, moon face, and central obesity are the shared phenotype. The diagnostic task is two-step: (1) prove cortisol excess biochemically, then (2) find the source. Cushing DISEASE (the pituitary subtype) is just one route to the throne, not the throne itself.
⚠️ Cushing SYNDROME (the hormonal state, any cause) ≠ Cushing DISEASE (specifically a pituitary ACTH-secreting adenoma). Mixing these two terms is the classic board trap.
2. Exogenous steroids (#1)
Iatrogenic/EXOGENOUS glucocorticoids (prednisone, dexamethasone, inhaled/topical/injected steroids) are by far the #1 cause of Cushing syndrome OVERALL. Because the exogenous steroid suppresses CRH→ACTH, these patients have LOW ACTH and LOW endogenous cortisol, with bilaterally small/atrophic adrenals. They also lack hyperpigmentation (low ACTH) and often lack the androgenic features (low adrenal androgens). Always take a meticulous drug history first.
⚠️ The most common OVERALL cause is exogenous steroids, NOT a pituitary adenoma. Pituitary adenoma is only the most common cause of ENDOGENOUS Cushing. Read which one the stem is asking.
3. ACTH-dependent 90%
Of ENDOGENOUS Cushing, ~80-90% is ACTH-dependent — the adrenal is being driven by high or inappropriately normal ACTH. This causes bilateral adrenal hyperplasia. The two sources are pituitary (Cushing disease, the large majority) and ectopic ACTH. High ACTH also drives MC1R → hyperpigmentation, a clue absent in adrenal/exogenous causes.
⚠️ 'ACTH-dependent' refers to ENDOGENOUS cases only; don't apply the 90% figure to all Cushing. And high/normal ACTH still requires further testing (high-dose dex, CRH, IPSS) to separate pituitary from ectopic.
4. Pituitary princess (Cushing disease)
The pituitary corticotroph (ACTH-secreting) adenoma = Cushing DISEASE — the single most common cause of ENDOGENOUS Cushing (~70-80%), usually a small microadenoma, classically in a young woman (note F:M ~4:1). It causes bilateral adrenal hyperplasia. It retains partial feedback: SUPPRESSES with high-dose dexamethasone and RESPONDS to CRH. First-line treatment is transsphenoidal resection.
⚠️ Cushing DISEASE = pituitary source ONLY; it is not synonymous with Cushing syndrome. Also, the pituitary lesion is often a tiny microadenoma that MRI can MISS — a normal pituitary MRI does not exclude Cushing disease (use IPSS).
5. Ectopic schemer
Ectopic ACTH (~10-15% of endogenous) — a non-pituitary tumor secreting ACTH: small-cell lung carcinoma (most common, smoker), bronchial/thymic carcinoid, medullary thyroid carcinoma, pheochromocytoma, pancreatic NET. Presentation is often RAPID with very high ACTH and cortisol, severe hypertension, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis, weight loss, and prominent hyperpigmentation — frequently WITHOUT the classic slow Cushingoid habitus (no time to develop).
⚠️ Ectopic ACTH does NOT suppress with high-dose dexamethasone and does NOT respond to CRH (the opposite of pituitary disease) — but bronchial carcinoid is a notorious exception that can mimic pituitary behavior, which is exactly why IPSS exists to settle it.
6. Hypokalemic alkalosis
Mechanism of the electrolyte picture in severe (esp. ectopic) Cushing: normally renal 11β-HSD2 inactivates cortisol to cortisone, protecting the mineralocorticoid receptor. Massive cortisol SATURATES 11β-HSD2, so cortisol floods the mineralocorticoid receptor → Na/water retention, K and H wasting → HYPOKALEMIC METABOLIC ALKALOSIS and hypertension. This is most marked in ectopic ACTH because cortisol levels are the highest.
⚠️ This is the same mechanism by which licorice (glycyrrhetinic acid inhibits 11β-HSD2) mimics mineralocorticoid excess — 'apparent mineralocorticoid excess.' Hypokalemic alkalosis with high cortisol points to a severe/ectopic source rather than a mild pituitary one.
7. ACTH-independent 10%
~10-20% of endogenous Cushing is ACTH-INDEPENDENT — the adrenal makes cortisol autonomously, so pituitary ACTH is SUPPRESSED (low). Causes: adrenal adenoma (most common), adrenal carcinoma, and rare bilateral conditions (macronodular hyperplasia, PPNAD/Carney). Because ACTH is low, the contralateral and non-tumor adrenal tissue atrophies — a key reason to taper/replace steroids after unilateral adrenalectomy.
⚠️ Low ACTH = adrenal source — go straight to adrenal CT, do NOT do high-dose dex or CRH testing (those are for sorting ACTH-DEPENDENT cases). Ordering pituitary tests in a low-ACTH patient is a classic wasted-step trap.
8. Adenoma lady
Benign cortisol-secreting ADRENAL ADENOMA — the most common ACTH-INDEPENDENT cause. Typically a small (<4 cm), homogeneous, lipid-rich (<10 HU) unilateral mass secreting cortisol ALONE (pure phenotype, no virilization). Treatment is unilateral adrenalectomy; expect transient adrenal insufficiency afterward because the suppressed contralateral gland is atrophic and needs glucocorticoid cover with a taper.
⚠️ A solitary adenoma secretes ONE hormone (cortisol only); when you see MIXED cortisol + androgen excess or a large heterogeneous mass, switch your answer to adrenocortical CARCINOMA, not adenoma.
9. Carcinoma knight (ACC)
Adrenocortical CARCINOMA — rare (~1% of Cushing) but aggressive. Imaging red flags: size >4 cm, unenhanced attenuation >20 HU (lipid-poor), heterogeneity/necrosis/calcification, slow contrast washout. Biochemistry red flag: MIXED steroid excess — cortisol PLUS androgens (virilization, deep voice, hirsutism) ± mineralocorticoid. Rapid-onset Cushing + virilization + large mass = ACC; treat with complete resection ± mitotane.
⚠️ Don't be reassured that a mass is 'functioning' — function does not equal benign. Size >4 cm, high HU, heterogeneity, and mixed-hormone output distinguish ACC from a benign adenoma.
10. Rare bilateral twins
Rare ACTH-independent BILATERAL disease: (1) Primary Pigmented Nodular Adrenocortical Disease (PPNAD) — small pigmented nodules, part of CARNEY complex (PRKAR1A mutation; also myxomas, lentigines/spotty skin pigmentation, schwannomas), often young patients with a paradoxical RISE in cortisol on dexamethasone. (2) Bilateral Macronodular Adrenal Hyperplasia (BMAH/ARMC5) — large nodules, sometimes driven by aberrant GPCRs (food/GIP-dependent, vasopressin, etc.).
⚠️ Both are BILATERAL with LOW ACTH — so a low ACTH does not always mean a single unilateral adenoma; bilateral adrenal enlargement with suppressed ACTH should make you think macronodular hyperplasia or PPNAD/Carney.
11. Prove cortisol first scale
The workup ALGORITHM in one image: STEP 1 — confirm hypercortisolism with ≥2 first-line screens (1 mg overnight dexamethasone suppression, late-night salivary cortisol, or 24-h urine free cortisol). STEP 2 — only THEN measure plasma ACTH to localize. Step 0 (the 'EXCLUDE' tag) is always ruling out exogenous steroids first. Imaging comes LAST, after biochemistry tells you where to look.
⚠️ Never image the adrenals or pituitary BEFORE biochemical confirmation — incidentalomas are common (~5% of CTs) and will mislead you. Also never check ACTH before you have proven cortisol excess.
12. Clinical pearl ACTH rule
The master localizing rule: LOW (suppressed) ACTH → ACTH-INDEPENDENT → ADRENAL source → adrenal CT. HIGH/normal ACTH → ACTH-DEPENDENT → PITUITARY or ECTOPIC → then use high-dose dexamethasone + CRH stimulation (pituitary suppresses & responds; ectopic does neither) and IPSS to break ties. Mnemonic: 'Low ACTH = look Low (adrenal); High ACTH = look High (pituitary) or hidden (ectopic).'
⚠️ Inappropriately NORMAL ACTH in a patient with proven cortisol excess counts as ACTH-DEPENDENT, not normal — autonomous adrenal disease should have FULLY suppressed it. Treat a non-suppressed ACTH as 'high.'
