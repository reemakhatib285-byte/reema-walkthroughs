📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Zenker diverticulum (pouch, old food)
Zenker diverticulum = a pulsion (false) outpouching through Killian's triangle above a dysfunctional cricopharyngeus muscle. Older patients. Food collects in the pouch and is regurgitated later. Caused by cricopharyngeal incoordination/high pressure.
2. Halitosis / regurgitation
Classic Zenker tetrad: halitosis (rotting retained food), regurgitation of undigested old food, dysphagia, and a sensation of neck fullness/gurgling. Stagnant food explains the bad breath, a key exam clue.
3. Aspiration / coughing
Regurgitated retained food can be aspirated, causing recurrent aspiration pneumonia and nocturnal cough, especially when supine. Aspiration is the major morbidity of an untreated Zenker.
4. Barium swallow = correct test (gold star)
Diagnose Zenker with a BARIUM esophagram (video-fluoroscopic swallow), which safely outlines the posterior pouch. It is the correct first test. Treatment of symptomatic Zenker is cricopharyngeal myotomy +/- diverticulectomy (open or endoscopic).
5. Endoscopy / capsule first (RED X)
Blind passage of an NG tube, endoscope, or video capsule FIRST in suspected Zenker risks intubating and PERFORATING the pouch. Get the barium study first; if endoscopy is later needed it is done carefully/under direct vision.
⚠️ 'endoscopy or video capsule first in suspected Zenker' -> wrong; blind passage can perforate the pouch; barium swallow first.
6. Pill esophagitis = deep focal ulcer
Pill (medication-induced) esophagitis: a pill lodges and erodes the mucosa, causing a deep, focal/localized ulcer, typically at the mid-esophagus (aortic arch/LA impression). Presents with sudden retrosternal odynophagia and chest pain.
7. Sudden odynophagia
Hallmark history: abrupt onset of severe odynophagia/retrosternal pain shortly after taking a pill, often with too little water or while supine/at bedtime. Usually self-limited once the offending habit is corrected.
8. Offenders: KCl, doxy, bisphosphonate, NSAID, iron
Common culprits: potassium chloride, tetracyclines/doxycycline, bisphosphonates (alendronate), NSAIDs, iron, ascorbic acid, quinidine. Each is caustic when lodged. Recognize the drug list on a stem with sudden odynophagia.
9. Habit card: pills upright with water (gold star)
Prevention = take pills with a full glass of water and remain UPRIGHT (sitting/standing) for >=30 min, avoid taking at bedtime. This is the management/prevention answer; the esophagitis itself heals with supportive care once the habit changes.
10. Candida = white plaques (fluconazole)
Candida esophagitis: linear adherent white plaques; common with immunosuppression/HIV (and inhaled steroids). Often with oral thrush. Treat with systemic fluconazole. Most common infectious esophagitis.
11. HSV = small punched-out ulcers (acyclovir)
HSV esophagitis: multiple small, shallow, 'punched-out' (volcano) ulcers; biopsy from ulcer EDGE shows Cowdry A inclusions/multinucleated giant cells. Treat with acyclovir. Seen in immunocompromised (occasionally immunocompetent).
12. CMV = large/deep linear ulcers (ganciclovir, gold star)
CMV esophagitis: large, deep, linear/serpiginous ulcers in advanced immunosuppression (HIV with CD4 <50, transplant); biopsy from ulcer BASE shows intranuclear 'owl-eye' inclusions. Treat with ganciclovir (or foscarnet/valganciclovir).
13. Low CD4 context
Infectious esophagitis flags immunosuppression: candida and HSV at moderate immunodeficiency, CMV at very low CD4 (<50). Odynophagia in an HIV patient -> think infectious esophagitis; CD4 count helps predict the organism.
14. HBV as a cause (RED X)
Hepatitis B virus does NOT cause esophagitis; the viral causes of infectious esophagitis are HSV, CMV and VZV (plus fungal Candida). HBV on an esophagitis stem is a distractor.
⚠️ 'HBV as a cause of esophagitis' -> wrong; viral esophagitis is HSV/CMV/VZV (and Candida); HBV is not a cause.
