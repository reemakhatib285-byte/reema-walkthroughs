📖 Read-through — all 17 symbols
1. Graves' = diffuse high uptake
Graves' disease is the most common cause of hyperthyroidism (esp. women 20–40) and shows DIFFUSE, homogeneously increased radioactive iodine uptake (RAIU) on scan — the whole gland is autonomously stimulated. The uptake scan separates high-uptake causes (Graves', toxic adenoma, toxic MNG) from low-uptake causes (thyroiditis, exogenous/iodine).
⚠️ Expecting patchy or focal uptake in Graves' — wrong; Graves' is DIFFUSE. Patchy = toxic MNG, single hot focus = toxic adenoma.
2. TRAb antibody
Graves' is driven by stimulating TSH-receptor antibodies (TRAb, also called TSI) that bind and activate the TSH receptor, causing autonomous hormone production and the diffuse uptake. TRAb is the diagnostic and the pathogenic antibody; it crosses the placenta and can cause neonatal thyrotoxicosis. Mechanism mnemonic: TRAb = TSH-Receptor Antibody = 'Turbo' on the receptor.
⚠️ Naming anti-TPO as the diagnostic antibody for Graves' — wrong; anti-TPO is the Hashimoto's antibody (and is often also positive in Graves' but is not what defines it). TRAb/TSI is the Graves'-specific stimulating antibody.
3. Graves' ophthalmopathy
Graves' orbitopathy (exophthalmos/proptosis, diplopia, periorbital edema) is an autoimmune process: TRAb-activated fibroblasts deposit glycosaminoglycans and fat in the orbit — it is SPECIFIC to Graves' and can progress independently of thyroid status. (Lid lag and stare from sympathetic overactivity are non-specific and occur in any thyrotoxicosis.) Smoking worsens it; severe disease is treated with glucocorticoids/orbital radiation.
⚠️ Attributing true infiltrative ophthalmopathy (proptosis) to a toxic nodule or MNG — wrong; only Graves' causes orbitopathy. Conversely, do not call non-specific lid lag/stare 'Graves' eye disease.'
4. Pretibial myxedema
Pretibial myxedema (thyroid dermopathy) — non-pitting, waxy, indurated plaques over the shins from dermal glycosaminoglycan deposition — is essentially pathognomonic for Graves' and, like orbitopathy and acropachy, is an extrathyroidal autoimmune manifestation. It is uncommon but high-yield because it confirms Graves'.
⚠️ Confusing 'pretibial myxedema' (a Graves'/HYPERthyroid skin sign) with generalized myxedema of HYPOthyroidism — opposite diseases, similar word.
5. Toxic adenoma
A toxic adenoma is a single autonomously functioning nodule, usually from a somatic activating (gain-of-function) mutation in the TSH receptor (or Gsα). On scan it is a solitary HOT focus with suppressed uptake in the surrounding normal gland. Treatment: RAI or surgery (lobectomy).
⚠️ Expecting diffuse uptake in a toxic adenoma — wrong; it is focal/single hot nodule. No autoimmune eye/skin disease (TRAb negative).
6. Toxic MNG
Toxic multinodular goiter develops in OLDER patients with a long-standing goiter; multiple autonomous nodules give a PATCHY ('hot and cold') uptake pattern. No autoimmune features (no orbitopathy/dermopathy, TRAb negative). Often unmasked by an iodine load (Jod-Basedow). Treatment: RAI or surgery.
⚠️ Looking for ophthalmopathy in toxic MNG — wrong; eye disease is Graves'-specific. Patchy uptake + elderly + no eye signs = toxic MNG, not Graves'.
7. T3 toxicosis
T3 toxicosis: TSH is suppressed but free T4 is normal — you must check FREE T3, which is elevated. It occurs early in Graves' or toxic nodular disease, where preferential T3 secretion precedes T4 elevation. Confirms thyrotoxicosis that a T4-only panel would miss.
⚠️ Excluding hyperthyroidism because free T4 is normal — wrong; with a suppressed TSH and normal T4 you must measure free T3 to catch T3 toxicosis.
8. Gestational / hCG
hCG shares an alpha-subunit with TSH and weakly cross-stimulates the TSH receptor; very high hCG (hyperemesis gravidarum, multiple/molar pregnancy, choriocarcinoma) causes transient gestational thyrotoxicosis. It is usually self-limited and resolves as hCG falls; molar pregnancy/trophoblastic disease needs gynecologic management.
⚠️ Treating gestational/trophoblastic thyrotoxicosis with antithyroid drugs — wrong; it is hCG-driven and transient (manage the underlying pregnancy/molar disease, supportive beta-blockers if needed). RAI is absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy.
9. Jod-Basedow
Jod-Basedow phenomenon: an iodine load (CT contrast, amiodarone, iodine supplementation) triggers hyperthyroidism in a gland with pre-existing autonomous nodules (toxic MNG/adenoma) — the autonomous tissue uses the substrate to overproduce hormone. It is the mirror image of the Wolff-Chaikoff effect (iodine load transiently SUPPRESSES a normal gland).
⚠️ Confusing Jod-Basedow (iodine → HYPERthyroid in autonomous gland) with Wolff-Chaikoff (iodine → transient HYPOthyroid in normal gland) — opposite responses to the same iodine load.
10. McCune-Albright
McCune-Albright syndrome: a postzygotic activating GNAS (Gsα) mutation causes constitutive cAMP signaling → autonomous endocrine hyperfunction (hyperthyroidism, precocious puberty, acromegaly), polyostotic fibrous dysplasia, and café-au-lait macules (irregular 'coast of Maine' borders).
⚠️ Overlooking the classic triad (fibrous dysplasia + café-au-lait + precocious puberty/autonomous endocrinopathy) and missing the Gsα mechanism shared with toxic adenoma.
11. Apathetic thyrotoxicosis
In the elderly, hyperthyroidism can present 'apathetically' — fatigue, depression, weight loss, and cardiac signs (atrial fibrillation, heart failure) rather than the classic hyperkinetic agitation/tremor of younger patients. A blunted, withdrawn presentation does not exclude thyrotoxicosis.
⚠️ Excluding hyperthyroidism because an older patient is lethargic/withdrawn rather than agitated — wrong; check TSH in any elderly patient with new AFib, unexplained weight loss, or heart failure.
12. AFib in elderly
New-onset atrial fibrillation should prompt thyroid testing — hyperthyroidism is a reversible, classic cause (excess thyroid hormone increases beta-adrenergic sensitivity and automaticity). Hyperthyroidism also raises the thromboembolic and rate-control challenge of AFib.
⚠️ Anticoagulating/rate-controlling new AFib without checking TSH — wrong; missing thyrotoxicosis as the reversible driver.
13. ↑ Warfarin & digoxin effect
Hyperthyroidism accelerates metabolism of clotting factors (potentiating warfarin → may need a LOWER warfarin dose) and increases digoxin clearance/volume of distribution while raising arrhythmia risk — so doses of warfarin and digoxin must be re-evaluated as thyroid status changes. (When the patient becomes euthyroid, requirements rise again.)
⚠️ Forgetting that thyroid status alters warfarin and digoxin requirements — failing to recheck INR/levels as the patient is treated toward euthyroidism.
14. Weight loss + ↑appetite
Hypermetabolism causes weight loss DESPITE an increased appetite — a hallmark that distinguishes thyrotoxicosis from most other causes of weight loss (where appetite falls). Accompanied by heat intolerance, sweating, tremor, palpitations, and hyperdefecation.
⚠️ Attributing weight loss with preserved/increased appetite to malignancy or diabetes without checking TSH — the appetite-up/weight-down combination is a thyrotoxicosis flag.
15. Thyroid bruit
An audible thyroid bruit (and palpable thrill) reflects the markedly increased vascularity of the hyperstimulated Graves' gland — a physical-exam sign specific to Graves' hyperthyroidism, not seen in nodular disease or thyroiditis.
⚠️ Dismissing a thyroid bruit as a transmitted carotid sound — a true thyroid bruit points specifically to Graves' hypervascularity.
16. Hyperdefecation / diarrhea
Increased gut motility from thyroid hormone excess causes hyperdefecation/diarrhea — part of the hypermetabolic syndrome along with heat intolerance and weight loss. (In hypothyroidism the opposite — constipation — occurs.)
⚠️ Reading 'diarrhea' in a thyrotoxic patient as primary GI disease and missing the hypermetabolic picture.
17. Palmar erythema / warm moist skin
Peripheral vasodilation from thyroid hormone excess produces warm, moist, velvety skin with palmar erythema — a peripheral sign of the hyperdynamic, high-output circulatory state of thyrotoxicosis.
⚠️ Equating palmar erythema only with chronic liver disease — in a hyperthyroid context it reflects the hyperdynamic circulation, not necessarily cirrhosis.
