📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Infection deaths
Infection is the LEADING cause of death in CLL (~30–50% of deaths), driven by hypogammaglobulinemia, T/NK-cell dysfunction, complement deficiency, neutropenia, and immunosuppressive therapy. Encapsulated bacteria (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae), plus opportunistic and viral reactivations.
⚠️ Immune compromise in CLL is intrinsic to the disease and present even in untreated, early-stage patients — it is not solely caused by chemotherapy.
2. Hypogammaglobulinemia
Hypogammaglobulinemia (low IgG/IgA/IgM) is common and progressive, causing recurrent sinopulmonary infections with encapsulated organisms. Replacement IVIG/SCIG is indicated when IgG is low (typically <500 mg/dL) AND there are recurrent serious/hospitalizing bacterial infections — not for low IgG alone.
⚠️ Give IVIG only when low IgG is paired with recurrent serious infections; asymptomatic hypogammaglobulinemia is not by itself an indication.
3. Vaccines before tx
Give inactivated/recombinant vaccines EARLY, ideally BEFORE starting therapy (especially anti-CD20 like rituximab/obinutuzumab, which abolish vaccine responses for months): annual influenza, COVID-19, Shingrix (recombinant zoster), RSV, and pneumococcal (PCV20 or PCV15→PPSV23).
⚠️ NO LIVE/attenuated vaccines in CLL (note the crossed-out LIVE VACCINES) — e.g., avoid live zoster (Zostavax), MMR, live nasal flu, yellow fever. Shingrix is recombinant and IS allowed.
4. Pneumococcal response
Vaccine responses are blunted in CLL (e.g., pneumococcal seroconversion ~58% vs ~100% in healthy controls), worsened by anti-CD20 therapy — reinforcing the rule to vaccinate as early as possible and before B-cell–depleting agents.
5. AIHA
Autoimmune hemolytic anemia is the most common autoimmune complication of CLL: WARM-type, IgG-mediated, with positive direct antiglobulin test (DAT/Coombs), high LDH/indirect bilirubin, low haptoglobin, and reticulocytosis.
⚠️ AIHA-driven anemia ≠ Rai stage III marrow failure — autoimmune cytopenia does NOT upstage Rai. The anemia is peripheral destruction, not marrow infiltration.
6. AIHA treatment
First-line AIHA in CLL = CORTICOSTEROIDS, with IVIG and/or rituximab added for severe/refractory cases — this is the autoimmune-directed pathway, distinct from treating the CLL bulk. CLL-directed therapy is reserved for refractory autoimmune disease or concurrent treatment-requiring CLL.
⚠️ Treat the autoimmune process first with steroids — do NOT jump to CLL chemoimmunotherapy for isolated AIHA/ITP. (Avoid fludarabine, which can trigger/worsen AIHA.)
7. Coombs positive
A positive direct antiglobulin test (DAT/Coombs) confirms immune-mediated (warm IgG) hemolysis and distinguishes autoimmune anemia from marrow-replacement anemia — the key test that determines management and staging implications.
8. ITP + TPO agonist
Immune thrombocytopenia in CLL = isolated low platelets with normal/increased marrow megakaryocytes; treat first-line with corticosteroids ± IVIG ± rituximab. TPO receptor agonists (eltrombopag/romiplostim) are options for refractory cases.
⚠️ Like AIHA, autoimmune ITP does NOT upstage Rai to stage IV — distinguish immune destruction from marrow-failure thrombocytopenia.
9. Autoimmune ≠ progression
The scene's banner: autoimmune cytopenias are IMMUNE-MEDIATED, not a marker of CLL tumor progression, and are managed separately (steroids/rituximab/IVIG) from CLL bulk reduction. Distinguishing autoimmune from marrow-failure cytopenia changes both staging and treatment.
⚠️ Do not treat AIHA/ITP as Rai stage III/IV requiring CLL chemoimmunotherapy — that misclassifies prognosis and selects the wrong therapy.
10. Richter transformation
Richter transformation = CLL/SLL evolving into an aggressive large B-cell lymphoma (most commonly DLBCL; rarely Hodgkin variant), incidence ~0.5%/yr (cumulative 2–10%). Higher risk with unmutated IGHV, TP53/NOTCH1 abnormalities, and complex karyotype.
⚠️ Sudden, asymmetric, RAPIDLY enlarging single node + B symptoms + markedly elevated LDH + hypercalcemia = suspect Richter; you must BIOPSY it to confirm.
11. PET hot spot
PET-CT localizes the most metabolically active node to target for EXCISIONAL biopsy when Richter is suspected: SUVmax >5 (especially ≥10) raises suspicion and guides where to biopsy; PET cannot replace biopsy for diagnosis.
⚠️ SUVmax <5 makes Richter unlikely (consistent with CLL); SUVmax >5 → biopsy the hottest node. PET directs the biopsy, it does not diagnose Richter by itself.
12. Clonal relation/prognosis
Prognosis hinges on clonal relationship to the original CLL: clonally RELATED Richter (≈80% of cases) is chemoresistant with dismal median survival ~6–16 months; clonally UNRELATED Richter behaves like de novo DLBCL with far better outcomes (multi-year survival).
13. 3x skin cancer
CLL substantially increases second-malignancy risk via immune surveillance failure — skin cancers especially (squamous cell, basal cell, and aggressive Merkel cell), up to 8–15× the general population. Requires diligent sun protection and at least yearly dermatologic skin exams.
⚠️ Don't dismiss new/changing skin lesions in CLL — aggressive SCC and Merkel cell carcinoma are markedly overrepresented and can metastasize.
