📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. 3 tiers
Hyperkalemia management has three sequential jobs: (1) STABILIZE the myocardial membrane (calcium), (2) SHIFT K+ into cells temporarily (insulin/glucose, albuterol, +/- bicarb), and (3) REMOVE K+ from the body (diuretics, binders, dialysis). Only the third job lowers total body potassium; the first two buy time. Severity (ECG changes, K+ level) dictates how aggressively you move through the tiers.
⚠️ WRONG answer: jumping straight to insulin or kayexalate when the ECG shows peaked T waves. Discriminator: with ECG changes you must give CALCIUM first to protect the heart before anything else — shifting and removing K+ take minutes to hours and won't prevent imminent arrhythmia.
2. Tier 1: stabilize
Tier 1 (membrane stabilization) is indicated when there are ECG changes (peaked T waves, then PR prolongation/widened QRS, loss of P waves, sine wave) OR when K+ is severe (commonly K+ >= 6.5). Calcium raises the threshold potential, restoring the gap between resting and threshold membrane potential that hyperkalemia had narrowed.
⚠️ WRONG answer: waiting for a 'high enough' number before treating a patient with ECG changes. Discriminator: ANY ECG manifestation of hyperkalemia is an emergency warranting immediate calcium regardless of the exact K+ value — the rhythm, not just the number, drives urgency.
3. IV calcium gluconate
IV calcium gluconate (typically 10 mL of 10% over 2–3 min, repeat in 5 min if ECG unchanged) is the FIRST drug given for hyperkalemia with ECG changes. It antagonizes the membrane effects of hyperkalemia within 1–3 minutes; the effect lasts only ~30–60 minutes, so it must be paired with shifting and removal.
⚠️ WRONG answer: believing calcium lowers the serum potassium. Discriminator: calcium does NOT change the K+ level at all — it only stabilizes the myocardium. A learner who gives calcium and stops has failed to treat the hyperkalemia; you still must shift and remove.
4. Central line for CaCl
Calcium chloride contains about 3x more elemental calcium than calcium gluconate (≈270 mg vs ≈90 mg per 10 mL ampule), so it is preferred in cardiac arrest/peri-arrest, but it is highly caustic and should be given through a CENTRAL line to avoid severe tissue necrosis on extravasation. Calcium gluconate is safer peripherally for routine use.
⚠️ WRONG answer: pushing calcium chloride through a small peripheral IV in a stable ward patient. Discriminator: extravasation causes severe local necrosis — use calcium gluconate peripherally; reserve CaCl for central access or a true arrest situation.
5. Digoxin caution
When hyperkalemia coexists with digoxin toxicity, give calcium cautiously and SLOWLY (over 20–30 min) because of the theoretical 'stone heart' — calcium added to digoxin-induced intracellular calcium overload may precipitate refractory contraction/arrhythmia. The definitive treatment is digoxin-specific antibody (Fab) fragments, which also correct the hyperkalemia.
⚠️ WRONG answer: rapidly bolusing IV calcium in a digoxin-toxic patient with hyperkalemia. Discriminator: classic teaching is to avoid/give calcium very cautiously here and prioritize digoxin-immune Fab — the hyperkalemia reflects pump poisoning that Fab reverses at its source.
6. Tier 2: shift
Tier 2 drives K+ from the extracellular space into cells via the Na/K-ATPase. It works in minutes and lasts a few hours, but total body K+ is UNCHANGED — K+ will leak back out as the agents wear off. Shifting is a bridge to definitive removal, not a cure, especially in anuric/ESRD patients who cannot excrete the load.
⚠️ WRONG answer: declaring the patient treated after the K+ drops with insulin/albuterol. Discriminator: shifting is temporary and total body K+ is the same — in a patient who cannot excrete K+ (oliguric AKI, ESRD), you MUST proceed to removal (dialysis) or the K+ will rebound.
7. Insulin + glucose
Regular insulin 10 units IV with 25 g of dextrose (one ampule of D50) is the fastest, most reliable shifting therapy; insulin activates the Na/K-ATPase to drive K+ into cells, lowering serum K+ by ~0.5–1.2 mEq/L within 15–30 minutes for several hours. Glucose is co-administered to prevent iatrogenic hypoglycemia.
⚠️ WRONG answer: omitting the glucose 'because the patient got insulin.' Discriminator: hypoglycemia is a common, sometimes severe complication 30–60 min later — always give dextrose (and monitor glucose), EXCEPT when the patient is already significantly hyperglycemic (e.g., glucose > 250), in which case give insulin alone and recheck.
8. Albuterol
High-dose nebulized albuterol (a beta-2 agonist, typically 10–20 mg — about 4–8x the bronchodilator dose) stimulates the Na/K-ATPase and shifts K+ into cells, lowering serum K+ by ~0.5–1.0 mEq/L. Its effect is ADDITIVE to insulin, making the two a standard combined shifting regimen.
⚠️ WRONG answer: relying on albuterol as monotherapy. Discriminator: up to ~40% of patients (especially those on beta-blockers) are poor responders, and the doses needed cause tachycardia — use it as an adjunct to insulin/glucose, not a substitute.
9. Bicarbonate
Sodium bicarbonate raises pH, which can shift K+ into cells in exchange for H+, but it is slow and weak and only meaningfully helps when a metabolic acidosis is present. It is most useful as an infusion in acidotic CKD patients rather than as an acute bolus.
⚠️ WRONG answer: giving a bicarbonate bolus as first-line for isolated, non-acidotic hyperkalemia. Discriminator: bicarbonate is NOT first-line and is largely ineffective without acidosis; in ESRD it risks volume overload and hypocalcemia/tetany and does not reliably lower K+ acutely.
10. Tier 3: remove
Tier 3 is the only category that lowers TOTAL BODY potassium: enhance renal excretion (loop diuretic), pull K+ out via the gut (binders), or extract it directly (dialysis). Every hyperkalemia patient ultimately needs a removal strategy once the membrane is stabilized and K+ is temporarily shifted.
⚠️ WRONG answer: treating with stabilize + shift only and discharging without a removal plan. Discriminator: without removal, shifted K+ returns within hours — match the removal modality to the cause (diuretic if kidneys work, dialysis if they don't, binders for chronic control).
11. Dialysis
Hemodialysis is the most effective and definitive removal method and is the treatment of choice for severe or refractory hyperkalemia, ESRD, or oliguric AKI. It can remove large amounts of K+ rapidly; note that K+ can rebound after a session as it redistributes from cells, so recheck levels.
⚠️ WRONG answer: delaying dialysis in an anuric ESRD patient while cycling more insulin and albuterol. Discriminator: if the kidneys cannot excrete K+, shifting only buys time — dialysis is the definitive answer and should be arranged early in severe/refractory cases.
12. Loop diuretic
Loop diuretics (e.g., furosemide) increase distal Na+ delivery and urine flow, enhancing renal K+ excretion — but they REQUIRE functioning kidneys and adequate volume status to work. They are useful in volume-overloaded patients with preserved renal function and as adjuncts in chronic management.
⚠️ WRONG answer: ordering furosemide to fix hyperkalemia in an anuric/ESRD patient. Discriminator: no urine output means no diuretic-mediated K+ loss — in advanced renal failure the loop diuretic is ineffective and dialysis is required instead.
13. K+ binders
GI cation exchangers bind K+ in the gut and remove it in stool, lowering total body K+: patiromer and sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC) are newer agents, while sodium polystyrene sulfonate (SPS/kayexalate) is the older one. SZC has the fastest onset of the binders (~1 hour) and is useful for both acute and chronic control; patiromer and SPS are slower (better for subacute/chronic).
⚠️ WRONG answer: relying on kayexalate for EMERGENCY hyperkalemia. Discriminator: SPS works slowly (hours) and carries a risk of colonic/intestinal NECROSIS (especially with sorbitol, post-op, or bowel disease) — it is not a substitute for calcium/insulin in a crisis; patiromer and SZC are newer and generally safer for ongoing management.
14. Stop offending drugs
For the stable outpatient, the foundation of management is removing contributors: stop or reduce ACEi/ARBs, potassium-sparing diuretics (spironolactone/eplerenone/amiloride), NSAIDs, and trimethoprim; restrict dietary potassium; and counsel patients to AVOID salt substitutes (which are potassium chloride). Chronic binders (patiromer/SZC) can allow continuation of needed RAAS therapy in heart failure/CKD.
⚠️ WRONG answer: telling a hyperkalemic patient to use a 'low-sodium' salt substitute for blood pressure. Discriminator: salt substitutes are KCl and are a hidden, common cause of outpatient hyperkalemia — review the med and diet list, including OTC supplements, before escalating therapy.
