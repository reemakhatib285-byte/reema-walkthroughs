⚠️ Board traps 12
  1. Activation recap — WRONG ANSWER: 'Order 1,25-vitamin D to assess for vitamin D deficiency.' Discriminator: measure 25-OH-D for STATUS — calcitriol (1,25-D) has a short half-life and is tightly regulated, so it can be normal or even HIGH in early deficiency (driven up by secondary PTH). 1,25-D testing is reserved for specific situations (suspected CKD, VDDR, granulomatous disease, oncogenic osteomalacia).
  2. Nutritional deficiency bed — WRONG ANSWER: 'High PTH means a parathyroid adenoma (primary hyperparathyroidism).' Discriminator: in vitamin D deficiency the high PTH is SECONDARY and APPROPRIATE — accompanied by LOW/normal calcium and LOW 25-OH-D. Primary HPT has HIGH calcium with high PTH. The calcium level and the low 25-OH-D distinguish secondary (deficiency) from primary HPT.
  3. VDDR type 1 panel — WRONG ANSWER: 'Treat VDDR1 with high-dose ergocalciferol/cholecalciferol.' Discriminator: the enzyme that ACTIVATES vitamin D (1-alpha-hydroxylase) is missing, so plain vitamin D can't be converted — give CALCITRIOL (or alfacalcidol). The clue is normal/high 25-D but LOW 1,25-D.
  4. VDDR type 2 / VDR resistance — WRONG ANSWER: 'High 1,25-D rules out a vitamin D problem — the patient is fine.' Discriminator: in VDDR2 calcitriol is HIGH but the VDR can't respond (receptor defect), so giving more vitamin D fails. ALOPECIA is the key clue distinguishing VDDR2 (high 1,25-D + alopecia) from VDDR1 (low 1,25-D, no alopecia).
  5. Vitamin D intoxication — WRONG ANSWER: 'High calcium with low PTH must be malignancy.' Discriminator: vitamin D intoxication raises BOTH Ca and PO4 (with low PTH and very high 25-D), whereas humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy (PTHrP) gives high Ca with LOW phosphate. The phosphate direction plus a sky-high 25-OH-D points to toxicity. Note it also differs from primary HPT (high Ca, LOW PO4, high PTH).
  6. Granulomatous / lymphoma — WRONG ANSWER: 'Treat sarcoid hypercalcemia with more vitamin D or with bisphosphonates as first-line.' Discriminator: this is 1,25-D-mediated hypercalcemia with SUPPRESSED PTH — give GLUCOCORTICOIDS (which shut off macrophage 1-alpha-hydroxylase) and AVOID vitamin D/sun. Giving vitamin D worsens it. High 1,25-D with low PTH and low 25-D-needs points to extra-renal activation.
  7. Extra-renal activation tag — WRONG ANSWER: 'Calcitriol production is always tightly controlled, so 1,25-D can't be the cause of hypercalcemia.' Discriminator: extra-renal (macrophage) 1-alpha-hydroxylase escapes feedback regulation, so 1,25-D rises unchecked — measure 1,25-D (HIGH) with suppressed PTH to identify granulomatous/lymphoma-driven hypercalcemia.
  8. Treat with calcitriol note — WRONG ANSWER: 'Plain vitamin D supplementation is sufficient in CKD and VDDR1.' Discriminator: when renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase is missing/impaired (CKD, VDDR1), plain vitamin D can't be activated — give CALCITRIOL or an active analog (paricalcitol in CKD to also help suppress secondary PTH).
  9. VDR resistance / end-organ — WRONG ANSWER: 'Just give calcitriol — it always corrects vitamin D-dependent rickets.' Discriminator: in VDDR2 the receptor can't respond, so calcitriol alone (which works for VDDR1) is frequently INSUFFICIENT — high-dose calcium (sometimes IV) is required. Alopecia and HIGH 1,25-D flag VDDR2 over VDDR1.
  10. CKD station — WRONG ANSWER: 'CKD hypocalcemia is best treated with plain vitamin D alone.' Discriminator: CKD loses the activating enzyme, so plain D can't be converted to calcitriol — use ACTIVE vitamin D (calcitriol/paricalcitol) and control phosphate first (giving active D with high phosphate risks raising the Ca×PO4 product and calcification).
  11. One-look pattern map — WRONG ANSWER: 'You can diagnose calcium/vitamin D disorders from calcium alone.' Discriminator: the diagnosis comes from the COMBINATION — always pair serum calcium with PTH first, then add phosphate and the vitamin D metabolites. The same calcium can mean opposite diseases depending on whether PTH is high or suppressed and whether phosphate is high or low.
  12. Secondary HPT in CKD — WRONG ANSWER: 'High PTH with hypercalcemia in a CKD patient is primary hyperparathyroidism.' Discriminator: in long-standing CKD this is usually TERTIARY HPT (autonomous glands after years of secondary stimulation) — high PTH, now high Ca, in the renal-failure context. Secondary HPT (the earlier stage) has high PTH with LOW/normal calcium. The calcium level and CKD history separate secondary, tertiary, and primary.
📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Activation recap
Vitamin D activation is a two-hydroxylation relay. Skin (UVB on 7-dehydrocholesterol) and diet supply D3 (cholecalciferol) and D2 (ergocalciferol). The LIVER performs 25-hydroxylation (CYP2R1) → 25-OH-D (calcidiol), the STORAGE form and the best marker of vitamin D status (it has the long half-life, ~2–3 weeks, and large pool). The KIDNEY performs 1-alpha-hydroxylation (CYP27B1) → 1,25-(OH)2-D (calcitriol), the ACTIVE form. 1-alpha-hydroxylase is stimulated by PTH and low phosphate, and inhibited by FGF23, high calcium, and calcitriol itself. Mnemonic: 'Liver gives the 25 status; Kidney activates to 1,25.'
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Order 1,25-vitamin D to assess for vitamin D deficiency.' Discriminator: measure 25-OH-D for STATUS — calcitriol (1,25-D) has a short half-life and is tightly regulated, so it can be normal or even HIGH in early deficiency (driven up by secondary PTH). 1,25-D testing is reserved for specific situations (suspected CKD, VDDR, granulomatous disease, oncogenic osteomalacia).
2. Nutritional deficiency bed
Nutritional vitamin D deficiency (low sun exposure, low intake, malabsorption, housebound elderly): low 25-OH-D leads to reduced intestinal Ca and PO4 absorption → low/low-normal serum Ca and PO4 → compensatory secondary hyperparathyroidism (HIGH PTH), which raises calcitriol toward normal and elevates ALP (osteoblast activity from osteomalacia). Deficiency threshold: 25-OH-D <20 ng/mL is deficient, 20–30 insufficient. Treat with cholecalciferol (D3) repletion.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'High PTH means a parathyroid adenoma (primary hyperparathyroidism).' Discriminator: in vitamin D deficiency the high PTH is SECONDARY and APPROPRIATE — accompanied by LOW/normal calcium and LOW 25-OH-D. Primary HPT has HIGH calcium with high PTH. The calcium level and the low 25-OH-D distinguish secondary (deficiency) from primary HPT.
3. VDDR type 1 panel
Vitamin D-dependent rickets type 1 (VDDR1, '1A') is an autosomal recessive deficiency of renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase (CYP27B1). The activation step fails: 25-OH-D is normal/HIGH but 1,25-D (calcitriol) is LOW. Result: hypocalcemia, secondary hyperPTH, hypophosphatemia, and rickets in infancy. Treatment is CALCITRIOL (active vitamin D) — bypassing the missing enzyme; plain vitamin D doesn't work.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Treat VDDR1 with high-dose ergocalciferol/cholecalciferol.' Discriminator: the enzyme that ACTIVATES vitamin D (1-alpha-hydroxylase) is missing, so plain vitamin D can't be converted — give CALCITRIOL (or alfacalcidol). The clue is normal/high 25-D but LOW 1,25-D.
4. VDDR type 2 / VDR resistance
Vitamin D-dependent rickets type 2 (VDDR2, hereditary vitamin D-resistant rickets) is an autosomal recessive END-ORGAN resistance from a mutated vitamin D receptor (VDR). Calcitriol is made fine — in fact 1,25-D is HIGH (compensatory) — but tissues can't respond. Result: hypocalcemia, secondary hyperPTH, rickets, and characteristic ALOPECIA (often total). Treatment: very high-dose calcitriol and calcium; many require IV/high-dose oral calcium because the receptor defect can't be overcome.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'High 1,25-D rules out a vitamin D problem — the patient is fine.' Discriminator: in VDDR2 calcitriol is HIGH but the VDR can't respond (receptor defect), so giving more vitamin D fails. ALOPECIA is the key clue distinguishing VDDR2 (high 1,25-D + alopecia) from VDDR1 (low 1,25-D, no alopecia).
5. Vitamin D intoxication
Vitamin D toxicity (excess supplements, megadosing): markedly HIGH 25-OH-D (often >100 ng/mL) saturates vitamin D-binding protein, freeing vitamin D metabolites that drive intestinal Ca AND PO4 absorption → HYPERcalcemia and HYPERphosphatemia with appropriately SUPPRESSED (low) PTH. Can cause nephrocalcinosis, AKI, and metastatic calcification. Treatment: stop vitamin D, hydration, and glucocorticoids if severe.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'High calcium with low PTH must be malignancy.' Discriminator: vitamin D intoxication raises BOTH Ca and PO4 (with low PTH and very high 25-D), whereas humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy (PTHrP) gives high Ca with LOW phosphate. The phosphate direction plus a sky-high 25-OH-D points to toxicity. Note it also differs from primary HPT (high Ca, LOW PO4, high PTH).
6. Granulomatous / lymphoma
Granulomatous diseases (sarcoidosis, TB, histoplasmosis, berylliosis) and some lymphomas cause hypercalcemia because activated MACROPHAGES express 1-alpha-hydroxylase EXTRA-RENALLY, converting 25-D → 1,25-D unchecked by normal feedback. Labs: HIGH 1,25-D, hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, SUPPRESSED PTH, and often elevated ACE (sarcoid). Treatment is GLUCOCORTICOIDS (inhibit macrophage 1-alpha-hydroxylase) plus sun/vitamin D avoidance.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Treat sarcoid hypercalcemia with more vitamin D or with bisphosphonates as first-line.' Discriminator: this is 1,25-D-mediated hypercalcemia with SUPPRESSED PTH — give GLUCOCORTICOIDS (which shut off macrophage 1-alpha-hydroxylase) and AVOID vitamin D/sun. Giving vitamin D worsens it. High 1,25-D with low PTH and low 25-D-needs points to extra-renal activation.
7. Extra-renal activation tag
Extra-renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase (in granuloma macrophages and some lymphoma cells) is NOT subject to the normal negative feedback that restrains the renal enzyme — it is not suppressed by high calcium or by calcitriol and is not driven by PTH. This unregulated calcitriol production is why granulomatous hypercalcemia is PTH-independent and corticosteroid-responsive.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Calcitriol production is always tightly controlled, so 1,25-D can't be the cause of hypercalcemia.' Discriminator: extra-renal (macrophage) 1-alpha-hydroxylase escapes feedback regulation, so 1,25-D rises unchecked — measure 1,25-D (HIGH) with suppressed PTH to identify granulomatous/lymphoma-driven hypercalcemia.
8. Treat with calcitriol note
Conditions where the kidney's activation step is impaired require ACTIVE vitamin D (calcitriol or analogs like alfacalcidol/paricalcitol), not plain D2/D3: VDDR1 (absent 1-alpha-hydroxylase) and CKD (lost renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase). Giving plain cholecalciferol can't help if the activating enzyme is gone — you must supply the already-activated hormone.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Plain vitamin D supplementation is sufficient in CKD and VDDR1.' Discriminator: when renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase is missing/impaired (CKD, VDDR1), plain vitamin D can't be activated — give CALCITRIOL or an active analog (paricalcitol in CKD to also help suppress secondary PTH).
9. VDR resistance / end-organ
VDDR2 is end-organ (post-receptor) resistance: the vitamin D receptor itself is defective, so even abundant calcitriol can't signal. Supplementation alone often fails; management relies on very high-dose calcitriol and high-dose oral or IV calcium to force calcium balance independent of the receptor. Severity correlates with the degree of receptor dysfunction.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'Just give calcitriol — it always corrects vitamin D-dependent rickets.' Discriminator: in VDDR2 the receptor can't respond, so calcitriol alone (which works for VDDR1) is frequently INSUFFICIENT — high-dose calcium (sometimes IV) is required. Alopecia and HIGH 1,25-D flag VDDR2 over VDDR1.
10. CKD station
CKD impairs vitamin D in two ways: loss of functional renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase (low calcitriol) and phosphate retention that raises FGF23 (which further suppresses 1-alpha-hydroxylase). Low calcitriol + hyperphosphatemia + hypocalcemia all drive secondary hyperparathyroidism. Management: phosphate binders, active vitamin D analogs (paricalcitol/calcitriol), and calcimimetics (cinacalcet) to control PTH.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'CKD hypocalcemia is best treated with plain vitamin D alone.' Discriminator: CKD loses the activating enzyme, so plain D can't be converted to calcitriol — use ACTIVE vitamin D (calcitriol/paricalcitol) and control phosphate first (giving active D with high phosphate risks raising the Ca×PO4 product and calcification).
11. One-look pattern map
The whole ward reduces to pattern recognition across 25-D, 1,25-D, Ca, PO4, and PTH. Anchors: Primary HPT = high Ca, low PO4, high PTH. Secondary HPT (deficiency/CKD) = low/normal Ca, high PTH, low 25-D or low calcitriol. Vitamin D toxicity = high Ca, high PO4, low PTH, very high 25-D. Granulomatous = high Ca, high 1,25-D, low PTH. PTHrP malignancy = high Ca, low PO4, low PTH, low PTHrP-positive. VDDR1 = low 1,25-D; VDDR2 = high 1,25-D + alopecia.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'You can diagnose calcium/vitamin D disorders from calcium alone.' Discriminator: the diagnosis comes from the COMBINATION — always pair serum calcium with PTH first, then add phosphate and the vitamin D metabolites. The same calcium can mean opposite diseases depending on whether PTH is high or suppressed and whether phosphate is high or low.
12. Secondary HPT in CKD
Secondary hyperparathyroidism in CKD: chronically low calcitriol, phosphate retention, and hypocalcemia continuously stimulate the parathyroids → HIGH PTH with LOW/normal calcium. Over years the glands hypertrophy and can become autonomous (tertiary HPT: persistently HIGH PTH now with HIGH calcium, often after transplant). Treatment escalates from binders/active D/calcimimetics to parathyroidectomy in refractory tertiary disease.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'High PTH with hypercalcemia in a CKD patient is primary hyperparathyroidism.' Discriminator: in long-standing CKD this is usually TERTIARY HPT (autonomous glands after years of secondary stimulation) — high PTH, now high Ca, in the renal-failure context. Secondary HPT (the earlier stage) has high PTH with LOW/normal calcium. The calcium level and CKD history separate secondary, tertiary, and primary.
