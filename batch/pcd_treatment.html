⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. IMiDs class — WRONG answer: 'IMiDs and proteasome inhibitors are interchangeable / share the same mechanism, so failing one predicts failing the other.' Different mechanisms (cereblon/IKZF degradation vs. proteasome blockade) — that's exactly why frontline regimens COMBINE them (e.g., VRd). Discriminator: lenalidomide-refractory disease still responds to a proteasome inhibitor or pomalidomide; do not assume cross-resistance.
  2. IMiD = VTE risk — WRONG answer: 'IMiDs cause cytopenias and bleeding, so anticoagulation/aspirin is contraindicated.' The dominant vascular hazard is THROMBOSIS, not bleeding — omitting prophylaxis is the classic error. Discriminator: risk-stratify and choose ASA (low risk) vs LMWH/DOAC (high risk); thrombocytopenia <50k may modify but does not eliminate the need to consider prophylaxis.
  3. Thalidomide teratogen — WRONG answer: 'only thalidomide is teratogenic; lenalidomide and pomalidomide are safe in pregnancy.' ALL IMiDs are teratogenic and all require the REMS/pregnancy-prevention program. Discriminator: never start any IMiD in a person of childbearing potential without documented contraception and negative pregnancy testing.
  4. Lenalidomide neuropathy-sparing — WRONG answer: 'new peripheral neuropathy in a patient on lenalidomide maintenance is from the lenalidomide.' Lenalidomide rarely causes neuropathy — suspect THALIDOMIDE or BORTEZOMIB instead, or another cause. Discriminator: lenalidomide's signature problems are cytopenias and VTE; stocking-glove neuropathy points to thalidomide/bortezomib.
  5. Proteasome inhibitors — WRONG answer: 'all proteasome inhibitors share the same toxicity profile.' They differ critically: bortezomib → peripheral neuropathy; carfilzomib → cardiotoxicity (spares nerves); ixazomib → GI/thrombocytopenia. Discriminator: match the adverse effect to the agent — neuropathy = bortezomib, heart failure/HTN = carfilzomib, oral convenience = ixazomib.
  6. Bortezomib neuropathy — WRONG answer: 'a patient starting bortezomib doesn't need antiviral prophylaxis.' PIs (especially bortezomib) markedly raise herpes-zoster reactivation risk — acyclovir/valacyclovir prophylaxis is standard. Discriminator: bortezomib = neuropathy + zoster risk (give SC weekly + acyclovir); carfilzomib substitutes cardiotoxicity for the neuropathy.
  7. Anti-CD38 — WRONG answer: 'an unexpected positive antibody screen / interference on the M-protein assay after starting daratumumab is a transfusion alloantibody or treatment failure.' Daratumumab binds CD38 on reagent RBCs → FALSE-positive indirect Coombs/antibody screen (mitigate with DTT-treated cells), and it migrates as IgG-kappa on immunofixation, mimicking/masking the M-spike. Discriminator: phenotype/genotype the patient BEFORE starting; use daratumumab-specific assays (e.g., DIRA) to interpret the M-protein.
  8. Transplant-eligible induction — WRONG answer: 'transplant-eligible patients should receive high-dose alkylator (e.g., melphalan) DURING induction.' Melphalan is AVOIDED in induction for transplant candidates because it damages the stem-cell pool and impairs later collection — it is reserved for the transplant conditioning step. Discriminator: PI+IMiD+steroid (±anti-CD38) for induction; melphalan only at conditioning.
  9. Age/fitness triage — WRONG answer: 'this 71-year-old is automatically ineligible for autologous transplant because of age.' Age ALONE does not exclude transplant — assess functional status and comorbidities. Discriminator: a fit older adult can proceed to ASCT, whereas significant frailty/organ dysfunction is the true disqualifier.
  10. Maintenance — WRONG answer: 'maintenance therapy is given for a fixed short course after transplant.' Standard lenalidomide maintenance is CONTINUOUS until progression (or intolerance), and this duration is what drives the survival benefit. Discriminator: stopping maintenance early forfeits the PFS/OS advantage; high-risk cytogenetics warrant intensified (PI-containing) maintenance.
  11. HDT + ASCT 'the bomb' — WRONG answer: 'autologous transplant is CURATIVE for multiple myeloma.' ASCT is NOT curative — it deepens response and extends remission but the disease invariably relapses; that is why maintenance follows. Discriminator: myeloma remains incurable with standard therapy; ASCT improves depth/duration of response, not cure.
  12. Stem-cell rescue — WRONG answer: 'the reinfused stem cells provide a graft-versus-myeloma immune effect.' Autologous cells are the patient's OWN — there is NO graft-versus-tumor and no GVHD; they simply restore hematopoiesis. Discriminator: graft-versus-myeloma/GVHD belong to ALLOGENEIC transplant, which is rarely used in myeloma.
  13. Allogeneic rarely used — WRONG answer: 'allogeneic transplant is the standard consolidative therapy for newly diagnosed myeloma.' Standard consolidation is AUTOLOGOUS transplant; allo is not standard of care due to prohibitive TRM/GVHD. Discriminator: 'autologous' = standard in eligible patients; 'allogeneic' = investigational/last-resort in myeloma.
  14. Tandem/early vs delayed — WRONG answer: 'every myeloma patient should receive tandem (double) autologous transplant for best outcomes.' Discriminator: reserve tandem for del(17p)/t(4;14)-type high-risk disease, and always collect stem cells early regardless of transplant timing.
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. IMiDs class
Immunomodulatory drugs (IMiDs) — Thalidomide, lenalidomide (Revlimid), Pomalidomide — are a core myeloma backbone. They bind CEREBLON, redirecting the E3 ubiquitin ligase to degrade transcription factors IKZF1/IKZF3 (Ikaros/Aiolos), killing plasma cells AND stimulating T/NK cells. Lenalidomide is the workhorse, especially for MAINTENANCE. Mnemonic: T-R-P shields = the three IMiDs; cereblon is the lock they fit.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'IMiDs and proteasome inhibitors are interchangeable / share the same mechanism, so failing one predicts failing the other.' Different mechanisms (cereblon/IKZF degradation vs. proteasome blockade) — that's exactly why frontline regimens COMBINE them (e.g., VRd). Discriminator: lenalidomide-refractory disease still responds to a proteasome inhibitor or pomalidomide; do not assume cross-resistance.
2. IMiD = VTE risk
All IMiDs (thalidomide, lenalidomide, pomalidomide) substantially raise VENOUS THROMBOEMBOLISM risk, amplified when combined with high-dose dexamethasone or erythropoiesis-stimulating agents. Every patient needs thromboprophylaxis: aspirin for low-risk patients, and LMWH or a DOAC (e.g., apixaban) for high-risk patients (prior VTE, immobility, high-risk regimen). Mnemonic: the DVT skull warns you to add prophylaxis before starting an IMiD.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'IMiDs cause cytopenias and bleeding, so anticoagulation/aspirin is contraindicated.' The dominant vascular hazard is THROMBOSIS, not bleeding — omitting prophylaxis is the classic error. Discriminator: risk-stratify and choose ASA (low risk) vs LMWH/DOAC (high risk); thrombocytopenia <50k may modify but does not eliminate the need to consider prophylaxis.
3. Thalidomide teratogen
Thalidomide is the infamous teratogen causing phocomelia (limb-shortening); lenalidomide and pomalidomide are likewise strictly teratogenic. All are dispensed under a mandatory REMS pregnancy-prevention program requiring two reliable contraception methods, regular pregnancy testing, and counseling. Thalidomide additionally causes sedation, constipation, and peripheral neuropathy. Mnemonic: the teratogen warning sign is nailed right next to thalidomide.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'only thalidomide is teratogenic; lenalidomide and pomalidomide are safe in pregnancy.' ALL IMiDs are teratogenic and all require the REMS/pregnancy-prevention program. Discriminator: never start any IMiD in a person of childbearing potential without documented contraception and negative pregnancy testing.
4. Lenalidomide neuropathy-sparing
Lenalidomide causes LITTLE peripheral neuropathy (unlike thalidomide and the PI bortezomib) — its main toxicities are MYELOSUPPRESSION (neutropenia, thrombocytopenia), VTE, diarrhea, rash, and a small increased risk of second primary malignancies with prolonged use. This favorable neuro profile makes it ideal for long-term maintenance. Mnemonic: lenalidomide spares the nerves but hits the marrow.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'new peripheral neuropathy in a patient on lenalidomide maintenance is from the lenalidomide.' Lenalidomide rarely causes neuropathy — suspect THALIDOMIDE or BORTEZOMIB instead, or another cause. Discriminator: lenalidomide's signature problems are cytopenias and VTE; stocking-glove neuropathy points to thalidomide/bortezomib.
5. Proteasome inhibitors
Proteasome inhibitors (PIs) — Bortezomib (reversible, IV/SC), Carfilzomib (irreversible, IV), Ixazomib (reversible, ORAL) — block the 26S proteasome's chymotryptic activity, so misfolded/ubiquitinated proteins accumulate and trigger plasma-cell apoptosis (plasma cells are exquisitely sensitive due to heavy immunoglobulin synthesis). Bortezomib is the prototype INDUCTION drug. Mnemonic: the hammer rack smashes the proteasome 'garbage disposal.'
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'all proteasome inhibitors share the same toxicity profile.' They differ critically: bortezomib → peripheral neuropathy; carfilzomib → cardiotoxicity (spares nerves); ixazomib → GI/thrombocytopenia. Discriminator: match the adverse effect to the agent — neuropathy = bortezomib, heart failure/HTN = carfilzomib, oral convenience = ixazomib.
6. Bortezomib neuropathy
Bortezomib's dose-limiting toxicity is painful PERIPHERAL NEUROPATHY; giving it SUBCUTANEOUS and WEEKLY (rather than IV twice-weekly) markedly reduces neuropathy without losing efficacy. It also reactivates VARICELLA-ZOSTER, so antiviral prophylaxis (acyclovir/valacyclovir) is required. Mnemonic: SC-weekly bortezomib + acyclovir shield = fewer nerves damaged, no shingles.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'a patient starting bortezomib doesn't need antiviral prophylaxis.' PIs (especially bortezomib) markedly raise herpes-zoster reactivation risk — acyclovir/valacyclovir prophylaxis is standard. Discriminator: bortezomib = neuropathy + zoster risk (give SC weekly + acyclovir); carfilzomib substitutes cardiotoxicity for the neuropathy.
7. Anti-CD38
Anti-CD38 monoclonal antibodies — daratumumab (SC preferred over IV) and isatuximab — target CD38 on plasma cells (ADCC/ADCP/CDC/direct apoptosis) and are now in NEARLY EVERY regimen, including frontline QUADRUPLET therapy (Dara-VRd / Dara-RVd). Premedicate to limit infusion reactions; expect neutropenia and infection risk. Mnemonic: CD38 rockets are now part of almost every launch.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'an unexpected positive antibody screen / interference on the M-protein assay after starting daratumumab is a transfusion alloantibody or treatment failure.' Daratumumab binds CD38 on reagent RBCs → FALSE-positive indirect Coombs/antibody screen (mitigate with DTT-treated cells), and it migrates as IgG-kappa on immunofixation, mimicking/masking the M-spike. Discriminator: phenotype/genotype the patient BEFORE starting; use daratumumab-specific assays (e.g., DIRA) to interpret the M-protein.
8. Transplant-eligible induction
Fit, transplant-ELIGIBLE patients get a 3- or 4-drug INDUCTION combining a PI + IMiD + steroid ± anti-CD38 — e.g., VRd (bortezomib-lenalidomide-dexamethasone) or quadruplet Dara-VRd, sometimes KRd — for roughly 4 cycles, then stem-cell collection, then autologous transplant. Combining classes attacks via complementary mechanisms for deeper response. Mnemonic: the transplant-eligible booth stocks VRd/Dara-VRd quad regimens.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'transplant-eligible patients should receive high-dose alkylator (e.g., melphalan) DURING induction.' Melphalan is AVOIDED in induction for transplant candidates because it damages the stem-cell pool and impairs later collection — it is reserved for the transplant conditioning step. Discriminator: PI+IMiD+steroid (±anti-CD38) for induction; melphalan only at conditioning.
9. Age/fitness triage
Transplant eligibility is determined by PERFORMANCE STATUS, frailty, and comorbidity burden (cardiac, pulmonary, renal, hepatic) — NOT a rigid chronologic age cutoff. Many fit patients in their early 70s undergo ASCT; conversely a frail 60-year-old may not be a candidate. Mnemonic: the triage officer weighs fitness and comorbidities, not just the birth date.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'this 71-year-old is automatically ineligible for autologous transplant because of age.' Age ALONE does not exclude transplant — assess functional status and comorbidities. Discriminator: a fit older adult can proceed to ASCT, whereas significant frailty/organ dysfunction is the true disqualifier.
10. Maintenance
After induction (and after transplant in eligible patients), continuous LENALIDOMIDE maintenance until progression improves both PFS and overall survival; high-risk patients often get a PI-containing maintenance (e.g., add bortezomib/ixazomib). Continue thromboprophylaxis and monitor counts and for second primary malignancies. Mnemonic: the maintenance chamber keeps lenalidomide running long-term.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'maintenance therapy is given for a fixed short course after transplant.' Standard lenalidomide maintenance is CONTINUOUS until progression (or intolerance), and this duration is what drives the survival benefit. Discriminator: stopping maintenance early forfeits the PFS/OS advantage; high-risk cytogenetics warrant intensified (PI-containing) maintenance.
11. HDT + ASCT 'the bomb'
Autologous stem-cell transplant = high-dose MELPHALAN (200 mg/m2) conditioning ('the bomb') to maximally cytoreduce, followed by rescue with the patient's own previously collected/cryopreserved stem cells. It deepens response (raises CR rate) and prolongs remission/PFS in eligible patients. Mnemonic: the melphalan bomb levels the marrow before the rescue.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'autologous transplant is CURATIVE for multiple myeloma.' ASCT is NOT curative — it deepens response and extends remission but the disease invariably relapses; that is why maintenance follows. Discriminator: myeloma remains incurable with standard therapy; ASCT improves depth/duration of response, not cure.
12. Stem-cell rescue
After melphalan conditioning, the cryopreserved AUTOLOGOUS hematopoietic stem cells are reinfused to rescue the ablated marrow and restore counts (engraftment over ~10–14 days). This rescue is what allows the otherwise marrow-lethal melphalan dose; it deepens CR by roughly 25–40%. Mnemonic: the stored stem cells parachute back down to rebuild the marrow.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'the reinfused stem cells provide a graft-versus-myeloma immune effect.' Autologous cells are the patient's OWN — there is NO graft-versus-tumor and no GVHD; they simply restore hematopoiesis. Discriminator: graft-versus-myeloma/GVHD belong to ALLOGENEIC transplant, which is rarely used in myeloma.
13. Allogeneic rarely used
Allogeneic transplant offers a potential graft-versus-myeloma effect but carries high treatment-related mortality and GVHD, so it is RARELY used — reserved for select young, very-high-risk or relapsed patients, ideally on a trial. Mnemonic: the allogeneic door is crossed out — 'high toxicity, rarely used.'
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'allogeneic transplant is the standard consolidative therapy for newly diagnosed myeloma.' Standard consolidation is AUTOLOGOUS transplant; allo is not standard of care due to prohibitive TRM/GVHD. Discriminator: 'autologous' = standard in eligible patients; 'allogeneic' = investigational/last-resort in myeloma.
14. Tandem/early vs delayed
Stem cells are COLLECTED EARLY (before extended therapy compromises the pool) even if the transplant itself is DELAYED — early vs delayed ASCT show similar overall survival, so timing can be individualized. TANDEM (two sequential autologous transplants) benefits mainly HIGH-RISK cytogenetic patients, not standard-risk. Mnemonic: collect now, transplant whenever; tandem only for the high-risk crowd.
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'every myeloma patient should receive tandem (double) autologous transplant for best outcomes.' Tandem ASCT benefits only HIGH-RISK cytogenetics; for standard-risk it adds toxicity without clear survival gain. Discriminator: reserve tandem for del(17p)/t(4;14)-type high-risk disease, and always collect stem cells early regardless of transplant timing.
