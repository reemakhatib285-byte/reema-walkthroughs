📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Minor bleeding — withhold doses
For MINOR/non-life-threatening bleeding on a DOAC the first move is simply to HOLD the next dose(s) and apply local hemostatic measures. DOACs have short half-lives (≈8–17 h), so anticoagulant effect falls quickly once dosing stops — usually no reversal agent is needed. Supportive care, mechanical compression and treating the bleeding source are sufficient.
⚠️ Don't reach for andexanet/idarucizumab or PCC for trivial or mucosal bleeding — reversal agents are reserved for LIFE-THREATENING or critical-site bleeding. Over-reversing a stable patient adds thrombotic risk for no benefit.
2. Recent overdose <4 h — activated charcoal
If a DOAC was INGESTED within the last 2–4 hours (overdose or accidental excess), ORAL ACTIVATED CHARCOAL can reduce further GI absorption of the drug. This is an early, time-limited intervention aimed at lowering peak drug levels before they are fully absorbed.
⚠️ Charcoal only helps for RECENT ingestion (≈<2–4 h). Past that window the drug is already absorbed and charcoal is useless — at that point management depends on bleeding severity and reversal agents, not decontamination.
3. >24-hour rule — find the cause
Once it is >24 hours since the last dose, most of a normally-cleared DOAC is gone, so persistent bleeding or a markedly abnormal coagulation test should prompt you to FIND ANOTHER CAUSE (renal failure causing drug accumulation, liver disease, a structural bleeding lesion, or concurrent antiplatelet/NSAID use) rather than blaming residual drug.
⚠️ After 24 h in a patient with normal renal function, don't assume ongoing 'DOAC effect' — look for accumulation (low CrCl) or an anatomic/second cause. Renal impairment is the classic reason a DOAC effect persists far longer than expected.
4. Dabigatran → idarucizumab
Dabigatran is a direct THROMBIN (factor IIa) inhibitor. Its specific antidote is IDARUCIZUMAB, a humanized monoclonal antibody FRAGMENT (Fab) that binds dabigatran with very high affinity and neutralizes it almost immediately (within minutes). Use it for life-threatening bleeding or before urgent/emergency surgery. Dabigatran is heavily RENALLY cleared, so accumulates in renal failure (and is partly dialyzable).
⚠️ Idarucizumab reverses ONLY dabigatran — it does NOT work on the -xabans (apixaban/rivaroxaban). Don't give andexanet for dabigatran; match the antidote to the mechanism (IIa inhibitor → idarucizumab, Xa inhibitor → andexanet).
5. Gator = dabigatran clue
The recurring GATOR is the visual hook for dabi-GATRAN (the direct thrombin inhibitor). It anchors the rule that this is the ONE oral DOAC with an antibody antidote (idarucizumab) and the one most dependent on the kidney for clearance.
⚠️ Keep the gator (dabigatran, anti-IIa) separate from the -xaban factor-Xa inhibitors — they have different antidotes (idarucizumab vs andexanet) and different monitoring assays.
6. Renal hold / extra days
Because dabigatran is ~80% renally cleared, patients with reduced CrCl need the drug HELD for EXTRA DAYS before surgery (its half-life lengthens markedly as renal function falls). The perioperative strip at the bottom shows holding 1–2 days for normal renal function and longer for impaired clearance or high-bleeding-risk procedures.
⚠️ A 'standard' 24–48 h hold is NOT enough in renal impairment for dabigatran — failure to extend the hold based on CrCl is a classic perioperative bleeding error.
7. Urgent surgery on the table
For EMERGENCY/urgent surgery in an anticoagulated patient who cannot wait for natural drug clearance, give the specific reversal agent first: idarucizumab for dabigatran, andexanet alfa (or 4-factor PCC if andexanet unavailable) for a -xaban. The goal is rapid restoration of hemostasis so the operation can proceed safely.
⚠️ Don't simply transfuse FFP hoping to 'reverse' a DOAC — FFP does not meaningfully reverse direct oral anticoagulants. Use the targeted antidote or PCC.
8. Xa inhibitor reversal — andexanet alfa
Apixaban and rivaroxaban (the -xabans) are direct FACTOR Xa inhibitors. Their specific antidote is ANDEXANET ALFA — a recombinant, catalytically INACTIVE decoy factor Xa that acts as a high-affinity bait: it binds and sequesters the Xa inhibitor (and TFPI), freeing real factor Xa to resume clotting. Used for life-threatening Xa-inhibitor bleeding (e.g., intracranial hemorrhage).
⚠️ Andexanet is a DECOY (inactive Xa), not active Xa — and it reverses only Xa inhibitors, not dabigatran. It is expensive, often non-stocked, and carries a thrombotic-risk signal, so 4F-PCC is a common alternative.
9. Heparin hunter / avoid bypass
Caution note: andexanet alfa also binds and partly neutralizes HEPARIN, so after andexanet, heparin may not work — a major problem if the patient then needs cardiopulmonary BYPASS (which depends on heparin anticoagulation). Plan reversal strategy with the surgical/anesthesia team if bypass is anticipated; consider PCC instead in that setting.
⚠️ Giving andexanet to a patient who will imminently need heparin for bypass can leave you unable to anticoagulate them for the pump — a real, tested pitfall.
10. 4F-PCC alternative
FOUR-FACTOR PROTHROMBIN COMPLEX CONCENTRATE (4F-PCC, ~25–50 units/kg) contains factors II, VII, IX, X and is the FIRST-LINE ALTERNATIVE for Xa-inhibitor-associated life-threatening bleeding when andexanet is unavailable, too costly, or contraindicated. It bypasses the inhibited factor by flooding the system with clotting factors.
⚠️ PCC does not specifically 'neutralize' the DOAC — it overwhelms the block by supplying downstream factors. It is also the go-to when andexanet would interfere with subsequently needed heparin.
11. aPCC (FEIBA) — second alt
ACTIVATED PCC (aPCC / FEIBA, ~50 units/kg) is a further fallback for DOAC-associated bleeding when 4F-PCC and andexanet are not options. It contains activated factors and bypasses the inhibited step, but carries a higher thrombotic risk, so it sits lower on the algorithm.
⚠️ aPCC is a later-line choice, not first-line — its higher prothrombotic potential means it is reserved for refractory cases.
12. rFVIIa — last resort
Recombinant activated factor VIIa is a last-resort hemostatic agent occasionally considered in catastrophic, refractory bleeding. Evidence for DOAC reversal is weak and thrombotic risk is significant, so it is essentially the bottom of the shelf.
⚠️ rFVIIa is NOT a validated DOAC antidote — do not pick it over idarucizumab/andexanet/4F-PCC for a DOAC bleed.
13. Perioperative strip — no bridging
DOACs do NOT require heparin BRIDGING around procedures because of their rapid ONSET and OFFSET — you simply hold them for a defined interval (≈1–2 days normal renal function, longer for high bleeding risk or impaired clearance) and restart afterward once hemostasis is secure. This is a key advantage over warfarin.
⚠️ Bridging a DOAC with LMWH is wrong and increases bleeding — bridging logic belongs to WARFARIN (slow offset/onset), not to short-acting DOACs.
14. Backup shelf empty / costly
Practical reality: specific DOAC antidotes (idarucizumab, andexanet) are EXPENSIVE and frequently NOT STOCKED in many hospitals. This is why supportive care, holding the drug, charcoal (if recent), and PCC remain central to real-world bleeding management — the 'shelf' may be bare when you need the branded agent.
⚠️ Don't plan management assuming the antidote is on hand — know the PCC-based fallback algorithm because andexanet/idarucizumab may be unavailable.
