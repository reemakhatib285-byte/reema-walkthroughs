📖 Read-through — all 11 symbols
1. A pattern, many causes
Mesangioproliferative GN is a histologic pattern — mesangial hypercellularity and matrix expansion — not a single disease. Defined by ≥3 mesangial cells per area on LM. The differential is driven by what the IF/EM deposits show.
⚠️ Reporting 'mesangioproliferative GN' without identifying the underlying cause is incomplete — always characterize the deposits and clinical context.
2. Mesangial hypercellularity + matrix expansion
The defining lesion: increased mesangial cell number and expanded mesangial matrix, expanding the mesangium on PAS. Capillary loops remain open (vs endocapillary/MPGN patterns). Deposits localize to the mesangium on EM.
⚠️ Mesangial-only proliferation (open loops) differs from MPGN's double contours and endocapillary GN's loop-filling proliferation — restricting to the mesangium is the key feature.
3. IgA nephropathy — most common cause
IgA nephropathy is the most common cause of a mesangioproliferative pattern: mesangial IgA deposits on IF, mesangial EM deposits, presenting with synpharyngitic gross hematuria or microhematuria.
⚠️ A mesangioproliferative biopsy with DOMINANT mesangial IgA = IgA nephropathy — the IF immunoglobulin class makes the diagnosis.
4. Lupus Class II
ISN/RPS Class II lupus nephritis = mesangial proliferative lupus: mesangial hypercellularity with mesangial immune deposits ('full house' IF: IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, C1q). Mild disease, good prognosis, usually no aggressive immunosuppression.
⚠️ Class II (mesangial) is benign and treated supportively — proliferative Class III/IV with subendothelial deposits is what needs cyclophosphamide/MMF.
5. Resolving post-infectious GN
In the healing phase of post-infectious (post-strep) GN, the florid endocapillary proliferation resolves into a residual mesangioproliferative pattern with normalizing complement — a recovering look-alike.
⚠️ A mesangioproliferative pattern weeks after pharyngitis with NORMALIZING C3 is resolving PIGN, not a new chronic GN — timing of complement recovery distinguishes.
6. Idiopathic
Idiopathic mesangioproliferative GN: a pattern without an identifiable cause or dominant Ig (sometimes IgM nephropathy or C1q nephropathy). Diagnosis of exclusion after ruling out IgA, lupus, and infection.
⚠️ Calling a case idiopathic before excluding IgA/lupus/infection and checking IF for IgM or C1q is premature — exclusion is mandatory.
7. Miscellaneous / secondary
Other secondary causes producing mesangial proliferation include IgA vasculitis (HSP), resolving other GNs, and early/mild forms of various immune-complex diseases. The pattern is a common final pathway.
⚠️ The pattern is nonspecific — anchoring on one diagnosis without IF/serology risks missing a systemic disease like lupus or vasculitis.
8. No endocapillary / no crescents
Defining the pattern by what's ABSENT: no endocapillary (loop-filling) proliferation, no GBM thickening/double contours, and no crescents. Pure mesangial expansion with patent capillaries = the milder mesangioproliferative end of the spectrum.
⚠️ Adding crescents or endocapillary proliferation moves you to crescentic or proliferative (Class IV/MPGN) disease — those carry worse prognosis and need immunosuppression.
9. Hematuria
Clinically presents with glomerular hematuria (dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts) — often microscopic, sometimes gross with intercurrent infection (IgA pattern). The nephritic signature of mesangial injury.
⚠️ Glomerular vs urologic hematuria: dysmorphic RBCs and RBC casts confirm a glomerular source — isomorphic RBCs suggest a urologic cause.
10. ± Proteinuria
Proteinuria is usually mild and sub-nephrotic in pure mesangioproliferative disease. Heavy/nephrotic proteinuria suggests a more aggressive lesion (FSGS transformation, proliferative lupus) rather than simple mesangial proliferation.
⚠️ Nephrotic-range proteinuria argues AGAINST bland mesangioproliferative GN — re-examine for FSGS or a proliferative class lesion.
11. Prognosis depends on cause
Because the pattern spans benign (Class II lupus, resolving PIGN) to progressive (proteinuric IgA) entities, outcome is driven by the underlying cause and severity — generally good for pure mesangial disease.
⚠️ There's no single prognosis for 'mesangioproliferative GN' — outcome tracks the etiology, so identifying the cause is the prognostic step.
