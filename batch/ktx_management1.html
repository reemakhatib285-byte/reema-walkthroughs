⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. Induction graft gate — Board trap: confusing induction with maintenance. Induction is a one-time peri-transplant burst (basiliximab/ATG), NOT the daily lifelong regimen. A stem asking 'which drug does the patient take indefinitely to prevent chronic rejection' is maintenance (tacrolimus + MMF + steroids), not an induction antibody.
  2. ATG (depleting) — Board trap: ATG-induced profound T-cell depletion is a classic CMV reactivation trigger — a febrile, leukopenic patient weeks after ATG for rejection is likely CMV, not 'expected ATG marrow effect' alone. Also, ATG (depleting) ≠ basiliximab (non-depleting); only the depleting agent is appropriate for established/steroid-resistant rejection.

  4. Basiliximab — Board trap: basiliximab does NOT deplete T cells and cannot reverse an established/ongoing acute rejection — choosing basiliximab to TREAT acute cellular rejection is wrong (answer is pulse steroids, then ATG if steroid-resistant). It is induction/prophylaxis only.

  6. Signal 2 — costimulation — Board trap: belatacept is CONTRAINDICATED in EBV-seronegative recipients because of a markedly increased risk of EBV-driven post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder (PTLD), particularly CNS PTLD. Selecting belatacept for an EBV-naive patient is a classic wrong answer; check EBV serostatus first.
  7. Calcineurin gorge — Board trap: tacrolimus (FK-506) is preferred over cyclosporine as first-line CNI — choosing cyclosporine as the modern default is wrong. 📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Induction graft gate
Induction is intense immunosuppression given at the time of transplant (day 0, peri-operatively) to blunt the strong early alloimmune response and prevent acute rejection during the highest-risk first weeks. Agents are antibody-based: depleting (ATG, alemtuzumab) for high immunologic risk, or non-depleting (basiliximab) for standard/low risk. Induction also permits lower/delayed calcineurin inhibitor dosing early on. Mnemonic: induction = brief, intense 'gatekeeper'; maintenance = lifelong, lower-intensity defense.
⚠️ Board trap: confusing induction with maintenance. Induction is a one-time peri-transplant burst (basiliximab/ATG), NOT the daily lifelong regimen. A stem asking 'which drug does the patient take indefinitely to prevent chronic rejection' is maintenance (tacrolimus + MMF + steroids), not an induction antibody.
2. ATG (depleting)
Anti-thymocyte globulin (ATG, typically rabbit/Thymoglobulin) is a polyclonal antibody that profoundly DEPLETES circulating T cells; used as induction in high immunologic risk (high PRA, D/R sensitization, repeat transplant, delayed graft function) and as treatment for steroid-resistant acute cellular rejection. Side effects: cytokine-release syndrome (premedicate with steroids/antihistamine/acetaminophen), leukopenia/thrombocytopenia, serum sickness, and markedly increased CMV/EBV/PTLD risk requiring prophylaxis.
⚠️ Board trap: ATG-induced profound T-cell depletion is a classic CMV reactivation trigger — a febrile, leukopenic patient weeks after ATG for rejection is likely CMV, not 'expected ATG marrow effect' alone. Also, ATG (depleting) ≠ basiliximab (non-depleting); only the depleting agent is appropriate for established/steroid-resistant rejection.
3. Alemtuzumab
Alemtuzumab is a monoclonal anti-CD52 antibody that causes prolonged depletion of T and B lymphocytes (and monocytes/NK cells); used as a potent depleting induction agent, sometimes allowing steroid- or CNI-minimization protocols. Because lymphocyte recovery is slow, it carries a sustained risk of opportunistic infection (CMV, PJP, fungal) mandating prolonged prophylaxis. CD52 is the target shared with its use in MS and CLL.
⚠️ Board trap: like ATG, alemtuzumab is depleting and immunosuppressively profound — answering that it is a 'mild, non-depleting' agent (the basiliximab role) is wrong. The prolonged lymphopenia means infection prophylaxis cannot be stopped early.
4. Basiliximab
Basiliximab is a monoclonal antibody against CD25 (the IL-2 receptor alpha chain) that blocks IL-2-driven T-cell proliferation WITHOUT depleting T cells; it is the standard NON-depleting induction agent for low/standard immunologic risk recipients. It is very well tolerated (no cytokine-release syndrome, no added CMV/PTLD burden beyond baseline), but it is purely preventive and has no role in treating established rejection.
⚠️ Board trap: basiliximab does NOT deplete T cells and cannot reverse an established/ongoing acute rejection — choosing basiliximab to TREAT acute cellular rejection is wrong (answer is pulse steroids, then ATG if steroid-resistant). It is induction/prophylaxis only.
5. Signal 1 — TCR/antigen
Signal 1 is antigen recognition: the antigen-presenting cell displays donor MHC/peptide to the T-cell receptor (TCR)-CD3 complex, initiating T-cell activation. Downstream this raises intracellular calcium, activating calcineurin → NFAT → IL-2 transcription. Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine) and steroids act on this proximal arm. Mnemonic: 'Signal 1 = the antigen handshake at the TCR.'
⚠️ Board trap: don't confuse Signal 1 (TCR/antigen, blocked downstream by CNIs at calcineurin) with Signal 2 (costimulation, blocked by belatacept). Mislabeling which signal a drug blocks is the core mechanism distractor in these questions.
6. Signal 2 — costimulation
Signal 2 is costimulation: T-cell CD28 binding APC B7 (CD80/CD86) is required for full activation; without it, the T cell becomes anergic. Belatacept is a CTLA-4–Ig fusion protein that outcompetes CD28 for B7, blocking costimulation; it is a CNI-sparing maintenance option that avoids nephrotoxicity but requires IV infusions. Mnemonic: 'Signal 2 = CD28/B7 handshake; belatacept = the CTLA-4 decoy.'
⚠️ Board trap: belatacept is CONTRAINDICATED in EBV-seronegative recipients because of a markedly increased risk of EBV-driven post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder (PTLD), particularly CNS PTLD. Selecting belatacept for an EBV-naive patient is a classic wrong answer; check EBV serostatus first.
7. Calcineurin gorge
Calcineurin inhibitors (CNIs) bind immunophilins (tacrolimus→FKBP-12; cyclosporine→cyclophilin) to inhibit calcineurin, preventing NFAT dephosphorylation and thus blocking IL-2 transcription — the backbone of maintenance therapy. Tacrolimus is first-line (more potent, lower acute rejection). Both are nephrotoxic (afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction) and need therapeutic drug monitoring; both are metabolized by CYP3A4 (azoles, diltiazem raise levels; rifampin lowers them).
⚠️ Board trap: tacrolimus (FK-506) is preferred over cyclosporine as first-line CNI — choosing cyclosporine as the modern default is wrong. Distinguish their toxicities: tacrolimus → neurotoxicity (tremor), new-onset diabetes, alopecia; cyclosporine → gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, hyperlipidemia. A rising Cr with a high tac level = CNI nephrotoxicity (reduce dose), NOT rejection (don't escalate).
8. IL-2 receptor / mTOR tower
Once IL-2 is produced (downstream of Signal 1/calcineurin), it binds the IL-2 receptor (CD25) and activates the PI3K/mTOR pathway that drives clonal T-cell proliferation — 'Signal 3.' This axis is the target of two distinct drug classes: basiliximab blocks the IL-2 receptor (CD25) extracellularly, while sirolimus/everolimus block mTOR intracellularly. Mnemonic: IL-2 = the growth command, mTOR = the proliferation engine.
⚠️ Board trap: don't conflate the two ways to hit this axis. Basiliximab blocks the IL-2 RECEPTOR (induction, non-depleting); sirolimus blocks mTOR (the downstream proliferation signal). Both touch IL-2 signaling but at different points and for different roles.
9. Sirolimus / everolimus
mTOR inhibitors (sirolimus, everolimus) bind FKBP-12 and inhibit mTOR, blocking IL-2-driven T-cell proliferation; they are CNI-sparing and notably antiproliferative/antineoplastic (reduce skin cancer and Kaposi sarcoma). Characteristic side effects: impaired wound healing, mouth ulcers, proteinuria, hyperlipidemia, pneumonitis, and cytopenias. They are favored when malignancy (e.g., recurrent skin cancer) is a concern.
⚠️ Board trap: AVOID mTOR inhibitors in the immediate peri-operative period — impaired wound healing causes dehiscence and lymphocele; substituting sirolimus on day 0 is wrong (use a CNI-based regimen, switch later). Also, sirolimus is a recognized cause of new/worsening proteinuria — don't reflexively call that proteinuria 'rejection.'
10. Purine forge — DNA block
Antimetabolites inhibit purine/DNA synthesis, suppressing proliferation of both T and B lymphocytes (which depend on de novo purine synthesis). This is the third pillar of triple maintenance therapy alongside a CNI and steroids. The two drugs are mycophenolate (inhibits IMPDH, the rate-limiting de novo purine enzyme) and azathioprine (a purine analog activated to thioguanine nucleotides).
⚠️ Board trap: antimetabolites are maintenance agents that suppress proliferation, not rapid-acting rescue drugs — they do not treat acute rejection (that's pulse steroids/ATG). Their main acute issue is myelosuppression, so a pancytopenic transplant patient should prompt review of MMF/azathioprine dosing and interactions.
11. Mycophenolate
Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) is hydrolyzed to mycophenolic acid, a reversible inhibitor of inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase (IMPDH), selectively blocking de novo purine synthesis on which lymphocytes rely — it is the FIRST-LINE maintenance antimetabolite. Side effects: dose-limiting GI toxicity (diarrhea, nausea — enteric-coated form helps) and myelosuppression (leukopenia). No TPMT issue and no allopurinol interaction (unlike azathioprine).
⚠️ Board trap: MMF is TERATOGENIC (first-trimester loss, ear/facial malformations) and must be stopped and switched (e.g., to azathioprine) before conception — continuing MMF in a patient planning pregnancy is wrong. Also, new diarrhea in a transplant patient is often MMF, but CMV colitis must be excluded before simply attributing it to the drug.
12. Azathioprine
Azathioprine is an older antimetabolite, a prodrug converted to 6-mercaptopurine and then thioguanine nucleotides that incorporate into DNA; inactivation is via thiopurine methyltransferase (TPMT) and xanthine oxidase. Low/absent TPMT activity → severe myelosuppression (check TPMT before use). It is the preferred antimetabolite in pregnancy (safer than MMF). Main toxicities: dose-dependent myelosuppression and hepatotoxicity.
⚠️ Board trap: allopurinol (xanthine oxidase inhibitor) blocks azathioprine catabolism → toxic accumulation and life-threatening pancytopenia; giving full-dose azathioprine with allopurinol (or febuxostat) is a classic fatal-interaction wrong answer — reduce azathioprine ~75% or switch agents. Also remember azathioprine, not MMF, is the antimetabolite of choice in pregnancy.
13. Steroids moon
Corticosteroids (prednisone for maintenance, IV methylprednisolone for pulses) are broad immunosuppressants that inhibit NF-κB, reducing macrophage/T-cell cytokine production (IL-1, IL-2, IL-6, TNF) and lymphocyte trafficking. They serve in all three roles: induction adjunct, low-dose maintenance, and FIRST-LINE pulse therapy for acute cellular rejection. Chronic toxicities: hyperglycemia, osteoporosis/avascular necrosis, infection, weight gain, cataracts, adrenal suppression.
⚠️ Board trap: high-dose pulse IV methylprednisolone is first-line for acute CELLULAR (T-cell-mediated) rejection — but steroids alone are inadequate for antibody-mediated rejection (needs plasmapheresis/IVIG ± rituximab). Don't treat a C4d+/DSA+ ABMR with steroid pulses alone.
14. Workhorse regimen band
Standard long-term maintenance is TRIPLE therapy targeting different points of the activation cascade: a calcineurin inhibitor (tacrolimus, Signal 1/IL-2 transcription) + an antimetabolite (mycophenolate, DNA/purine synthesis) + low-dose steroid (broad cytokine suppression). Layering mechanisms maximizes efficacy while limiting any single drug's toxicity. Reflex regimen for the boards: 'tacrolimus + MMF + prednisone.' Don't forget concurrent prophylaxis (TMP-SMX for PJP, valganciclovir for CMV).
⚠️ Board trap: the default maintenance answer is tacrolimus + mycophenolate + prednisone — substituting an induction antibody (basiliximab/ATG) into the lifelong regimen, or naming cyclosporine as first-line CNI, is wrong. Equally, forgetting that maintenance patients need PJP (TMP-SMX) and CMV prophylaxis is a common omission.
