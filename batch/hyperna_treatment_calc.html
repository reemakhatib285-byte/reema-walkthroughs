📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Total water needed
The total daily water prescription has TWO components: the calculated baseline free-water deficit PLUS ongoing free-water losses (urine, insensible ~10 mL/kg/day, GI). Correction rate must stay ≤10 mEq/L drop in serum Na per 24h. Plan: replace the deficit over 48–72h, add the day's ongoing losses, and recheck Na q4–6h to titrate.
⚠️ WRONG on both sides: replacing only the deficit and forgetting ongoing losses (undertreats — Na keeps climbing, classic in DI), OR aiming to normalize Na in 24h (overcorrects → cerebral edema). Discriminator — deficit + ongoing losses, corrected ≤10 mEq/L/day.
2. TBW factor
Total body water (TBW) = weight(kg) × a fraction that falls with age and female sex due to lower muscle/higher fat: young men 0.6, young women and elderly men 0.5, elderly women 0.45. TBW is the denominator of the deficit formula, so picking the wrong fraction skews the volume to give.
⚠️ WRONG: using 0.6 for an elderly woman (true factor 0.45). Discriminator — women and the elderly hold proportionally less water (more adipose), so lower fractions; using 0.6 universally overestimates TBW and the deficit.
3. ≤10 mM/day
The hard safety ceiling: lower serum Na by no more than ~10 mEq/L per 24h (some sources say up to 12; ~0.5 mEq/L/h). In acute hypernatremia (<48h, e.g. salt poisoning) faster correction up to ~1 mEq/L/h is tolerated, but for the typical chronic case stay at ≤10/day to protect adapted brain cells.
⚠️ WRONG: applying a 'fast is fine' rule to chronic hypernatremia. Discriminator — only documented ACUTE hypernatremia tolerates rapid correction; assume chronic and cap at ≤10 mEq/L/24h unless onset <48h is proven.
4. Osmolytes (chronic)
In chronic hypernatremia, neurons generate idiogenic osmolytes (taurine, myo-inositol, glutamine/glutamate) over ~48h to match serum tonicity and prevent cell shrinkage. These osmolytes clear slowly, so if you drop serum Na faster than the brain can offload them, water rushes into the still-hyperosmolar neurons → cerebral edema.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming the brain re-equilibrates instantly so rapid correction is safe. Discriminator — adaptive osmolytes are why chronic hypernatremia must be corrected slowly; their slow clearance is the mechanism of correction-induced cerebral edema.
5. Correct over 48–72h
Replace the total free-water DEFICIT gradually over 48–72 hours, not in a single day. Spreading it out keeps the per-day Na fall within the ≤10 mEq/L limit. Combine with frequent Na monitoring (q4–6h) so you can slow or speed the infusion as the trajectory dictates.
⚠️ WRONG: front-loading the entire deficit on day 1. Discriminator — the deficit is a 48–72h target; day-1 infusion is sized to drop Na ≤10 mEq/L, not to erase the whole deficit at once.
6. Rapid → edema
Overly rapid lowering of a chronically elevated Na drives free water into osmolyte-loaded neurons → cerebral edema, seizures, coma, and death. This is the mirror image of osmotic demyelination in hyponatremia. It is the central safety rule that the whole calculation exists to enforce.
⚠️ WRONG: blaming new neuro decline during correction on 'worsening hypernatremia' and speeding up fluids. Discriminator — neuro deterioration WHILE the Na is falling fast = iatrogenic cerebral edema; the fix is to SLOW correction, not accelerate it.
7. Free water deficit
Free-water deficit (L) = TBW × (current Na / target Na − 1), with TBW = weight × factor. Target Na is ultimately 140, but for day-1 pacing you often calculate toward 145–150 so the planned fall respects ≤10 mEq/L. This gives the volume of electrolyte-free water (delivered as D5W or oral water) needed to reach the target.
⚠️ WRONG: plugging the final target of 140 into the day-1 calculation. Discriminator — day-1 math uses an intermediate target (~150) to keep the drop ≤10 mEq/L; 140 is the eventual goal over several days, not the first 24h.
8. Day 1 target 150
For the first day, calculate toward a Na of ~150 (or current Na minus 10), NOT straight to 140. Example: Na 160 → day-1 target 150 = a 10 mEq/L drop, exactly at the ceiling. This staged targeting is how the formula is operationalized safely.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: 'calculate the deficit to reach Na 140 today.' Why wrong — from a starting Na of 160–170 that exceeds the safe 10 mEq/L/24h drop. Discriminator — set day-1 target = starting Na − 10 (≈150), then continue toward 140 on subsequent days.
9. Worked example
Worked case: 70 kg patient, Na 160. TBW = 70 × 0.5 = 35 L. Total deficit to 140 = 35 × (160/140 − 1) = 5 L. But for day 1 target 150: 35 × (160/150 − 1) ≈ 2.3 L, given as D5W over 24h ≈ 95 mL/h, PLUS ongoing/insensible losses. Recheck Na to confirm the ~10 mEq/L fall.
⚠️ WRONG: infusing the full 5 L (the to-140 deficit) on day 1. Discriminator — 5 L is the multi-day deficit; only ~2.3 L (the to-150 portion) plus ongoing losses belongs in day 1 to honor the ≤10 mEq/L cap.
10. EFWC station
Electrolyte-free water clearance (EFWC) = urine volume × [1 − (UNa + UK)/serum Na] estimates how much pure water the kidney is losing per day. A positive EFWC means ongoing renal free-water loss that must be ADDED to the replacement, or the serum Na will keep rising despite deficit correction.
⚠️ WRONG: ignoring renal free-water losses and treating only the static deficit. Discriminator — in polyuric states EFWC quantifies the ongoing loss you must replace on top of the deficit; omitting it explains 'why won't the Na come down?'
11. DI = huge losses
In diabetes insipidus, dilute urine output is massive (can exceed 3–10 L/day of nearly free water), so ongoing losses dwarf the baseline deficit. You must replace these losses AND treat the cause — desmopressin (DDAVP) for central DI; remove offending drug / thiazide + low-solute diet for nephrogenic DI — or the Na keeps climbing.
⚠️ WRONG: calculating a fixed deficit for a DI patient and stopping there. Discriminator — DI is dominated by ENORMOUS ongoing renal free-water losses; without replacing them plus giving DDAVP (central) the Na won't normalize regardless of the deficit math.
12. Don't aim 140 in 24h
Pacing trap #1: do NOT try to normalize Na to 140 within 24h from a high baseline. The per-day fall must stay ≤10 mEq/L, so reaching 140 from 160–170 takes 2–3 days. Set staged daily targets instead of one-shot normalization.
⚠️ WRONG: prescribing fluids to hit 140 by tomorrow. Discriminator — full normalization in 24h breaches the ≤10 mEq/L/day limit and risks cerebral edema; normalize over 48–72h.
13. Don't forget ongoing
Pacing trap #2: the calculated deficit is only half the prescription — add ongoing free-water losses (insensible ~10 mL/kg/day, urine via EFWC, GI/diarrhea, fever). Omitting them is the classic reason serum Na fails to fall despite 'correct' deficit replacement.
⚠️ WRONG: replacing the deficit alone and being puzzled when Na stays high. Discriminator — total day-1 water = deficit + ongoing losses; the missing ongoing-loss term is the usual culprit for stalled correction.
14. Overcorrected → don't re-induce
Pacing trap #3: if Na is overcorrected (dropped too fast) in ADULTS, generally do NOT actively re-raise the sodium — just slow/hold free water and monitor, as adults tolerate this better than the reverse. In CHILDREN, where correction-induced cerebral edema risk is higher, relowering/active management may be considered.
⚠️ WRONG: aggressively re-raising Na with hypertonic saline after an adult overshoots downward. Discriminator — adults: pause free water and observe; the child-specific concern about cerebral edema does not mandate re-inducing hypernatremia in adults.
