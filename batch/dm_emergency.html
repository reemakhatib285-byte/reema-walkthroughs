⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. DKA precipitants — WRONG: 'Treat the DKA and discharge once glucose normalizes without searching for a cause.' Missing the precipitant (e.g. silent MI or pyelonephritis) is a classic error. Discriminator: an MI or sepsis can both cause AND complicate DKA — the underlying trigger must be identified and treated.
  2. Step 1 — Fluids first — WRONG: 'Bolus IV insulin as the first intervention.' Giving insulin before volume resuscitation worsens hypovolemic shock (intracellular fluid shift) and can precipitate fatal hypokalemia. Discriminator: fluids first, ALWAYS — and check K+ before the insulin drip.
  3. Step 2 — Insulin infusion — WRONG: 'Hold insulin entirely once the drip is started until labs return.' OR 'Give a large SC insulin dose.' The 'pause button' applies only when K+ <3.3. Discriminator: in DKA, IV infusion (not SC) is standard, and the only reason to hold it is K+ <3.3.
  4. DKA clinical picture — WRONG: 'The abdominal pain mandates surgical exploration / CT for appendicitis.' DKA-related abdominal pain usually resolves with correction of acidosis — treat the DKA first, reassess. Discriminator: pain that clears as the anion gap closes was metabolic, not surgical.
  5. K+ management panel — WRONG: 'Serum K+ is 5.5, so the patient has too much potassium — withhold replacement entirely and don't worry about it.' Total-body K+ is low; once insulin/fluids start, serum K+ plummets. Discriminator: a 'normal' or high serum K+ in DKA still means depletion — anticipate the fall.
  6. Bicarbonate — only if pH<7.0 — WRONG: 'Give an amp of bicarb for a pH of 7.1 to speed recovery.' At pH >7.0 bicarb provides no benefit and adds risk. Discriminator: the only DKA bicarb indication is pH <7.0 — moderate acidosis is treated with insulin and fluids alone.
  7. HHS picture — WRONG: 'The patient is obtunded with glucose 800 but pH 7.35 and no ketones — this can't be a hyperglycemic emergency.' That IS HHS. Discriminator: HHS is defined by osmolality + AMS, NOT by acidosis/ketones; degree of AMS tracks osmolality (>320).
  8. HHS dehydration / sunken eyes — WRONG: 'AMS in this elderly diabetic must be a primary CNS event (stroke), so go straight to neuro workup.' Hyperosmolar dehydration alone causes the obtundation, which reverses with fluids. Discriminator: check glucose/osm before attributing AMS to a stroke — though a precipitating stroke must still be excluded.
  9. HHS fluid treatment — WRONG: 'Correct the glucose/osmolality rapidly to normal in the first few hours.' Over-rapid osmolar correction risks cerebral edema and central pontine injury. Discriminator: lower osmolality SLOWLY; fluids (not aggressive insulin) are the mainstay in HHS.
  10. Euglycemic DKA corner — WRONG: 'Glucose is 180, so rule out DKA and stop the workup.' SGLT2i-associated euglycemic DKA is easily missed this way. Discriminator: in any acidotic patient on an SGLT2i, check the anion gap and serum/urine ketones regardless of a near-normal glucose.
  11. Anion gap drives diagnosis — WRONG: 'Follow the urine ketone dipstick to gauge improvement.' As DKA improves, beta-OHB converts to acetoacetate, so urine ketones can paradoxically RISE while the patient is getting better. Discriminator: track the anion gap (and serum beta-OHB), not urine ketones, for resolution.

📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. DKA precipitants
Always hunt the trigger — DKA rarely happens without one. The 5 I's: Infection (most common — pneumonia, UTI; check WBC, cultures, CXR), Infarction (MI — often silent in diabetics; get ECG/troponin), Insulin missed/nonadherence (leading cause in young T1DM), Ischemia/stroke, and Iatrogenic/Intoxication (SGLT2i, steroids, antipsychotics, cocaine). A modestly elevated WBC is expected from stress/dehydration, but bandemia or fever points to true infection.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Treat the DKA and discharge once glucose normalizes without searching for a cause.' Missing the precipitant (e.g. silent MI or pyelonephritis) is a classic error. Discriminator: an MI or sepsis can both cause AND complicate DKA — the underlying trigger must be identified and treated.
2. Step 1 — Fluids first
FIRST step in DKA = aggressive IV fluids, isotonic 0.9% NS, ~1-1.5 L in the first hour (15-20 mL/kg), NOT insulin. Patients have a 3-6 L deficit; fluids alone drop glucose by hemodilution + improved renal perfusion/clearance. Volume expansion also restores tissue perfusion before insulin shifts K+ and water intracellularly.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Bolus IV insulin as the first intervention.' Giving insulin before volume resuscitation worsens hypovolemic shock (intracellular fluid shift) and can precipitate fatal hypokalemia. Discriminator: fluids first, ALWAYS — and check K+ before the insulin drip.
3. Step 2 — Insulin infusion
After fluids are started and K+ confirmed >=3.3, begin regular insulin IV infusion at 0.1 U/kg/hr (optionally a 0.1 U/kg bolus first; bolus is omitted in children). Target glucose fall ~50-100 mg/dL/hr; if it does not drop ~10% in the first hour, increase the rate or recheck fluids. Insulin halts ketogenesis and lipolysis — this, not glucose lowering, is the therapeutic goal.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Hold insulin entirely once the drip is started until labs return.' OR 'Give a large SC insulin dose.' The 'pause button' applies only when K+ <3.3. Discriminator: in DKA, IV infusion (not SC) is standard, and the only reason to hold it is K+ <3.3.
4. DKA clinical picture
DKA presentation: Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid breathing = respiratory compensation for metabolic acidosis, blowing off CO2), fruity/acetone breath (exhaled acetone), nausea, vomiting, and diffuse abdominal pain that can mimic an acute abdomen. Onset is rapid (<24h), typically T1DM/younger. The abdominal pain correlates with the degree of acidosis and resolves as the gap closes.
⚠️ WRONG: 'The abdominal pain mandates surgical exploration / CT for appendicitis.' DKA-related abdominal pain usually resolves with correction of acidosis — treat the DKA first, reassess. Discriminator: pain that clears as the anion gap closes was metabolic, not surgical.
5. K+ management panel
Total-body potassium is profoundly DEPLETED in DKA (osmotic diuresis + acidosis shifting K+ out of cells), even when serum K+ reads high. Rules: K+ <3.3 → HOLD insulin, replete K+ first (insulin would crash it fatally). K+ 3.3-5.0 → give insulin AND add 20-30 mEq K+ per liter. K+ >5.0 → start insulin, no K+ yet, recheck q2h. Insulin and correction of acidosis both drive K+ intracellularly.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Serum K+ is 5.5, so the patient has too much potassium — withhold replacement entirely and don't worry about it.' Total-body K+ is low; once insulin/fluids start, serum K+ plummets. Discriminator: a 'normal' or high serum K+ in DKA still means depletion — anticipate the fall.
6. Bicarbonate — only if pH<7.0
Bicarbonate is NOT routine in DKA — insulin + fluids correct the acidosis by clearing ketones. Reserve it for severe acidemia pH <7.0 (some say <6.9), where myocardial contractility/pressor response is impaired. Harms: paradoxical CSF acidosis, worsened hypokalemia, delayed ketone clearance, rebound alkalosis, and possible cerebral edema in children.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Give an amp of bicarb for a pH of 7.1 to speed recovery.' At pH >7.0 bicarb provides no benefit and adds risk. Discriminator: the only DKA bicarb indication is pH <7.0 — moderate acidosis is treated with insulin and fluids alone.
7. HHS picture
HHS = Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State: profound hyperglycemia (typically >600 mg/dL), serum effective osmolality >320 mOsm/kg, severe altered mental status, with little/no ketosis and pH >7.3, HCO3 >18. Occurs in T2DM/elderly because residual insulin suppresses ketogenesis but not hyperglycemia. Onset is insidious (days). Mortality (~10-20%) is HIGHER than DKA, mostly from the precipitant and the patient's age/comorbidities.
⚠️ WRONG: 'The patient is obtunded with glucose 800 but pH 7.35 and no ketones — this can't be a hyperglycemic emergency.' That IS HHS. Discriminator: HHS is defined by osmolality + AMS, NOT by acidosis/ketones; degree of AMS tracks osmolality (>320).
8. HHS dehydration / sunken eyes
HHS produces extreme dehydration — free-water deficit ~8-12 L, larger than DKA's ~3-6 L — because the osmotic diuresis runs for days. Signs: sunken eyes, dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, hypotension, tachycardia, and AMS proportional to hyperosmolality. Calculate effective osm = 2×Na + glucose/18 (urea is freely permeable, so it's excluded from the 'effective' figure).
⚠️ WRONG: 'AMS in this elderly diabetic must be a primary CNS event (stroke), so go straight to neuro workup.' Hyperosmolar dehydration alone causes the obtundation, which reverses with fluids. Discriminator: check glucose/osm before attributing AMS to a stroke — though a precipitating stroke must still be excluded.
9. HHS fluid treatment
HHS treatment is fluid-driven: start 0.9% NS (1-1.5 L/hr initially) to restore perfusion, then switch to 0.45% NaCl guided by corrected sodium/osmolality (if corrected Na is high/normal → half-NS; if low → stay on NS). Insulin is also given (0.1 U/kg/hr) but is secondary — fluids do most of the glucose lowering. Aim to drop osmolality no faster than ~3 mOsm/kg/hr.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Correct the glucose/osmolality rapidly to normal in the first few hours.' Over-rapid osmolar correction risks cerebral edema and central pontine injury. Discriminator: lower osmolality SLOWLY; fluids (not aggressive insulin) are the mainstay in HHS.
10. Euglycemic DKA corner
Euglycemic DKA = full ketoacidosis (high anion gap, ketones, low pH) with glucose <250 (often 150-200). Causes: SGLT2 inhibitors (glucosuria masks hyperglycemia), pregnancy, prolonged fasting/starvation, heavy alcohol use, and reduced carbohydrate intake. Treat exactly like DKA but add dextrose EARLIER (D5/D10) so you can keep insulin running to clear ketones without causing hypoglycemia.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Glucose is 180, so rule out DKA and stop the workup.' SGLT2i-associated euglycemic DKA is easily missed this way. Discriminator: in any acidotic patient on an SGLT2i, check the anion gap and serum/urine ketones regardless of a near-normal glucose.
11. Anion gap drives diagnosis
The anion gap (Na − [Cl + HCO3], normal ~8-12) is BOTH the diagnostic marker and the resolution marker for DKA. Beta-hydroxybutyrate is the dominant ketone (measure it directly; the nitroprusside/urine test detects acetoacetate and underestimates early severity). Resolution = anion gap normalized (and HCO3 >=15-18, pH >7.3), NOT a normal glucose.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Follow the urine ketone dipstick to gauge improvement.' As DKA improves, beta-OHB converts to acetoacetate, so urine ketones can paradoxically RISE while the patient is getting better. Discriminator: track the anion gap (and serum beta-OHB), not urine ketones, for resolution.
12. Add dextrose at glucose ~200
Once glucose falls to ~200 mg/dL (DKA) or ~250-300 (HHS), add D5 (or D5-half-NS) to the IV fluids so you can CONTINUE the insulin infusion. Insulin must keep running to suppress ketogenesis and close the anion gap; dextrose just prevents hypoglycemia in the meantime. You can also titrate the insulin rate down slightly while keeping it on.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Glucose hit 200, so turn off the insulin drip.' Stopping insulin halts ketone clearance and the gap reopens (rebound ketoacidosis). Discriminator: at glucose ~200, ADD dextrose and KEEP insulin — never stop insulin to chase a normal glucose.
13. Inpatient management — no sliding scale
Non-critically-ill inpatient glucose target is 140-180 mg/dL (per NICE-SUGAR, tight control <110 increased mortality from hypoglycemia). Use scheduled basal-bolus (basal + nutritional + correction) insulin, NOT sliding scale alone. Sliding scale is purely reactive — it chases highs after they occur and never provides basal coverage, causing wide swings.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Manage the hospitalized diabetic with sliding-scale insulin only' OR 'aim for tight control <110.' Both worsen outcomes. Discriminator: basal-bolus with a 140-180 target; sliding-scale-monotherapy and tight <110 control are the wrong answers.
14. Steroid-induced DM corner
Glucocorticoids cause predominantly POSTPRANDIAL and afternoon/evening hyperglycemia with a relatively spared morning fasting glucose, because once-daily morning prednisone peaks midday. NPH insulin given WITH the morning steroid dose matches this pharmacokinetic curve (NPH peaks ~6-8h later, in the afternoon). Monitor pre-lunch/pre-dinner and bedtime glucose to catch the pattern.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Fasting glucose is normal, so the steroids aren't causing hyperglycemia.' Steroid hyperglycemia characteristically spares the morning FPG. Discriminator: a normal FPG does NOT exclude steroid-induced DM — you must check afternoon/postprandial values.
