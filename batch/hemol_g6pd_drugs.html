⚠️ Board traps 19
  1. Prima-ballerina PRIMAQUINE, top-left high-risk zone, breaking red oxidant balls. — WRONG: 'A normal G6PD level during an acute hemolytic episode safely excludes deficiency, so primaquine can be continued.' Discriminator: during acute hemolysis the oldest, most deficient cells are already lysed and reticulocytes (which have higher G6PD) dominate, falsely normalizing the assay — repeat testing 2-3 months after the episode.
  2. TAFENOQUINE figure with a mallet beside primaquine. — WRONG: 'Tafenoquine is safer than primaquine in G6PD deficiency because it is a single dose.' Discriminator: the single dose is actually MORE dangerous — its prolonged half-life makes oxidant hemolysis unstoppable once started, which is exactly why a quantitative (not just qualitative) G6PD assay is required.
  3. Yellow sulfur DAPSONE figure (rotten-egg) smashing oxidant balls. — WRONG: 'Cyanosis in a dapsone-treated patient with a normal arterial PaO2 indicates pulmonary shunting.' Discriminator: a normal PaO2 with low pulse-oximetry SpO2 (the 'saturation gap') points to methemoglobinemia, not lung pathology — confirm with co-oximetry, not standard ABG.
  4. Green goblin RASBURICASE leaking H2O2, lower-left. — Rasburicase is safe in G6PD deficiency. WRONG — rasburicase is absolutely CONTRAINDICATED: the H2O2 byproduct triggers brisk oxidant hemolysis and methemoglobinemia. Discriminator: in a high-TLS-risk patient of Mediterranean/African/Asian descent, screen G6PD first and substitute allopurinol if deficient; never give rasburicase empirically.
  5. Green PEG-goblin PEGLOTICASE beside rasburicase. — WRONG: 'Pegloticase, being a chronic gout drug rather than an oncology agent, does not require G6PD screening.' Discriminator: mechanism (uricase → H2O2), not indication, drives the risk — both pegloticase and rasburicase carry the same contraindication and require pre-treatment G6PD testing.
  6. METHYLENE BLUE PAINTER in blue overalls, bottom-left. — Methylene blue is the treatment for G6PD methemoglobinemia. WRONG — it is contraindicated/ineffective in G6PD deficiency. Discriminator: methylene blue requires NADPH (the very thing G6PD deficiency lacks) to work; without it the drug acts as an oxidant. The right answer in a G6PD-deficient patient with methemoglobinemia is high-dose vitamin C or exchange transfusion.
  7. TOLUIDINE BLUE figure next to the methylene-blue painter. — WRONG: 'Toluidine blue can be safely substituted for methylene blue in a G6PD-deficient patient with methemoglobinemia.' Discriminator: both are NADPH-dependent oxidant dyes — swapping one for the other does not solve the problem; choose ascorbic acid or exchange transfusion.
  8. ASA high dose — All aspirin doses are forbidden in G6PD. WRONG — only high anti-inflammatory doses (>3 g/day) carry meaningful hemolytic risk; low-dose (81 mg) aspirin is generally safe. Discriminator: G6PD drug risk is dose-dependent for several agents — do not withhold cardioprotective low-dose aspirin from a G6PD-deficient patient who needs it.
  9. Ascorbic acid >1 g (scurvy pirate) — Vitamin C is always safe in G6PD. WRONG — high-dose IV ascorbic acid (>1 g) is a pro-oxidant that causes hemolysis. Discriminator: dose dictates direction — low-dose vitamin C is the preferred antidote for methemoglobinemia in G6PD deficiency, but gram-scale IV vitamin C precipitates hemolysis; the naphthalene mothball exposure is the classic pediatric trigger.
  10. Anthracycline / doxorubicin — WRONG: 'Cytotoxic chemotherapy has no special hemolytic risk in G6PD deficiency.' Discriminator: ROS-generating agents (anthracyclines, rasburicase) add oxidant burden — watch for hemolysis on top of expected myelosuppression.
  11. Probenecid (probation officer) — WRONG: 'Phenazopyridine should be given freely for UTI dysuria in G6PD deficiency because it is on the low-risk list.' Discriminator: phenazopyridine is lower-risk than sulfa drugs but can still cause methemoglobinemia and hemolysis at high or prolonged doses, especially in renal impairment — acetaminophen is the truly safe analgesic anchor here.
  12. Vitamin K analogues — WRONG: 'All vitamin K preparations must be avoided in G6PD deficiency.' Discriminator: phytonadione (K1) is SAFE — only synthetic menadione (K3) is the oxidant to avoid.
  13. Quinine/chloroquine — WRONG: 'Chloroquine must never be given to a G6PD-deficient patient with acute malaria.' Discriminator: chloroquine is moderate-risk and the danger of untreated malaria exceeds the hemolytic risk — it is permitted for acute treatment; it is PRIMAQUINE/tafenoquine (radical cure) that demand pre-screening.
  14. Hydroxychloroquine — WRONG: 'Hydroxychloroquine carries the same high hemolytic risk as primaquine and must never be used in G6PD deficiency.' Discriminator: the high-risk antimalarials are the 8-aminoquinolines (primaquine, tafenoquine) for hypnozoite radical cure; the 4-aminoquinolines hydroxychloroquine and chloroquine are low/moderate risk and are routinely used in SLE/RA without G6PD screening.
  15. Sulfasalazine / sulfadimidine — WRONG: 'Sulfasalazine is an aminosalicylate, not a sulfonamide, so it is safe in G6PD deficiency.' Discriminator: sulfasalazine = sulfapyridine + 5-ASA — the sulfapyridine moiety is an oxidant sulfonamide and carries hemolytic risk.
  16. Sulfamethoxazole (SMX fox) — WRONG: 'A G6PD-deficient patient allergic-list says avoid sulfa, so thiazide diuretics and sulfonylureas are also unsafe.' Discriminator: it is the antibacterial sulfonamides (sulfamethoxazole, sulfadiazine, sulfasalazine) that trigger hemolysis; non-antibiotic sulfonamides (thiazides, glipizide, furosemide, celecoxib) do not cross-react and are not significant oxidant triggers.
  17. Nitrofurantoin & fluoroquinolones — WRONG: 'Nitrofurantoin is contraindicated in G6PD only because of its known pulmonary and hepatic toxicity, not hemolysis.' Discriminator: nitrofurantoin is a direct OXIDANT that causes acute hemolysis in G6PD deficiency — the smear shows bite cells and Heinz bodies; its separate pulmonary/hepatic toxicities are unrelated to the G6PD contraindication.

📖 Read-through — all 19 symbols
1. Prima-ballerina PRIMAQUINE, top-left high-risk zone, breaking red oxidant balls.
Primaquine is the classic high-risk oxidant antimalarial in G6PD deficiency — it generates reactive oxygen species that overwhelm a G6PD-deficient cell's limited NADPH/glutathione reserve, oxidizing hemoglobin to Heinz bodies and causing acute intravascular hemolysis 2-4 days after exposure. It is given for radical cure of P. vivax/ovale hypnozoites, so ALWAYS screen G6PD level BEFORE prescribing. Mnemonic for oxidant drugs: 'Dr. PANIC' — Dapsone, Primaquine, Aspirin (high-dose), Nitrofurantoin, Isoniazid/Isobutyl(naphthalene), Ciprofloxacin/Sulfa.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A normal G6PD level during an acute hemolytic episode safely excludes deficiency, so primaquine can be continued.' Discriminator: during acute hemolysis the oldest, most deficient cells are already lysed and reticulocytes (which have higher G6PD) dominate, falsely normalizing the assay — repeat testing 2-3 months after the episode.
2. TAFENOQUINE figure with a mallet beside primaquine.
Tafenoquine is a long-acting 8-aminoquinoline antimalarial (single 300 mg dose for vivax radical cure) and, like primaquine, is a potent oxidant that triggers severe hemolysis in G6PD deficiency. Because its long half-life (~15 days) means exposure cannot be withdrawn quickly, the FDA mandates documented quantitative G6PD activity >70% of normal before use. Contraindicated in pregnancy and breastfeeding of a G6PD-deficient infant.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Tafenoquine is safer than primaquine in G6PD deficiency because it is a single dose.' Discriminator: the single dose is actually MORE dangerous — its prolonged half-life makes oxidant hemolysis unstoppable once started, which is exactly why a quantitative (not just qualitative) G6PD assay is required.
3. Yellow sulfur DAPSONE figure (rotten-egg) smashing oxidant balls.
Dapsone is a sulfone antibiotic (used for PCP prophylaxis, leprosy, dermatitis herpetiformis) and one of the strongest clinically encountered oxidants — it causes dose-dependent hemolysis AND methemoglobinemia even in patients with normal G6PD, but far worse in deficiency. Screen G6PD before starting; methemoglobinemia presents with cyanosis unresponsive to oxygen and a saturation gap (low SpO2, normal PaO2). Co-administer with cimetidine to reduce the toxic hydroxylamine metabolite.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Cyanosis in a dapsone-treated patient with a normal arterial PaO2 indicates pulmonary shunting.' Discriminator: a normal PaO2 with low pulse-oximetry SpO2 (the 'saturation gap') points to methemoglobinemia, not lung pathology — confirm with co-oximetry, not standard ABG.
4. Green goblin RASBURICASE leaking H2O2, lower-left.
Rasburicase is a recombinant urate oxidase used for tumor lysis syndrome; it converts uric acid to allantoin, generating hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) as a byproduct that directly oxidizes G6PD-deficient RBCs → severe hemolysis AND methemoglobinemia. It is absolutely contraindicated in G6PD deficiency — screen high-risk (Mediterranean, African, Asian) patients before TLS prophylaxis, and use allopurinol/rasburicase-alternative instead. Onset of hemolysis can be within 24-48 h.
⚠️ Rasburicase is safe in G6PD deficiency. WRONG — rasburicase is absolutely CONTRAINDICATED: the H2O2 byproduct triggers brisk oxidant hemolysis and methemoglobinemia. Discriminator: in a high-TLS-risk patient of Mediterranean/African/Asian descent, screen G6PD first and substitute allopurinol if deficient; never give rasburicase empirically.
5. Green PEG-goblin PEGLOTICASE beside rasburicase.
Pegloticase is a PEGylated recombinant uricase for refractory chronic gout; like rasburicase it generates H2O2 during urate breakdown and is therefore contraindicated in G6PD deficiency (boxed warning). Screen G6PD before the first infusion. Both uricases share the 'goblin/H2O2' visual because the peroxide byproduct is the shared mechanism of oxidant hemolysis.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Pegloticase, being a chronic gout drug rather than an oncology agent, does not require G6PD screening.' Discriminator: mechanism (uricase → H2O2), not indication, drives the risk — both pegloticase and rasburicase carry the same contraindication and require pre-treatment G6PD testing.
6. METHYLENE BLUE PAINTER in blue overalls, bottom-left.
Methylene blue is the standard antidote for acquired methemoglobinemia (1-2 mg/kg IV) because it is reduced by NADPH-dependent methemoglobin reductase back to leukomethylene blue, which then reduces Fe3+ to Fe2+. In G6PD deficiency there is insufficient NADPH to drive this pathway, so methylene blue is ineffective AND itself becomes an oxidant → paradoxically worsens both methemoglobinemia and hemolysis. Use ascorbic acid or exchange transfusion instead in G6PD-deficient methemoglobinemia.
⚠️ Methylene blue is the treatment for G6PD methemoglobinemia. WRONG — it is contraindicated/ineffective in G6PD deficiency. Discriminator: methylene blue requires NADPH (the very thing G6PD deficiency lacks) to work; without it the drug acts as an oxidant. The right answer in a G6PD-deficient patient with methemoglobinemia is high-dose vitamin C or exchange transfusion.
7. TOLUIDINE BLUE figure next to the methylene-blue painter.
Toluidine blue is an alternative methemoglobinemia agent and tissue/dye stain that, like methylene blue, is an oxidant dye to be avoided in G6PD deficiency. It shares the same NADPH-dependent limitation and oxidant risk. Grouped with methylene blue as 'blue dye oxidants' on the high-risk side of the scene.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Toluidine blue can be safely substituted for methylene blue in a G6PD-deficient patient with methemoglobinemia.' Discriminator: both are NADPH-dependent oxidant dyes — swapping one for the other does not solve the problem; choose ascorbic acid or exchange transfusion.
8. ASA high dose
Aspirin is dose-dependent: high anti-inflammatory doses (>3 g/day) can precipitate oxidant hemolysis in G6PD deficiency, whereas low-dose antiplatelet aspirin (81 mg, cardioprotective) is generally safe. The board point is that risk is a function of cumulative oxidant burden and dose, not an all-or-none drug-class ban. Most everyday analgesic/antiplatelet use does not require avoidance.
⚠️ All aspirin doses are forbidden in G6PD. WRONG — only high anti-inflammatory doses (>3 g/day) carry meaningful hemolytic risk; low-dose (81 mg) aspirin is generally safe. Discriminator: G6PD drug risk is dose-dependent for several agents — do not withhold cardioprotective low-dose aspirin from a G6PD-deficient patient who needs it.
9. Ascorbic acid >1 g (scurvy pirate)
High-dose IV vitamin C (>1 g, especially gram-scale infusions) and naphthalene (mothballs, dimethyl-naphthalene) are oxidants that cause hemolysis in G6PD deficiency — classic peds vignette is a G6PD-deficient infant exposed to mothball-stored clothing who develops hemolytic anemia. Note the irony: low-dose vitamin C is actually a REDUCING agent and is used as an antidote for methemoglobinemia in G6PD patients, but supraphysiologic doses flip to a pro-oxidant role.
⚠️ Vitamin C is always safe in G6PD. WRONG — high-dose IV ascorbic acid (>1 g) is a pro-oxidant that causes hemolysis. Discriminator: dose dictates direction — low-dose vitamin C is the preferred antidote for methemoglobinemia in G6PD deficiency, but gram-scale IV vitamin C precipitates hemolysis; the naphthalene mothball exposure is the classic pediatric trigger.
10. Anthracycline / doxorubicin
Anthracyclines (doxorubicin, daunorubicin) generate reactive oxygen species as part of their cytotoxic mechanism, and in severe G6PD deficiency this added oxidant load can precipitate hemolysis. They sit on the 'caution in chemotherapy protocols' shelf rather than the absolute-avoid list, but a G6PD-deficient oncology patient warrants awareness and monitoring. Group them mentally with the other oxidative-stress generators (rasburicase, high-dose vitamin C).
⚠️ WRONG: 'Cytotoxic chemotherapy has no special hemolytic risk in G6PD deficiency.' Discriminator: ROS-generating agents (anthracyclines, rasburicase) add oxidant burden — watch for hemolysis on top of expected myelosuppression.
11. Probenecid (probation officer)
Probenecid (uricosuric), acetaminophen, phenazopyridine, and phenacetin are LOW-risk at usual therapeutic doses — useful 'safe' contrast items so students don't reflexively avoid every drug. Acetaminophen is the preferred analgesic/antipyretic in G6PD deficiency. Phenazopyridine and phenacetin can rarely cause methemoglobinemia/hemolysis at high doses, but standard dosing is generally tolerated.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Phenazopyridine should be given freely for UTI dysuria in G6PD deficiency because it is on the low-risk list.' Discriminator: phenazopyridine is lower-risk than sulfa drugs but can still cause methemoglobinemia and hemolysis at high or prolonged doses, especially in renal impairment — acetaminophen is the truly safe analgesic anchor here.
12. Vitamin K analogues
Vitamin K splits on oxidant risk by form: synthetic MENADIONE (vitamin K3) is a water-soluble quinone that redox-cycles and generates oxidative stress → hemolysis and even kernicterus in G6PD-deficient/neonatal patients, whereas PHYTONADIONE (vitamin K1) is safe and is the form used clinically for warfarin reversal and neonatal prophylaxis. The board point is 'know which vitamin K': K1 safe, K3 dangerous.
⚠️ WRONG: 'All vitamin K preparations must be avoided in G6PD deficiency.' Discriminator: phytonadione (K1) is SAFE — only synthetic menadione (K3) is the oxidant to avoid.
13. Quinine/chloroquine
Quinine and chloroquine are moderate-risk oxidant antimalarials — at standard therapeutic antimalarial doses hemolysis is uncommon in mild (Class II-III) G6PD variants, and the WHO permits chloroquine for acute malaria treatment even in deficiency because the risk of untreated malaria outweighs mild hemolysis. Risk rises with severe (Mediterranean) variants. They sit in the 'medium-low risk' zone of the scene, distinct from primaquine.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Chloroquine must never be given to a G6PD-deficient patient with acute malaria.' Discriminator: chloroquine is moderate-risk and the danger of untreated malaria exceeds the hemolytic risk — it is permitted for acute treatment; it is PRIMAQUINE/tafenoquine (radical cure) that demand pre-screening.
14. Hydroxychloroquine
Hydroxychloroquine (used for malaria, SLE, rheumatoid arthritis) is, like chloroquine, only a LOW-to-moderate oxidant risk in G6PD deficiency at standard doses — clinically significant hemolysis is uncommon and it is generally not contraindicated, in contrast to primaquine/tafenoquine. The board point is to separate the 4-aminoquinolines (chloroquine, hydroxychloroquine = lower risk) from the 8-aminoquinolines (primaquine, tafenoquine = high risk requiring pre-screening). Routine G6PD screening is not mandated before hydroxychloroquine.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Hydroxychloroquine carries the same high hemolytic risk as primaquine and must never be used in G6PD deficiency.' Discriminator: the high-risk antimalarials are the 8-aminoquinolines (primaquine, tafenoquine) for hypnozoite radical cure; the 4-aminoquinolines hydroxychloroquine and chloroquine are low/moderate risk and are routinely used in SLE/RA without G6PD screening.
15. Sulfasalazine / sulfadimidine
Sulfasalazine (IBD/RA) and sulfadimidine are sulfonamide-containing drugs that fall in the MEDIUM-risk band: their sulfa component can trigger oxidant hemolysis, uncommon in mild A- variants at usual doses but a real risk in severe Mediterranean-type deficiency. They share the 'sulfa' liability with sulfamethoxazole but are generally lower-yield than TMP-SMX.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Sulfasalazine is an aminosalicylate, not a sulfonamide, so it is safe in G6PD deficiency.' Discriminator: sulfasalazine = sulfapyridine + 5-ASA — the sulfapyridine moiety is an oxidant sulfonamide and carries hemolytic risk.
16. Sulfamethoxazole (SMX fox)
Sulfonamide antibiotics — sulfamethoxazole (in TMP-SMX/Bactrim), sulfadiazine, sulfasalazine, sulfadimidine — are classic oxidant triggers of G6PD hemolysis and appear on virtually every 'avoid' list. TMP-SMX is high-yield because it is so widely prescribed (UTI, PCP prophylaxis). Note the related sulfa drug dapsone is also a sulfone oxidant. Distinguish from non-antibiotic sulfonamides (thiazides, sulfonylureas, celecoxib), which carry low hemolytic risk.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A G6PD-deficient patient allergic-list says avoid sulfa, so thiazide diuretics and sulfonylureas are also unsafe.' Discriminator: it is the antibacterial sulfonamides (sulfamethoxazole, sulfadiazine, sulfasalazine) that trigger hemolysis; non-antibiotic sulfonamides (thiazides, glipizide, furosemide, celecoxib) do not cross-react and are not significant oxidant triggers.
17. Nitrofurantoin & fluoroquinolones
Nitrofurantoin (a nitrofuran UTI antibiotic) is a well-established oxidant trigger of G6PD hemolysis; some fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, norfloxacin, moxifloxacin) are also implicated. The board scenario is a woman treated for a UTI with nitrofurantoin who develops dark urine, jaundice, and anemia with bite cells/Heinz bodies. Choose an alternative such as fosfomycin or a beta-lactam in a known G6PD-deficient patient.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Nitrofurantoin is contraindicated in G6PD only because of its known pulmonary and hepatic toxicity, not hemolysis.' Discriminator: nitrofurantoin is a direct OXIDANT that causes acute hemolysis in G6PD deficiency — the smear shows bite cells and Heinz bodies; its separate pulmonary/hepatic toxicities are unrelated to the G6PD contraindication.
18. Low-risk bottom shelf (acetaminophen SAFE)
The bottom shelf is the SAFE/contrast list. ACETAMINOPHEN (paracetamol) is safe at standard doses and is the preferred analgesic/antipyretic in G6PD deficiency — it is NOT an oxidant despite a common misconception. Acetanilide and phenacetin (historical paracetamol precursors) caused methemoglobinemia via aniline metabolites but acetaminophen itself does not. Phenazopyridine is the borderline item — a urinary azo-dye analgesic that CAN cause methemoglobinemia and hemolysis at high doses, so it is 'low-but-not-zero' risk.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Acetaminophen is contraindicated in G6PD deficiency.' Discriminator: acetaminophen is SAFE and preferred — the real low-shelf trap is phenazopyridine, which can still cause hemolysis/methemoglobinemia at high doses.
19. Severity & screen-first rule
Variant severity decides how strict the drug list is: G6PD A- (African) is the MILD class-III variant — more drugs are tolerable and self-limited hemolysis only affects older cells, whereas Mediterranean (MED) is a SEVERE class-II variant where all listed oxidants should be avoided. Always SCREEN G6PD before an 8-aminoquinoline (primaquine, tafenoquine) — fatal hemolysis has occurred when missed, and during an acute episode a level can be falsely normal because the most deficient cells have already lysed.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A single G6PD drug-avoidance list applies equally to every patient.' Discriminator: A- (mild) tolerates more than Mediterranean (severe); and a normal G6PD assay DURING acute hemolysis does not exclude deficiency — repeat 2-3 months later.
