📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. ANCA small-vessel vasculitis
ANCA-associated vasculitis is a pauci-immune SMALL-vessel vasculitis comprising three entities — GPA (granulomatosis with polyangiitis), MPA (microscopic polyangiitis), and EGPA (eosinophilic GPA / Churg-Strauss). ANCAs (antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies) activate primed neutrophils that degranulate and damage capillary/venule/arteriole walls, classically producing a pulmonary-renal syndrome. It is the most common cause of rapidly progressive (crescentic) glomerulonephritis in adults.
⚠️ Do not confuse small-vessel ANCA vasculitis with MEDIUM-vessel polyarteritis nodosa (PAN): PAN is ANCA-NEGATIVE, spares the lungs and glomeruli, causes microaneurysms ('rosary beads' on angiography), and is associated with hepatitis B — picking PAN for an alveolar-hemorrhage-plus-GN stem is wrong.
2. Pauci-immune
Immunofluorescence shows FEW or NO immune deposits ('pauci-immune'), and electron microscopy shows scant deposits — the inflammation is antibody-driven against neutrophils, not from immune-complex or anti-GBM deposition. This is one of the three IF patterns of crescentic GN (the others being linear anti-GBM and granular immune-complex).
⚠️ The WRONG answer is LINEAR IgG along the GBM — that is anti-GBM (Goodpasture) disease, which also causes pulmonary-renal syndrome but is treated with urgent plasmapheresis + immunosuppression and is distinguished by anti-GBM antibodies. Granular ('lumpy-bumpy') IF instead indicates immune-complex GN (e.g., lupus, post-infectious).
3. Crescentic RPGN
ANCA vasculitis causes crescentic, rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN): biopsy shows cellular crescents (proliferating parietal epithelial cells, macrophages, fibrin) filling Bowman's space and focal segmental necrotizing GN. Clinically the nephritic picture is hematuria with dysmorphic RBCs and RBC casts, sub-nephrotic proteinuria, hypertension, and a creatinine that doubles over days to weeks. A renal-limited form (no extrarenal disease) exists.
⚠️ RPGN is a NEPHROLOGIC EMERGENCY — the trap is delaying diagnosis to 'observe,' allowing irreversible glomerular scarring. Days-to-weeks of rising creatinine with active urine sediment demands urgent biopsy and empiric immunosuppression, not watchful waiting.
4. Normal complement
Serum complement (C3, C4) is NORMAL in ANCA vasculitis because the disease is pauci-immune and not complement-consuming. This helps separate it from the LOW-complement nephritic GNs.
⚠️ The WRONG answer is LOW C3/C4 — that signals a complement-consuming/immune-complex GN: post-streptococcal GN, lupus nephritis, membranoproliferative GN, or cryoglobulinemia. Normal complement with a pulmonary-renal syndrome points to ANCA vasculitis (or anti-GBM), not these.
5. GPA = PR3 / c-ANCA
GPA (formerly Wegener's) is associated with c-ANCA / anti-PR3 (proteinase-3) in ~80–90% of cases and features NECROTIZING GRANULOMATOUS inflammation of the upper airway (sinusitis, saddle-nose deformity, otitis, subglottic stenosis), lower airway (nodules/cavities, hemoptysis), and kidney. It tends to relapse more than MPA. Induction is steroids plus rituximab or cyclophosphamide.
⚠️ The WRONG pairing is MPO / p-ANCA for GPA — GPA is the PR3 / c-ANCA disease with GRANULOMAS and prominent ENT involvement. Assigning p-ANCA or 'no granulomas' to GPA is the classic swap with MPA.
6. MPA = MPO / p-ANCA
MPA is associated with p-ANCA / anti-MPO (myeloperoxidase) and causes pauci-immune necrotizing small-vessel vasculitis (glomerulonephritis, pulmonary capillaritis/alveolar hemorrhage, palpable purpura, mononeuritis multiplex) WITHOUT granulomas and WITHOUT the destructive upper-airway/sinus disease of GPA.
⚠️ The WRONG feature is GRANULOMAS — MPA has NONE (that is GPA). Also avoid assigning nasopharyngeal/saddle-nose disease to MPA. The discriminator from GPA: MPO/p-ANCA, no granulomas, no ENT destruction.
7. EGPA = asthma + eosinophilia
EGPA (Churg-Strauss) features late-onset ASTHMA, allergic rhinitis/nasal polyps, marked peripheral EOSINOPHILIA (>10%), eosinophilic tissue infiltration, mononeuritis multiplex, and cardiac involvement (a leading cause of mortality). Only ~40–60% are ANCA-positive, and when positive it is usually MPO/p-ANCA; ANCA-positive patients more often have GN/neuropathy while ANCA-negative patients more often have cardiac/lung disease. Mepolizumab (anti-IL-5) is used in addition to steroids.
⚠️ The trap is expecting EGPA to be reliably ANCA-positive — it is ANCA-negative in roughly half of cases, so a negative ANCA does NOT exclude it. The clinching combination is asthma + eosinophilia + vasculitis, not the serology alone.
8. Hemoptysis / pulmonary
Pulmonary capillaritis causes diffuse alveolar hemorrhage — hemoptysis, dropping hemoglobin, diffuse alveolar infiltrates on imaging, and a RISING DLCO from intra-alveolar blood — which together with the glomerulonephritis constitutes the pulmonary-renal syndrome. This is a life-threatening presentation requiring urgent immunosuppression and often plasmapheresis.
⚠️ The differential for pulmonary-renal syndrome includes anti-GBM (Goodpasture) and lupus, not just ANCA vasculitis — the trap is assuming hemoptysis-plus-GN is automatically ANCA; serology (ANCA vs anti-GBM vs ANA/complement) and biopsy IF distinguish them.
9. High mortality if untreated
Untreated ANCA-associated GN historically carried very high mortality and rapid progression to ESRD; outcomes improved dramatically with immunosuppression. Treat empirically once the diagnosis is strongly suspected rather than waiting for full confirmation, because every day of delay costs nephrons.
⚠️ The trap is withholding therapy pending every confirmatory result — in suspected RPGN with active disease, empiric high-dose steroids are started immediately while biopsy/serology are pending; delay leads to irreversible renal loss and death.
10. Plasmapheresis (severe)
Plasma exchange removes circulating ANCA and inflammatory mediators and is reserved for SEVERE disease — notably diffuse alveolar hemorrhage and severe renal failure (e.g., dialysis-dependent or rapidly rising creatinine). After the PEXIVAS trial its routine use was scaled back, but it remains indicated for alveolar hemorrhage and concomitant anti-GBM disease.
⚠️ The trap is offering plasmapheresis as routine first-line for ALL ANCA vasculitis — it is NOT; it is reserved for severe presentations (alveolar hemorrhage, severe/dialysis-requiring renal failure, or overlap with anti-GBM). Standard induction is steroids + rituximab/cyclophosphamide.
11. Induction: steroids + cyclo/rituximab
Induction therapy is high-dose glucocorticoids (often IV methylprednisolone pulses then oral prednisone taper) PLUS either rituximab (anti-CD20; preferred for relapsing disease, PR3/GPA, and to spare fertility) or cyclophosphamide. Avacopan (C5a-receptor antagonist) is a newer steroid-sparing add-on. Add PJP prophylaxis (TMP-SMX) given the immunosuppression.
⚠️ The trap is using steroids ALONE — monotherapy is inadequate for organ-threatening ANCA vasculitis; a steroid-sparing agent (rituximab or cyclophosphamide) is required for induction. Forgetting PJP prophylaxis is a second common oversight.
12. Maintenance 1–2 yrs
After remission, maintenance immunosuppression continues for at least 1–2 years (often longer in relapsing/PR3 disease) using rituximab (preferred) or azatioprine/methotrexate, with the steroid tapered off. ANCA titer trends and clinical surveillance guide relapse detection.
⚠️ The trap is stopping immunosuppression once the patient is in remission — relapse risk is high (especially GPA/PR3), so maintenance therapy and monitoring are continued for years, not discontinued at first remission.
13. Biopsy / ANCA titer
Diagnosis rests on ANCA serology (PR3/c-ANCA vs MPO/p-ANCA) PLUS tissue confirmation — renal biopsy showing pauci-immune necrotizing crescentic GN. ANCA titers can track disease activity and relapse, though they should be interpreted alongside clinical findings rather than treated in isolation.
⚠️ The trap is diagnosing or re-treating on ANCA titer alone — a rising titer in an asymptomatic patient does not by itself mandate therapy, and a positive ANCA without compatible clinical/biopsy findings is not sufficient. Tissue biopsy remains the gold standard for confirmation.
