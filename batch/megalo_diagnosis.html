📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. MCV >100 opens case
Macrocytosis (MCV >100 fL; megaloblastic anemia often >110-115) triggers the workup. First branch: megaloblastic (B12/folate deficiency, antimetabolite drugs) vs non-megaloblastic (alcohol, liver disease, hypothyroidism, MDS, reticulocytosis). The presence of hypersegmented neutrophils points to megaloblastic. Mnemonic: MCV up = open the case, then split mega vs non-mega.
⚠️ WRONG: treating every high MCV as B12/folate deficiency. Discriminator: non-megaloblastic causes (alcohol, liver, hypothyroidism, MDS) have NORMAL B12/folate and NO hypersegmented neutrophils — confirm with smear + vitamin levels before replacing.
2. Hypersegmented neutrophil
Hypersegmented neutrophils (≥5 lobes, or any cell with 6+) are an EARLY and relatively specific clue to megaloblastic anemia and can appear before macrocytosis or anemia. They reflect impaired DNA synthesis in the granulocyte line. Mnemonic: 5+ lobes = megaloblastic flag.
⚠️ WRONG: dismissing a few hypersegmented neutrophils when the MCV is normal. Discriminator: hypersegmentation can precede anemia/macrocytosis and persists for days after treatment — it warrants B12/folate testing even with a normal CBC.
3. Oval macrocytes / anisopoikilo
The smear shows OVAL macrocytes (macro-ovalocytes) with anisocytosis and poikilocytosis (high RDW). Oval shape helps distinguish megaloblastic macrocytosis from the round macrocytes of liver disease/alcohol. Mnemonic: megaloblastic = oval macrocytes; non-megaloblastic = round macrocytes.
⚠️ WRONG: equating round and oval macrocytes. Discriminator: round macrocytes + target cells suggest liver disease/alcohol (non-megaloblastic); oval macrocytes + hypersegmented neutrophils suggest B12/folate deficiency (megaloblastic).
4. Hypercellular marrow, megaloblasts
Marrow is HYPERcellular with megaloblasts (large cells with nuclear-cytoplasmic asynchrony — immature open nucleus in a mature/hemoglobinized cytoplasm) and giant bands/metamyelocytes. Despite the busy marrow, output is poor (ineffective erythropoiesis). Mnemonic: full factory, broken conveyor belt → cells die before shipping.
⚠️ WRONG: confusing megaloblastic hypercellular marrow with aplastic anemia. Discriminator: aplastic anemia is HYPOcellular (fatty marrow); megaloblastic anemia is HYPERcellular with megaloblasts and ineffective erythropoiesis (high LDH/indirect bili).
5. Mimics AML
A florid megaloblastic marrow with nuclear-cytoplasmic asynchrony can mimic AML/MDS. ALWAYS check B12, folate, MMA, and homocysteine BEFORE committing to a bone-marrow malignancy diagnosis or chemotherapy. Mnemonic: confirm the vitamins before you call it leukemia.
⚠️ WRONG (the trap): treating the marrow picture as acute leukemia immediately. Discriminator: megaloblastic change corrects fully with B12/folate replacement, whereas AML does not — a reversible vitamin deficiency must be excluded first to avoid unnecessary chemotherapy.
6. LDH & indirect bili high
Intramedullary (ineffective) erythropoiesis destroys precursors in the marrow → markedly HIGH LDH, high indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin, low haptoglobin, and increased urobilinogen — a hemolysis-like pattern WITHOUT external bleeding. LDH is often strikingly elevated. Mnemonic: cells die in the marrow → high LDH, high indirect bili, low hapto.
⚠️ WRONG: diagnosing primary hemolytic anemia from high LDH + indirect bili + low haptoglobin. Discriminator: in megaloblastic anemia the reticulocyte count is LOW (production failure) and the DAT/Coombs is negative, whereas true hemolysis shows a HIGH reticulocyte count.
7. Serum B12 (suspect 1)
A low serum B12 (roughly <148 pmol/L or <200 pg/mL; clearly deficient often <118-148) supports B12 deficiency, but the assay is imperfect — pregnancy, OCPs, and myeloma can falsely lower it, and high levels occur in liver disease/myeloproliferative states. Mnemonic: low B12 is suggestive, not definitive — there's a borderline gray zone.
⚠️ WRONG: relying on serum B12 alone in the borderline range (~150-300 ng/L). Discriminator: in the gray zone, confirm with MMA + homocysteine (both high → B12). Beware false-low B12 (pregnancy, OCP, myeloma) and false-NORMAL B12 in true deficiency (haptocorrin binding).
8. Serum folate (suspect 2)
Serum folate reflects RECENT intake and can normalize after a single good meal or rise paradoxically in B12 deficiency (methylfolate trap). A low serum folate suggests folate deficiency but is a weak standalone test. Mnemonic: serum folate = yesterday's salad, not the body's stores.
⚠️ WRONG: trusting a normal serum folate to exclude folate deficiency, or a low one to confirm it. Discriminator: use RBC folate (tissue stores) for a more reliable assessment, and remember B12 deficiency can raise serum folate via the methylfolate trap.
9. RBC folate more reliable
RBC folate reflects tissue folate over the prior ~120 days and is more reliable than serum folate, but it FALLS in both folate AND B12 deficiency (methylfolate trap depletes usable tissue folate). Mnemonic: RBC folate = tissue truth, but it drops in both deficiencies.
⚠️ WRONG: using a low RBC folate to confirm folate deficiency specifically. Discriminator: RBC folate is low in B12 deficiency too — check B12 + MMA before labeling it isolated folate deficiency.
10. MMA high in B12 only
Methylmalonic acid (MMA) is HIGH in B12 deficiency (adenosyl-B12 is the cofactor for methylmalonyl-CoA mutase) but NORMAL in folate deficiency — the single most useful test to separate the two and to resolve a borderline B12. Mnemonic: MMA = B12's fingerprint (folate leaves no print).
⚠️ WRONG (key distractor): 'MMA is high in both B12 and folate deficiency.' It is NOT — folate has a NORMAL MMA. Discriminator: high MMA + high homocysteine = B12; normal MMA + high homocysteine = folate.
11. Homocysteine high in both
Homocysteine is elevated in BOTH B12 and folate deficiency (both are methionine-synthase cofactors), so it confirms a deficiency exists but cannot tell which vitamin. Pair it with MMA to separate them. Mnemonic: homocysteine = shared road; MMA = the fork.
⚠️ WRONG: using homocysteine to distinguish B12 from folate. Discriminator: it rises in both — only MMA (high in B12, normal in folate) makes the distinction. Isolated high homocysteine with normal MMA favors folate (or B6/MTHFR causes).
12. Borderline → MMA + homocysteine
For an equivocal/borderline B12 (~150-300 ng/L), do NOT decide on the level alone — order MMA + homocysteine. BOTH elevated → true B12 deficiency; both normal → B12 deficiency effectively excluded. Mnemonic: gray-zone B12? Let MMA + homocysteine be the tiebreaker.
⚠️ WRONG: calling a borderline B12 'normal' and stopping. Discriminator: functional deficiency hides in the gray zone — confirm with metabolites (MMA/homocysteine) and treat if elevated, especially when neuro signs are present.
13. Iron deficiency masks macro
Coexisting iron deficiency (microcytic) offsets the macrocytosis of B12/folate deficiency → a NORMAL MCV but HIGH RDW and a dimorphic smear (both micro- and macrocytes). Mnemonic: micro + macro = normal-looking MCV; the high RDW betrays it.
⚠️ WRONG (trap): 'a normal MCV rules out megaloblastic anemia.' It does NOT. Discriminator: a high RDW, hypersegmented neutrophils, or anemia with a normal MCV should prompt iron studies + B12/folate — combined deficiencies hide behind a normal MCV.
14. Reticulocyte count low
Despite the hemolysis-like labs (high LDH/indirect bili), the reticulocyte count is INAPPROPRIATELY LOW because the underlying problem is production failure (ineffective erythropoiesis), not increased peripheral destruction. After treatment, a reticulocyte response peaks at days 5-7. Mnemonic: marrow can't ship cells → low retics now, retic spike after vitamins.
⚠️ WRONG: a high LDH + low haptoglobin leading to a primary hemolysis diagnosis. Discriminator: a LOW reticulocyte count plus oval macrocytes and hypersegmented neutrophils points to megaloblastic production failure, not hemolysis (which would show high reticulocytes).
