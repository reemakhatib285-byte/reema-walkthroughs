📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Clinical scenario board
Always compute the CSF:serum glucose RATIO, not the absolute CSF glucose. A ratio <0.4 (here 50/150 = 0.33) indicates a glucose-consuming process — bacterial, TB, or fungal — because organisms and inflammatory cells metabolize glucose. Normal ratio is ~0.6 (CSF ≈ two-thirds of serum). A weeks-long subacute course in a 75yo with a low ratio points strongly to TB or fungal rather than acute bacterial.
⚠️ WRONG answer: calling this viral because the absolute CSF glucose (50) looks normal. A 'normal-looking' CSF glucose can still be low relative to a high serum glucose — the ratio 0.33 is the discriminator. Hyperglycemic/diabetic patients especially need the ratio, not the raw number.
2. Opening pressure (bacterial)
Bacterial meningitis classically raises opening pressure (>180 mmH2O, often 200-500), present in ~90% of cases, from purulent exudate impairing CSF resorption at the arachnoid granulations. Normal OP is 6-20 cmH2O (≈80-200 mmH2O) measured with the patient in the lateral decubitus position with legs extended. Very high OP also raises concern for cryptococcal meningitis, which may need serial LPs for pressure relief.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming a normal opening pressure rules out bacterial meningitis. OP can be normal early or in partially treated cases — diagnosis rests on the full CSF profile (PMN pleocytosis, low glucose, high protein, Gram stain), not pressure alone.
3. Bacterial: PMN cell count
Bacterial CSF shows a high WBC count, typically 1,000-5,000 cells/uL (range 100-10,000), with NEUTROPHIL (PMN) predominance (>80%). The purulent cellular response makes the fluid turbid/cloudy. A markedly elevated count with PMN dominance plus low glucose is the bacterial signature.
⚠️ WRONG: interpreting any PMN-predominant CSF as automatically bacterial. Very EARLY viral meningitis (first 24-48h) can be PMN-predominant before shifting to lymphocytes; the discriminator is glucose — viral keeps NORMAL glucose while bacterial drops it. Repeat the LP in 12-24h if uncertain.
4. Bacterial: low glucose, high protein
Bacterial CSF = LOW glucose (<40 mg/dL or ratio <0.4) and HIGH protein (often >100-200 mg/dL; normal <45). Low glucose reflects bacterial/leukocyte metabolism and impaired glucose transport; high protein reflects blood-brain barrier breakdown. Gram stain is positive in 60-90% and culture in ~80% (yield falls after antibiotics).
⚠️ WRONG: choosing viral because protein is 'only mildly elevated.' Partially treated bacterial meningitis can blunt the classic findings (less profound hypoglycorrhachia, lower yield Gram stain) — recent antibiotic exposure is the clue, and empiric therapy should not be withheld.
5. Viral: clear tube
Viral (aseptic) meningitis CSF is CLEAR/colorless with normal to mildly elevated opening pressure. Enteroviruses cause >85% of cases (summer-fall); diagnosis is by CSF RT-PCR. Patients are typically well-appearing with intact mentation — altered mentation should redirect you toward encephalitis or bacterial disease.
⚠️ WRONG: equating 'clear CSF' with 'no meningitis.' Early bacterial meningitis or partially treated cases can also look grossly clear; the cell differential and glucose define the category, not gross appearance.
6. Viral: lymphocytes
Viral CSF shows a modest LYMPHOCYTIC pleocytosis, usually 10-500 cells/uL (rarely >1,000), with mononuclear predominance. The count is far lower than the thousands typical of bacterial disease. Enterovirus, HSV-2, VZV, and West Nile all produce this lymphocytic pattern.
⚠️ WRONG: ruling out viral because the very first tap shows PMNs. Early enteroviral meningitis commonly has an initial neutrophilic shift that converts to lymphocytic within 12-24h — the preserved glucose distinguishes it from bacterial. Recheck rather than over-treating.
7. Viral: normal glucose
Viral CSF = NORMAL glucose (ratio >0.4-0.6) with normal to mildly elevated protein (20-80 mg/dL). Normal glucose is THE single best discriminator separating viral from bacterial/TB/fungal meningitis. Mild protein elevation reflects limited barrier disruption.
⚠️ WRONG: calling lymphocytic CSF with LOW glucose 'viral.' Low glucose plus lymphocytes is the hallmark of TB or fungal meningitis (and sometimes Listeria), NOT viral — viral preserves glucose. Mumps and LCMV are rare viral exceptions that can mildly lower glucose.
8. West Nile / WNV warning
West Nile virus is diagnosed by CSF (and serum) IgM antibody, not Gram stain or culture (which are negative). It is mosquito-borne with birds/crows as the reservoir, peaks in late summer, and favors older adults. CSF is lymphocytic with normal glucose, and it classically causes acute asymmetric FLACCID paralysis (poliomyelitis-like, anterior horn involvement).
⚠️ WRONG: ordering only CSF viral PCR for suspected West Nile. WNV PCR has LOW sensitivity in CSF because viremia has often cleared by symptom onset — CSF IgM is the test of choice. Picking PCR misses the diagnosis.
9. TB/Fungal: hazy tube
TB and fungal (cryptococcal, coccidioidal) meningitis produce a HAZY/xanthochromic CSF with a subacute-to-chronic course over weeks. A pellicle (spider-web clot) may form on standing in TB. Cryptococcus is identified by India ink (~70-80%) or, better, CSF cryptococcal antigen (CrAg, >95% sensitive), especially in HIV with CD4 <100.
⚠️ WRONG: attributing a chronic lymphocytic meningitis purely to one infectious cause without considering the differential — chronic basilar lymphocytic meningitis also includes neurosyphilis, sarcoidosis, and carcinomatous meningitis. TB classically involves the basal cisterns and can cause cranial nerve palsies and hydrocephalus.
10. TB/Fungal: lymphocytes
TB/fungal CSF shows a LYMPHOCYTIC pleocytosis (typically 100-500 cells/uL). Early TB meningitis can have a transient PMN phase before becoming lymphocytic. The combination of lymphocytes with markedly LOW glucose is what separates it from viral (which keeps glucose normal).
⚠️ WRONG: stopping the workup at 'lymphocytic = viral.' Lymphocytic pleocytosis with LOW glucose and HIGH protein over a subacute course is TB/fungal until proven otherwise — send AFB smear/culture, TB PCR (Xpert MTB/RIF), and cryptococcal antigen, not just viral PCR.
11. TB/Fungal: very high protein, low glucose
TB/fungal CSF = markedly HIGH protein (100-500 mg/dL, can exceed 1 g/dL in TB with CSF block) and LOW glucose — biochemically mimicking bacterial meningitis but with a LYMPHOCYTIC rather than neutrophilic differential. Very high protein in TB correlates with spinal block and worse prognosis.
⚠️ WRONG: choosing acute bacterial therapy and stopping there in a low-glucose, high-protein CSF that is LYMPHOCYTIC. The lymphocyte predominance plus subacute weeks-long course flags TB/fungal — you must add anti-TB therapy/antifungals and targeted testing, not treat as pyogenic bacterial alone.
12. AFB smear / culture
TB meningitis AFB smear has LOW yield (10-40%), so send LARGE-volume CSF (≥6-10 mL, ideally serial taps) for AFB smear, mycobacterial culture (gold standard but slow, 2-6 weeks), and NAAT (Xpert MTB/RIF Ultra). Empiric RIPE therapy plus adjunctive corticosteroids should be started on clinical suspicion without waiting for culture.
⚠️ WRONG: excluding TB meningitis because the AFB smear is negative. A single negative smear does NOT rule out TB given its poor sensitivity — multiple large-volume taps, culture, and PCR are needed, and empiric treatment should begin when suspicion is high because delay drives mortality.
13. Clock: subacute course
Tempo is a key discriminator: acute bacterial meningitis evolves over hours to 1-2 days, viral over days, while TB and fungal meningitis progress SUBACUTELY over weeks. A weeks-long history of headache, low-grade fever, and malaise with a lymphocytic, low-glucose CSF should trigger a TB/fungal workup.
⚠️ WRONG: defaulting to acute pyogenic bacterial therapy in a patient with weeks of symptoms. The subacute timeline argues against typical pyogenic bacteria and for TB/fungal (or partially treated bacterial) — anchoring on 'bacterial' misses the slow-burn organisms.
14. Comparison summary strip
Master pattern to memorize: BACTERIAL = cloudy, PMN, glucose LOW, protein HIGH, OP high; VIRAL = clear, lymphocytes, glucose NORMAL, protein mildly up; TB/FUNGAL = hazy, lymphocytes, glucose LOW, protein VERY high, subacute. The pivotal two-variable shortcut is CELL TYPE (PMN vs lymphocyte) × GLUCOSE (low vs normal).
⚠️ WRONG: memorizing cell type alone. The classic exam trap is lymphocytes-with-LOW-glucose (TB/fungal) being mislabeled viral, or early PMN viral being mislabeled bacterial — you must read cell type AND glucose together, and factor in tempo and recent antibiotics.
15. Normal CSF reference values
Added reference anchor: normal CSF has WBC <5 cells/uL (all mononuclear; up to 20 in neonates), glucose 45-80 mg/dL with ratio ~0.6, protein 15-45 mg/dL, and opening pressure 6-20 cmH2O. Every abnormal profile is judged against these baselines.
⚠️ WRONG: ignoring a 'few' WBCs as normal. In an adult, even 6-10 WBC/uL is abnormal pleocytosis and warrants explanation; only <5 mononuclear cells is truly normal (neonates tolerate more).
