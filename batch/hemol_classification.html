⚠️ Board traps 13
  1. Hemolysis = destruction — WRONG: 'Acute GI blood loss is a form of hemolysis because both cause anemia with high reticulocytes.' Hemolysis destroys RBCs in vivo (high LDH, high indirect bili, low haptoglobin); blood loss removes intact cells from the body with NORMAL LDH/bilirubin/haptoglobin. Discriminator: the hemolysis biochemical panel is normal in pure blood loss.
  2. Blood loss = right — WRONG: 'A high reticulocyte count always indicates hemolysis.' Reticulocytosis is also driven by acute blood loss and by recovery from a treated nutritional deficiency. Discriminator: confirm true hemolysis with high LDH, high indirect bilirubin, and LOW haptoglobin — all normal in simple blood loss.
  3. Inherited vs acquired — WRONG: 'Intrinsic defects are always inherited and extrinsic causes are always acquired.' PNH breaks this rule — it is an ACQUIRED intrinsic (intracorpuscular) defect from a somatic PIGA mutation. Discriminator: classify PNH as acquired-but-intrinsic; diagnose by flow cytometry (CD55/CD59/FLAER loss), not family history.
  4. Acute vs chronic — WRONG: 'In chronic hereditary hemolysis a sudden severe drop in Hgb with a LOW reticulocyte count means worsening hemolysis.' A falling Hgb with collapsing reticulocytes signals an aplastic crisis — classically parvovirus B19 shutting down erythropoiesis. Discriminator: hemolysis raises retics; a low retic during chronic hemolysis means the marrow stopped (parvovirus), not more destruction.
  5. Intravascular stall — WRONG: 'Intravascular hemolysis is recognized mainly by a high unconjugated bilirubin.' The dominant findings are hemoglobinuria, hemosiderinuria, and very low haptoglobin; bilirubin is the hallmark of EXTRAvascular hemolysis. Discriminator: free Hb in plasma/urine and absent haptoglobin define intravascular; indirect hyperbilirubinemia with splenomegaly defines extravascular.
  6. Hemoglobinuria — WRONG: 'A positive urine dipstick for blood with no RBCs on microscopy means hematuria from a stone.' Heme-positive dipstick with no RBCs indicates hemoglobinuria (intravascular hemolysis) or myoglobinuria (rhabdomyolysis), not hematuria. Discriminator: check plasma color/free Hb and CK — pink plasma + low haptoglobin = hemoglobinuria; high CK + normal haptoglobin = myoglobinuria.
  7. Low haptoglobin — WRONG: 'Haptoglobin rises in hemolysis as part of the acute-phase response.' Haptoglobin is CONSUMED by binding free Hb, so it DROPS in hemolysis. Pitfall: because it is an acute-phase reactant, concurrent inflammation/infection can falsely keep it normal — interpret alongside LDH, bilirubin, and the smear.
  8. PNH/G6PD/MAHA — WRONG: 'PNH causes hemolysis only, so treatment is just transfusion and steroids.' PNH's major killer is THROMBOSIS (especially hepatic/abdominal veins) from complement activation; terminal complement inhibitors (eculizumab/ravulizumab) reduce hemolysis and thrombosis. Discriminator: diagnose by flow cytometry (FLAER, CD55/CD59), vaccinate against meningococcus before complement inhibition.
  9. Extravascular stall — WRONG: 'Extravascular hemolysis produces hemoglobinuria and very low haptoglobin like intravascular disease.' Extravascular lysis happens inside macrophages, so free plasma Hb is minimal — no hemoglobinuria, and haptoglobin is only modestly reduced. Discriminator: the dominant clue is indirect hyperbilirubinemia with splenomegaly, not hemoglobinuria.
  10. Unconjugated bilirubin — WRONG: 'Conjugated (direct) hyperbilirubinemia is the marker of hemolysis.' Hemolysis raises UNCONJUGATED (indirect) bilirubin; conjugated/direct elevation indicates hepatobiliary disease or obstruction. Discriminator: indirect-predominant hyperbilirubinemia with no bilirubinuria + hemolysis labs = hemolysis; direct hyperbilirubinemia with bilirubinuria = liver/biliary pathology.
  11. HS / WAIHA / PK — WRONG: 'Spherocytes confirm hereditary spherocytosis as the cause of extravascular hemolysis.' Warm AIHA also produces spherocytes via partial macrophage membrane removal. Discriminator: the DAT separates them — DAT-positive spherocytes = warm AIHA; DAT-negative with family history/high MCHC = hereditary spherocytosis (confirm EMA-binding test).
  12. Reticulocytes — neglected test — WRONG: 'A low reticulocyte count is expected during active hemolysis.' Active hemolysis should drive retics UP (often 4-6×); a low retic during hemolysis is a red flag for an aplastic crisis (parvovirus B19) or a coexisting deficiency (iron/folate). Discriminator: low retic + falling Hgb in a known hemolyzer = marrow shutdown, not more hemolysis.
  13. aHUS inherited exception — WRONG: 'A patient with MAHA, thrombocytopenia, and AKI should get urgent plasma exchange for presumed TTP and nothing else.' If ADAMTS13 is normal/near-normal and there is no Shiga-toxin source, suspect aHUS, which responds to complement inhibition (eculizumab), not plasma exchange. Discriminator: check ADAMTS13 (very low <10% = TTP) and stool Shiga toxin (typical HUS) before locking in TTP.
📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Hemolysis = destruction
Hemolytic anemia means RBCs are destroyed faster than the marrow (which can ramp up ~6-8×) can replace them, shortening the normal ~120-day lifespan. Destruction releases intracellular contents and heme breakdown products → high LDH, high indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin, low haptoglobin, and a compensatory reticulocytosis. The reticulocyte response is what distinguishes hemolysis from a production-failure anemia.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Acute GI blood loss is a form of hemolysis because both cause anemia with high reticulocytes.' Hemolysis destroys RBCs in vivo (high LDH, high indirect bili, low haptoglobin); blood loss removes intact cells from the body with NORMAL LDH/bilirubin/haptoglobin. Discriminator: the hemolysis biochemical panel is normal in pure blood loss.
2. Blood loss = right
Blood loss is the great mimic but is NOT hemolysis: intact RBCs exit the body, so there is no in-vivo cell rupture, no heme catabolism surge — LDH, indirect bilirubin, and haptoglobin stay normal. Reticulocytes do rise (after 3-5 days) as the marrow compensates, which is why retic alone cannot distinguish blood loss from hemolysis.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A high reticulocyte count always indicates hemolysis.' Reticulocytosis is also driven by acute blood loss and by recovery from a treated nutritional deficiency. Discriminator: confirm true hemolysis with high LDH, high indirect bilirubin, and LOW haptoglobin — all normal in simple blood loss.
3. Inherited vs acquired
First classification axis. INHERITED (usually intrinsic/intracorpuscular): membrane (hereditary spherocytosis, elliptocytosis), enzyme (G6PD, pyruvate kinase), hemoglobin (sickle cell, thalassemia). ACQUIRED (usually extrinsic/extracorpuscular): immune (AIHA, drug-induced), microangiopathic (TTP/HUS/DIC), infection, hypersplenism, and the key exception PNH (acquired but intrinsic).
⚠️ WRONG: 'Intrinsic defects are always inherited and extrinsic causes are always acquired.' PNH breaks this rule — it is an ACQUIRED intrinsic (intracorpuscular) defect from a somatic PIGA mutation. Discriminator: classify PNH as acquired-but-intrinsic; diagnose by flow cytometry (CD55/CD59/FLAER loss), not family history.
4. Acute vs chronic
Second axis: tempo. Acute hemolytic crises (G6PD after oxidant exposure, ABO transfusion reaction, TTP, severe cold agglutinin episode, malaria) present abruptly with rapid Hgb drop, dark urine, and systemic symptoms. Chronic hemolysis (hereditary spherocytosis, sickle cell, thalassemia, stable PNH) presents with longstanding anemia, splenomegaly, pigment gallstones, and aplastic crises with parvovirus B19.
⚠️ WRONG: 'In chronic hereditary hemolysis a sudden severe drop in Hgb with a LOW reticulocyte count means worsening hemolysis.' A falling Hgb with collapsing reticulocytes signals an aplastic crisis — classically parvovirus B19 shutting down erythropoiesis. Discriminator: hemolysis raises retics; a low retic during chronic hemolysis means the marrow stopped (parvovirus), not more destruction.
5. Intravascular stall
Intravascular hemolysis = lysis inside the vasculature → free plasma hemoglobin, hemoglobinemia, hemoglobinuria, hemosiderinuria, markedly LOW/absent haptoglobin, and very high LDH. Causes: PNH, G6PD oxidative crisis, MAHA (TTP/HUS/DIC) and mechanical valves, ABO-incompatible transfusion, and infections (malaria, Babesia, Clostridium).
⚠️ WRONG: 'Intravascular hemolysis is recognized mainly by a high unconjugated bilirubin.' The dominant findings are hemoglobinuria, hemosiderinuria, and very low haptoglobin; bilirubin is the hallmark of EXTRAvascular hemolysis. Discriminator: free Hb in plasma/urine and absent haptoglobin define intravascular; indirect hyperbilirubinemia with splenomegaly defines extravascular.
6. Hemoglobinuria
Hemoglobinuria (red-brown urine, dipstick positive for blood but NO RBCs on microscopy) occurs when plasma free hemoglobin exceeds haptoglobin's binding capacity and is filtered by the kidney — specific for intravascular hemolysis. Distinguish from hematuria (RBCs present on micro) and myoglobinuria (rhabdomyolysis, high CK).
⚠️ WRONG: 'A positive urine dipstick for blood with no RBCs on microscopy means hematuria from a stone.' Heme-positive dipstick with no RBCs indicates hemoglobinuria (intravascular hemolysis) or myoglobinuria (rhabdomyolysis), not hematuria. Discriminator: check plasma color/free Hb and CK — pink plasma + low haptoglobin = hemoglobinuria; high CK + normal haptoglobin = myoglobinuria.
7. Low haptoglobin
Haptoglobin is a hepatic acute-phase protein that binds free hemoglobin; the complex is cleared by macrophages, so haptoglobin falls (often to undetectable) in hemolysis — most dramatically intravascular. A LOW haptoglobin is the most specific single biochemical marker of hemolysis, and combined with a high LDH it is highly diagnostic.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Haptoglobin rises in hemolysis as part of the acute-phase response.' Haptoglobin is CONSUMED by binding free Hb, so it DROPS in hemolysis. Pitfall: because it is an acute-phase reactant, concurrent inflammation/infection can falsely keep it normal — interpret alongside LDH, bilirubin, and the smear.
8. PNH/G6PD/MAHA
Classic intravascular causes: PNH (acquired PIGA mutation → CD55/CD59 loss → complement lysis, treat with eculizumab/ravulizumab, thrombosis risk), ABO-incompatible transfusion (acute hemolytic reaction), G6PD crisis (favism/oxidant drugs, bite cells/Heinz bodies), and mechanical/MAHA (TTP/HUS/DIC, prosthetic valve). All show hemoglobinuria and very low haptoglobin.
⚠️ WRONG: 'PNH causes hemolysis only, so treatment is just transfusion and steroids.' PNH's major killer is THROMBOSIS (especially hepatic/abdominal veins) from complement activation; terminal complement inhibitors (eculizumab/ravulizumab) reduce hemolysis and thrombosis. Discriminator: diagnose by flow cytometry (FLAER, CD55/CD59), vaccinate against meningococcus before complement inhibition.
9. Extravascular stall
Extravascular hemolysis = splenic (and hepatic) macrophages phagocytose abnormal/antibody-coated RBCs. Findings: HIGH indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin, jaundice, splenomegaly, pigment gallstones, and only mildly low haptoglobin; NO hemoglobinuria. Causes: warm AIHA, hereditary spherocytosis, pyruvate kinase deficiency, and the thalassemias.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Extravascular hemolysis produces hemoglobinuria and very low haptoglobin like intravascular disease.' Extravascular lysis happens inside macrophages, so free plasma Hb is minimal — no hemoglobinuria, and haptoglobin is only modestly reduced. Discriminator: the dominant clue is indirect hyperbilirubinemia with splenomegaly, not hemoglobinuria.
10. Unconjugated bilirubin
Hemolysis raises INDIRECT (unconjugated) bilirubin because heme catabolism floods the liver with bilirubin faster than it can be conjugated/excreted; the conjugated fraction stays normal. This produces jaundice without dark urine/bilirubinuria (unconjugated bilirubin is albumin-bound and not filtered) and predisposes to calcium-bilirubinate pigment gallstones.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Conjugated (direct) hyperbilirubinemia is the marker of hemolysis.' Hemolysis raises UNCONJUGATED (indirect) bilirubin; conjugated/direct elevation indicates hepatobiliary disease or obstruction. Discriminator: indirect-predominant hyperbilirubinemia with no bilirubinuria + hemolysis labs = hemolysis; direct hyperbilirubinemia with bilirubinuria = liver/biliary pathology.
11. HS / WAIHA / PK
Classic extravascular causes: Hereditary Spherocytosis (ankyrin/spectrin/band 3 defect, DAT-negative spherocytes, high MCHC, positive EMA-binding/osmotic fragility, splenectomy curative), Warm AIHA (IgG, DAT-positive, spherocytes, treat with steroids ± rituximab; look for CLL/lymphoma/lupus/drugs), and Pyruvate Kinase deficiency (autosomal recessive ATP depletion, no Heinz bodies, echinocytes).
⚠️ WRONG: 'Spherocytes confirm hereditary spherocytosis as the cause of extravascular hemolysis.' Warm AIHA also produces spherocytes via partial macrophage membrane removal. Discriminator: the DAT separates them — DAT-positive spherocytes = warm AIHA; DAT-negative with family history/high MCHC = hereditary spherocytosis (confirm EMA-binding test).
12. Reticulocytes — neglected test
Reticulocytosis confirms the marrow is mounting an appropriate response to ongoing destruction and is a key first test in suspected hemolysis (RPI >2-3 = effective response). High retics also cause a mildly elevated MCV (large young cells) and polychromasia on smear. Persistently HIGH retics distinguish a destructive/loss process from a hypoproliferative anemia.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A low reticulocyte count is expected during active hemolysis.' Active hemolysis should drive retics UP (often 4-6×); a low retic during hemolysis is a red flag for an aplastic crisis (parvovirus B19) or a coexisting deficiency (iron/folate). Discriminator: low retic + falling Hgb in a known hemolyzer = marrow shutdown, not more hemolysis.
13. aHUS inherited exception
Atypical HUS (aHUS) is complement-mediated thrombotic microangiopathy from uncontrolled alternative-pathway activation (mutations/autoantibodies to complement regulators like factor H, factor I, MCP/CD46, or C3) — bridging the inherited and acquired columns. It presents as the MAHA triad (microangiopathic hemolysis + thrombocytopenia + acute kidney injury), with normal ADAMTS13 (unlike TTP) and no preceding bloody diarrhea (unlike Shiga-toxin HUS); treat with eculizumab.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A patient with MAHA, thrombocytopenia, and AKI should get urgent plasma exchange for presumed TTP and nothing else.' If ADAMTS13 is normal/near-normal and there is no Shiga-toxin source, suspect aHUS, which responds to complement inhibition (eculizumab), not plasma exchange. Discriminator: check ADAMTS13 (very low <10% = TTP) and stool Shiga toxin (typical HUS) before locking in TTP.
