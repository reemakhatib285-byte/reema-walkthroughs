📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Target 2.0-3.0
The standard INR target is 2.0-3.0 (goal 2.5) for the vast majority of indications: atrial fibrillation, treatment/prevention of VTE (DVT/PE), bioprosthetic valves (first 3 months), and most other indications. In nonvalvular AFib, anticoagulate when CHA2DS2-VASc ≥2 in men or ≥3 in women. INR <2.0 is subtherapeutic (thrombosis risk), INR >3.0 raises bleeding.
⚠️ WRONG: 'push the INR to 3.5-4 in AFib for stronger stroke protection.' Above an INR of ~3 the bleeding risk (especially intracranial hemorrhage) rises sharply with no added thromboembolic benefit — 2-3 is the AFib target, not higher.
2. Target 2.5-3.5
A HIGHER target of 2.5-3.5 (goal 3.0) is used for higher-thrombotic-risk mechanical valves — specifically mechanical MITRAL valves, and mechanical aortic valves with additional risk factors (AFib, prior thromboembolism, LV dysfunction). Low-risk newer-generation mechanical aortic valves target 2.0-3.0. Low-dose aspirin is often added.
⚠️ WRONG: 'a mechanical mitral valve can be managed with an INR target of 2.0-3.0 like AFib.' The mitral position is highly thrombogenic and requires the higher 2.5-3.5 target — under-targeting risks valve thrombosis and stroke.
3. Mechanical valve = warfarin
Mechanical heart valves REQUIRE warfarin; DOACs are contraindicated. The RE-ALIGN trial showed dabigatran caused MORE thromboembolism AND more bleeding than warfarin in mechanical valves, because contact activation on the artificial surface drives clotting that direct thrombin/Xa inhibitors fail to control. Warfarin is also preferred in moderate-to-severe (rheumatic) mitral stenosis.
⚠️ WRONG: 'switch a mechanical-valve patient from warfarin to apixaban for convenience.' DOACs are absolutely contraindicated in mechanical valves (RE-ALIGN: worse outcomes) — warfarin is the only approved oral anticoagulant for them.
4. INR 2.5-10, no bleed
Supratherapeutic INR WITHOUT significant bleeding is managed by withholding warfarin and rechecking. INR 4.5-10 with no bleeding: simply hold warfarin (routine vitamin K is NOT recommended). INR >10 with no bleeding: hold warfarin AND give low-dose ORAL vitamin K 2.5-5 mg. INR slightly above range (3-4.5): lower or hold a dose. Always look for the cause (drug interaction, diet, illness).
⚠️ WRONG: 'give FFP or 4-factor PCC for an INR of 8 in a patient who is not bleeding.' Reversal products (PCC/FFP) and high-dose IV vitamin K are reserved for serious bleeding — for asymptomatic high INR you hold warfarin ± low-dose oral vitamin K, avoiding overcorrection and rebound thrombosis.
5. 4-factor PCC
For MAJOR or life-threatening bleeding on warfarin, 4-factor prothrombin complex concentrate (4F-PCC; contains factors II, VII, IX, X plus proteins C and S) is the PREFERRED reversal agent — dosed by weight and INR, it works within minutes, requires small volume, and needs no blood-type matching. ALWAYS co-administer IV vitamin K because PCC's effect is short-lived.
⚠️ WRONG: 'give 4F-PCC alone and skip vitamin K because the INR corrected immediately.' PCC's transfused factors are consumed within hours and the INR will rebound; IV vitamin K must be given concurrently to sustain reversal by restoring endogenous factor synthesis.
6. IV vitamin K
In major bleeding give IV vitamin K 5-10 mg (slow infusion) together with PCC/FFP — it provides DURABLE reversal by allowing hepatic synthesis of functional factors over ~12-24 h, covering the gap after PCC wanes. IV (not oral) is used for serious bleeding because it acts faster. Give slowly due to a small risk of anaphylactoid reaction.
⚠️ WRONG: 'give a large IV vitamin K dose (e.g. 10 mg) to a stable patient just to normalize the INR fast.' Over-reversing with high-dose vitamin K in a non-bleeding patient causes prolonged warfarin RESISTANCE for days, making re-anticoagulation difficult — high-dose IV vitamin K is for active major bleeding only.
7. FFP only if no PCC
Fresh frozen plasma is SECOND-LINE for warfarin reversal, used only when 4F-PCC is unavailable. FFP requires large volumes (risk of volume overload/TACO), takes time to thaw and type-match, and reverses less completely/quickly than PCC. It supplies all coagulation factors but is inferior for urgent warfarin reversal.
⚠️ WRONG: 'reach for FFP first in a warfarin patient with intracranial hemorrhage.' 4F-PCC is preferred (faster, smaller volume, more complete); FFP is only a fallback when PCC is not on hand — delaying with FFP in ICH costs critical time.
8. Stop 5 days pre-op
Hold warfarin about 5 days before major surgery to let the INR drift down to <1.5 (≈5 days = ~5 half-lives covering the long-lived factor II). Check INR the day before surgery; if still ≥1.5 give low-dose oral vitamin K 1-2.5 mg. Resume warfarin 12-24 h post-op once hemostasis is secure.
⚠️ WRONG: 'stop warfarin the night before surgery.' Warfarin's effect (via long-half-life prothrombin) persists for days; a single night is not enough — you need ~5 days for the INR to normalize before major surgery.
9. Bridge if high risk
Peri-procedural BRIDGING with therapeutic LMWH (or UFH) is reserved for HIGH thrombotic risk: mechanical mitral valve, recent (<3 mo) VTE/stroke, CHA2DS2-VASc ≥7-9, or known caged-ball valves. Start LMWH when INR falls <2, stop LMWH ~24 h pre-op, resume after hemostasis. The BRIDGE trial showed most AFib patients do NOT benefit from bridging.
⚠️ WRONG: 'bridge every warfarin patient with LMWH around surgery.' The BRIDGE trial found that in low/moderate-risk AFib, bridging did not reduce thromboembolism but TRIPLED major bleeding — only high-thrombotic-risk patients should be bridged.
10. Skin necrosis
Warfarin-induced skin necrosis appears within the first ~3-5 days, striking fatty areas (breast, thigh, buttock, abdomen) as painful purple lesions progressing to necrosis. It results from the early steep fall of protein C (short half-life) before procoagulant factors decline, causing microvascular thrombosis — risk is highest in protein C or protein S deficiency.
⚠️ WRONG: 'warfarin skin necrosis means the patient is over-anticoagulated, so stop warfarin and wait.' It is an EARLY hypercoagulable complication, not over-anticoagulation; management is stop warfarin, give vitamin K, start a non-warfarin anticoagulant (heparin), and replace protein C — and always bridge with heparin to prevent it.
11. Crosses placenta
Warfarin crosses the placenta and is TERATOGENIC: first-trimester exposure causes fetal warfarin syndrome (nasal/bone hypoplasia, stippled epiphyses); later exposure causes CNS abnormalities and fetal/intracranial hemorrhage. It is contraindicated in pregnancy (the narrow exception is some mechanical-valve patients in specialized centers, with counseling).
⚠️ WRONG: 'continue warfarin through pregnancy in a VTE patient because heparin is inconvenient.' Warfarin's teratogenicity and fetal bleeding risk mandate switching to LMWH; warfarin should not be used in pregnancy for routine VTE.
12. Use LMWH in pregnancy
LMWH is the anticoagulant of choice in pregnancy because it does NOT cross the placenta (large molecule) — it is not teratogenic and does not cause fetal bleeding. It requires dose adjustment as weight/GFR change and may need anti-Xa monitoring. Unfractionated heparin is an alternative, also placenta-safe.
⚠️ WRONG: 'use a DOAC in pregnancy as a convenient warfarin alternative.' DOACs are NOT recommended in pregnancy (insufficient safety data, cross the placenta); LMWH is the correct substitute for warfarin in pregnant patients.
13. Safe in breastfeeding
Warfarin is COMPATIBLE with breastfeeding — it is highly protein-bound and does not pass into breast milk in clinically significant amounts, so it does not anticoagulate the infant. This contrasts sharply with its pregnancy contraindication; the placenta and breast milk are different barriers.
⚠️ WRONG: 'a breastfeeding mother needing anticoagulation must avoid warfarin like she did in pregnancy.' Warfarin (and LMWH/UFH) are all safe during lactation — the teratogenic/placental concern does not apply to breast milk.
14. LAC = unreliable INR
In antiphospholipid syndrome with a lupus anticoagulant (LAC), the antibody can interfere with the phospholipid-dependent PT/INR assay, making the INR unreliable for monitoring warfarin. Use chromogenic factor X activity (target ~20-40%) to gauge true anticoagulation intensity. APS VTE is treated with warfarin (DOACs inferior in triple-positive APS).
⚠️ WRONG: 'trust the standard INR to dose-adjust warfarin in a lupus-anticoagulant patient.' The LAC artifactually alters the INR, so the displayed value may not reflect actual anticoagulation — monitor with chromogenic factor X activity instead.
