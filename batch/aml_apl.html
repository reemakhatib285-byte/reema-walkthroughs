📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. APL ship
Acute promyelocytic leukemia (FAB M3) is defined by t(15;17)(q24;q21) producing the PML::RARA fusion, which blocks myeloid maturation at the promyelocyte stage. Blasts/abnormal promyelocytes show heavy azurophilic granules and bundles of Auer rods (faggot cells), are strongly MPO-positive, and are characteristically CD34-/HLA-DR-negative on flow.
⚠️ APL is the one AML subtype that is a true medical emergency from the moment it is SUSPECTED — and it is the most curable; missing it early (death from hemorrhage) is the classic fatal error.
2. DIC bleeding sea
APL classically presents with disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) plus hyperfibrinolysis: the abnormal promyelocytes release tissue factor and annexin II, consuming clotting factors and fibrinogen. Labs: low fibrinogen, low platelets, high D-dimer, prolonged PT/aPTT. This bleeding diathesis is the leading cause of early death.
⚠️ The APL emergency is COAGULOPATHY/hemorrhage (DIC + hyperfibrinolysis), not leukostasis — and the WBC is often low/normal, so don't be reassured by a low count.
3. Bleeding sites (GI/brain/lung)
DIC-driven hemorrhage causes intracranial hemorrhage (the most feared, leading early-death cause), pulmonary hemorrhage, and GI bleeding. Early death from bleeding can occur before or during the first days of treatment, which is why blood-product support and ATRA must start immediately.
⚠️ A new headache or neuro change in a suspected APL patient is intracranial hemorrhage until proven otherwise — act on it emergently.
4. FFP / Cryo / Plt support
Aggressive coagulopathy support is mandatory: transfuse to keep fibrinogen >100-150 mg/dL (cryoprecipitate/FFP) and platelets >30,000-50,000, especially while bleeding or pre-procedure. Continue until DIC resolves with differentiation therapy.
⚠️ Do not wait for active bleeding to transfuse in APL — prophylactically correct fibrinogen and platelets because spontaneous fatal hemorrhage is common.
5. ATRA man
All-trans retinoic acid (ATRA, a vitamin A derivative) binds the RARA portion of the fusion and forces terminal differentiation of the malignant promyelocytes, rapidly correcting the coagulopathy. Start ATRA on clinical SUSPICION of APL, before cytogenetic/molecular confirmation, because it both treats the leukemia and resolves the life-threatening DIC.
⚠️ Do NOT wait for cytogenetics/PCR to confirm t(15;17) before starting ATRA — empiric ATRA on suspicion saves lives and is reversible if APL is excluded.
6. Arsenic trioxide (green)
Arsenic trioxide (ATO) degrades the PML::RARA fusion protein. The combination ATRA + ATO is curative and chemo-FREE for low/intermediate-risk APL (Lo-Coco trial: superior to ATRA + chemo). ATO prolongs the QT interval — monitor ECG and replete K+/Mg2+.
⚠️ ATRA + ATO replaces cytotoxic chemo entirely in standard/low-risk APL; remember ATO's QT prolongation and electrolyte requirements.
7. ~85% cure
With prompt ATRA-based therapy, APL has the BEST prognosis of any AML subtype — cure rates ~85-90% (and >95% for low-risk treated with ATRA+ATO). The historically worst-prognosis leukemia became the most curable thanks to differentiation therapy.
⚠️ APL went from worst to best prognosis among AMLs — but only if treated promptly; early hemorrhagic death remains the main threat to that 85-90% cure rate.
8. WBC ≤10,000 low risk
Sanz risk stratification uses presenting WBC: WBC ≤10,000/µL = low/intermediate risk → treat with ATRA + ATO without cytotoxic chemotherapy.
⚠️ Risk in APL is defined by WBC count (and platelets), NOT by genetics — t(15;17) is present in all APL; the WBC threshold of 10,000 separates the treatment arms.
9. WBC >10,000 high risk
WBC >10,000/µL at presentation = high-risk APL → add cytotoxic chemotherapy (an anthracycline such as idarubicin/daunorubicin, sometimes gemtuzumab ozogamicin) to ATRA ± ATO for cytoreduction and leukostasis prevention.
⚠️ High WBC APL needs added chemo/cytoreduction; this is also the group at highest risk for differentiation syndrome.
10. No chemo (low risk)
Standard/low-risk APL is cured with ATRA + arsenic trioxide and avoids cytotoxic chemotherapy entirely, sparing patients its toxicity and secondary-malignancy risk.
⚠️ Reflexively giving 7+3 to an APL patient is wrong — low-risk APL is a chemo-free cure with ATRA+ATO.
11. Differentiation syndrome bay
Differentiation (APL/retinoic acid) syndrome occurs within days-weeks of starting ATRA or ATO: fever, dyspnea, pulmonary infiltrates, pleural/pericardial effusions, peripheral edema, weight gain, hypotension, and acute kidney injury, driven by a cytokine surge from differentiating promyelocytes. Higher risk with rising WBC.
⚠️ New dyspnea, edema, or pulmonary infiltrates on ATRA/ATO = differentiation syndrome, NOT pneumonia or CHF — and steroids, not antibiotics/diuretics alone, are the answer.
12. Dexamethasone
Treat differentiation syndrome with dexamethasone 10 mg IV q12h promptly at first suspicion; in severe cases temporarily hold ATRA/ATO. Prophylactic steroids or prophylactic cytoreduction may be used in high-WBC patients.
⚠️ Do not stop differentiation therapy permanently for mild differentiation syndrome — add dexamethasone and continue/resume ATRA once controlled.
13. PCR monitoring
Monitor PML::RARA transcripts by RT-PCR for measurable residual disease and molecular relapse. Molecular CR (PCR-negative) is the goal after consolidation; a rising/positive PCR signals impending relapse and prompts pre-emptive therapy.
⚠️ In APL, molecular (PCR) monitoring of PML-RARA — not just morphologic CR — drives relapse detection and consolidation decisions.
14. 10% mortality sign
Despite excellent overall cure rates, early death (mostly from hemorrhage, and from differentiation syndrome) still accounts for roughly 10% of APL mortality, concentrated in the first weeks — underscoring why immediate ATRA and aggressive coagulopathy support matter.
⚠️ The main cause of APL death is EARLY (hemorrhage/differentiation syndrome in the induction window), not late relapse — prognosis hinges on surviving the first weeks.
15. APL syndrome 3-week timer
Differentiation syndrome characteristically arises within the first ~1-3 weeks of starting ATRA/ATO; vigilance is highest during this early window when the WBC is rising.
⚠️ Differentiation syndrome is a time-limited early complication — falsely attributing week-2 dyspnea to infection delays the steroids that are actually needed.
