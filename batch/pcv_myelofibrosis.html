📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Clonal megakaryocytes
Primary myelofibrosis (PMF) is driven by a clonal proliferation of atypical, dysplastic megakaryocytes that form dense clusters in the marrow. These abnormal megakaryocytes secrete a storm of fibrogenic cytokines (PDGF, TGF-beta, VEGF, bFGF) that recruit and activate normal fibroblasts. The malignant clone is the megakaryocyte/myeloid lineage — the resulting fibrosis is a downstream reactive consequence, not the cancer itself. Same driver mutations as the other MPNs: JAK2, CALR, or MPL.
⚠️ WRONG: the fibroblasts depositing collagen are the neoplastic clone. They are NOT — fibroblasts are polyclonal/reactive bystanders responding to megakaryocyte-derived cytokines. The discriminator: clonality markers (JAK2/CALR/MPL, cytogenetics) localize to the hematopoietic megakaryocyte/myeloid lineage, never to the stromal fibroblasts.
2. Fibrosis is reactive, not clonal
Marrow fibrosis in PMF is a SECONDARY, reactive process. Cytokines released by the clonal megakaryocytes (chiefly TGF-beta and PDGF) drive non-neoplastic fibroblasts to lay down reticulin then collagen. This is the conceptual key to the whole disease: the cancer is hematopoietic, the scar is reactive stroma.
⚠️ WRONG: fibroblasts are the malignant clone. Discriminator — if you transplant the patient's hematopoietic stem cells into a new marrow, fibrosis recurs from the donor-derived clone driving host fibroblasts; the fibroblasts themselves carry no driver mutation.
3. TGF-beta drives fibrosis
TGF-beta (transforming growth factor-beta), released by clonal megakaryocytes and platelets, is the dominant pro-fibrotic cytokine in PMF. It stimulates fibroblasts to synthesize type I/III collagen and reticulin and upregulates TIMPs that block collagen breakdown. Mnemonic: TGF-beta = 'Tissue Gets Fibrotic.'
⚠️ WRONG: TGF-beta is immunostimulatory and promotes marrow regeneration here. In PMF its net effect is profibrotic/immunosuppressive — it shifts the matrix balance toward deposition, not repair.
4. Collagen deposition
Progressive reticulin and then collagen deposition replaces functioning marrow, producing the classic 'dry tap' on aspiration and forcing hematopoiesis out to the spleen and liver (extramedullary hematopoiesis). Fibrosis is graded MF-0 through MF-3 on biopsy; collagen/osteosclerosis marks advanced disease.
⚠️ WRONG: collagen fibrosis is fully reversible with cytoreduction. Discriminator — ruxolitinib and other JAK inhibitors improve spleen size and symptoms but do NOT reliably reverse established collagen fibrosis; only allogeneic transplant can clear it.
5. TIMP / collagen balance
Tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases (TIMPs) block matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), the enzymes that normally degrade collagen. In PMF the TIMP:MMP ratio is pushed toward inhibition, so collagen breakdown falls and net matrix accumulates. Think of TIMPs as taking the scissors away from collagen.
⚠️ WRONG: high MMP activity is what causes the fibrosis. The opposite — it is MMP INHIBITION (excess TIMP) that tips the balance toward collagen accumulation.
6. PDGF / sprout
Platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), released by the abnormal megakaryocytes/platelets, is a potent fibroblast mitogen that drives fibroblast proliferation and collagen output, working alongside TGF-beta. PDGF = the 'sprout' growing new fibroblasts.
⚠️ WRONG: PDGF acts directly on hematopoietic stem cells to cause fibrosis. It does not — PDGF targets the stromal FIBROBLASTS (proliferation/collagen), part of the reactive, non-clonal response.
7. VEGF → vessels (angiogenesis)
Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) drives marrow neovascularization (increased microvessel density), a recognized feature of PMF marrow alongside fibrosis. Angiogenesis supports the expanding clone and is part of the disordered marrow microenvironment.
⚠️ WRONG: increased marrow angiogenesis argues against MPN and points to a benign reactive marrow. On the contrary, marked microvessel density is characteristic of PMF and other MPNs.
8. OPG / osteosclerosis
Cytokine imbalance (including osteoprotegerin/OPG dysregulation favoring osteoblast activity) produces osteosclerosis — thickened, sclerotic bone seen on imaging in advanced PMF. Radiographs may show diffusely increased bone density, especially axial skeleton.
⚠️ WRONG: PMF causes lytic (punched-out) bone lesions. Those are myeloma — PMF classically causes osteoSCLEROSIS (dense bone), the discriminator on plain films.
9. JAK2 ~55%
JAK2 V617F is the most common driver in PMF, present in roughly 50-60% of cases. It is a gain-of-function exon-14 mutation causing constitutive, ligand-independent JAK-STAT signaling. The SAME mutation drives PV (~95%) and ET (~50-60%); JAK2 is shared across all three classic BCR-ABL-negative MPNs.
⚠️ WRONG: detecting JAK2 V617F confirms polycythemia vera. JAK2 V617F is shared by PV, ET, and PMF — it identifies an MPN but does NOT specify which one; the phenotype (Hct, smear, marrow biopsy) makes the distinction.
10. CALR ~36% (better prognosis)
Calreticulin (CALR) mutations occur in roughly 25-35% of PMF and define a JAK2-wild-type subset. Type 1 (52-bp deletion) CALR carries the BEST prognosis of all PMF genotypes — longer survival, lower leukemic transformation. CALR mutations also occur in ET but never in PV.
⚠️ WRONG: CALR-mutated PMF has a worse prognosis than triple-negative disease. Reversed — CALR (especially type 1) confers the BEST survival; triple-negative PMF has the WORST prognosis.
11. MPL ~4%
MPL (thrombopoietin receptor) mutations, typically MPL W515L/K, are found in a small minority (~4-8%) of PMF and ET. They activate the same JAK-STAT axis downstream of the TPO receptor, mimicking ligand binding.
⚠️ WRONG: MPL mutations are found in polycythemia vera. MPL drives JAK2-negative ET and PMF, not PV — PV is essentially always JAK2-mutated (V617F or exon-12).
12. Triple-negative ~5%
Triple-negative PMF (no detectable JAK2, CALR, or MPL mutation, ~5-10%) carries the WORST prognosis — highest rates of leukemic transformation and shortest survival. These patients often harbor high-molecular-risk subclonal mutations (e.g., ASXL1, SRSF2).
⚠️ WRONG: triple-negative status means a benign, non-clonal reactive process. Triple-negative PMF is still clonal (driven by other lesions) and is the most aggressive subgroup.
13. Least common MPN, older men
PMF is the LEAST common of the three classic Ph-negative MPNs (PV and ET are more frequent). It typically presents in the 6th-7th decade with a slight male predominance. Median age at diagnosis is ~65; pediatric/young-adult PMF is rare.
⚠️ WRONG: PMF is the most common MPN and presents in young women. It is the LEAST common classic MPN and favors older men — useful demographic discriminator on exam vignettes.
14. Diagnosis of exclusion
PRIMARY MF is diagnosed only after excluding secondary causes of marrow fibrosis (metastatic carcinoma, infection like TB, autoimmune myelofibrosis, hairy cell leukemia, MDS) AND post-PV / post-ET myelofibrosis. WHO criteria require megakaryocytic atypia with reticulin/collagen fibrosis plus a clonal marker (JAK2/CALR/MPL) or another clonal/exclusion criterion.
⚠️ WRONG: teardrop cells plus splenomegaly are enough to call primary myelofibrosis. Those are non-specific — leukoerythroblastosis and dacrocytes occur in marrow infiltration of any cause; you must exclude secondary fibrosis and prior PV/ET first.
