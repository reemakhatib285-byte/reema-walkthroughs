📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Control banner
Stepping decisions are driven by symptom control over the last 4 weeks: daytime symptoms ≤2×/week, NO night waking, reliever use ≤2×/week, and no activity limitation = well-controlled. 1–2 of these = partly controlled; 3–4 = uncontrolled. Future-risk factors (≥1 exacerbation/year, low FEV1, high SABA use, poor adherence) are assessed separately and independently justify stepping up even when current symptoms look acceptable.
⚠️ Boards test that 'control' is symptom-based over 4 weeks — a single normal spirometry does not equal control, and good current symptoms with a recent exacerbation still counts as not-controlled because exacerbation risk is its own domain.
2. Before stepping up checklist
Before escalating GINA step, verify the 'big five': correct diagnosis (confirm reversible obstruction/variable airflow, not COPD/VCD/heart failure), inhaler technique (most patients use devices wrong), adherence, ongoing trigger/allergen/smoking exposure, and comorbidities (rhinosinusitis, GERD, obesity, OSA). Fixing these resolves most apparent treatment failure without adding drugs.
⚠️ The classic vignette: 'uncontrolled' asthmatic on escalating doses — the answer is to check technique/adherence/diagnosis first, NOT to step up therapy or add a biologic.
3. Biologic phenotypes key
Step-5 biologic choice is phenotype/biomarker driven. Anti-IgE (omalizumab) → allergic asthma with ↑IgE and perennial aeroallergen sensitization. Anti-IL-5/5R (mepolizumab, reslizumab, benralizumab) → eosinophilic asthma (high blood eos, frequent exacerbations). Anti-IL-4Rα (dupilumab) blocks IL-4 AND IL-13 → eosinophilic or high-FeNO asthma, also helps nasal polyps/atopic dermatitis. Anti-TSLP (tezepelumab) acts at the top of the type-2 cascade and works across phenotypes including low-eosinophil disease.
⚠️ Tezepelumab (anti-TSLP) is the one biologic effective regardless of eosinophil/biomarker status; dupilumab is the cross-over choice when nasal polyps or atopic dermatitis coexist.
4. Step 1 — PRN reliever only
Mildest asthma. GINA Track 1 (preferred) = as-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol taken whenever symptomatic — this delivers an anti-inflammatory dose with every reliever puff and cuts severe exacerbations versus SABA. Track 2 (alternative) = take an ICS whenever a SABA is taken.
⚠️ SABA-only is NO LONGER recommended at any step — even mild/intermittent asthma needs ICS-containing therapy because SABA-alone increases exacerbation and death risk.
5. Step 2 — low-dose ICS or PRN ICS-formoterol
Persistent asthma. Track 1: as-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol. Track 2: daily low-dose maintenance ICS PLUS as-needed SABA. Daily ICS reduces symptoms, exacerbations, and remodeling; adherence is the main limitation for the daily-ICS option.
⚠️ LTRA (montelukast) is only an alternative controller here — it is inferior to ICS and carries a boxed warning for neuropsychiatric effects, so it is not first-line.
6. Step 3 — low-dose ICS/formoterol
Add a LABA to low-dose ICS. Track 1 preferred = low-dose ICS-formoterol used as MART (Maintenance And Reliever Therapy): the same inhaler is taken daily AND for symptom relief. Formoterol is the chosen LABA because its rapid onset lets it double as a reliever, unlike salmeterol.
⚠️ MART only works with formoterol-containing combinations — you cannot use a salmeterol inhaler as a reliever because salmeterol has a slow onset.
7. Step 4 — medium-dose ICS/formoterol
Step up to medium-dose ICS-LABA (ideally medium-dose ICS-formoterol MART). Consider adding LAMA (tiotropium) here if still not controlled, and refer to a specialist for phenotyping. Doubling ICS to high dose gives diminishing returns relative to side effects, so adding a second class is favored.
⚠️ Adding a LAMA (tiotropium) is the next step before high-dose ICS or biologics — and tiotropium is added to ICS-LABA, never used as monotherapy in asthma.
8. Step 5 — high-dose ICS/LABA + LAMA ± biologic
Severe asthma: high-dose ICS-LABA + LAMA (triple therapy) and refer for phenotype-targeted biologic (anti-IgE / anti-IL5 / anti-IL4Rα / anti-TSLP). Add-on azithromycin can reduce exacerbations in some adults. Chronic oral corticosteroids are the absolute last resort.
⚠️ Chronic OCS cause systemic toxicity (osteoporosis, diabetes, cataracts, adrenal suppression) — start a biologic to spare steroids rather than committing a severe asthmatic to maintenance prednisone.
9. Reliever lane (ICS-formoterol)
The reliever 'lane' runs through every step. In GINA Track 1 the reliever at all steps is as-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol; in Track 2 it is as-needed SABA (with ICS taken concurrently). MART (Track 1) means one ICS-formoterol inhaler serves as both controller and reliever, simplifying the regimen and giving anti-inflammatory dosing at the moment of symptoms.
⚠️ Mixing the tracks is the trap: you cannot use a SABA as the reliever for a patient on MART ICS-formoterol — the reliever must match the maintenance strategy.
10. Step down after 3 months control
Once asthma is well-controlled for ~3 months, step down to find the lowest effective therapy, typically by reducing ICS dose ~25–50% at a time while keeping some ICS on board. Never stop ICS entirely in persistent asthma. Choose a low-risk time to step down (not during a respiratory infection, pregnancy, or travel) and re-check in 1–3 months.
⚠️ Stepping down by removing the ICS and leaving LABA behind is the dangerous error — LABA monotherapy is contraindicated. Always taper ICS dose while retaining ICS, and don't step down during instability.
11. Frequent SABA = poor control
Using ≥3 SABA canisters per year (or needing reliever >2×/week) flags inadequate controller therapy and is an independent predictor of exacerbations and asthma death. High SABA use marks under-treated underlying inflammation.
⚠️ The board answer to a high-SABA-use patient is to optimize/step up the ICS-containing controller — NOT to simply refill the SABA prescription.
12. LABA-only never in asthma
LABA monotherapy is contraindicated in asthma. The SMART/SALPACA-era data showed LABA-alone increases asthma-related deaths because it masks worsening inflammation while providing bronchodilation. LABA must always be paired with ICS.
⚠️ Any answer offering a standalone LABA (salmeterol/formoterol) for asthma is wrong — the LABA must be combined with an ICS. (Note: LABA/LAMA-without-ICS is also wrong in asthma, even though it's used in COPD.)
