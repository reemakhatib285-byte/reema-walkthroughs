📖 Read-through — all 16 symbols
1. Primary MR (valve problem)
Primary (organic) MR means the valve leaflets/apparatus themselves are diseased while the ventricle is initially normal. The leak is driven by structural valve damage, so the definitive fix is mechanical — repair or replace the valve.
⚠️ Treating any severe MR as primary — first decide valve problem (primary) vs ventricle problem (secondary); management diverges.
2. Mitral valve prolapse / myxomatous
Myxomatous degeneration causing mitral valve prolapse is the single most common cause of primary MR in developed countries. Redundant, billowing leaflets fail to coapt and prolapse into the LA during systole.
⚠️ Naming rheumatic disease as the most common cause of primary MR — in developed countries it is myxomatous MVP.
3. Ruptured chordae
Chordae tendineae rupture (spontaneous in myxomatous disease, or from trauma/ischemia) causes a flail leaflet and acute or chronic primary MR. It is a classic cause of sudden severe regurgitation.
⚠️ Overlooking flail leaflet from ruptured chordae as a cause of acute primary MR presenting with pulmonary edema.
4. Rheumatic primary MR
Rheumatic disease can scar and retract the mitral leaflets, producing primary MR (and is still a leading cause worldwide). It is one of several organic valve etiologies in the primary group.
⚠️ Forgetting rheumatic disease remains a top cause of primary MR globally even where MVP dominates.
5. Endocarditis
Infective endocarditis destroys leaflet tissue or causes perforation, producing acute primary MR. New regurgitation with fever and emboli should raise this diagnosis.
⚠️ Missing endocarditis as a cause of new/acute primary MR in a febrile patient.
6. Holosystolic murmur at apex → axilla
Chronic severe MR gives a holosystolic (pansystolic) murmur loudest at the apex radiating to the axilla. The constant pressure gradient from LV to LA throughout systole makes the murmur uniform across the whole of systole.
⚠️ Expecting the murmur to crescendo–decrescendo like AS — chronic MR is holosystolic, plateau in shape.
7. Soft / absent S1
With poor leaflet coaptation in chronic MR, valve closure is incomplete, so S1 is soft or absent. It is a supportive sign of significant regurgitation.
⚠️ Expecting a loud S1 — in MR the incompetent valve gives a SOFT/absent S1.
8. Widely split S2
Severe MR shortens LV ejection time and brings aortic valve closure (A2) earlier, widening the A2–P2 split. It reflects rapid LV emptying into the low-pressure atrium.
⚠️ Reading the wide split as pulmonary disease — in MR it is from early A2 due to rapid LV unloading.
9. S3 gallop
A third heart sound in chronic severe MR reflects the large regurgitant volume returning rapidly to fill the LV in early diastole. It is a marker of volume overload, not necessarily systolic failure.
⚠️ Equating an S3 in MR with systolic heart failure — here it signals high regurgitant volume overload.
10. Displaced apex
Chronic volume overload dilates the LV, displacing the apical impulse laterally and inferiorly. It signals long-standing significant MR and remodeling.
⚠️ Mistaking the laterally displaced, hyperdynamic apex of MR for the sustained heave of pressure overload (AS/HTN).
11. Acute severe MR: soft short early murmur
In acute severe MR (ruptured papillary muscle or chordae), the non-compliant LA pressure rises fast, equalizing the LV–LA gradient early, so the murmur is soft, short and early — often with an S3 and flash pulmonary edema. Loudness does NOT track severity.
⚠️ Using murmur loudness to gauge acute MR severity — a catastrophic acute leak can be quiet/short, not loud.
12. Loud ≠ worse (acute)
The intensity of the regurgitant murmur correlates poorly with severity in acute MR. Severe acute regurgitation may produce only a faint murmur because LA and LV pressures rapidly equalize.
⚠️ Reassuring on a quiet murmur — in acute MR a soft murmur can accompany life-threatening regurgitation.
13. Primary severe MR → repair first
For primary severe MR, surgery is the definitive treatment and valve REPAIR is preferred over replacement. Operate (Class I) if symptomatic, or if asymptomatic with LV dysfunction — EF 60% or less, or LV end-systolic diameter 40 mm or greater.
⚠️ Waiting for symptoms in primary MR — asymptomatic patients with EF ≤60% or LVESD ≥40 mm already meet a Class I operative threshold.
14. Secondary/functional MR (ventricle problem)
Secondary (functional) MR has a normal valve that leaks because the ventricle is diseased — annular dilation and leaflet tethering from ischemic or dilated cardiomyopathy, or HOCM with systolic anterior motion (SAM). The problem is the ventricle, not the leaflets.
⚠️ Operating on the valve first in functional MR — the leak is secondary to the ventricle; correct the ventricle/medical disease first.
15. Secondary causes: ischemic / dilated CM / HOCM-SAM
Functional MR arises from ischemic cardiomyopathy, dilated cardiomyopathy, or HOCM where systolic anterior motion of the mitral leaflet causes the leak. Recognizing the underlying myopathy directs therapy at the ventricle, not the valve.
⚠️ In HOCM, jumping to mitral surgery — the SAM-related MR often improves with treating the obstruction, not valve replacement.
16. Secondary MR: GDMT → CRT → TEER
Functional MR is treated by optimizing the ventricle: guideline-directed medical therapy first, then cardiac resynchronization therapy where indicated, then transcatheter edge-to-edge repair (TEER/MitraClip) for persistent severe symptomatic MR. Primary valve surgery is not the first move.
⚠️ Jumping straight to valve surgery in secondary MR — the sequence is GDMT, then CRT, then TEER; surgery-first is the wrong answer.
