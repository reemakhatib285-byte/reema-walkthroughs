📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Imatinib (1st gen)
Imatinib is the first-generation ATP-competitive BCR-ABL1 inhibitor (IRIS trial, 2001) that transformed CML into a chronic disease. It is now generic and inexpensive, well tolerated, and a standard first-line option for low/intermediate-risk chronic-phase CML when survival is the goal. Also inhibits KIT and PDGFR (hence its use in GIST).
⚠️ Imatinib side effects: periorbital edema/fluid retention, muscle cramps, rash, cytopenias. It is slower to deep response than 2nd-gen agents — fine for survival, less ideal if aiming for fast deep response/TFR.
2. ATP-competitive target
Imatinib, dasatinib, nilotinib, and bosutinib are all ATP-competitive inhibitors that occupy the ATP-binding pocket of the BCR-ABL1 kinase, blocking phosphorylation of downstream substrates. This shared mechanism is why a single pocket mutation (T315I) can confer cross-resistance to all of them.
⚠️ Because these four bind the ATP pocket, the T315I 'gatekeeper' mutation blocks ALL of them — only ponatinib (ATP-pocket but T315I-active) and asciminib (allosteric/myristoyl) retain activity.
3. Dasatinib
Dasatinib is a highly potent (~300x imatinib) 2nd-generation TKI, once daily, that also inhibits SRC-family kinases and penetrates the CNS (useful in lymphoid blast crisis/CNS disease). Achieves faster, deeper responses than imatinib.
⚠️ Dasatinib's signature toxicity is PLEURAL EFFUSION (and pulmonary arterial hypertension) — AVOID in patients with pre-existing lung disease/COPD or heart failure.
4. Dasatinib pleural effusion
Pleural effusion is the classic, dose-related dasatinib adverse effect (can occur years into therapy), along with pulmonary arterial hypertension. Managed by dose interruption/reduction ± diuretics/steroids; switch TKI if recurrent.
⚠️ New dyspnea/pleural effusion in a CML patient on a TKI → think DASATINIB. Avoid dasatinib if the patient has lung disease.
5. Nilotinib
Nilotinib is a 2nd-generation TKI (~30x imatinib potency), dosed twice daily and must be taken on an empty stomach (food increases absorption → toxicity). Produces fast, deep molecular responses, good for pursuing TFR.
⚠️ Nilotinib causes HYPERGLYCEMIA, QT prolongation, and vascular/arterial occlusive events (PAD, MI, stroke) — AVOID in diabetics and patients with cardiovascular disease. Also pancreatitis/lipase elevation. Empty-stomach dosing + QT risk makes it the 'metabolic/cardiac' TKI.
6. Bosutinib
Bosutinib is a 2nd-generation dual SRC/ABL inhibitor used mainly after failure or intolerance of other TKIs (and as a first-line option). It has relatively little cardiac/pulmonary toxicity, making it attractive when those organ systems are a concern.
⚠️ Bosutinib's signature toxicities are DIARRHEA (very common, usually early/self-limited) and HEPATOTOXICITY (transaminitis) — caution with hepatic impairment. Choose it to avoid the cardiac/pulmonary toxicities of nilotinib/dasatinib.
7. Ponatinib
Ponatinib is the 3rd-generation TKI specifically active against the T315I gatekeeper mutation (and other resistant clones). Reserved for T315I-positive disease or failure of ≥2 prior TKIs. Often dose-reduced (45→15 mg) once a response is achieved to limit toxicity.
⚠️ Ponatinib carries the HIGHEST risk of arterial thrombotic/cardiovascular events (MI, stroke, PAD) and causes PANCREATITIS — reserve for T315I or multi-TKI failure, and reduce dose after response.
8. T315I gatekeeper mutation
T315I is the 'gatekeeper' kinase-domain mutation: a threonine-to-isoleucine substitution that sterically blocks ATP-competitive TKIs from binding. It confers resistance to imatinib and all standard 2nd-generation TKIs and is the most common cause of pan-TKI resistance.
⚠️ T315I → use PONATINIB or ASCIMINIB; it does NOT respond to imatinib, dasatinib, nilotinib, or bosutinib. (Contrast: Y253H/E255K → dasatinib; V299L/F317L → nilotinib.)
9. Highest CV risk flag
Ponatinib has the highest cardiovascular/arterial occlusive toxicity of any TKI — a black-box concern. Mitigation includes aggressive cardiovascular risk-factor control and dose reduction (to 15 mg) once a molecular response is reached.
⚠️ If a board stem describes a CML patient who develops MI/stroke/limb ischemia on therapy, think ponatinib (highest CV risk) — or nilotinib (vascular events). Imatinib has the cleanest cardiovascular profile.
10. Asciminib (STAMP)
Asciminib is the first allosteric BCR-ABL1 inhibitor — a STAMP (Specifically Targeting the ABL Myristoyl Pocket) agent. By binding the myristoyl pocket rather than the ATP site, it works against many TKI-resistant clones, including T315I at higher dose (200 mg BID vs 40 mg BID standard). Used after ≥2 prior TKIs.
⚠️ Asciminib is NOT ATP-competitive — it targets the myristoyl (allosteric) pocket, so it retains activity against T315I and many ATP-pocket mutations that defeat conventional TKIs.
11. Omacetaxine
Omacetaxine (homoharringtonine) is a protein-synthesis inhibitor — NOT a tyrosine kinase inhibitor — given subcutaneously for chronic/accelerated CML after failure of ≥2 TKIs. It acts independently of the kinase domain, so it can work even with T315I.
⚠️ Omacetaxine is NOT a TKI (mechanism = protein synthesis inhibition); it is a multi-TKI-failure salvage option, including in T315I disease.
12. Survival = generic imatinib
When the treatment goal is overall survival alone, generic imatinib is sufficient first-line for most chronic-phase patients and is the most cost-effective choice — long-term survival is comparable across first-line TKIs.
⚠️ Overall survival is similar across first-line TKIs; the advantage of 2nd-gen agents is faster/deeper response, not necessarily longer life. So 'goal = survival' → generic imatinib is a perfectly correct answer.
13. High risk/TFR -> 2nd gen
For high-risk disease (high Sokal/ELTS score, young patients, those prioritizing treatment-free remission) start a 2nd-generation TKI (dasatinib/nilotinib/bosutinib) first-line, because they produce faster and deeper molecular responses — the prerequisite for attempting TFR.
⚠️ Choose the first-line TKI by GOAL and COMORBIDITY: survival/elderly/low-risk → imatinib; high-risk or aiming for TFR → 2nd-gen, but match the agent to the patient (avoid dasatinib in lung disease, nilotinib in DM/CVD, bosutinib in GI/liver disease).
