📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. CURB-65 wheel
CURB-65 is a 5-point mortality/disposition tool awarding 1 point each for Confusion, Urea >7 mmol/L (BUN >19 mg/dL), Respiratory rate ≥30, low Blood pressure (SBP <90 or DBP ≤60), and age ≥65. Score 0-1 → outpatient, 2 → consider short hospitalization, ≥3 → hospitalize and assess for ICU. It estimates 30-day mortality and is simpler than the more comprehensive PSI/PORT score.
⚠️ CURB-65 guides SITE OF CARE (admission/ICU), not antibiotic selection — picking antibiotics 'because the CURB-65 is high' is a category error. It can also UNDERESTIMATE severity in a young, hypoxemic patient (no oxygenation variable), so don't send home a hypoxic patient just because the score is 0-1.
2. C — Confusion
New-onset confusion/disorientation scores 1 point and reflects severe disease and/or hypoperfusion. It is an especially important sign in the elderly, in whom altered mental status may be the dominant or only presentation of pneumonia.
⚠️ Don't dismiss new confusion as 'baseline dementia' or attribute it solely to a UTI — in an older adult it may be the only clue to pneumonia/sepsis. It must be NEW confusion to count.
3. U — Urea >7 mmol/L
Blood urea >7 mmol/L (equivalently BUN >19-20 mg/dL) scores 1 point, marking end-organ hypoperfusion/dehydration and worse prognosis. It is one reason a basic metabolic panel is part of the initial CAP workup.
⚠️ It is UREA / BUN that is scored, NOT creatinine — a normal creatinine with an elevated BUN still earns the point. (Note: the abbreviated 'CRB-65,' used without labs, drops this variable.)
4. R — RR ≥30
A respiratory rate ≥30 breaths/min scores 1 point and is an early, sensitive marker of respiratory compromise and impending failure. Tachypnea often precedes hypoxemia and hypotension.
⚠️ Tachypnea is frequently undercounted at the bedside — don't overlook it when the other vitals look 'okay.' RR ≥30 is also one of the IDSA/ATS minor ICU criteria.
5. B — Blood pressure low
Hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg OR DBP ≤60 mmHg) scores 1 point and signals impending septic shock. It overlaps with the severe-CAP framework: hypotension requiring aggressive fluid resuscitation is a minor ICU criterion, and vasopressor-requiring septic shock is a MAJOR criterion.
⚠️ Hypotension that needs fluids/vasopressors is itself a severe-CAP/ICU trigger — don't treat a hypotensive pneumonia patient as routine ward care. Distinguish fluid-responsive hypotension (minor criterion) from vasopressor-dependent shock (major criterion → automatic ICU).
6. 65 — age
Age ≥65 years scores 1 point, reflecting the steep rise in pneumonia mortality with age due to comorbidity, immunosenescence, and atypical presentations.
⚠️ Older age alone can tip disposition toward admission even when vitals look mild — and elders may lack fever/leukocytosis, presenting instead with confusion or functional decline, so don't be falsely reassured.
7. CURB-65 score interpretation
Mortality climbs with score: 0 ≈ 0.6-1.5% (outpatient appropriate), 1-2 ≈ low-to-moderate (consider admission, especially at 2), and ≥3 ≈ ~22% 30-day mortality (admit and assess for ICU). The PSI/PORT score is an alternative, more granular tool that better identifies low-risk patients suitable for outpatient care.
⚠️ Treating a high CURB-65 (≥3) patient as an outpatient is a clear wrong answer. Remember the score informs disposition, not the choice of drug, and clinical judgment/hypoxemia can override a deceptively low score.
8. ICU major criteria
The IDSA/ATS severe-CAP definition has 2 MAJOR criteria — invasive mechanical ventilation (respiratory failure requiring intubation) and septic shock requiring vasopressors. Either ONE alone mandates ICU admission.
⚠️ A single major criterion is sufficient for ICU — don't wait to accumulate minor criteria. Conversely, don't downgrade a vasopressor-dependent or ventilated patient to the ward.
9. ICU minor criteria (need ≥3)
There are 9 IDSA/ATS MINOR criteria: RR ≥30, PaO2/FiO2 ≤250, multilobar infiltrates, confusion/disorientation, uremia (BUN ≥20), leukopenia (WBC <4000), thrombocytopenia (<100k), hypothermia (<36°C), and hypotension requiring aggressive fluid resuscitation. Meeting ≥3 minor criteria defines severe CAP warranting ICU or high-level monitoring.
⚠️ A patient with ≥3 minor criteria needs ICU-level care even WITHOUT overt shock or ventilation — don't be lulled by the absence of a major criterion. Note the counterintuitive ones: LEUKOPENIA (not leukocytosis), HYPOthermia, and thrombocytopenia all count as severity markers.
10. Radiographic clues board
Radiographic patterns narrow the differential: cavitation/multilobar disease = more severe (Klebsiella, S. aureus, anaerobes); thin-walled pneumatoceles → S. aureus; upper-lobe/apical cavitation → reactivation TB; gravity-dependent (posterior upper, superior lower segments, right > left) → aspiration. Multilobar infiltrates are also an ICU minor criterion.
⚠️ Upper-lobe cavitation should trigger a TB workup (acid-fast, isolation), NOT be assumed to be bacterial CAP — and definitely don't start an empiric respiratory FQ that could partially treat and mask TB. Multiple bilateral peripheral cavities suggest septic emboli, not simple CAP.
11. Sputum Gram stain & culture
Sputum Gram stain and culture help identify and target the pathogen and are most useful in severe disease, before antibiotics, and when a resistant/unusual organism is suspected (prior MRSA/Pseudomonas). A good specimen has >25 neutrophils and <10 epithelial cells per low-power field. They are NOT routinely required for mild outpatient CAP.
⚠️ Don't delay empiric antibiotics in severe CAP while waiting for sputum results — collect specimens but treat promptly (time-to-antibiotics matters in severe disease/sepsis). A contaminated, epithelial-cell-rich specimen is uninterpretable, so heavy oral flora ≠ the true pathogen.
12. Blood cultures
Blood cultures (two sets before antibiotics) are recommended in severe/ICU CAP and in patients with risk factors (MRSA/Pseudomonas, cavitary disease, asplenia, leukopenia) to detect bacteremia and tailor therapy. Pneumococcus is the most common blood-culture isolate.
⚠️ Routine blood cultures are LOW-yield in mild outpatient CAP and not required — reserve them for severe disease or specific risk factors. Don't withhold/delay antibiotics in a septic patient just to draw cultures; obtain them rapidly and treat.
13. Legionella & pneumococcal urinary antigen
Urinary antigen tests give rapid, antibiotic-insensitive results: the Legionella urinary antigen detects L. pneumophila serogroup 1, and the pneumococcal urinary antigen detects S. pneumoniae. They are recommended in severe CAP and when there is a Legionella outbreak/travel exposure, and can allow targeted de-escalation.
⚠️ A NEGATIVE Legionella urinary antigen does NOT exclude disease — it misses non-serogroup-1 strains; if suspicion is high, pursue lower-respiratory culture (BCYE agar) or PCR. Antigen positivity can also persist for weeks, so it may reflect a recent rather than current infection.
14. Procalcitonin (PCT)
Procalcitonin rises with bacterial infection and falls with viral/resolving infection, and can help GUIDE de-escalation/duration. However, current IDSA/ATS guidance is that PCT should NOT be used alone to decide whether to START antibiotics in suspected CAP — the clinical and radiographic diagnosis drives initiation.
⚠️ A low procalcitonin does NOT rule out bacterial pneumonia and does NOT justify withholding empiric antibiotics in a patient with a clinical/radiographic CAP diagnosis — 'don't treat because PCT is low' is the wrong answer.
15. Elderly presentation pearl
In older adults, pneumonia may present WITHOUT fever, cough, or leukocytosis — altered mental status, functional decline, falls, tachypnea, or decompensation of a chronic illness may be the only signs. A low threshold for chest imaging and prompt evaluation is essential.
⚠️ Don't anchor on UTI or 'delirium' and miss pneumonia in a confused elder — always include pneumonia in the differential for new confusion, and don't be reassured by an absent fever or normal WBC (leukopenia/hypothermia are actually severity markers).
