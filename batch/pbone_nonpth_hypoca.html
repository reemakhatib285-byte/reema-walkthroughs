⚠️ Board traps 12
  1. Secondary hyperPTH = milder — WRONG: seeing low Ca + high PTH and diagnosing 'hyperparathyroidism.' That combination is APPROPRIATE secondary hyperPTH responding to hypocalcemia, not a primary gland problem — work up the cause of low Ca (check 25-OH-D, creatinine, magnesium, phosphate). Primary hyperPTH would show high Ca with high PTH, the opposite calcium.
  2. Vit D deficiency -> osteomalacia — WRONG: diagnosing vitamin D deficiency with a 1,25-OH-D (calcitriol) level. 1,25-D can be NORMAL or even HIGH in deficiency because secondary hyperPTH upregulates renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase. Always order 25(OH)D (the storage form) to diagnose deficiency. Also expect HIGH ALP and HIGH PTH — a normal PTH would be inappropriate.
  3. Malabsorption causes — WRONG: treating a malabsorptive patient with standard ORAL vitamin D/calcium and expecting normalization. They malabsorb the replacement too — use higher doses, sometimes parenteral vitamin D, and co-replete magnesium. Also wrong to forget post-bariatric patients need lifelong Ca + vit D (and B12/iron) surveillance.
  4. Drugs lowering vit D — WRONG: overlooking the medication list as the driver of low Ca/low vitamin D and bone loss. A patient on chronic phenytoin or long-term glucocorticoids needs proactive vitamin D + calcium repletion and bone-density monitoring; the fix is often dose adjustment plus supplementation rather than a hunt for occult malabsorption.
  5. CKD: high FGF23, low 1,25-D — WRONG: giving plain ergocalciferol/cholecalciferol alone for CKD hypocalcemia. The failing kidney cannot activate it (1-alpha-hydroxylase is suppressed) — you must give ACTIVE vitamin D (calcitriol or paricalcitol) PLUS phosphate binders to control phosphate first. Giving Ca/calcitriol without addressing high phosphate risks vascular/metastatic calcification.
  6. Phosphate binders + calcitriol — WRONG: using cinacalcet in a hypocalcemic CKD patient without correcting Ca first. Cinacalcet LOWERS Ca (it activates CaSR -> suppresses PTH) and can precipitate symptomatic hypocalcemia — it is for high-PTH states with adequate/high Ca, not for fixing low Ca. Also avoid calcium-based binders if the patient is hypercalcemic or has vascular calcification.
  7. Acute pancreatitis — WRONG: ignoring hypocalcemia as incidental in pancreatitis. Hypocalcemia (Ca <8 mg/dL or ionized fall) is a marker of SEVERE disease and is a Ranson criterion (Ca <8 at 48 h) — it signals poor prognosis. Don't dismiss it; recognize it as a severity flag and check ionized Ca and magnesium.
  8. Rhabdo / tumor lysis — WRONG: aggressively giving IV calcium to correct the lab hypocalcemia in tumor lysis/rhabdo. Unless the patient is SYMPTOMATIC (tetany, seizures, arrhythmia), withhold calcium — adding Ca to a high-phosphate milieu drives METASTATIC calcium-phosphate deposition (worsens kidney injury, soft-tissue calcification). Lower the phosphate first; treat the underlying process.
  9. Citrate / blood products — WRONG: trusting TOTAL calcium during massive transfusion or citrate CRRT. Citrate binds the IONIZED (physiologically active) fraction, so total Ca can be misleadingly normal while ionized Ca is dangerously low — always monitor IONIZED Ca and give calcium gluconate/chloride to keep it in range.
  10. Hungry bone (post-PTHx) — WRONG: expecting transient, easily-corrected hypocalcemia after parathyroid surgery. Hungry bone causes SEVERE, sustained hypocalcemia (low Ca, low PO4, low Mg) needing aggressive IV/oral calcium PLUS calcitriol PLUS magnesium repletion over days. Distinguish it from surgical HYPOPARATHYROIDISM (low PTH) — hungry bone has low PO4 whereas hypoPTH has HIGH phosphate.
  11. Hypomagnesemia triangle — WRONG: pouring in more calcium when hypocalcemia 'won't correct' despite repletion. If Ca is refractory, CHECK AND REPLETE MAGNESIUM FIRST — until Mg is restored, PTH cannot be secreted or act, so Ca will not rise no matter how much you give. Refractory hypocalcemia = think magnesium.
  12. IV calcium gluconate (acute) — WRONG: giving IV calcium without simultaneously checking/correcting MAGNESIUM and being cautious of the patient's DIGOXIN status — rapid IV calcium can precipitate fatal digoxin toxicity ('stone heart'). And in hyperphosphatemic states (tumor lysis/rhabdo) reserve IV Ca for symptomatic patients only, to avoid metastatic calcification.
📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Secondary hyperPTH = milder
In NON-parathyroid hypocalcemia the glands are intact, so a falling Ca drives a compensatory rise in PTH (secondary hyperparathyroidism). This high PTH mobilizes Ca from bone, blunting the serum Ca drop — so hypocalcemia is usually milder/more chronic than in true hypoparathyroidism, but at the cost of skeletal demineralization (osteomalacia, renal osteodystrophy). The labs that separate the two camps: hypocalcemia with HIGH PTH = secondary (vit D deficiency, CKD); hypocalcemia with LOW/inappropriate PTH = hypoparathyroidism.
⚠️ WRONG: seeing low Ca + high PTH and diagnosing 'hyperparathyroidism.' That combination is APPROPRIATE secondary hyperPTH responding to hypocalcemia, not a primary gland problem — work up the cause of low Ca (check 25-OH-D, creatinine, magnesium, phosphate). Primary hyperPTH would show high Ca with high PTH, the opposite calcium.
2. Vit D deficiency -> osteomalacia
Low 25(OH)D (sun deprivation, poor intake, malabsorption) -> reduced gut Ca AND phosphate absorption -> hypocalcemia -> secondary hyperPTH. The classic biochemical signature: LOW or low-normal Ca, LOW phosphate (PTH-driven phosphaturia), HIGH PTH, HIGH alkaline phosphatase, and LOW 25(OH)D (<20 ng/mL deficient). Chronically this causes osteomalacia in adults (Looser's zones/pseudofractures, bone pain, proximal myopathy) and rickets in children. Treat with cholecalciferol/ergocalciferol (e.g., 50,000 IU weekly x6-8 wk, then maintenance).
⚠️ WRONG: diagnosing vitamin D deficiency with a 1,25-OH-D (calcitriol) level. 1,25-D can be NORMAL or even HIGH in deficiency because secondary hyperPTH upregulates renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase. Always order 25(OH)D (the storage form) to diagnose deficiency. Also expect HIGH ALP and HIGH PTH — a normal PTH would be inappropriate.
3. Malabsorption causes
Fat malabsorption impairs absorption of fat-soluble vitamin D and binds luminal calcium to unabsorbed fatty acids. Causes: CELIAC disease, CROHN's/IBD, chronic pancreatitis, short-bowel, and BARIATRIC surgery (especially Roux-en-Y gastric bypass, which bypasses the duodenum/proximal jejunum where Ca and vitamin D are absorbed). Result: combined vitamin D + calcium deficiency, hypocalcemia, secondary hyperPTH, osteomalacia, and often low magnesium too.
⚠️ WRONG: treating a malabsorptive patient with standard ORAL vitamin D/calcium and expecting normalization. They malabsorb the replacement too — use higher doses, sometimes parenteral vitamin D, and co-replete magnesium. Also wrong to forget post-bariatric patients need lifelong Ca + vit D (and B12/iron) surveillance.
4. Drugs lowering vit D
Enzyme-inducing drugs accelerate vitamin D catabolism via hepatic CYP3A4/CYP24A1 induction, lowering 25(OH)D and active 1,25-D: classically PHENYTOIN, phenobarbital, carbamazepine (anticonvulsants), RIFAMPIN, and glucocorticoids (which also reduce gut Ca absorption and increase renal Ca loss). Over time this produces drug-induced osteomalacia/osteoporosis.
⚠️ WRONG: overlooking the medication list as the driver of low Ca/low vitamin D and bone loss. A patient on chronic phenytoin or long-term glucocorticoids needs proactive vitamin D + calcium repletion and bone-density monitoring; the fix is often dose adjustment plus supplementation rather than a hunt for occult malabsorption.
5. CKD: high FGF23, low 1,25-D
CKD-Mineral Bone Disorder: failing kidneys retain PHOSPHATE -> FGF23 rises (early marker) -> FGF23 suppresses renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase (CYP27B1) -> LOW 1,25-D -> reduced gut Ca absorption -> HYPOCALCEMIA. Hyperphosphatemia also directly binds Ca. The result is high phosphate, low/low-normal Ca, low 1,25-D, and progressively HIGH PTH (secondary hyperPTH). If parathyroids become autonomous over years -> tertiary hyperPTH (high PTH AND high Ca).
⚠️ WRONG: giving plain ergocalciferol/cholecalciferol alone for CKD hypocalcemia. The failing kidney cannot activate it (1-alpha-hydroxylase is suppressed) — you must give ACTIVE vitamin D (calcitriol or paricalcitol) PLUS phosphate binders to control phosphate first. Giving Ca/calcitriol without addressing high phosphate risks vascular/metastatic calcification.
6. Phosphate binders + calcitriol
CKD-MBD management triad: (1) control phosphate with binders taken WITH meals — non-calcium binders (SEVELAMER, lanthanum) preferred, or calcium acetate/carbonate if Ca is low; (2) replace ACTIVE vitamin D (calcitriol/paricalcitol) to restore gut Ca absorption and suppress PTH; (3) CINACALCET (a calcimimetic that sensitizes the CaSR) to lower PTH without raising Ca/phosphate, useful in secondary hyperPTH on dialysis.
⚠️ WRONG: using cinacalcet in a hypocalcemic CKD patient without correcting Ca first. Cinacalcet LOWERS Ca (it activates CaSR -> suppresses PTH) and can precipitate symptomatic hypocalcemia — it is for high-PTH states with adequate/high Ca, not for fixing low Ca. Also avoid calcium-based binders if the patient is hypercalcemic or has vascular calcification.
7. Acute pancreatitis
In acute pancreatitis, released pancreatic lipase liberates free fatty acids from peripancreatic and mesenteric fat; these FFAs chelate calcium (SAPONIFICATION) and deposit it as insoluble calcium soaps -> hypocalcemia. Glucagon-stimulated calcitonin and hypomagnesemia may also contribute. The degree of hypocalcemia tracks with severity.
⚠️ WRONG: ignoring hypocalcemia as incidental in pancreatitis. Hypocalcemia (Ca <8 mg/dL or ionized fall) is a marker of SEVERE disease and is a Ranson criterion (Ca <8 at 48 h) — it signals poor prognosis. Don't dismiss it; recognize it as a severity flag and check ionized Ca and magnesium.
8. Rhabdo / tumor lysis
Massive cell breakdown — RHABDOMYOLYSIS (crush/trauma) and TUMOR LYSIS SYNDROME (post-chemo of bulky/leukemia-lymphoma) — dumps intracellular phosphate into blood. The resulting hyperphosphatemia drives calcium-phosphate precipitation into tissues -> HYPOCALCEMIA (also hyperkalemia, hyperuricemia, high LDH, AKI). In rhabdo, Ca that precipitated in damaged muscle may be released LATER as a rebound hypercalcemia during recovery.
⚠️ WRONG: aggressively giving IV calcium to correct the lab hypocalcemia in tumor lysis/rhabdo. Unless the patient is SYMPTOMATIC (tetany, seizures, arrhythmia), withhold calcium — adding Ca to a high-phosphate milieu drives METASTATIC calcium-phosphate deposition (worsens kidney injury, soft-tissue calcification). Lower the phosphate first; treat the underlying process.
9. Citrate / blood products
Stored blood products (PRBCs, FFP, platelets) and continuous renal replacement therapy use CITRATE as anticoagulant. Citrate chelates ionized calcium; with massive transfusion or impaired hepatic citrate metabolism (liver disease, hypothermia, shock), ionized Ca falls -> hypocalcemia (and can cause QT prolongation, hypotension). Total Ca may look normal because the chelated Ca is still 'counted.'
⚠️ WRONG: trusting TOTAL calcium during massive transfusion or citrate CRRT. Citrate binds the IONIZED (physiologically active) fraction, so total Ca can be misleadingly normal while ionized Ca is dangerously low — always monitor IONIZED Ca and give calcium gluconate/chloride to keep it in range.
10. Hungry bone (post-PTHx)
Hungry bone syndrome occurs after PARATHYROIDECTOMY (or thyroidectomy, or correction of severe hyperthyroidism) when chronically high PTH is abruptly removed: the previously resorbing skeleton avidly re-mineralizes, rapidly pulling Ca, PHOSPHATE, and MAGNESIUM out of the blood -> profound, prolonged hypocalcemia (can last days to weeks). Risk highest with high preop PTH/ALP and large bone disease.
⚠️ WRONG: expecting transient, easily-corrected hypocalcemia after parathyroid surgery. Hungry bone causes SEVERE, sustained hypocalcemia (low Ca, low PO4, low Mg) needing aggressive IV/oral calcium PLUS calcitriol PLUS magnesium repletion over days. Distinguish it from surgical HYPOPARATHYROIDISM (low PTH) — hungry bone has low PO4 whereas hypoPTH has HIGH phosphate.
11. Hypomagnesemia triangle
Severe hypomagnesemia (Mg <1 mg/dL) causes hypocalcemia by a DOUBLE hit: it impairs PTH SECRETION and also induces end-organ PTH RESISTANCE -> functional hypoparathyroidism with an inappropriately low/normal PTH despite low Ca. Causes of low Mg: alcoholism, diarrhea/malabsorption, loop diuretics, PPIs, aminoglycosides, cisplatin, Gitelman. The three often fall together (Mg, Ca, PO4), so check all three.
⚠️ WRONG: pouring in more calcium when hypocalcemia 'won't correct' despite repletion. If Ca is refractory, CHECK AND REPLETE MAGNESIUM FIRST — until Mg is restored, PTH cannot be secreted or act, so Ca will not rise no matter how much you give. Refractory hypocalcemia = think magnesium.
12. IV calcium gluconate (acute)
Acute SYMPTOMATIC hypocalcemia (tetany, Chvostek/Trousseau signs, laryngospasm, seizures, QT prolongation/arrhythmia) is treated with IV CALCIUM GLUCONATE 1-2 g in D5W over 5-10 min, then a continuous infusion, with cardiac/telemetry monitoring. Calcium gluconate is preferred peripherally (calcium chloride is more concentrated/irritating, reserved for central lines/codes). Transition to oral calcium + calcitriol once stable.
⚠️ WRONG: giving IV calcium without simultaneously checking/correcting MAGNESIUM and being cautious of the patient's DIGOXIN status — rapid IV calcium can precipitate fatal digoxin toxicity ('stone heart'). And in hyperphosphatemic states (tumor lysis/rhabdo) reserve IV Ca for symptomatic patients only, to avoid metastatic calcification.
