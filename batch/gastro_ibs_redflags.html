📖 Read-through — all 10 symbols
1. IBS city gate / relapsing line
IBS runs a relapsing-remitting course — symptoms come and go over years. A waxing/waning pattern SUPPORTS functional disease rather than a progressive organic process. Long, fluctuating history is reassuring.
2. 'Comes and goes' suitcase (gold star)
Relapsing-remitting symptoms are a positive supportive feature of IBS. Gold star = correct/reassuring. Contrast with steadily progressive symptoms, which raise concern for organic disease.
3. Small volume / no blood (gold star)
IBS-D produces SMALL-volume diarrhea WITHOUT blood. Large-volume or bloody/nocturnal diarrhea is NOT IBS. Absence of blood and small stool volume are supportive features.
4. Honest gate guard 75%/year
About 75% of IBS patients switch subtype (IBS-D ↔ IBS-C ↔ IBS-M) within a single year — so subtype switching is COMMON, not rare. Tested as a distractor against the 'switching is rare' claim.
5. Weight loss (red X)
Unintentional weight loss is a RED FLAG arguing against IBS — mandates workup for malignancy, IBD, or malabsorption. Functional IBS does not cause weight loss.
⚠️ "Weight loss is consistent with IBS" → wrong; it is an alarm feature mandating investigation.
6. Nocturnal pain (red X)
Pain that wakes the patient at night is an organic red flag — functional IBS pain typically does not disrupt sleep. Nocturnal symptoms mandate evaluation (colonoscopy, calprotectin, celiac serology).
⚠️ "Nocturnal pain fits IBS" → wrong; night-waking pain is a red flag for organic disease.
7. Nocturnal diarrhea (red X)
Diarrhea that wakes the patient from sleep (nocturnal diarrhea) is a red flag pointing to organic disease such as IBD or infection. IBS-D does not characteristically occur at night.
⚠️ "Nocturnal diarrhea is typical of IBS" → wrong; it is an alarm feature requiring workup.
8. New onset in older age (red X)
New-onset symptoms in an older patient (e.g., >50) are a red flag — colorectal cancer and other organic disease rise with age, mandating colonoscopy. IBS usually presents in younger adults.
⚠️ "New IBS commonly starts in the elderly" → wrong; new onset in older age mandates workup for organic disease.
9. Steatorrhea (red X)
Steatorrhea (fatty, floating, foul stool) signals fat malabsorption — celiac, pancreatic insufficiency, or small-bowel disease — not IBS. A red flag mandating malabsorption workup.
⚠️ "Steatorrhea is part of IBS-D" → wrong; it indicates malabsorption and needs investigation.
10. 'Switching is rare' agitator (red X)
FALSE claim: subtype switching is rare. In reality ~75% of patients switch subtype within a year, so switching is COMMON. This is the explicit board trap on this scene.
⚠️ "Switching between diarrhea and constipation is rare" → wrong; ~75% of patients switch subtype within a year.
