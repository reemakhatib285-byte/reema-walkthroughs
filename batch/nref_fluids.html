⚠️ Board traps 11
  1. 0.9% Normal Saline — Normal saline is the universally 'safe' default fluid. WRONG — large volumes cause a hyperchloremic non-anion-gap metabolic acidosis and chloride-mediated renal afferent vasoconstriction; balanced crystalloids (LR/Plasma-Lyte) are preferred for most resuscitation (SMART/SALT-ED trials showed fewer major adverse kidney events).
  2. Avoid NS in hyperchloremic acidosis — Give more NS to 'flush out' an existing hyperchloremic acidosis. WRONG — adding chloride deepens the NAGMA; switch to a balanced/buffered crystalloid whose lactate or acetate is metabolized to bicarbonate.
  3. Ringer's Lactate / Plasma-Lyte — LR is contraindicated in lactic acidosis because it 'adds lactate.' WRONG — the lactate is metabolized to bicarbonate and does not meaningfully raise measured lactate; the real cautions are hyperkalemia (LR has K+) and co-infusion with ceftriaxone or blood through the same line (calcium → precipitation/clot risk).
  4. LR contains potassium — LR is dangerous in all renal failure because of its potassium. WRONG — the K+ load is small and often net-lowering via acidosis correction; the clean board contraindication is significant HYPERKALEMIA, where NS is preferred to avoid adding any K+.

WRONG — it is reserved for specific indications (rhabdo, severe acidemia, certain intoxications); overuse causes metabolic alkalosis, hypokalemia, hypocalcemia (alkalosis lowers ionized Ca, worsening rhabdo tetany), and volume overload.
  6. Bicarb for metabolic acidosis — Always give bicarbonate to correct DKA's low pH. WRONG — bicarbonate is avoided in DKA unless pH <7.0–7.1; insulin + fluids resolve the ketoacidosis and regenerate bicarbonate, and exogenous bicarb risks hypokalemia and cerebral edema (especially in children).

  10. HES increases AKI risk — A starch colloid is a good volume expander for the septic patient. WRONG — HES is a classic exam 'harmful choice': it raises AKI, RRT, and death risk in critical illness and is off the market for these indications. Use balanced crystalloid.

WRONG — boards test scenario-matching: hemorrhage → blood products; hyperchloremic acidosis → balanced crystalloid; rhabdo → isotonic fluids ± bicarbonate; hypernatremia/free-water deficit → D5W; hyperkalemia → potassium-free NS.
📖 Read-through — all 11 symbols
1. 0.9% Normal Saline
0.9% NaCl contains Na+ 154 and Cl- 154 mEq/L with osmolarity ~308 — isotonic, distributes through the extracellular space (~1/3 stays intravascular, so ~3 mL given per 1 mL of intravascular expansion). First-line for hypovolemic resuscitation, hypercalcemia (saline diuresis), and hyponatremia volume repletion. The supraphysiologic chloride (plasma Cl- is ~100) is its weakness.
⚠️ Normal saline is the universally 'safe' default fluid. WRONG — large volumes cause a hyperchloremic non-anion-gap metabolic acidosis and chloride-mediated renal afferent vasoconstriction; balanced crystalloids (LR/Plasma-Lyte) are preferred for most resuscitation (SMART/SALT-ED trials showed fewer major adverse kidney events).
2. Avoid NS in hyperchloremic acidosis
Each liter of NS adds a 54 mEq/L chloride excess over plasma; by the Stewart strong-ion approach, rising plasma Cl- lowers the strong-ion difference and drops bicarbonate → worsening NAGMA. Avoid in DKA recovery, diarrheal/RTA acidosis, and any established hyperchloremic acidosis; reach for LR/Plasma-Lyte instead.
⚠️ Give more NS to 'flush out' an existing hyperchloremic acidosis. WRONG — adding chloride deepens the NAGMA; switch to a balanced/buffered crystalloid whose lactate or acetate is metabolized to bicarbonate.
3. Ringer's Lactate / Plasma-Lyte
Lactated Ringer's: Na+ 130, Cl- 109, K+ 4, Ca2+ 3, lactate 28 (hepatically converted to bicarbonate). Plasma-Lyte: Na+ 140, Cl- 98, K+ 5, Mg2+, with acetate/gluconate buffers and no calcium. Both have near-physiologic chloride, so they avoid hyperchloremic acidosis and are preferred in surgical/ICU resuscitation, sepsis, and pancreatitis.
⚠️ LR is contraindicated in lactic acidosis because it 'adds lactate.' WRONG — the lactate is metabolized to bicarbonate and does not meaningfully raise measured lactate; the real cautions are hyperkalemia (LR has K+) and co-infusion with ceftriaxone or blood through the same line (calcium → precipitation/clot risk).
4. LR contains potassium
LR carries ~4 mEq/L K+ and Plasma-Lyte ~5 mEq/L. In normal kidneys this is trivial and LR may even lower serum K+ (correcting acidosis shifts K+ intracellularly), but in hyperkalemic AKI/ESRD the prudent board answer is to avoid potassium-containing fluids and use NS.
⚠️ LR is dangerous in all renal failure because of its potassium. WRONG — the K+ load is small and often net-lowering via acidosis correction; the clean board contraindication is significant HYPERKALEMIA, where NS is preferred to avoid adding any K+.
5. D5W + NaHCO3 (bicarbonate)
An isotonic bicarbonate drip is made by adding ~150 mEq NaHCO3 to 1 L D5W. In rhabdomyolysis it alkalinizes the urine (goal urine pH >6.5) to reduce myoglobin–Tamm-Horsfall cast precipitation and limit pigment cast nephropathy; aggressive volume (target UOP 200–300 mL/h) is the more important intervention. Also used to alkalinize urine in salicylate or TCA toxicity and to treat severe metabolic acidosis.
⚠️ Bicarbonate fluid is a routine resuscitation fluid. WRONG — it is reserved for specific indications (rhabdo, severe acidemia, certain intoxications); overuse causes metabolic alkalosis, hypokalemia, hypocalcemia (alkalosis lowers ionized Ca, worsening rhabdo tetany), and volume overload.
6. Bicarb for metabolic acidosis
IV bicarbonate is reserved for severe acidemia (pH <7.1, especially with hemodynamic instability) and for non-anion-gap losses (diarrhea, RTA). In high-anion-gap lactic acidosis or DKA, bicarbonate is generally NOT given unless pH <7.0–7.1, since treating the cause regenerates bicarbonate and exogenous bicarb risks rebound alkalosis and paradoxical intracellular/CNS acidosis.
⚠️ Always give bicarbonate to correct DKA's low pH. WRONG — bicarbonate is avoided in DKA unless pH <7.0–7.1; insulin + fluids resolve the ketoacidosis and regenerate bicarbonate, and exogenous bicarb risks hypokalemia and cerebral edema (especially in children).
7. Packed RBCs
pRBCs restore oxygen-carrying capacity, not just volume. In massive hemorrhage use balanced resuscitation (~1:1:1 RBC:plasma:platelets) and permissive hypotension until bleeding is controlled; transfusion threshold is generally Hb <7 g/dL (<8 in ACS/cardiac disease). One unit raises Hb ~1 g/dL.
⚠️ Resuscitate active hemorrhagic shock with crystalloid alone. WRONG — large-volume crystalloid in hemorrhage causes dilutional coagulopathy, hypothermia, and acidosis (the 'lethal triad'); when blood is the loss, give blood products, not just saline.
8. Colloids (albumin)
Albumin (5% for volume, 25% for oncotic pull) stays intravascular longer than crystalloid per mL. Evidence-based niches: spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (1.5 g/kg day 1, 1 g/kg day 3 to prevent HRS), large-volume paracentesis (~6–8 g per L removed), and hepatorenal syndrome. The SAFE trial showed no general resuscitation benefit over saline (and possible harm in TBI).
⚠️ Albumin is a superior general resuscitation fluid. WRONG — SAFE showed equivalence to saline overall and harm in traumatic brain injury; albumin's role is narrow (SBP, LVP, HRS), not routine volume resuscitation.
9. Albumin in hepatorenal syndrome
HRS-AKI treatment = albumin (1 g/kg/day, max ~100 g) PLUS a splanchnic vasoconstrictor — terlipressin (first-line where available) or midodrine + octreotide, or norepinephrine in the ICU. Albumin expands central volume and binds vasoactive mediators; this combination is a bridge, not a cure.
⚠️ Vasoconstrictors alone treat HRS. WRONG — albumin must be combined with the vasoconstrictor (terlipressin or midodrine/octreotide); and pharmacotherapy is only a bridge — definitive treatment of HRS is liver transplantation.
10. HES increases AKI risk
Synthetic hydroxyethyl starch colloids (Voluven, Hextend) increased AKI, need for renal replacement therapy, and mortality in critically ill/septic patients (VISEP, 6S, CHEST trials) and were withdrawn/restricted. Starch is deposited in renal tubules causing osmotic nephrosis.
⚠️ A starch colloid is a good volume expander for the septic patient. WRONG — HES is a classic exam 'harmful choice': it raises AKI, RRT, and death risk in critical illness and is off the market for these indications. Use balanced crystalloid.
11. The overarching rule
Fluid choice = composition of the loss + current acid-base/electrolyte state + goal (resuscitation vs maintenance). Maintenance ≈ '4-2-1' rule for rate, typically D5 1/2NS + 20 mEq KCl to cover free-water and glucose needs; resuscitation = isotonic balanced crystalloid boluses (e.g., 500 mL–1 L) titrated to perfusion. D5W (free water) is for hypernatremia/free-water deficit, NOT resuscitation (only ~1/12 stays intravascular).
⚠️ There is one best fluid for every patient. WRONG — boards test scenario-matching: hemorrhage → blood products; hyperchloremic acidosis → balanced crystalloid; rhabdo → isotonic fluids ± bicarbonate; hypernatremia/free-water deficit → D5W; hyperkalemia → potassium-free NS.
