📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Primary PCI gold star
Primary PCI is the preferred reperfusion strategy for STEMI whenever it can be delivered in a timely fashion at a PCI-capable center. It restores flow more reliably than lytics, has lower rates of death, reinfarction, and intracranial hemorrhage, and lets you mechanically open the culprit lesion. Indications: any STEMI (ST elevation or new LBBB) within 12h of symptom onset, and still beneficial up to 12–24h if ongoing ischemia, shock, or hemodynamic instability. Mnemonic: 'balloon beats clot-buster when the cath lab is close.'
⚠️ WRONG: choosing fibrinolysis over PCI when a patient presents to a PCI-capable hospital. Discriminator: if the cath lab is available and door-to-balloon can be met, PCI is always preferred — lytics are the fallback only when PCI cannot be delivered in time.
2. Door-to-balloon 90 MIN
At a PCI-capable hospital the target is door-to-balloon (first medical contact to device) ≤90 minutes. This is the benchmark quality metric for primary PCI and every minute of delay increases infarct size ('time is muscle'). The fastest reperfusion correlates with the best myocardial salvage and survival.
⚠️ WRONG answer: stating the door-to-balloon goal is 120 minutes. Discriminator: 90 min is the in-house PCI target; 120 min is the OUTSIDE limit of total time-to-PCI (including transfer) before you should give lytics instead — don't confuse the two thresholds.
3. DIDO 30
Door-in–door-out (DIDO) target is ≤30 minutes: at a non-PCI hospital, the patient should be diagnosed and shipped out within 30 min so the total transfer-to-balloon time can still meet ≤120 min. A DIDO >30 min predicts worse outcomes and may tip the decision toward giving lytics locally.
⚠️ WRONG: DIDO target is 90 minutes. Discriminator: 30 min is for getting the patient back OUT the door of a transferring hospital; confusing it with the 90-min in-house balloon time is the classic mix-up.
4. PCI within 120 MIN
If the patient is at a non-PCI center, transfer for primary PCI is preferred only if total first-medical-contact-to-device time is ≤120 minutes. If PCI cannot be achieved within 120 min, give fibrinolysis first (within 30-min door-to-needle) then transfer. This is the single most tested branch point in STEMI triage.
⚠️ WRONG: transfer everyone for PCI regardless of how long it takes. Discriminator: beyond an anticipated 120 min to balloon, lytics save more muscle than waiting for a delayed PCI — choose pharmacologic reperfusion, not endless transfer.
5. Fibrinolysis door-to-needle 30
When lysis is the strategy, door-to-needle should be ≤30 minutes. Preferred agents are fibrin-specific lytics: tenecteplase (TNK, single weight-based IV bolus — easiest), alteplase (tPA, bolus + infusion), or reteplase. Give with adjunctive aspirin, a P2Y12 inhibitor (clopidogrel), and anticoagulation (enoxaparin or UFH). Always pair with a plan for routine angiography 3–24h later (pharmacoinvasive strategy).
⚠️ WRONG: door-to-needle target is 90 minutes. Discriminator: needle time is fast (≤30 min) — the 90-min figure belongs to in-house door-to-balloon, not to lytic administration.
6. 0–2 H / 3–12 H window
Fibrinolysis is most effective when given early — greatest myocardial salvage in the first 0–2 hours from symptom onset, with declining but still real benefit through ~12 hours. Beyond 12h, lytics offer little benefit and rising harm, so they are generally not given unless ongoing ischemia in a setting where PCI is truly unavailable. The benefit curve is steepest early.
⚠️ WRONG: fibrinolysis is useful out to 24 hours regardless. Discriminator: the lytic window is roughly ≤12h from symptom onset (best ≤2–3h); after 12h the bleeding risk outweighs benefit — late presenters who still need reperfusion go to PCI, not lysis.
7. Contraindication board
This board lists ABSOLUTE contraindications to FIBRINOLYSIS — they do not apply to primary PCI. This is why PCI is favored in any patient with bleeding risk: PCI has no bleeding-based contraindications of this kind. Whenever a STEMI patient has any item on this board, mechanical reperfusion (PCI) becomes the route of choice.
⚠️ WRONG: these contraindicate primary PCI. Discriminator: every item here forbids LYTICS only — a patient with active bleeding or prior intracranial hemorrhage should be sent for PCI, not denied reperfusion altogether.
8. Prior hemorrhagic stroke — ANY TIME
Any prior intracranial hemorrhage or hemorrhagic stroke at ANY point in life is an absolute contraindication to fibrinolysis — the risk of recurrent catastrophic intracranial bleed is unacceptable. Also absolute: known cerebral AVM, intracranial malignancy, or structural cerebrovascular lesion. Send these patients for PCI.
⚠️ WRONG: only a RECENT hemorrhagic stroke matters. Discriminator: for hemorrhagic/intracranial bleed the time window is 'ever' — any history at any time permanently rules out lytics, unlike ischemic stroke which uses a 3-month/1-year cutoff.
9. Ischemic stroke within 1 YEAR
An ischemic stroke within the recent window (classically within 3 months by ACC/AHA, with caution out to a year) is a contraindication to fibrinolysis. Acute ischemic stroke can hemorrhagically transform under lytic therapy. Older remote ischemic strokes become relative rather than absolute. PCI is the alternative.
⚠️ WRONG: ischemic stroke is never a contraindication to lysis. Discriminator: a RECENT ischemic stroke is contraindicated; the nuance vs hemorrhagic stroke (any time = absolute) is the favorite distractor pairing.
10. Severe HTN
Severe uncontrolled hypertension (SBP >180 or DBP >110 mmHg) is a contraindication to fibrinolysis because of intracranial hemorrhage risk; it is relative if BP can be lowered before lytic. A history of chronic severe poorly controlled HTN is also a relative contraindication. Control the pressure or choose PCI.
⚠️ WRONG: blood pressure is irrelevant to lysis decisions. Discriminator: markedly elevated BP at presentation (>180/110) sharply raises bleeding risk — either treat it down first or default to PCI.
11. Aortic dissection
Suspected or known aortic dissection is an absolute contraindication to fibrinolysis — lysing a dissection can cause fatal rupture/tamponade. A type A dissection can also occlude a coronary ostium and mimic STEMI, so always consider it in chest pain with a widened mediastinum, pulse deficit, or new aortic regurgitation before giving lytics.
⚠️ WRONG: a dissection is safe to lyse. Discriminator: lysing a dissection is lethal — the STEMI may itself be caused by the dissection, so exclude dissection clinically/by imaging before any lytic.
12. Active internal bleeding
Active internal bleeding or a known bleeding diathesis (excluding menses) is an absolute contraindication to fibrinolysis. Also note recent major surgery/trauma and recent internal bleeding as contraindications. These patients should undergo primary PCI, which carries no such bleeding-based prohibition.
⚠️ WRONG: an active GI bleed is only a RELATIVE contraindication. Discriminator: active internal bleeding (other than menstruation) is ABSOLUTE — do not lyse; route to PCI instead.
13. TIMI 3 trophy
The angiographic goal of reperfusion is TIMI 3 (normal, brisk, complete) flow in the culprit artery. The TIMI scale runs 0 (no flow) → 1 (penetration without perfusion) → 2 (partial/delayed) → 3 (normal). Achieving TIMI 3 flow correlates with the best survival; PCI achieves it far more reliably (>90%) than lytics (~50–60%).
⚠️ WRONG: TIMI 1 flow is an adequate endpoint. Discriminator: only TIMI 3 (full normal flow) is the real success endpoint — TIMI 1–2 means inadequate reperfusion and predicts worse outcomes.
14. Shock = culprit only
In STEMI complicated by cardiogenic shock, PCI of the CULPRIT vessel only is favored — the CULPRIT-SHOCK trial showed that immediate routine multivessel PCI increased death/renal failure at 30 days vs culprit-only revascularization. Stage non-culprit lesions later if needed. Emergent revascularization in shock is indicated regardless of time from onset.
⚠️ WRONG: in shock, perform complete multivessel PCI all at once. Discriminator: CULPRIT-SHOCK established culprit-only PCI as superior in shock; immediate full multivessel intervention worsens outcomes — defer non-culprit lesions.
