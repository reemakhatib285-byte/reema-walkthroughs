📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. What ratio tells you
BUN:Cr compares two nitrogenous wastes that the kidney handles differently. Urea is freely filtered but PASSIVELY reabsorbed in the proximal tubule and collecting duct following water — so when avid water reabsorption is on (low flow, ADH), urea follows and BUN climbs. Creatinine is filtered and essentially NOT reabsorbed, making it a cleaner GFR marker. The ratio therefore separates 'flow/volume' problems (urea-dominant) from true parenchymal GFR loss (creatinine-dominant).
⚠️ A rising BUN is a reliable standalone marker of falling GFR. WRONG — BUN is confounded by protein load/catabolism (GI bleed, steroids, feeds) and by avid reabsorption (low flow), all independent of GFR; only creatinine tracks GFR.
2. Normal ratio
Normal BUN:Cr is 10–15:1 (some texts say 10–20:1). In intrinsic renal failure such as ATN, both rise proportionally so the ratio stays NORMAL (~10–15). A normal ratio with elevated creatinine points AWAY from prerenal and toward an intrinsic tubular process.
⚠️ A normal BUN:Cr ratio rules out kidney disease. WRONG — in ATN/intrinsic AKI both BUN and Cr rise together, keeping the ratio normal even with severe AKI. The ratio discriminates prerenal vs intrinsic, it does not screen for the presence of AKI.
3. Prerenal >20:1
Prerenal azotemia → ratio >20:1. Low renal perfusion (hypovolemia, CHF, cirrhosis, sepsis early) triggers RAAS + ADH, driving avid proximal Na/water reabsorption; urea passively follows the water back into blood while creatinine does not. Supporting prerenal labs: FENa <1%, urine osmolality >500, urine Na <20. Treatment is restoring perfusion (IV fluids for hypovolemia, diuresis/afterload reduction for cardiorenal).
⚠️ A BUN:Cr >20:1 is diagnostic of prerenal AKI. WRONG — GI bleed, high-protein/steroid catabolism, and tetracycline also push the ratio >20 with normal kidneys. Always corroborate with urine Na, FENa, and clinical volume status before calling it prerenal.
4. BUN rises header
BUN is a confounded number: it rises by two independent routes — increased urea PRODUCTION (protein/nitrogen load or catabolism) or increased urea REABSORPTION (low flow). Only the reabsorption route reflects true volume/perfusion status. This is why creatinine, not BUN, anchors GFR estimates.
⚠️ You can read volume status straight off the BUN. WRONG — only the reabsorption route reflects perfusion; a high BUN from production (catabolism, GI bleed, protein feeds) tells you nothing about volume. Confirm with FENa, urine Na, and exam.
5. ↑Urea production
Production drivers raise BUN with a NORMAL GFR: upper GI bleed (hemoglobin digested → amino acids → urea), high-protein enteral feeds (e.g., Ensure/TPN), corticosteroids and tetracyclines (anti-anabolic/catabolic), thyrotoxicosis, burns, and fever. These inflate BUN:Cr without any drop in perfusion.
⚠️ A patient on high-dose steroids with a rising BUN must be volume-depleted. WRONG — steroids are catabolic and raise urea production directly; creatinine and FENa stay normal. Look at the creatinine and urine indices, not the BUN alone.
6. ↑Urea reabsorption
The reabsorption route is the true prerenal mechanism: hypoperfusion → RAAS/ADH activation → enhanced proximal Na+water reabsorption with passive urea backflow → disproportionate BUN rise vs creatinine. This is the only high-ratio cause that responds to fluids/perfusion correction.
⚠️ All high BUN:Cr ratios will improve with IV fluids. WRONG — only the reabsorption (prerenal) cause is fluid-responsive; a high ratio from GI bleed, steroids, or protein feeds will not normalize with fluids and may signal the bleed itself.
7. Creatinine behavior
Creatinine is generated at a steady rate from muscle creatine and excreted almost purely by filtration, so it tracks GFR and is immune to diet/catabolism. Caveats: it LAGS GFR by 24–72h in acute injury (a normal early creatinine can hide severe AKI), and low muscle mass (elderly, cachectic, cirrhotic) gives a falsely low creatinine that overstates GFR. Some drugs (trimethoprim, cimetidine) block tubular creatinine secretion and bump creatinine WITHOUT a true GFR change.
⚠️ Creatinine is a perfect, instantaneous GFR marker. WRONG — it lags acute GFR loss by days and is biased by muscle mass and by secretion-blocking drugs (trimethoprim, cimetidine). A 'normal' creatinine early in AKI does not equal a normal GFR.
8. BUN:Cr HIGH list
Five classic high-ratio (>20:1) causes: (1) prerenal hypoperfusion (reabsorption), plus four production/load causes — (2) GI bleed, (3) high-protein feeds, (4) steroids/catabolism, (5) tetracyclines. Only #1 is a volume/perfusion problem.
⚠️ A ratio >20:1 on the panel equals prerenal AKI. WRONG — four of the five high-ratio causes (GI bleed, protein feeds, steroids, tetracyclines) are production-driven with normal kidneys and normal FENa; only hypoperfusion is true prerenal physiology.
9. Prerenal (reabsorption)
Prerenal is the prototype high-ratio state caused by avid tubular reabsorption — confirm with FENa <1%, urine osm >500, urine Na <20, and bland urine sediment (hyaline casts at most). It is fully reversible if perfusion is restored before tubular ischemia converts it to ATN.
⚠️ Every prerenal AKI shows FENa <1%. WRONG — prerenal on a background of diuretics or CKD can have FENa >1%; use FEUrea (<35% = prerenal) when the patient is on diuretics.
10. GI bleed / protein load
An UPPER GI bleed delivers a large protein (hemoglobin) load to the gut; bacterial breakdown → absorbed amino acids → hepatic urea synthesis → BUN spikes while creatinine stays flat → ratio often >30–40. A high BUN:Cr in a patient with melena/coffee-ground emesis is a clue to GI bleeding itself.
⚠️ A high BUN:Cr from a GI bleed means the patient is simply volume-depleted. WRONG (or only half-right) — the dominant mechanism is the digested-protein urea load, not pure prerenal physiology; the ratio can be very high even before significant hypovolemia. Treat the bleed and assess volume separately.
11. High protein / steroids
High-protein enteral nutrition (Ensure, TPN) and catabolic states (corticosteroids, sepsis, trauma, GI bleed) raise urea production with a normal GFR → isolated BUN elevation and high ratio. Tetracyclines act similarly via an anti-anabolic effect. These resolve when the load/drug is withdrawn.
⚠️ An ICU patient on tube feeds and steroids with an isolated BUN bump has worsening renal function. WRONG — catabolism plus a high-protein load raises urea production with a normal GFR; the creatinine and urine indices stay normal. Don't escalate fluids or call it AKI on BUN alone.
12. BUN:Cr LOW / normal
Low/normal ratio (<10–15) occurs when creatinine rises out of proportion to BUN OR when urea production is low. Causes: (1) rhabdomyolysis — necrotic muscle dumps creatinine directly, so creatinine outruns BUN; (2) advanced liver disease/malnutrition — the failing liver makes little urea so BUN is low; (3) SIADH and overhydration — dilution; (4) low protein intake. A low ratio in a patient with crush injury, statin/seizure/immobility, and CK in the thousands points to rhabdo with pigment nephropathy.
⚠️ Rhabdomyolysis produces a HIGH BUN:Cr ratio. WRONG — rhabdo releases creatinine straight from damaged muscle, so creatinine rises faster than BUN and the ratio is LOW or normal. The other low-ratio trap is cirrhosis, where a poorly functioning liver underproduces urea and masks azotemia.
