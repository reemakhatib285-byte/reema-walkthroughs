⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. Primary VF room — Board says: 'Primary VF reflects extensive pump failure and predicts poor outcome.' WRONG — that describes SECONDARY VF (occurs with HF/cardiogenic shock and marks a large infarct). Discriminator: primary VF happens <48h in a hemodynamically intact heart and does NOT worsen long-term prognosis once the patient is revascularized.
  2. First 48 hours — Board says: 'Primary VF occurs after day 2 / late in the hospital course.' WRONG. Discriminator: by definition primary (early) VF is WITHIN 48h; arrhythmia beyond 48h is 'late VF/VT' with a different, worse implication and is the type that argues for an ICD.
  3. Excellent long-term prognosis — Board says: 'A patient who had VF on day 1 of his STEMI needs an ICD before discharge because his long-term survival is poor.' WRONG. Discriminator: early primary VF that is defibrillated and revascularized confers EXCELLENT long-term prognosis — the correct answer is reassess EF in ≥40 days, not implant now.
  4. No ICD for primary VF — Board says: 'Any VF episode during an MI is an automatic, lifelong ICD indication.' WRONG. Discriminator: timing is everything — VF <48h = reversible ischemic trigger (no ICD); sustained VT/VF >48h or low EF measured later = ICD.
  5. Secondary VF / poor prognosis — Board says: 'Secondary VF carries an excellent prognosis like primary VF.' WRONG. Discriminator: secondary VF is a marker of extensive myocardial damage and HF, so in-hospital mortality is markedly ELEVATED — the opposite of primary VF.
  6. In-hospital mortality elevated — Board says: 'VF does not change in-hospital mortality regardless of when it occurs.' WRONG. Discriminator: secondary VF (with HF/shock) significantly raises in-hospital mortality; primary VF (<48h, intact pump) does not change long-term mortality — the context, not the rhythm name, drives the answer.
  7. EF may recover — don't implant early — Board says: 'Implant an ICD during the initial STEMI admission because the EF is 30%.' WRONG. Discriminator: an acutely low EF may recover — the guideline-correct answer is to optimize GDMT and reassess EF at ≥40 days post-MI (or ≥90 days post-revascularization) before deciding on an ICD.
  8. 30 days ≠ final EF — Board says: 'The EF obtained on day 1–2 of the MI determines ICD eligibility.' WRONG. Discriminator: the qualifying EF must be measured after the recovery window (≥40 days post-MI / ≥90 days post-revasc), not acutely — using the early EF over-implants devices.
  9. 40 days post-MI hourglass — Board says: 'Refer for ICD at 7 days post-MI if EF is low.' WRONG. Discriminator: primary-prevention ICD eval is deferred to ≥40 days post-MI — implanting at one week showed no benefit (DINAMIT) and is the classic distractor.
  10. 90 days post-revasc hourglass — Board says: 'Implant an ICD immediately after PCI in a patient with EF 30%.' WRONG. Discriminator: post-revascularization you must wait ~90 days and re-check EF, since the procedure may raise EF above the ICD threshold — immediate implantation is not guideline-supported.
  11. EF ≤35% + NYHA II–III vest — Board says: 'An ICD is only indicated when the patient is NYHA class IV.' WRONG. Discriminator: NYHA IV (non-ambulatory, end-stage) patients are generally NOT candidates for a primary-prevention ICD (consider CRT-D, transplant, or palliation). 📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Primary VF room
Primary VF is ventricular fibrillation occurring in the first 48h of an MI from acute electrical instability of ischemic myocardium (re-entry around the infarct border zone, catecholamine surge, hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia), NOT from pump failure or shock. It is the most common cause of pre-hospital sudden cardiac death in MI. Treat immediately with unsynchronized defibrillation (200 J biphasic) plus amiodarone 300 mg IV bolus; correct K+ to >4.0 and Mg2+. Mnemonic: 'primary = pure electrical, prognosis preserved.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Primary VF reflects extensive pump failure and predicts poor outcome.' WRONG — that describes SECONDARY VF (occurs with HF/cardiogenic shock and marks a large infarct). Discriminator: primary VF happens <48h in a hemodynamically intact heart and does NOT worsen long-term prognosis once the patient is revascularized.
2. First 48 hours
The 48-hour window defines 'primary/early' VF. VF or sustained VT in the first 48h after STEMI is attributed to transient ischemia/reperfusion and electrical instability, so it does NOT by itself trigger an ICD. After 48h, ventricular arrhythmia is 'late' and signals a fixed arrhythmogenic substrate (scar re-entry) — that DOES carry prognostic weight and may warrant ICD evaluation. Mnemonic: 'the 48-hour clock resets the prognosis.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Primary VF occurs after day 2 / late in the hospital course.' WRONG. Discriminator: by definition primary (early) VF is WITHIN 48h; arrhythmia beyond 48h is 'late VF/VT' with a different, worse implication and is the type that argues for an ICD.
3. Excellent long-term prognosis
Patients who survive early primary VF and are successfully revascularized have long-term survival similar to MI patients who never had VF. The risk was confined to the acute peri-infarct period, so once discharged their prognosis is excellent and no ICD is needed for that event alone. This is why you do NOT implant a device for an isolated early VF episode. Mnemonic: 'survive the first 48h → survive long-term.'
⚠️ Board says: 'A patient who had VF on day 1 of his STEMI needs an ICD before discharge because his long-term survival is poor.' WRONG. Discriminator: early primary VF that is defibrillated and revascularized confers EXCELLENT long-term prognosis — the correct answer is reassess EF in ≥40 days, not implant now.
4. No ICD for primary VF
An ICD is NOT indicated for VF/VT occurring within the first 48h of MI. The arrhythmia is a transient ischemic phenomenon, not a marker of a permanent substrate, and trials (DINAMIT, IRIS) showed early ICD implantation post-MI provides no mortality benefit and may harm. ICDs are reserved for sustained VT/VF that is unrelated to a transient/reversible cause, or for primary prevention based on EF measured ≥40 days out. Mnemonic: 'early VF, no ICD.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Any VF episode during an MI is an automatic, lifelong ICD indication.' WRONG. Discriminator: timing is everything — VF <48h = reversible ischemic trigger (no ICD); sustained VT/VF >48h or low EF measured later = ICD.
5. Secondary VF / poor prognosis
Secondary VF occurs in the setting of heart failure, cardiogenic shock, or hemodynamic collapse and reflects a large infarct with pump failure — not just electrical instability. It carries high in-hospital mortality (the death is driven by the underlying pump failure). Management is defibrillation plus aggressive treatment of the shock/HF (revascularization, inotropes/pressors, mechanical support). Mnemonic: 'secondary = shock-associated, survival suffers.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Secondary VF carries an excellent prognosis like primary VF.' WRONG. Discriminator: secondary VF is a marker of extensive myocardial damage and HF, so in-hospital mortality is markedly ELEVATED — the opposite of primary VF.
6. In-hospital mortality elevated
The excess mortality of secondary VF comes from the large infarct/pump failure it accompanies, not the arrhythmia per se. These patients are typically Killip class III–IV. Even with successful defibrillation, in-hospital death is high because the underlying cardiogenic shock persists. This is why secondary VF is a poor-prognosis marker while primary VF is not. Mnemonic: 'big infarct → secondary VF → big mortality.'
⚠️ Board says: 'VF does not change in-hospital mortality regardless of when it occurs.' WRONG. Discriminator: secondary VF (with HF/shock) significantly raises in-hospital mortality; primary VF (<48h, intact pump) does not change long-term mortality — the context, not the rhythm name, drives the answer.
7. EF may recover — don't implant early
Acutely depressed LV function after MI often improves once stunned/hibernating myocardium recovers following revascularization and optimal GDMT. Implanting an ICD during the index admission based on an acutely low EF over-treats patients whose EF will rise above threshold. Therefore defer the ICD decision and re-measure EF after a waiting period. Mnemonic: 'stunned muscle wakes up — wait before you wire.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Implant an ICD during the initial STEMI admission because the EF is 30%.' WRONG. Discriminator: an acutely low EF may recover — the guideline-correct answer is to optimize GDMT and reassess EF at ≥40 days post-MI (or ≥90 days post-revascularization) before deciding on an ICD.
8. 30 days ≠ final EF
EF measured at or before 30 days is not the 'final' EF for ICD decisions. Guidelines require reassessment at ≥40 days after the MI, or ≥90 days after revascularization (PCI/CABG), because recovery continues over weeks. The later, stabilized EF — combined with NYHA class — determines primary-prevention ICD eligibility. Mnemonic: '40 days for the MI, 90 days for the wire (revasc).'
⚠️ Board says: 'The EF obtained on day 1–2 of the MI determines ICD eligibility.' WRONG. Discriminator: the qualifying EF must be measured after the recovery window (≥40 days post-MI / ≥90 days post-revasc), not acutely — using the early EF over-implants devices.
9. 40 days post-MI hourglass
Primary-prevention ICD evaluation must wait ≥40 days after the MI (per MADIT-II/SCD-HeFT-derived guidelines and the DINAMIT/IRIS early-implant null results). This waiting period allows EF recovery and avoids implanting in patients with high early non-arrhythmic mortality. At 40 days, re-measure EF; if EF ≤35% with NYHA II–III (or ≤30% NYHA I), implant. Mnemonic: 'wait forty days after the MI, then decide.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Refer for ICD at 7 days post-MI if EF is low.' WRONG. Discriminator: primary-prevention ICD eval is deferred to ≥40 days post-MI — implanting at one week showed no benefit (DINAMIT) and is the classic distractor.
10. 90 days post-revasc hourglass
If the patient was revascularized (PCI or CABG), wait ~90 days before primary-prevention ICD evaluation, because revascularization itself can improve EF over that period. Re-image EF at ≥90 days; implant only if it remains ≤35% (NYHA II–III) or ≤30% (NYHA I). The two clocks are distinct: 40 days post-MI vs 90 days post-revascularization. Mnemonic: 'revascularized = give it 90 days to revive.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Implant an ICD immediately after PCI in a patient with EF 30%.' WRONG. Discriminator: post-revascularization you must wait ~90 days and re-check EF, since the procedure may raise EF above the ICD threshold — immediate implantation is not guideline-supported.
11. EF ≤35% + NYHA II–III vest
Core primary-prevention ICD criterion (SCD-HeFT): EF ≤35% with NYHA class II–III heart failure on optimal GDMT, measured after the appropriate waiting period. The ICD prevents sudden cardiac death from scar-related VT/VF. Patient must have a reasonable expected survival >1 year with good functional status. Mnemonic: 'thirty-five and class two-three → ICD for me.'
⚠️ Board says: 'An ICD is only indicated when the patient is NYHA class IV.' WRONG. Discriminator: NYHA IV (non-ambulatory, end-stage) patients are generally NOT candidates for a primary-prevention ICD (consider CRT-D, transplant, or palliation). The sweet spot is NYHA II–III with EF ≤35%.
12. EF ≤30% NYHA I vest
Primary-prevention ICD is ALSO indicated at EF ≤30% even in NYHA class I (asymptomatic) patients post-MI (MADIT-II criterion). The lower EF threshold compensates for the absence of symptoms. So two pathways qualify: EF ≤35% with NYHA II–III, OR EF ≤30% with NYHA I. Mnemonic: 'thirty or under, symptoms or not.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Asymptomatic (NYHA I) patients never qualify for an ICD.' WRONG. Discriminator: an asymptomatic post-MI patient with EF ≤30% (measured ≥40 days out) DOES qualify for a primary-prevention ICD per MADIT-II.
13. EF ≥45% improving (no ICD)
If the reassessed EF has recovered above threshold (e.g., ≥45%, and certainly if normalized), there is NO indication for a primary-prevention ICD — the arrhythmic risk has fallen with the improved function. This is the payoff of waiting 40/90 days: you spare patients an unnecessary device. Reassessment, not the acute nadir EF, governs the decision. Mnemonic: 'EF bounced back → no device.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Implant the ICD anyway even though repeat EF improved to 50%.' WRONG. Discriminator: once EF recovers above the threshold, the patient no longer meets criteria — the correct action is continued GDMT and surveillance, not implantation.
14. EF ≤40% + NSVT monitor
In the intermediate zone — EF 36–40% with non-sustained VT (NSVT) on monitoring — patients do not automatically qualify by EF alone. The classic MUSTT/MADIT-I pathway is an electrophysiology (EP) study: if programmed stimulation induces sustained VT, an ICD is indicated. This risk-stratifies patients who fall between clear-cut thresholds. Mnemonic: 'borderline EF + NSVT → send to the EP lab.'
⚠️ Board says: 'A patient with EF 38% and runs of NSVT needs no further workup.' WRONG. Discriminator: this intermediate group warrants an EP study; inducible sustained VT then justifies an ICD (MADIT-I/MUSTT). Ignoring NSVT in a patient with reduced EF misses a guided ICD indication.
