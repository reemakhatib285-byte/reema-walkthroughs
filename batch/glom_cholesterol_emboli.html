📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Post-procedure / anticoag trigger
Atheroembolic (cholesterol crystal embolism) disease is typically triggered by disruption of aortic plaque — recent angiography/catheterization, vascular surgery, or anticoagulation/thrombolysis. Showers of cholesterol crystals lodge in small arteries of kidney, skin, and gut.
⚠️ Suspect cholesterol emboli (not contrast nephropathy) when AKI appears DAYS-TO-WEEKS after a procedure with skin/eye findings — contrast nephropathy peaks within 3–5 days and resolves.
2. Aorta — aging atherosclerosis
The embolic source is an atherosclerotic, often aneurysmal aorta in an elderly arteriopath. Cholesterol crystals released from ulcerated plaques shower distally into end-arteries.
⚠️ The source is the AORTA's atheromatous plaque — this is an embolic (not thrombotic) and not an immune/glomerular primary process.
3. Catheter trigger
Mechanical disruption by an intra-arterial catheter (cardiac cath, angiography, aortic surgery) is the classic precipitant, dislodging crystals. Onset of renal dysfunction is often subacute, days to weeks afterward.
⚠️ A subacute, stepwise rising creatinine WEEKS after catheterization favors cholesterol emboli over the early, transient creatinine bump of contrast-induced nephropathy.
4. Anticoagulation trigger
Anticoagulation and thrombolytic therapy can precipitate cholesterol emboli by preventing protective clot over ulcerated plaque, allowing crystal release. Warfarin is a classic offender.
⚠️ Anticoagulation can CAUSE cholesterol embolization — bleeding into/destabilizing plaque — so 'more anticoagulation' is not the fix.
5. Acute vs subacute shower
Embolization can be acute (a single large shower) or recurrent/subacute ('silent' stuttering showers) producing a stepwise decline in renal function over weeks. This tempo distinguishes it from the rapid course of contrast or ATN.
⚠️ The classic STEPWISE/subacute creatinine rise over weeks separates atheroemboli from the abrupt-and-recovering pattern of contrast nephropathy or ATN.
6. Biconvex clefts — diagnostic
Biopsy shows pathognomonic biconvex, needle-shaped CLEFTS within small arteries/arterioles — the cholesterol itself dissolves during tissue processing, leaving the empty cleft. This confirms the diagnosis.
⚠️ The cholesterol crystals DISSOLVE in fixation; you see empty BICONVEX CLEFTS, not visible cholesterol — recognizing the cleft is the diagnostic clue.
7. Eosinophilia + eosinophiluria
A characteristic lab triad includes transient peripheral EOSINOPHILIA, eosinophiluria, and hypocomplementemia, sometimes with fever and elevated inflammatory markers — a systemic embolic syndrome mimicking vasculitis or AIN.
⚠️ Eosinophilia + AKI also occurs in acute interstitial nephritis — but cholesterol emboli add livedo reticularis, blue toes, and a recent vascular procedure.
8. Blue toe / livedo reticularis
Peripheral skin findings — livedo reticularis and 'blue toe syndrome' with intact distal pulses — are key clinical clues that crystals are showering systemically. Look at the feet in any post-cath AKI.
⚠️ Blue toes WITH palpable pedal pulses is classic for cholesterol emboli (microvascular), distinguishing it from large-vessel limb ischemia (absent pulses).
9. Ocular Hollenhorst plaques
Bright refractile cholesterol crystals at retinal artery bifurcations (Hollenhorst plaques) confirm systemic atheroembolization and may cause amaurosis/visual field loss. Brain showers can cause TIA/stroke.
⚠️ Hollenhorst plaques on fundoscopy are visible evidence of cholesterol emboli — a window into the same process injuring the kidney.
10. Renal — FSGS-like, hematuria
Renally, cholesterol emboli cause a subacute decline in GFR with mild proteinuria and bland-to-mild hematuria; chronic ischemia can produce secondary FSGS-like glomerular changes. Prognosis for renal recovery is often poor.
⚠️ Proteinuria is usually MILD (not nephrotic) and the picture is ischemic — not a primary nephrotic glomerulopathy despite any FSGS-like change.
11. No reversal — irreversible
There is no specific therapy to reverse established crystal embolization — management is supportive (statins, blood pressure control, avoiding further instrumentation/anticoagulation) and treating organ failure. Outcomes are frequently poor.
⚠️ Unlike a thrombus, cholesterol emboli have NO thrombolytic/reversal therapy — anticoagulation may worsen it.
12. Steroids don't help / rejected
Despite the eosinophilia and vasculitis-like presentation, corticosteroids are NOT proven beneficial for atheroembolic renal disease. Management focuses on risk-factor control and statins.
⚠️ Don't reflexively give steroids for the eosinophilia — they are ineffective here, unlike in true AIN or vasculitis.
13. Prevention only
Because there is no cure, prevention dominates: aggressive statin/lipid lowering, blood pressure control, smoking cessation, and minimizing unnecessary arterial instrumentation or anticoagulation in high-risk arteriopaths.
⚠️ The 'treatment' of cholesterol emboli is really PREVENTION and risk-factor modification — once crystals shower, the renal injury is largely fixed.
