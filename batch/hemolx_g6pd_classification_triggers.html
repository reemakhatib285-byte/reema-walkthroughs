📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. WHO classes by activity
G6PD severity is graded by enzyme activity: severe (chronic non-spherocytic hemolytic anemia, <10–20%), moderate Mediterranean-type with episodic hemolysis, and mild African A- variant. Activity level predicts whether hemolysis is chronic or only triggered.
⚠️ Class is by residual enzyme activity, not by ethnicity alone; the African A- variant is mild/episodic while Mediterranean is moderate-severe.
2. X-linked recessive (Xp28)
G6PD is X-linked recessive (Xp28). Hemizygous males are fully affected; the trait is the most common human enzyme defect (~500 million carriers worldwide). Confers malaria protection — a balanced polymorphism.
⚠️ Don't call it autosomal — X-linkage explains male predominance and the variable female phenotype.
3. Variable female ratio (lyonization)
Heterozygous females show variable severity due to random X-inactivation (lyonization), creating a mosaic of normal and deficient red cells. Some heterozygotes are as affected as males.
⚠️ A heterozygous female is NOT automatically protected — skewed lyonization can produce clinically significant hemolysis.
4. Geographic variants
Common variants map to malaria-endemic regions: G6PD A- (African, mild), Mediterranean (severe favism), and Asian variants (Canton, Viangchan). Geography predicts severity and trigger sensitivity.
⚠️ Mediterranean variant patients get severe favism; African A- patients usually do not — variant identity changes management.
5. Oxidative drug triggers
Oxidant drugs deplete NADPH and overwhelm glutathione: dapsone, primaquine/tafenoquine, rasburicase, nitrofurantoin, sulfonamides, methylene blue. These generate H2O2/ROS that the deficient cell cannot detoxify, causing acute hemolysis.
⚠️ Rasburicase and methylene blue are absolutely contraindicated; methylene blue (the antidote for other methemoglobinemia) paradoxically worsens it in G6PD deficiency.
6. Favism — fava beans
Fava (broad) beans contain vicine/convicine, potent oxidants causing acute hemolysis ('favism'), especially in Mediterranean variant children. Ingestion is the trigger; broad beans are unsafe.
⚠️ Favism is variant-dependent — classically Mediterranean, often absent in African A-. Don't assume all G6PD patients react to fava beans.
7. Infection trigger
Infection is actually the MOST common precipitant of hemolysis in G6PD deficiency — neutrophil oxidative burst generates ROS. Pneumonia, hepatitis, typhoid, and other febrile illnesses can trigger episodes.
⚠️ Students fixate on drugs/fava beans, but infection is the leading trigger overall — a frequent board point.
8. Primaquine special rule
Always screen G6PD before primaquine/tafenoquine for malaria radical cure, since these 8-aminoquinolines cause dose-dependent hemolysis. Single low-dose primaquine (gametocytocidal) is considered safe even in deficiency per WHO.
⚠️ Screen-first for radical cure; but a single low-dose primaquine for transmission-blocking is acceptable without screening — know the distinction.
9. Self-limited episodic hemolysis
In the common African A- variant, hemolysis is self-limited: only older red cells (lowest enzyme) lyse, then reticulocytes with higher enzyme replace them. Supportive care and removing the trigger usually suffice.
⚠️ This self-limiting age-dependent pattern is why hemolysis abates despite continued exposure in mild variants — not seen in severe Mediterranean disease.
10. AHA: dark urine, day 2-3 peak
Acute hemolysis begins 1–3 days after trigger: dark urine (hemoglobinuria), jaundice, back/abdominal pain, with anemia peaking around day 7. Intravascular hemolysis releases free hemoglobin.
⚠️ Timing matters — a normal day-0 enzyme assay during acute hemolysis can be falsely normal; recheck after recovery.
11. Acute renal failure risk
Massive intravascular hemolysis can precipitate acute kidney injury from free hemoglobin and pigment nephropathy. Severe cases need transfusion and aggressive hydration.
⚠️ Free-hemoglobin AKI is a complication of severe episodes — manage with hydration; transfuse only for severe symptomatic anemia.
12. Bite cells & blister cells
Splenic macrophages pit denatured hemoglobin (Heinz bodies), leaving 'bite cells' and 'blister/hemighost' cells on peripheral smear — hallmark morphology of oxidative hemolysis.
⚠️ Bite cells point to oxidative injury (G6PD); spherocytes point to HS/AIHA. Don't confuse the two morphologies.
13. Heinz bodies (supravital stain)
Heinz bodies are precipitated denatured hemoglobin seen only with supravital stains (crystal violet/new methylene blue), not on routine Wright stain. NADPH from G6PD normally keeps glutathione reduced to prevent this.
⚠️ Heinz bodies are invisible on standard smear — you must order a supravital stain to see them.
14. False-normal enzyme during crisis
During acute hemolysis the enzyme-deficient old cells are already lysed, and young reticulocytes have higher G6PD, so a quantitative assay can read falsely normal. Repeat testing 2–3 months after recovery.
⚠️ Classic trap: a 'normal' G6PD level during a crisis does NOT exclude deficiency — retest after the reticulocytosis resolves.
15. Quantitative assay is definitive
Quantitative spectrophotometric G6PD assay measuring NADPH generation is the confirmatory diagnostic test. Fluorescent spot screening is a rapid alternative. Confirm timing relative to the hemolytic episode.
⚠️ Point-of-care fluorescent spot tests screen but the quantitative assay confirms — and both can mislead during active hemolysis.
