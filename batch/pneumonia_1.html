📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. H. influenzae
H. influenzae is a 'typical' gram-negative coccobacillus that gram-stains and grows on chocolate agar (needs factors X/hemin and V/NAD). It is a leading CAP organism in COPD/smokers and the elderly, where loss of mucociliary clearance lets it colonize and infect. Empiric beta-lactams (amox-clavulanate, 3rd-gen cephalosporins) cover it; nontypeable strains may be beta-lactamase producers.
⚠️ Classic board trap: H. influenzae is grouped with the 'typicals' even though its name sounds like 'atypical' — it DOES gram-stain and culture on standard (enriched) media, unlike Mycoplasma/Chlamydia/Legionella. Don't reflexively drop beta-lactams; instead worry about beta-lactamase production (use amox-clavulanate, not plain amoxicillin).
2. S. pneumoniae
S. pneumoniae is the #1 bacterial cause of CAP — a lancet-shaped, alpha-hemolytic, optochin-sensitive, bile-soluble gram-positive diplococcus with a polysaccharide capsule (the key virulence factor and vaccine target). It classically causes lobar pneumonia with rust-colored sputum and is the most common cause of bacteremic/pneumococcal pneumonia. Resistance is via altered penicillin-binding proteins (not beta-lactamase), so it is overcome by high-dose amoxicillin (1 g TID).
⚠️ Every empiric CAP regimen MUST cover pneumococcus — picking an atypical-only regimen (e.g., azithromycin alone in a high-resistance area, or doxycycline alone for a sick patient) is a classic wrong answer. Remember the resistance is PBP-mediated, so adding a beta-lactamase inhibitor does nothing; raise the dose instead.
3. K. pneumoniae
Klebsiella is a heavily encapsulated (mucoid) gram-negative rod that causes severe lobar pneumonia in alcoholics, diabetics, and aspiration. The classic buzzwords are 'currant-jelly' sputum, bulging fissure sign on CXR, and early cavitation/necrosis with high mortality. Treat per susceptibilities (3rd-gen cephalosporin or carbapenem if ESBL); the capsule resists phagocytosis.
⚠️ Cavitation/abscess in an alcoholic is Klebsiella (or anaerobes), NOT classic uncomplicated pneumococcal lobar pneumonia, which rarely cavitates. Also keep ESBL/carbapenem-resistant Klebsiella on the differential in healthcare-associated or recently-hospitalized patients.
4. S. aureus
S. aureus causes necrotizing, often cavitary pneumonia, classically as a post-influenza superinfection and in IV drug users (septic emboli). Radiographic clues are pneumatoceles, multilobar infiltrates, and rapid cavitation. MSSA is treated with nafcillin/cefazolin; MRSA requires vancomycin or linezolid.
⚠️ Post-influenza clinical worsening with new cavities is a bacterial superinfection (S. aureus or pneumococcus), NOT a relapse of the original viral pneumonia — add empiric anti-staph (and anti-MRSA if PVL/necrotizing features). Beta-lactams like ceftriaxone do NOT cover MRSA.
5. P. aeruginosa
Pseudomonas is an oxidase-positive, non-lactose-fermenting gram-negative rod that infects patients with structural lung disease (bronchiectasis, cystic fibrosis, severe COPD), prior broad-spectrum antibiotics, frequent steroids, or recent hospitalization. Empiric coverage uses an antipseudomonal beta-lactam (piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, meropenem); severe disease may warrant a second antipseudomonal agent until susceptibilities return.
⚠️ Do NOT add routine anti-pseudomonal coverage for ordinary CAP — it is reserved for documented risk factors (structural lung disease, recent antibiotics/hospitalization, prior Pseudomonas isolation). Over-covering low-risk patients is a common wrong answer and drives resistance.
6. Epidemiologic clue grid
The exposure history fingerprints the pathogen: hotel/cruise/water → Legionella; birds (parrots) → Chlamydia psittaci; farm animals/parturient cattle → Coxiella burnetii (Q fever); cats (parturient) → also Coxiella; Southwest US deserts → Coccidioides; Ohio/Mississippi valleys + caves/bats → Histoplasma; rabbits → Francisella (tularemia). Always ask about travel, animals, and occupation when the stem highlights an exposure.
⚠️ When the vignette gives a strong, specific exposure, match the bug to the clue rather than defaulting to pneumococcus — that's the whole point of the question. Conversely, an exposure clue does not exclude routine pneumococcal coverage; cover both when treating empirically.
7. Mycoplasma pneumoniae
Mycoplasma is the classic 'walking pneumonia' of young, otherwise healthy adults (teens, military, dorms), causing mild diffuse/interstitial infiltrates out of proportion to a well-appearing patient. It has NO cell wall (so it cannot be gram-stained and beta-lactams are useless) and is hard to culture. Extrapulmonary clues: cold-agglutinin hemolytic anemia, bullous myringitis, and erythema multiforme/Stevens-Johnson.
⚠️ Beta-lactams fail Mycoplasma because it has no cell wall — treat with a macrolide, tetracycline (doxycycline), or respiratory fluoroquinolone. A patient who 'looks too well for the CXR' plus cold agglutinins is the buzzword setup.
8. Chlamydia pneumoniae
Chlamydophila (Chlamydia) pneumoniae is an obligate intracellular atypical causing mild, often biphasic CAP (pharyngitis/hoarseness preceding the cough) in young adults; it accounts for <1% of CAP. Like other atypicals it is cell-wall-deficient intracellularly and intrinsically resistant to beta-lactams. Treat with a macrolide, doxycycline, or respiratory fluoroquinolone.
⚠️ As an atypical it will NOT respond to beta-lactams. Distinguish from C. psittaci (bird/parrot exposure, more severe) — the board uses the avian exposure as the discriminator.
9. Legionella spp.
Legionella pneumophila is a fastidious gram-negative atypical (needs buffered charcoal yeast extract agar) acquired from aerosolized water — cooling towers, hot tubs, hotels/cruises, hospital plumbing. The classic syndrome is severe CAP with high fever, relative bradycardia, GI symptoms (diarrhea), hyponatremia, elevated LFTs/transaminases, and confusion. Diagnose rapidly with a urinary antigen (detects serogroup 1).
⚠️ Severe atypical-feeling pneumonia with hyponatremia + diarrhea + recent water/travel exposure is Legionella — cover with a respiratory fluoroquinolone or a macrolide (azithromycin), NOT a beta-lactam alone. A NEGATIVE urinary antigen does not exclude non-serogroup-1 Legionella, so culture/PCR may still be needed if suspicion is high.
10. Respiratory viruses
Respiratory viruses (influenza, RSV, parainfluenza, human metapneumovirus, coronaviruses including SARS-CoV-2) cause roughly 20-30% of CAP and damage the respiratory epithelium, predisposing to bacterial superinfection. Influenza specifically primes the airway for post-viral S. aureus (including MRSA) and pneumococcal pneumonia. PCR multiplex panels identify the virus; treat influenza with oseltamivir when indicated.
⚠️ A confirmed viral etiology does NOT exclude bacterial co-infection — the classic post-influenza biphasic course (initial improvement, then worsening with focal consolidation) demands empiric antibacterials, especially anti-staph/anti-MRSA. Don't withhold antibiotics just because a viral PCR is positive.
11. Atypicals = beta-lactam resistant
The 'atypicals' (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia/Chlamydophila, Legionella) are intrinsically resistant to ALL beta-lactams because they lack a conventional cell-wall target (no peptidoglycan synthesis for the drug to block) or are intracellular. They require agents that penetrate cells and act on protein/DNA synthesis: macrolides, tetracyclines, or respiratory fluoroquinolones. This is exactly why empiric CAP regimens pair a beta-lactam WITH a macrolide (or use a respiratory FQ alone) to cover atypicals.
⚠️ Never treat suspected atypical CAP with a beta-lactam alone — and never assume a 'failure to improve on ceftriaxone' is resistance when the real issue is an uncovered atypical (add a macrolide/doxycycline or switch to a respiratory FQ).
12. HCAP abandoned
The healthcare-associated pneumonia (HCAP) category was abandoned in the 2016/2019 IDSA/ATS guidelines because labeling patients by healthcare contact (nursing home, dialysis, recent hospitalization) led to massive antibiotic overtreatment without improving outcomes. Pneumonia is now classified as CAP, HAP (≥48 h after admission), or VAP (≥48 h after intubation). MDR risk is now assessed by individual risk factors (prior IV antibiotics within 90 days, prior MDR isolation, local resistance) rather than the HCAP label.
⚠️ Do NOT reflexively give broad MDR/anti-MRSA/anti-pseudomonal coverage just because a patient came from a nursing home or had recent healthcare contact — risk-stratify each patient. Choosing 'treat as HCAP with vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam' for a stable nursing-home patient with no specific MDR risk is now a wrong answer.
13. CA-MRSA features
Community-acquired MRSA carries the SCCmec type IV cassette (mecA gene → altered PBP2a) and frequently the Panton-Valentine leukocidin (PVL) toxin, which destroys neutrophils and causes necrotizing, cavitary pneumonia with gross hemoptysis, profound leukopenia, and high mortality, often after influenza in young healthy hosts. PVL is a pore-forming exotoxin driving the tissue destruction. Severe disease is treated with vancomycin or linezolid (linezolid also suppresses toxin production).
⚠️ Standard CAP beta-lactams (including ceftriaxone) do NOT cover MRSA — escalate to vancomycin or linezolid. A young, previously healthy post-influenza patient with rapidly cavitating, hemoptysis-heavy pneumonia and leukopenia is CA-MRSA, not ordinary S. aureus or pneumococcus.
14. CA-MRSA susceptibilities
CA-MRSA is generally MORE susceptible than hospital MRSA — often covered by TMP-SMX, clindamycin, or tetracyclines for skin/soft-tissue disease. However, for serious necrotizing pneumonia or bacteremia the answer is vancomycin or linezolid; linezolid and clindamycin add the benefit of suppressing toxin (PVL) production. Always check clindamycin D-test (inducible erm resistance) before relying on it.
⚠️ Beta-lactams — including ceftriaxone and other cephalosporins (except the anti-MRSA ceftaroline) — do NOT cover MRSA, so 'add ceftriaxone' is wrong for MRSA pneumonia. Also, oral TMP-SMX/doxycycline are fine for outpatient SSTI but are NOT first-line for severe MRSA pneumonia — use IV vancomycin/linezolid.
