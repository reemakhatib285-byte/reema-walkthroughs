📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Suppressed renin
Aldosterone is normally driven by angiotensin II and by hyperkalemia acting on the zona glomerulosa. In PRIMARY aldosteronism the adrenal secretes aldosterone autonomously, so the resulting Na/volume expansion feeds back to the juxtaglomerular cells and SUPPRESSES renin. The hallmark biochemistry is therefore HIGH aldosterone with LOW (suppressed) renin → an elevated aldosterone:renin ratio (ARR). This suppressed renin is exactly what separates primary from secondary aldosteronism.
⚠️ Renin is HIGH — that describes SECONDARY aldosteronism (renal artery stenosis, renin-secreting tumor, diuretics, heart failure/cirrhosis), where high renin drives high aldo. Primary aldo = LOW renin.
2. Hypertension pipe
Na and volume retention drive the hypertension, which is the dominant clinical feature and is often RESISTANT (uncontrolled on ≥3 drugs including a diuretic). Primary aldosteronism is the most common SECONDARY/curable cause of HTN (5-10% of hypertensives, up to ~20% of resistant HTN). Aldosterone also causes direct vascular/cardiac damage beyond the BP effect.
⚠️ Dismissing it as essential HTN — young-onset HTN, resistant HTN, or HTN with hypokalemia/adrenal incidentaloma all mandate ARR screening.
3. ENaC sodium door
Aldosterone binds the cytoplasmic mineralocorticoid receptor in principal cells of the cortical collecting duct; the activated receptor increases transcription of the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC) on the luminal membrane and the Na-K-ATPase on the basolateral side. Na is reabsorbed through ENaC. ENaC is the channel blocked by amiloride/triamterene and constitutively activated in Liddle syndrome.
4. Sodium reabsorbed
Na reabsorption through ENaC expands extracellular volume and raises blood pressure. Reabsorbing Na also creates a lumen-negative electrical gradient that DRIVES the secretion of K+ (via ROMK) and H+ — this is why hypokalemia and metabolic alkalosis accompany the HTN.
5. Mineralocorticoid receptor
The mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) is the molecular target of aldosterone and the therapeutic target of MR antagonists: spironolactone (non-selective, causes gynecomastia/menstrual irregularity) and eplerenone (selective, fewer anti-androgen effects). The MR cannot itself distinguish cortisol from aldosterone — protection comes from 11β-HSD2 converting cortisol to inactive cortisone (its failure causes AME).
6. Potassium wasted (ROMK)
K+ is secreted into the tubular lumen through ROMK channels, driven by the lumen-negative voltage from Na reabsorption, producing renal potassium wasting and hypokalemia. Classic findings include K <3.5, inappropriately high urinary K (>30 mEq/day) despite hypokalemia.
⚠️ Requiring hypokalemia to diagnose — MOST patients with primary aldosteronism are NORMOKALEMIC. Normal K does NOT rule it out; screen with ARR regardless.
7. Acid out / alkalosis
Aldosterone stimulates H+ secretion by the type A intercalated cell H+-ATPase, and the hypokalemia drives intracellular H+ shift plus increased renal ammoniagenesis — together producing a hypokalemic METABOLIC ALKALOSIS (high bicarbonate). Classic primary aldo labs: HTN + hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis + low renin.
8. Why not hypernatremia
Despite avid Na reabsorption, serum sodium stays in the normal-to-high-normal range rather than frankly elevated. This is because of aldosterone escape (below) and ADH-driven water retention that dilutes the Na. A mildly high-normal Na in a hypertensive patient is actually a subtle clue toward primary aldo.
⚠️ Expecting overt hypernatremia — it is the absence of dramatic hypernatremia/edema (due to escape) that is characteristic.
9. Aldosterone escape
Aldosterone escape: after initial Na/volume gain, pressure natriuresis (raised BP increases renal Na excretion) plus atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP, released by atrial stretch) restore Na balance. This prevents progressive volume overload and massive edema — so primary aldosteronism causes HTN WITHOUT edema. Note escape applies to the Na/volume axis only; K wasting CONTINUES.
⚠️ Expecting peripheral edema — its absence (because of escape) is the rule, not the exception.
10. Hypomagnesemia
Renal magnesium wasting can accompany the potassium loss. Hypomagnesemia perpetuates refractory hypokalemia (you cannot fully correct K until Mg is repleted) and contributes to the arrhythmia risk.
11. Symptoms box
Symptoms stem from hypokalemia: muscle weakness/cramps, fatigue, polyuria and polydipsia (hypokalemia causes nephrogenic diabetes insipidus), paresthesias, and arrhythmias (with ECG changes such as U waves, flattened T waves). Many patients are asymptomatic and present only with hard-to-control HTN.
12. End-organ damage
Aldosterone exerts BP-INDEPENDENT toxicity: myocardial and perivascular fibrosis (LVH, atrial fibrillation, heart failure), vascular remodeling/stiffness, glomerular hyperfiltration and proteinuria, and increased bone turnover. Patients with primary aldo have higher rates of MI, stroke, AF and renal injury than BP-matched essential hypertensives — a key reason to identify and treat it specifically with MR blockade or surgery.
⚠️ Assuming controlling the BP alone is enough — the aldosterone excess itself must be blocked (MRA) or removed (adrenalectomy) to prevent fibrotic end-organ damage.
13. Clinical pearl
One-line summary: aldosterone retains Na (via ENaC), wastes K (via ROMK) and dumps acid (H+-ATPase) → HTN + hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis; renin is suppressed (high ARR); aldosterone escape keeps Na near-normal and prevents edema; treat the MR (spironolactone/eplerenone) or remove the source (adrenalectomy).
