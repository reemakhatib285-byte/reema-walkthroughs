⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. CXR: cardiomegaly — WRONG: a normal-sized heart on CXR rules out heart failure. Discriminator: acute HF (e.g., flash pulmonary edema from HTN or acute MI/papillary rupture) and HFpEF can present with a normal cardiac silhouette — the lungs (congestion) tell the acute story, not heart size.
  2. Kerley B lines / alveolar edema — WRONG: Kerley B lines and bilateral interstitial markings are specific for HF. Discriminator: they also occur in lymphangitic carcinomatosis, interstitial lung disease and viral pneumonitis — combine the CXR with BNP, JVP and echo before committing to HF.
  3. ECG: low voltage, LBBB, AF — WRONG: a normal ECG is irrelevant in suspected HF. Discriminator: a completely normal ECG has a high negative predictive value and makes systolic HF unlikely, redirecting the workup — use it as a screening clue, not a throwaway.
  4. BNP / NT-proBNP machine — WRONG: a high BNP confirms heart failure as the cause of dyspnea. Discriminator: BNP is a sensitive RULE-OUT test (high NPV), not a specific rule-in — it rises with AF, PE, sepsis, renal failure and age, so an elevated value needs an echo to confirm cardiac dysfunction.
  5. Falsely LOW: obesity — WRONG: obesity raises BNP. Discriminator: it LOWERS it — the classic board trap is the obese, dyspneic patient with a deceptively normal BNP who actually has HFpEF; don't be falsely reassured.
  6. Falsely HIGH: AF, PE, PAH — WRONG: an elevated BNP in a dyspneic patient means the cause is left heart failure, so anticoagulation for PE can wait. Discriminator: AF, PE and PAH all raise BNP via RV/atrial stretch — a mildly raised BNP must not anchor you onto HF and away from a missed PE.
  7. Falsely HIGH: CKD, older/female — WRONG: renal failure lowers NT-proBNP. Discriminator: it RAISES it because NT-proBNP is cleared by the kidney — a high value in a CKD patient may reflect reduced clearance, not new HF, so interpret against an age-/renal-adjusted threshold.
  8. Echo = first-line imaging — WRONG: a normal LVEF on echo excludes heart failure. Discriminator: HFpEF has EF ≥50% with abnormal diastolic indices (raised E/e′, LA enlargement) — you must look at diastology and filling pressures, not just the ejection fraction number.
  9. Lab workup (TSH, iron, etc.) — WRONG: checking TSH and iron studies in HF is low-yield box-ticking. Discriminator: both thyroid disease and iron deficiency are reversible/treatable contributors — missing thyrotoxic or hypothyroid cardiomyopathy, or iron deficiency amenable to IV iron, is a real management miss.
  10. DDx — HF (JVP, orthopnea, diuretics help) — WRONG: peripheral edema by itself diagnoses heart failure. Discriminator: edema is nonspecific (venous insufficiency, nephrotic syndrome, cirrhosis, calcium-channel blockers) — it is the COMBINATION with elevated JVP, S3 and orthopnea that points to HF.
  11. DDx — COPD (normal JVP) — WRONG: wheezing means asthma/COPD, so it cannot be heart failure. Discriminator: 'cardiac asthma' (HF can wheeze from peribronchial edema) — check JVP and BNP; normal JVP + normal BNP favors primary lung disease, raised both favor HF.
  12. DDx — renal edema (poor diuretic response) — WRONG: bilateral leg edema with poor diuretic response must be decompensated heart failure. Discriminator: a FLAT JVP points away from HF toward renal/hepatic/hypoalbuminemic edema — check urine protein and albumin rather than escalating cardiac therapy.
  13. Cardiorenal syndrome — WRONG: a creatinine bump during HF diuresis means you are over-diuresing and should stop the loop diuretic. Discriminator: in congestive cardiorenal syndrome continued decongestion frequently IMPROVES renal function — venous congestion, not volume depletion, is the driver; assess volume status before pulling back.
  14. GDMT drugs at base — WRONG: titrate one HFrEF pillar to its target dose before adding the next class. Discriminator: modern strategy (STRONG-HF) favors rapid simultaneous initiation of all four classes at low doses then up-titration — early quadruple therapy reduces mortality and readmissions versus slow sequential dosing.
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. CXR: cardiomegaly
On chest X-ray, HF shows cardiomegaly defined as a cardiothoracic ratio >0.5 on a PA film, plus signs of pulmonary venous congestion (cephalization of vessels, perihilar 'bat-wing' edema). Cardiomegaly reflects chronic chamber dilation/remodeling and supports a cardiac cause of dyspnea.
⚠️ WRONG: a normal-sized heart on CXR rules out heart failure. Discriminator: acute HF (e.g., flash pulmonary edema from HTN or acute MI/papillary rupture) and HFpEF can present with a normal cardiac silhouette — the lungs (congestion) tell the acute story, not heart size.
2. Kerley B lines / alveolar edema
Kerley B lines are short horizontal peripheral lines from engorged interlobular septa; with alveolar/interstitial edema and pleural effusions (often right-sided) they indicate elevated pulmonary venous/wedge pressure. The radiographic congestion sequence: redistribution → interstitial (Kerley B) → alveolar edema as wedge pressure climbs above ~18–25 mmHg.
⚠️ WRONG: Kerley B lines and bilateral interstitial markings are specific for HF. Discriminator: they also occur in lymphangitic carcinomatosis, interstitial lung disease and viral pneumonitis — combine the CXR with BNP, JVP and echo before committing to HF.
3. ECG: low voltage, LBBB, AF
ECG clues in HF: low QRS voltage with a 'thick' heart on echo suggests infiltrative cardiomyopathy (cardiac amyloidosis — voltage/mass mismatch); LBBB with EF ≤35% and QRS ≥150 ms flags a CRT candidate; AF both causes (tachycardia-mediated CM) and complicates HF. Q waves point to prior infarction/ischemic etiology.
⚠️ WRONG: a normal ECG is irrelevant in suspected HF. Discriminator: a completely normal ECG has a high negative predictive value and makes systolic HF unlikely, redirecting the workup — use it as a screening clue, not a throwaway.
4. BNP / NT-proBNP machine
Natriuretic peptides are released from stretched ventricular myocytes. Use rule-out cutoffs in acute dyspnea: BNP <100 pg/mL or NT-proBNP <300 pg/mL make HF unlikely (high NPV). Rule-IN (age-adjusted NT-proBNP): >450 (age <50), >900 (50–75), >1800 (>75). NT-proBNP is the inactive cleavage fragment with a longer half-life and renal clearance; sacubitril (ARNI) raises BNP but lowers NT-proBNP, so monitor NT-proBNP on ARNI.
⚠️ WRONG: a high BNP confirms heart failure as the cause of dyspnea. Discriminator: BNP is a sensitive RULE-OUT test (high NPV), not a specific rule-in — it rises with AF, PE, sepsis, renal failure and age, so an elevated value needs an echo to confirm cardiac dysfunction.
5. Falsely LOW: obesity
Obesity FALSELY LOWERS BNP/NT-proBNP (likely via increased clearance/receptor expression in adipose tissue and altered handling). In an obese patient, a 'normal' or only mildly elevated BNP does NOT exclude HF — lower your threshold and lean on echo. This is the single most tested BNP pitfall.
⚠️ WRONG: obesity raises BNP. Discriminator: it LOWERS it — the classic board trap is the obese, dyspneic patient with a deceptively normal BNP who actually has HFpEF; don't be falsely reassured.
6. Falsely HIGH: AF, PE, PAH
BNP/NT-proBNP rise from any cause of myocardial stretch or RV strain, not just LV failure: atrial fibrillation, pulmonary embolism, pulmonary arterial hypertension, sepsis, high-output states. In PE, an elevated BNP/troponin marks RV strain and worse prognosis — but it is not diagnostic of LV HF.
⚠️ WRONG: an elevated BNP in a dyspneic patient means the cause is left heart failure, so anticoagulation for PE can wait. Discriminator: AF, PE and PAH all raise BNP via RV/atrial stretch — a mildly raised BNP must not anchor you onto HF and away from a missed PE.
7. Falsely HIGH: CKD, older/female
BNP and especially NT-proBNP rise with renal impairment (reduced clearance — NT-proBNP is renally cleared and most affected), with advancing age, and with female sex. This is exactly why NT-proBNP cutoffs are AGE-ADJUSTED (>450 / >900 / >1800). In CKD, use higher rule-in thresholds (e.g., NT-proBNP >1200) before attributing dyspnea to HF.
⚠️ WRONG: renal failure lowers NT-proBNP. Discriminator: it RAISES it because NT-proBNP is cleared by the kidney — a high value in a CKD patient may reflect reduced clearance, not new HF, so interpret against an age-/renal-adjusted threshold.
8. Echo = first-line imaging
Transthoracic echocardiography is the FIRST-LINE imaging test in HF: it gives the LVEF (defining HFrEF ≤40, HFmrEF 41–49, HFpEF ≥50), wall motion, chamber size, diastolic function (E/e′), valve disease, pericardium and estimated PA pressures. Cardiac MRI is reserved for tissue characterization (amyloid, sarcoid, myocarditis, scar/viability).
⚠️ WRONG: a normal LVEF on echo excludes heart failure. Discriminator: HFpEF has EF ≥50% with abnormal diastolic indices (raised E/e′, LA enlargement) — you must look at diastology and filling pressures, not just the ejection fraction number.
9. Lab workup (TSH, iron, etc.)
Baseline HF labs hunt for reversible drivers and comorbidities: CBC (anemia), electrolytes/renal function (baseline before RAAS/MRA and to track), LFTs/bilirubin (congestive hepatopathy), TSH (thyroid CM is reversible), iron studies (ferritin + transferrin sat — deficiency is common and treatable with IV iron), HbA1c/glucose, and natriuretic peptides.
⚠️ WRONG: checking TSH and iron studies in HF is low-yield box-ticking. Discriminator: both thyroid disease and iron deficiency are reversible/treatable contributors — missing thyrotoxic or hypothyroid cardiomyopathy, or iron deficiency amenable to IV iron, is a real management miss.
10. DDx — HF (JVP, orthopnea, diuretics help)
HF dyspnea pattern: elevated JVP, an S3 gallop (volume overload, the most specific bedside sign of HFrEF), orthopnea and paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, bilateral basal crackles and edema, and clear symptomatic relief with diuresis. The most specific findings are S3 and raised JVP.
⚠️ WRONG: peripheral edema by itself diagnoses heart failure. Discriminator: edema is nonspecific (venous insufficiency, nephrotic syndrome, cirrhosis, calcium-channel blockers) — it is the COMBINATION with elevated JVP, S3 and orthopnea that points to HF.
11. DDx — COPD (normal JVP)
COPD/asthma dyspnea: expiratory wheeze, prolonged expiration, cough with sputum, hyperinflation on CXR, NORMAL JVP, and a BNP that is typically normal or only mildly raised. Relief comes from bronchodilators, not diuresis. (Cor pulmonale from advanced COPD can raise JVP and BNP via RV strain — a later overlap.)
⚠️ WRONG: wheezing means asthma/COPD, so it cannot be heart failure. Discriminator: 'cardiac asthma' (HF can wheeze from peribronchial edema) — check JVP and BNP; normal JVP + normal BNP favors primary lung disease, raised both favor HF.
12. DDx — renal edema (poor diuretic response)
Renal/nephrotic edema: heavy proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, FLAT JVP, normal cardiac output, and a relatively poor/blunted response to loop diuretics (low oncotic pressure and avid sodium retention). Distinguished from HF by the flat JVP and the absence of S3/orthopnea.
⚠️ WRONG: bilateral leg edema with poor diuretic response must be decompensated heart failure. Discriminator: a FLAT JVP points away from HF toward renal/hepatic/hypoalbuminemic edema — check urine protein and albumin rather than escalating cardiac therapy.
13. Cardiorenal syndrome
Cardiorenal syndrome is renal dysfunction in HF driven largely by elevated central VENOUS pressure/congestion (renal venous congestion lowering net glomerular perfusion), not merely by low forward cardiac output. The counterintuitive treatment is often decongestion (continue/intensify diuresis) rather than holding diuretics for a rising creatinine.
⚠️ WRONG: a creatinine bump during HF diuresis means you are over-diuresing and should stop the loop diuretic. Discriminator: in congestive cardiorenal syndrome continued decongestion frequently IMPROVES renal function — venous congestion, not volume depletion, is the driver; assess volume status before pulling back.
14. GDMT drugs at base
Once HFrEF is diagnosed (EF ≤40%), start the FOUR pillars early and titrate: ARNI sacubitril-valsartan (preferred over ACEi/ARB), an evidence-based beta-blocker (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, or bisoprolol), an MRA (spironolactone/eplerenone), and an SGLT2i (dapagliflozin/empagliflozin). Current practice initiates all four rapidly (in parallel) rather than sequentially over months.
⚠️ WRONG: titrate one HFrEF pillar to its target dose before adding the next class. Discriminator: modern strategy (STRONG-HF) favors rapid simultaneous initiation of all four classes at low doses then up-titration — early quadruple therapy reduces mortality and readmissions versus slow sequential dosing.
