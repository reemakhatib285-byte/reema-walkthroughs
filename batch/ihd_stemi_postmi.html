⚠️ Board traps 14

Discriminator: troponin stays elevated for 7–10 days; that is why it cannot reliably detect early REINFARCTION — for a suspected re-MI within days, use CK-MB, which has already returned toward baseline.
  2. Troponin 7–10 days — Board says: 'A patient with chest pain on day 4 post-MI and a still-elevated troponin has definitely reinfarcted.' WRONG. Discriminator: troponin is expected to remain elevated 7–10 days; to confirm reinfarction you need a NEW rise (CK-MB, or ≥20% re-rise in troponin), not just a persistently high value.
  3. CK-MB reinfarction detector — Board says: 'Use troponin rather than CK-MB to detect reinfarction a few days after an MI.' WRONG. Discriminator: troponin is still elevated and uninformative in that window; CK-MB has normalized, so its re-rise specifically flags REINFARCTION — CK-MB is the right tool here.

Discriminator: ACEi/ARB are guideline-recommended post-MI (class I when EF ≤40%/HF/DM/HTN) and reduce mortality — true contraindications are limited (hyperkalemia, bilateral RAS, pregnancy, angioedema, hypotension), not the MI itself.

Discriminator: beta-blockers are continued LONG-TERM (especially with reduced EF) for ongoing mortality benefit — they are not a short-course drug. (The acute caution is avoiding them in decompensated HF/shock, not stopping them at one week.)
  10. Electrolyte station K & Mg — Board says: 'Electrolytes no longer matter once the artery is revascularized.' WRONG. 📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Biomarker kitchen — troponin
Cardiac troponin (I or T) is the most sensitive and specific marker of myocardial necrosis and the preferred test for diagnosing MI. It rises ~3–12h after injury, peaks around 24h, and stays elevated for 7–10 days (troponin T) / 5–7 days (troponin I). Diagnosis requires a rise and/or fall with at least one value above the 99th percentile, plus ischemic context. Mnemonic: 'troponin: up by 3–12h, peaks at a day, lingers a week-plus.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Troponin normalizes within 24h of an MI.' WRONG. Discriminator: troponin stays elevated for 7–10 days; that is why it cannot reliably detect early REINFARCTION — for a suspected re-MI within days, use CK-MB, which has already returned toward baseline.
2. Troponin 7–10 days
Because troponin remains elevated for 7–10 days, it is excellent for diagnosing an MI even days after presentation, but POOR for detecting reinfarction during that window (a persistently high level can't distinguish old from new injury). In the reinfarction window, look for a new RISE in CK-MB or a re-rise (≥20%) in troponin from a prior plateau. Mnemonic: 'troponin lingers a week+ → blind to re-MI in that window.'
⚠️ Board says: 'A patient with chest pain on day 4 post-MI and a still-elevated troponin has definitely reinfarcted.' WRONG. Discriminator: troponin is expected to remain elevated 7–10 days; to confirm reinfarction you need a NEW rise (CK-MB, or ≥20% re-rise in troponin), not just a persistently high value.
3. CK-MB reinfarction detector
CK-MB rises ~3–6h, peaks at ~24h, and returns to baseline by 48–72h. This short, predictable course makes CK-MB the marker of choice to detect EARLY REINFARCTION: after the first CK-MB normalizes, a new rise indicates fresh myocardial necrosis. Its lower specificity (also in skeletal muscle) is the trade-off. Mnemonic: 'CK-MB clears in 2–3 days → catches the second hit.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Use troponin rather than CK-MB to detect reinfarction a few days after an MI.' WRONG. Discriminator: troponin is still elevated and uninformative in that window; CK-MB has normalized, so its re-rise specifically flags REINFARCTION — CK-MB is the right tool here.
4. Onset 6–12H
Biomarkers and ECG findings EVOLVE over the first 6–12h, so SERIAL testing is mandatory — a single early measurement can miss an evolving MI. Obtain troponin at presentation and repeat at 3–6h (and again if needed); follow serial ECGs. The classic 0/1h or 0/3h high-sensitivity troponin protocols exist precisely because of this time-dependence. Mnemonic: 'one normal troponin is not enough — recheck over 6–12h.'
⚠️ Board says: 'A single normal troponin at presentation rules out MI.' WRONG. Discriminator: troponin may not rise until 3–12h after symptom onset; a single early normal value does NOT exclude MI — serial troponins (and ECGs) are required.
5. Secondary prevention shelf
Every post-MI patient leaves on a lifelong secondary-prevention bundle: antiplatelet therapy (aspirin indefinitely + P2Y12 inhibitor), high-intensity statin, beta-blocker, and ACE inhibitor/ARB (especially with reduced EF/HF/DM/HTN), plus an aldosterone antagonist if EF ≤40% with HF or DM. Add risk-factor control (BP, glucose, smoking cessation) and cardiac rehab. Mnemonic: 'ABCDE — Aspirin/Antiplatelet, Beta-blocker, Cholesterol (statin)/Cardiac rehab, Diet/Diabetes, Exercise + ACEi.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Secondary-prevention medications can be stopped once the patient is discharged and feels well.' WRONG. Discriminator: these agents (aspirin, statin, beta-blocker, ACEi) are continued LONG-TERM/lifelong to reduce reinfarction and death — feeling well is not a reason to stop them.
6. Aspirin indefinitely
Aspirin (81 mg daily) is continued INDEFINITELY after MI. Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) adds a P2Y12 inhibitor — clopidogrel 75 mg, ticagrelor 90 mg BID, or prasugrel 10 mg — for at least 12 months after ACS (shorter if high bleeding risk, longer if high ischemic/low bleeding risk). Antiplatelet therapy gives roughly a 25% relative risk reduction in recurrent vascular events. Mnemonic: 'aspirin forever, the partner (P2Y12) for about a year.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Stop aspirin at 12 months after the MI.' WRONG. Discriminator: it is the P2Y12 INHIBITOR that is typically stopped around 12 months; ASPIRIN is continued INDEFINITELY/lifelong. Stopping aspirin removes the lifelong antiplatelet backbone.
7. Statin high-intensity
All post-MI patients get a HIGH-INTENSITY statin (atorvastatin 40–80 mg or rosuvastatin 20–40 mg) regardless of baseline LDL, for both LDL lowering and pleiotropic plaque-stabilizing effects. Goal LDL <70 mg/dL (many guidelines now target <55 mg/dL for very-high-risk patients); add ezetimibe ± a PCSK9 inhibitor if goals are not met. Mnemonic: 'every MI patient gets a high-intensity statin — no LDL excuse.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Start a statin only if the LDL is elevated.' WRONG. Discriminator: post-MI, high-intensity statin is indicated for ALL patients regardless of baseline LDL — the benefit is independent of starting cholesterol and includes plaque stabilization.
8. ACEi if EF ≤40% / HF / DM
Start an ACE inhibitor (or ARB if intolerant) within 24h in stable patients, with the greatest benefit when there is LVEF ≤40%, heart failure, anterior MI, hypertension, diabetes, or CKD — it limits adverse remodeling and reduces mortality. Titrate to target while monitoring K+ and creatinine. Add an aldosterone antagonist (spironolactone/eplerenone) if EF ≤40% with HF or DM. Mnemonic: 'ACEi for the weak/diabetic/failing heart — blocks remodeling.'
⚠️ Board says: 'ACE inhibitors are contraindicated after MI.' WRONG. Discriminator: ACEi/ARB are guideline-recommended post-MI (class I when EF ≤40%/HF/DM/HTN) and reduce mortality — true contraindications are limited (hyperkalemia, bilateral RAS, pregnancy, angioedema, hypotension), not the MI itself.
9. Beta-blocker shelf
Oral beta-blockers (metoprolol, carvedilol, bisoprolol) are started within 24h in stable patients and continued LONG-TERM; they reduce ischemia, reinfarction, arrhythmia, and mortality, with the largest benefit when EF is reduced. AVOID IV beta-blockers / early initiation in patients with signs of heart failure, hypotension, cardiogenic shock, or high heart-block risk. Mnemonic: 'beta-blocker long-term — but not while the patient is in shock or florid HF.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Beta-blockers are only needed for the first week after an MI.' WRONG. Discriminator: beta-blockers are continued LONG-TERM (especially with reduced EF) for ongoing mortality benefit — they are not a short-course drug. (The acute caution is avoiding them in decompensated HF/shock, not stopping them at one week.)
10. Electrolyte station K & Mg
Maintain potassium >4.0 mEq/L and magnesium >2.0 mg/dL after MI to lower the risk of ventricular arrhythmias (VT/VF) in the irritable peri-infarct period. Hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia are correctable arrhythmia triggers, so aggressive repletion is standard. Mnemonic: 'keep K above 4, Mg above 2 — quiet the irritable myocardium.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Electrolytes no longer matter once the artery is revascularized.' WRONG. Discriminator: even after successful PCI, hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia provoke peri-infarct ventricular arrhythmias — maintaining K+ >4.0 and Mg2+ >2.0 remains important for arrhythmia prevention.
11. Discharge gate ~24h
Modern post-STEMI care favors EARLY mobilization (out of bed within ~24h) and short stays: an uncomplicated, successfully reperfused STEMI patient is often dischargeable in a few days (sometimes 48–72h for low-risk PCI patients). Early ambulation prevents deconditioning and venous thromboembolism while the secondary-prevention bundle is started. Mnemonic: 'reperfused and uncomplicated → up early, home in days.'
⚠️ Board says: 'Patients need strict bed rest for weeks after a STEMI.' WRONG. Discriminator: prolonged bed rest is outdated and harmful (VTE, deconditioning); current practice is EARLY mobilization and short hospitalization for uncomplicated, reperfused MI.
12. Sexual activity ~1 week
Sexual activity can usually be resumed ~1 week after an uncomplicated MI in patients who are asymptomatic and can achieve modest exertion (~3–5 METs, e.g., climbing two flights of stairs without symptoms). Counsel that PDE5 inhibitors are CONTRAINDICATED with nitrates (risk of severe hypotension). Mnemonic: 'two flights of stairs symptom-free → back to the bedroom (~1 week).'
⚠️ Board says: 'Sexual activity is prohibited for 6 months after an MI.' WRONG. Discriminator: after an uncomplicated MI, sexual activity may resume in about 1 week once the patient tolerates ~3–5 METs — a blanket 6-month ban is incorrect (the real caveat is no nitrates with PDE5 inhibitors).
13. Return to work 2–4 weeks
Most patients with an uncomplicated MI can return to work in about 2–4 weeks, individualized by job physical demands, LV function, and results of risk stratification (e.g., exercise testing). Physically strenuous jobs may require longer or a graded return; sedentary work can resume sooner. Cardiac rehab supports a safe return. Mnemonic: 'uncomplicated MI → back to work in 2–4 weeks.'
⚠️ Board says: 'All MI patients must stay off work for 6 months.' WRONG. Discriminator: return to work after an uncomplicated MI is typically 2–4 weeks, individualized to job demands and functional capacity — a universal 6-month restriction is incorrect.
14. 3–5 METs / 2 flights of stairs
Functional capacity is gauged in METs: climbing two flights of stairs (~3–5 METs) without symptoms is the practical benchmark that the heart can tolerate ordinary activities, including sexual activity and many jobs. Formal exercise/stress testing before discharge or early after can quantify capacity and risk-stratify. Cardiac rehabilitation is a class I recommendation to improve fitness, mortality, and quality of life. Mnemonic: 'two flights of stairs ≈ 3–5 METs ≈ green light for daily activity.'
⚠️ Board says: 'No functional assessment is needed before clearing a post-MI patient for activity.' WRONG. Discriminator: activity clearance is based on demonstrated functional capacity (e.g., ~3–5 METs / two flights of stairs, often via exercise testing) and cardiac rehab — not given blindly without assessment.
