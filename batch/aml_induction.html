📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Induction furnace
Induction is the first, intensive phase of AML therapy whose goal is complete remission (CR) — defined as <5% marrow blasts with hematologic recovery — not cure. CR is necessary but not sufficient: nearly all patients relapse without post-remission (consolidation) therapy, so induction is always followed by consolidation chemo and/or allo-HCT depending on ELN risk. Fit patients <60-75 yr with good performance status are candidates for intensive induction; APL (t(15;17)) is the exception and is treated with ATRA + arsenic trioxide, not 7+3.
⚠️ CR (<5% blasts) is the goal of induction, but achieving CR alone does NOT cure AML — consolidation is mandatory. Do not confuse induction with the curative endpoint.
2. Cytarabine 7 days
The 7+3 regimen pairs cytarabine (Ara-C) as a continuous IV infusion for 7 days with an anthracycline given on the first 3 days. Cytarabine is the antimetabolite backbone and is dosed at standard 100-200 mg/m2/day continuous infusion in induction (high-dose cytarabine is reserved mainly for consolidation). The '7' in 7+3 always refers to the cytarabine days.
⚠️ In 7+3 the 7 days is cytarabine and the 3 days is the anthracycline — reversing them is a classic distractor. High-dose cytarabine belongs to consolidation, not standard induction.
3. Cytarabine = antimetabolite
Cytarabine is a pyrimidine (cytidine) analog antimetabolite: after intracellular phosphorylation to Ara-CTP it incorporates into DNA and inhibits DNA polymerase, halting S-phase synthesis. Its signature high-dose toxicities are cerebellar dysfunction (ataxia, nystagmus — check cerebellar exam before each dose and hold if abnormal), conjunctivitis (give prophylactic steroid eye drops), and chemical pancreatitis. It is the single most important drug in AML therapy.
⚠️ High-dose cytarabine neurotoxicity is cerebellar (ataxia/dysarthria), not peripheral neuropathy — and is an indication to HOLD the drug. Prophylactic steroid eye drops are required to prevent the cytarabine keratoconjunctivitis.
4. Anthracycline 3 days
The '3' of 7+3 is an anthracycline given on days 1-3, most commonly daunorubicin or idarubicin. Anthracyclines provide the cytotoxic punch through topoisomerase II inhibition and free-radical DNA damage. Their dose-limiting long-term toxicity is cumulative, dose-dependent cardiomyopathy/heart failure, so a baseline echocardiogram (LVEF) is obtained before induction.
⚠️ Anthracycline cardiotoxicity is cumulative and dose-dependent (irreversible dilated cardiomyopathy); check baseline LVEF. The acute red urine after daunorubicin/idarubicin is harmless and must not be mistaken for hematuria.
5. Dauno 60 / Ida 12
Standard induction doses are daunorubicin 60-90 mg/m2/day or idarubicin 12 mg/m2/day on days 1-3. Daunorubicin 90 mg/m2 (intensified) improves outcomes in younger fit patients versus 45 mg/m2. CPX-351 (liposomal daunorubicin + cytarabine) is the preferred induction for therapy-related AML and AML with myelodysplasia-related changes in older adults.
⚠️ For secondary/therapy-related AML and AML-MRC in older adults, CPX-351 (liposomal dauno/cytarabine) is preferred over conventional 7+3 — a frequently tested substitution.
6. Topo II / DNA crushed
Anthracyclines act primarily by inhibiting topoisomerase II, trapping the enzyme-DNA cleavage complex and generating double-strand DNA breaks; they also intercalate DNA and generate reactive oxygen species. This same topo-II mechanism is why prior etoposide/anthracycline exposure causes short-latency therapy-related AML with KMT2A (11q23) rearrangements.
⚠️ Topoisomerase II inhibition is the anthracycline mechanism — and prior topo-II exposure later causes therapy-related AML with KMT2A/11q23 rearrangement and short (1-3 yr) latency, distinct from alkylator-induced t-AML.
7. FLT3 TKI add-on
If AML is FLT3-mutated (ITD or TKD), add the FLT3 inhibitor midostaurin to 7+3 induction and consolidation — this improved overall survival in the RATIFY trial. For relapsed/refractory FLT3-ITD AML, gilteritinib is the agent of choice. FLT3-ITD carries adverse prognostic weight, so these patients are generally referred for allo-HCT in CR1.
⚠️ Midostaurin is added to 7+3 up front for FLT3-mutated AML (induction/consolidation); gilteritinib is for relapsed/refractory FLT3 disease. FLT3-ITD-positive patients should proceed to allo-HCT in CR1.
8. HMA + venetoclax (unfit)
Older (typically ≥75 yr) or unfit patients who cannot tolerate intensive 7+3 receive a hypomethylating agent (azacitidine or decitabine) plus the BCL-2 inhibitor venetoclax — the VIALE-A regimen, which roughly tripled survival versus azacitidine alone. Venetoclax requires careful ramp-up with tumor lysis prophylaxis. IDH inhibitors (ivosidenib for IDH1, enasidenib for IDH2) are alternatives in mutated, unfit patients.
⚠️ Frail/elderly or comorbid patients → azacitidine/decitabine + venetoclax, NOT intensive 7+3. Venetoclax needs a ramp-up dose schedule with TLS prophylaxis; starting at full dose can precipitate fatal tumor lysis.
9. <5% blasts = CR
Complete remission requires <5% blasts in a marrow with adequate cellularity, no Auer rods, no extramedullary disease, AND peripheral count recovery (ANC ≥1000, platelets ≥100k). CR with incomplete count recovery (CRi) and measurable residual disease (MRD) positivity by flow/PCR predict relapse, so MRD status increasingly guides whether to escalate to transplant.
⚠️ CR demands BOTH <5% marrow blasts AND peripheral count recovery — <5% blasts without count recovery is only CRi. MRD positivity in apparent CR still portends relapse and may change management.
10. ANC / PLT recovery
Hematologic recovery thresholds defining true CR are ANC >1000/µL and platelets >100,000/µL. A day-14 marrow is often checked to confirm blast clearance; persistent disease may prompt re-induction. Expect a profound 3-4 week aplastic nadir after 7+3 during which neutropenic fever and bleeding risk are highest.
⚠️ The CR count thresholds are ANC >1000 and platelets >100k — values short of these (e.g., persistent thrombocytopenia) downgrade the response to CRi, not CR.
11. Transfusion support
During the induction aplasia, supportive care is as important as the chemo: transfuse platelets prophylactically (<10k, or higher if febrile/bleeding) and RBCs for symptomatic anemia, use leukoreduced and irradiated products to prevent CMV transmission and transfusion-associated GVHD, and give broad-spectrum antibacterial/antifungal/antiviral prophylaxis plus prompt empiric antibiotics for neutropenic fever.
⚠️ Blood products for AML/transplant candidates must be irradiated (prevents TA-GVHD) and leukoreduced (CMV risk, alloimmunization). Neutropenic fever is a medical emergency requiring empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics within an hour, before cultures result.
12. Allo / rasburicase
Tumor lysis syndrome prophylaxis starts before/with induction: aggressive IV hydration plus allopurinol (xanthine oxidase inhibitor, prevents new urate formation) for lower-risk disease, and rasburicase (recombinant urate oxidase, degrades existing uric acid) for high tumor burden/hyperuricemia. Monitor for the TLS tetrad — hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hyperuricemia, hypocalcemia — with rising creatinine.
⚠️ Rasburicase, not allopurinol, is used when uric acid is already high (it breaks down existing urate) and is contraindicated in G6PD deficiency (causes hemolysis/methemoglobinemia). Allopurinol only prevents new urate and does not lower an already-elevated level.
