📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. PAN safari figure
Polyarteritis nodosa is a necrotizing vasculitis of MEDIUM-sized muscular arteries, sparing the smallest vessels (arterioles, capillaries, venules). It presents with constitutional symptoms plus multi-organ ischemia: renal (microaneurysms, infarcts, renovascular HTN), GI (mesenteric angina, bowel infarction), skin (livedo, nodules, ulcers), nerves (mononeuritis multiplex), and testicles. Mnemonic: PAN spares the Pulmonary And glomerular (no lungs, no GN).
⚠️ PAN is medium-vessel and ANCA-NEGATIVE — a vignette with palpable purpura + glomerulonephritis + alveolar hemorrhage is small-vessel/ANCA disease (MPA/GPA), not PAN. Choosing PAN for a pulmonary-renal syndrome is the trap; PAN spares both lungs and glomeruli.
2. Renal angiography — microaneurysms
PAN renal disease is VASCULAR, not glomerular: catheter angiography of renal and mesenteric arteries shows microaneurysms and alternating stenoses, plus cortical infarcts. Conventional angiography is more sensitive than CT/MR angiography for these small microaneurysms. Diagnosis rests on angiography and/or biopsy of an affected accessible site (skin, nerve/muscle, testis).
⚠️ Renal involvement in PAN is ischemic (microaneurysms/infarcts, renovascular HTN), NOT glomerulonephritis. Expecting RBC casts/crescentic GN points to ANCA vasculitis instead. Also, choosing renal BIOPSY for suspected PAN risks bleeding from microaneurysms — angiography is preferred.
3. Bead-like aneurysms
The hallmark angiographic finding is 'beads on a string' — alternating saccular microaneurysms (often ~1 cm) and segmental stenoses along medium muscular arteries, classically in renal and mesenteric beds. These aneurysms can rupture, causing retroperitoneal or intra-abdominal hemorrhage. Histology shows segmental transmural necrotizing inflammation with fibrinoid necrosis at vessel bifurcations.
⚠️ 'Beading' microaneurysms are characteristic of PAN but not unique — they also occur in segmental arterial mediolysis, fibromuscular dysplasia, and amphetamine/drug-induced vasculopathy. The discriminator is the systemic vasculitic picture (mononeuritis, livedo, HTN, HBV link) plus biopsy.
4. HBV — antiviral first
A significant minority of PAN is hepatitis B-associated (immune-complex driven by HBV antigenemia). Treat HBV-PAN with antiviral therapy (e.g., entecavir/tenofovir) PLUS a SHORT course of glucocorticoids and plasma exchange to clear immune complexes — minimize/avoid prolonged immunosuppression, which promotes viral replication. Idiopathic (non-HBV) PAN is treated with steroids ± cyclophosphamide for severe disease.
⚠️ Giving prolonged steroids/cyclophosphamide ALONE for HBV-associated PAN is the trap — unopposed immunosuppression drives HBV replication and worsens outcomes. Antiviral therapy must come first (with plasma exchange + a short steroid course).
5. HTN (renin)
PAN causes renovascular (renin-mediated) hypertension from renal artery involvement and parenchymal infarcts in up to ~60% of patients. Blood-pressure control is a treatment priority because uncontrolled HTN drives renal and cardiovascular morbidity. ACE inhibitors/ARBs are useful given the renin-angiotensin contribution.
⚠️ New severe hypertension in a patient with myalgias, neuropathy, and skin nodules is a clue to PAN, not 'essential HTN.' Treating the BP alone without recognizing the underlying vasculitis is the trap.
6. Mononeuritis multiplex 51%
Mononeuritis multiplex — asymmetric, painful, stepwise involvement of named peripheral nerves (foot drop from common peroneal, wrist drop, etc.) — occurs in roughly half of PAN cases from ischemia of the vasa nervorum. Nerve/muscle (e.g., sural nerve + gastrocnemius) biopsy is a high-yield, accessible way to confirm the diagnosis. It is one of the most characteristic PAN features.
⚠️ Mononeuritis multiplex is non-specific (also EGPA, MPA, GPA, RA, cryoglobulinemia, diabetes). The discriminator for PAN is the accompanying medium-vessel picture (renovascular HTN, GI angina, testicular pain, HBV) with NO lung/GN involvement and a negative ANCA.
7. Livedo / skin
Cutaneous PAN manifestations: livedo reticularis/racemosa, tender subcutaneous nodules along arteries, digital ischemia/gangrene, and punched-out ulcers. A skin biopsy must be deep (reaching the dermal-subcutaneous junction) to capture the medium-sized arteries; a superficial punch biopsy may miss the diagnosis. Cutaneous-limited PAN is a milder subset confined to skin.
⚠️ A shallow/superficial skin biopsy can miss medium-vessel PAN because the affected arteries sit at the deep dermis/subcutis. Calling a biopsy 'negative for vasculitis' when it was too superficial is the trap — request a deep incisional/excisional biopsy.
8. Testicular / MSK
Testicular pain/orchitis from testicular artery vasculitis is a classic, relatively specific PAN clue. Musculoskeletal symptoms — myalgias and arthralgias — are very common (~64%). Testicular involvement, when present and accessible, can even be a biopsy target.
⚠️ Unexplained testicular pain plus systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, neuropathy, HTN) should prompt PAN, not just a urologic/infectious workup. Attributing it solely to epididymo-orchitis and missing systemic vasculitis is the trap.
9. No GN / No lungs
PAN characteristically SPARES the lungs (no pulmonary capillaritis/hemorrhage) and does NOT cause glomerulonephritis or form granulomas — because it affects medium arteries, not the small vessels/capillaries where GN and alveolar hemorrhage arise. These three 'NOs' separate PAN from ANCA-associated small-vessel vasculitis.
⚠️ Pulmonary involvement or glomerulonephritis essentially EXCLUDES PAN — those point to ANCA-associated vasculitis (MPA/GPA). Diagnosing PAN in a patient with hemoptysis/alveolar hemorrhage or crescentic GN is the trap.
10. No EOS / No ANCA
PAN is ANCA-NEGATIVE and is not an eosinophilic disease — it lacks the eosinophilia/asthma of EGPA and the ANCA of GPA/MPA. Diagnosis is made by angiography (beading microaneurysms) and/or deep tissue biopsy, supported by HBV serology. A positive ANCA should redirect you toward GPA/MPA/EGPA.
⚠️ A positive ANCA argues AGAINST PAN — choosing PAN in an ANCA-positive vignette is the trap. Likewise, marked eosinophilia + asthma points to EGPA, not PAN.
11. DADA2 — TNFi
DADA2 (Deficiency of Adenosine Deaminase 2) is an autosomal-recessive monogenic disease (ADA2/CECR1 mutation) presenting as PAN-like medium-vessel vasculitis in children/young adults, with early strokes (lacunar), livedo racemosa, fevers, and immunodeficiency/cytopenias. TNF inhibitors prevent strokes and are the treatment of choice. Suspect it in young/familial 'PAN' with recurrent strokes.
⚠️ Treating young-onset, stroke-prone, familial PAN-like vasculitis with standard cyclophosphamide while missing DADA2 is the trap — TNF inhibitors are the effective therapy, and anticoagulation/antiplatelets can cause hemorrhage in DADA2. Genetic testing changes management.
12. 5-yr untreated 10-20%
Untreated PAN has a dismal prognosis (historically ~10-20% 5-year survival), dramatically improved by glucocorticoids ± cyclophosphamide (and antivirals for HBV-PAN). Blood-pressure control is crucial given renovascular hypertension. The Five-Factor Score (renal insufficiency, proteinuria, GI, cardiac, CNS involvement) stratifies severity and guides whether to add cyclophosphamide.
⚠️ PAN is potentially fatal if untreated, so 'observe and monitor' for organ-threatening disease is the trap. Severe PAN (high Five-Factor Score) requires prompt steroids PLUS cyclophosphamide, not steroids alone.
13. Kawasaki child / strawberry
Kawasaki disease: medium-vessel vasculitis in children <5 yr (peak ~80% under 5). Diagnosis: fever ≥5 days PLUS ≥4 of — bilateral non-exudative conjunctivitis, oral changes (strawberry tongue, cracked red lips), polymorphous rash, extremity changes (palm/sole erythema/edema then periungual desquamation), and unilateral cervical lymphadenopathy (CRASH and Burn mnemonic).
⚠️ Incomplete/atypical Kawasaki (fever + only 2-3 criteria) still occurs, especially in infants who paradoxically have the HIGHEST coronary aneurysm risk. Withholding treatment because full criteria aren't met — when inflammatory markers and echo support it — is the trap.
14. Coronary aneurysms / IVIG
Untreated Kawasaki leads to coronary artery aneurysms in ~20-25%, peaking at weeks 3-4, risking thrombosis, MI, and sudden death. Treatment: IVIG 2 g/kg single infusion + aspirin, given EARLY (ideally within 10 days of fever onset) to cut aneurysm risk to ~3-5%. Follow with serial echocardiography; IVIG-resistant cases may need a second IVIG dose, steroids, or infliximab.
⚠️ Delaying IVIG beyond day 10 markedly reduces its protective effect on coronaries — the danger is waiting. Also note Kawasaki is the exception where aspirin IS used in a child (anti-inflammatory then antiplatelet doses), despite usual Reye-syndrome caution.
15. MIS-C
Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children (MIS-C) is a post-SARS-CoV-2 hyperinflammatory, Kawasaki-like illness, typically 2-6 weeks after infection, in older children than classic Kawasaki. It features prominent GI symptoms, myocarditis/ventricular dysfunction, and shock, with very high inflammatory markers and frequent positive COVID serology. Treatment overlaps Kawasaki: IVIG ± glucocorticoids, with cardiac support.
⚠️ MIS-C skews to OLDER children with prominent GI symptoms, myocardial dysfunction, and shock — calling it 'classic Kawasaki' or missing the cardiac/shock component is the trap. Check COVID serology/exposure and obtain echo + troponin/BNP early.
