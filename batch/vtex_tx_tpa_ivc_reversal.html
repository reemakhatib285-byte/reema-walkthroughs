📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. IVC filter umbrella
An IVC filter is indicated only when anticoagulation is absolutely contraindicated (active bleeding) or for recurrent PE despite adequate anticoagulation. Retrievable filters are preferred and should be removed once anticoagulation can resume.
⚠️ IVC filter is NOT a substitute for anticoagulation — it prevents embolization but doesn't treat existing clot; resume anticoagulation and retrieve the filter as soon as safe.
2. Absolute contraindication — active bleeding
The core indication for an IVC filter is active bleeding or another absolute contraindication to anticoagulation in a patient with VTE who still needs embolus protection.
⚠️ Don't place a filter for a routine PE that can be anticoagulated — only when anticoagulation is truly contraindicated.
3. Recurrent PE despite anticoagulation
The second filter indication: breakthrough VTE despite therapeutic anticoagulation. First confirm adherence/adequacy before attributing failure.
⚠️ 'Recurrent PE on anticoagulation' → verify therapeutic dosing/compliance first; only then add a filter.
4. Filter paradoxically ↑ DVT
Indwelling filters become a thrombosis nidus, paradoxically increasing the rate of lower-extremity DVT over time — another reason to retrieve early.
⚠️ Long-term filters RAISE DVT risk — they are not benign; remove retrievable filters once anticoagulation is possible.
5. Risk stratification map
Treatment is tier-driven: low-risk (normotensive, normal RV) → anticoagulation alone; submassive (RV dysfunction, normotensive) → anticoagulation, lytics only if deteriorating; massive (hypotension/RV failure) → immediate reperfusion (tPA).
⚠️ Match therapy to tier: RV strain WITHOUT hypotension is submassive (anticoagulate), only hypotension = massive (thrombolyse).
6. Massive → primary reperfusion
Massive (high-risk) PE with hypotension or shock requires immediate systemic thrombolysis (tPA) unless contraindicated; catheter-directed therapy or surgical embolectomy if lytics fail/contraindicated.
⚠️ Hemodynamic instability is the trigger for thrombolysis — don't wait for arrest; treat the hypotensive PE immediately.
7. tPA — alteplase dose
Systemic thrombolysis for PE: alteplase 100 mg IV over 2 hours. It rapidly lyses clot and offloads the RV in massive PE. Always overlap/surround with heparin before and after tPA.
⚠️ Surround tPA with HEPARIN before and after — and tPA is for MASSIVE PE; in stroke the dose/window differ, don't conflate protocols.
8. tPA contraindications
Absolute thrombolysis contraindications include prior intracranial hemorrhage, recent ischemic stroke, intracranial neoplasm/AVM, active bleeding, and recent surgery/major trauma (≤2-3 weeks).
⚠️ Recent surgery/trauma (≤2-3 wk) or any intracranial lesion contraindicates systemic lytics → use catheter-directed therapy or surgical embolectomy instead.
9. Catheter-directed thrombolysis
Catheter-directed thrombolysis delivers low-dose tPA locally, reducing systemic bleeding — useful when systemic lytics are relatively contraindicated or as backup in high-risk PE.
⚠️ Catheter-directed therapy is the bleeding-sparing 'backup' — choose it over full-dose systemic tPA when bleeding risk is elevated but reperfusion is needed.
10. Surgical embolectomy — last resort
Surgical (or catheter) embolectomy is reserved for massive PE when thrombolysis fails or is contraindicated, often with VA-ECMO support as a bridge.
⚠️ Embolectomy is the LAST resort — try thrombolysis/catheter-directed first unless absolutely contraindicated or failed.
11. VA-ECMO support
VA-ECMO bridges patients in refractory obstructive shock or arrest from massive PE until clot is cleared by lytics/embolectomy.
⚠️ ECMO is supportive bridge, not definitive therapy — it buys time for reperfusion, it doesn't dissolve the clot.
12. Duration traffic light
Duration depends on provocation: major transient risk factor → ~3 months; unprovoked or recurrent → extended/indefinite anticoagulation weighing bleeding risk; active cancer → DOAC or LMWH ongoing.
⚠️ Provoked by a transient factor = 3 months; UNPROVOKED VTE often warrants indefinite anticoagulation — don't auto-stop at 3 months for unprovoked clots.
13. Cancer-associated VTE
Cancer-associated VTE is treated with DOACs (apixaban/rivaroxaban/edoxaban) or LMWH for extended duration; LMWH is preferred with GI/GU lesions or drug interactions. Warfarin is less effective here.
⚠️ For cancer VTE prefer DOAC or LMWH over WARFARIN — warfarin has higher recurrence in malignancy.
14. Andexanet alfa — Xa reversal
Andexanet alfa reverses factor-Xa-inhibitor DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban). Idarucizumab reverses dabigatran (a DTI). PCC is an alternative for Xa-inhibitor bleeding; protamine reverses heparin; vitamin K/PCC reverses warfarin.
⚠️ Match reversal to drug: andexanet/PCC for Xa-inhibitors, idarucizumab for dabigatran, protamine for heparin — don't cross them.
15. Protamine — heparin reversal
Protamine sulfate fully reverses unfractionated heparin and partially reverses LMWH. It does not reverse fondaparinux or DOACs.
⚠️ Protamine works on HEPARIN/UFH (partial for LMWH) — it does NOT reverse fondaparinux or oral Xa/DTI agents.
