⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. The point mutation — Trap: HbS is 'Glu→Lys at β6' — WRONG; that lysine substitution at the SAME codon 6 is HbC. HbS is Glu→VALINE. Also do not confuse with HbE (β26 Glu→Lys). Discriminator: same position (β6), different amino acid — Val = S, Lys = C.
  2. Sticky Val patch — Trap: 'sickling happens equally whether Hb is oxygenated or deoxygenated.' Discriminator: polymerization is conformation-dependent — only DEOXY-HbS exposes the binding pocket, so hypoxia/acidosis/high altitude promote sickling and oxygen reverses it (early sickling is reversible).
  3. Polymerization ∝ concentration — Trap: 'lowering HbS concentration only modestly affects sickling.' Discriminator: because polymerization scales with ~the 30th power of concentration, small reductions in intracellular HbS (more HbF, better hydration) produce huge reductions in sickling — the rationale behind hydroxyurea and avoiding dehydration.
  4. Malaria protection (trait) — Trap: 'sickle trait protects against ALL malaria species/all infections.' Discriminator: the well-established protection is specifically against P. falciparum (severe malaria), and it is the HETEROZYGOTE who is protected and survives — homozygous HbSS does not benefit and has high childhood mortality, which is what keeps the allele in balance rather than eliminating it.
  5. 1. Vaso-occlusion — Trap: viewing sickling as a purely passive 'red cells get stuck' clog. Discriminator: vaso-occlusion is an active multicellular adhesion process (P-selectin, neutrophils, platelets, endothelium) — which is why crizanlizumab (anti-P-selectin) reduces crises. The viscosity phenotype tracks with HIGHER Hgb, opposite the hemolysis phenotype.
  6. Vaso-occlusive targets — Trap: dactylitis presenting as a swollen, painful hand/foot in an infant is mistaken for cellulitis or trauma. Discriminator: symmetric dactylitis around 6 months of age in an at-risk infant is the classic FIRST sickle presentation (timed to HbF decline). Bone pain + fever → distinguish infarct from osteomyelitis (Salmonella classic, S. aureus most common overall).
  7. ↑HbF = best defense — Trap: thinking hydroxyurea works mainly by 'thinning the blood/myelosuppression.' Discriminator: its principal benefit is inducing HbF (it also lowers WBC/platelet adhesion and increases NO) — the HbF rise is why it reduces VOC, ACS, and mortality across genotypes.
  8. 2. Hemolysis pathway — Trap: attributing ALL complications to vaso-occlusion. Discriminator: SCD has two partly opposing phenotypes — the hemolysis/NO-depletion axis (pulmonary HTN, leg ulcers, priapism, hemorrhagic stroke; tracks with LOW Hgb and HIGH LDH/reticulocytes) versus the viscosity/VOC axis (pain, ACS, osteonecrosis; HIGHER Hgb). Mixing these up is a common exam pitfall.
  9. Hemolysis complications — Trap: a stem clustering pulmonary HTN + leg ulcers + priapism is read as unrelated comorbidities. Discriminator: these all share ONE mechanism (intravascular hemolysis → NO scavenging) and segregate with high hemolytic markers and low Hgb — recognizing the shared pathway is the tested concept, and it explains why this phenotype differs from the pain-crisis phenotype.
  10. HbAS — trait — Trap: telling a sickle-trait patient they have 'mild sickle cell disease' or that they are anemic. Discriminator: trait is NOT a disease — normal Hgb, no chronic hemolysis; the only consistent organ effect is renal concentrating defect/hematuria. Watch for sudden death/rhabdomyolysis during extreme exertion and the renal medullary carcinoma association.
  11. HbSS — most severe — Trap: expecting any HbA on the electrophoresis of an untransfused HbSS patient. Discriminator: HbSS makes NO HbA at all — seeing HbA means either sickle TRAIT, an S/β+ thalassemia (some HbA), or recent transfusion. Conversely, S/β0-thal also shows no HbA and mimics SS.
  12. HbS/β0-thal — Trap: calling an electrophoresis with 'no HbA, high HbS' definitively HbSS. Discriminator: HbS/β0-thal ALSO shows no HbA and is clinically as severe as SS — distinguish them by MCV (low/microcytic in Sβ0-thal vs normal in SS) and a higher HbA2. Management is the same severe-disease approach.
  13. HbS/β+-thal — Trap: assuming any compound-heterozygote sickle syndrome is severe. Discriminator: presence of HbA on electrophoresis (in an untransfused patient) points to S/β+-thal (or trait) and predicts MILDER disease than SS/Sβ0. The amount of HbA produced inversely correlates with severity.
  14. HbSC disease — Trap: 'HbSC is mild, so it needs no surveillance.' Discriminator: despite milder anemia, HbSC has the HIGHEST risk of proliferative sickle retinopathy and prominent AVN — mandating regular dilated eye exams. Don't equate 'milder overall' with 'no end-organ disease.'
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. The point mutation
HbS arises from a single missense point mutation in the β-globin gene (HBB) on chromosome 11: codon 6 GAG→GTG, replacing glutamate (Glu, acidic) with valine (Val, hydrophobic) — written Glu6Val (β6 Glu→Val). Inheritance is autosomal recessive; HbSS = two mutant alleles. The mutation destroys an MstII/Dde restriction site, which historically enabled prenatal RFLP diagnosis.
⚠️ Trap: HbS is 'Glu→Lys at β6' — WRONG; that lysine substitution at the SAME codon 6 is HbC. HbS is Glu→VALINE. Also do not confuse with HbE (β26 Glu→Lys). Discriminator: same position (β6), different amino acid — Val = S, Lys = C.
2. Sticky Val patch
The substituted valine creates a hydrophobic 'sticky patch' on the β-chain surface that, ONLY in the deoxygenated (T-state) conformation, fits into a complementary hydrophobic pocket (β85 Phe/β88 Leu) on an adjacent HbS tetramer. This intermolecular contact nucleates 14-strand polymer fibers that distort the RBC into the rigid sickle shape. Oxygenated (R-state) Hb hides the pocket, so polymer melts when reoxygenated.
⚠️ Trap: 'sickling happens equally whether Hb is oxygenated or deoxygenated.' Discriminator: polymerization is conformation-dependent — only DEOXY-HbS exposes the binding pocket, so hypoxia/acidosis/high altitude promote sickling and oxygen reverses it (early sickling is reversible).
3. Polymerization ∝ concentration
The rate/extent of deoxy-HbS polymerization is exquisitely concentration-dependent — proportional to roughly the 30th power of intracellular HbS concentration. Therefore anything that raises MCHC (cellular dehydration, e.g., via Gardos channel K+/water loss) dramatically accelerates sickling, while diluting HbS (raising HbF, hydration) protects. This steep dependence explains why even modest HbF or hydration changes have outsized clinical benefit.
⚠️ Trap: 'lowering HbS concentration only modestly affects sickling.' Discriminator: because polymerization scales with ~the 30th power of concentration, small reductions in intracellular HbS (more HbF, better hydration) produce huge reductions in sickling — the rationale behind hydroxyurea and avoiding dehydration.
4. Malaria protection (trait)
Heterozygous HbAS (sickle trait) confers resistance to Plasmodium falciparum malaria (infected cells sickle and are cleared; impaired parasite growth), a balanced-polymorphism/heterozygote-advantage that maintains high carrier frequencies (~8% of African-Americans; up to 25–30% in parts of sub-Saharan Africa) and in Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, and Indian populations. This is the classic example of balancing selection in human genetics.
⚠️ Trap: 'sickle trait protects against ALL malaria species/all infections.' Discriminator: the well-established protection is specifically against P. falciparum (severe malaria), and it is the HETEROZYGOTE who is protected and survives — homozygous HbSS does not benefit and has high childhood mortality, which is what keeps the allele in balance rather than eliminating it.
5. 1. Vaso-occlusion
Pathway 1 — VASO-OCCLUSION/viscosity phenotype: rigid sickled RBCs plus adhesion molecules cause adherent leukocytes, activated platelets, and inflamed endothelium to plug the microcirculation → ischemia, infarction, and acute pain. This phenotype dominates in patients with HIGHER hemoglobin/hematocrit (HbSC, Sβ+) and underlies pain crises, ACS, osteonecrosis, and dactylitis.
⚠️ Trap: viewing sickling as a purely passive 'red cells get stuck' clog. Discriminator: vaso-occlusion is an active multicellular adhesion process (P-selectin, neutrophils, platelets, endothelium) — which is why crizanlizumab (anti-P-selectin) reduces crises. The viscosity phenotype tracks with HIGHER Hgb, opposite the hemolysis phenotype.
6. Vaso-occlusive targets
Vaso-occlusion strikes high-flow/marrow-rich tissues: hand-foot syndrome (DACTYLITIS) is often the FIRST manifestation in infants ~6 months (when HbF falls); acute chest syndrome (lungs, #1 killer); ischemic stroke (brain, children); bone infarcts/osteonecrosis; and priapism. Bone infarction also predisposes to Salmonella osteomyelitis.
⚠️ Trap: dactylitis presenting as a swollen, painful hand/foot in an infant is mistaken for cellulitis or trauma. Discriminator: symmetric dactylitis around 6 months of age in an at-risk infant is the classic FIRST sickle presentation (timed to HbF decline). Bone pain + fever → distinguish infarct from osteomyelitis (Salmonella classic, S. aureus most common overall).
7. ↑HbF = best defense
Fetal hemoglobin (HbF, α2γ2) physically cannot enter the deoxy-HbS polymer, so it dilutes intracellular HbS and (per the steep concentration dependence) powerfully blocks sickling. Higher baseline HbF predicts milder disease; pharmacologically raising HbF — chiefly with hydroxyurea (also via BCL11A-targeted gene therapy) — is the central protective lever in SCD. Patients with co-inherited hereditary persistence of HbF run benign courses.
⚠️ Trap: thinking hydroxyurea works mainly by 'thinning the blood/myelosuppression.' Discriminator: its principal benefit is inducing HbF (it also lowers WBC/platelet adhesion and increases NO) — the HbF rise is why it reduces VOC, ACS, and mortality across genotypes.
8. 2. Hemolysis pathway
Pathway 2 — HEMOLYSIS/endothelial-dysfunction phenotype: chronic intravascular hemolysis releases cell-free hemoglobin (which scavenges nitric oxide) and arginase (which depletes arginine, the NO substrate) → NO deficiency, vasoconstriction, platelet activation, and endothelial dysfunction. This phenotype dominates in patients with the MOST hemolysis/lowest Hgb.
⚠️ Trap: attributing ALL complications to vaso-occlusion. Discriminator: SCD has two partly opposing phenotypes — the hemolysis/NO-depletion axis (pulmonary HTN, leg ulcers, priapism, hemorrhagic stroke; tracks with LOW Hgb and HIGH LDH/reticulocytes) versus the viscosity/VOC axis (pain, ACS, osteonecrosis; HIGHER Hgb). Mixing these up is a common exam pitfall.
9. Hemolysis complications
NO depletion from hemolysis produces: pulmonary hypertension (TR jet ≥2.5 m/s predicts mortality), chronic malleolar leg ulcers, priapism, hemorrhagic stroke (more in adults), nephropathy/albuminuria, and — from heme catabolism — pigment (bilirubin) gallstones. These cluster together as the hemolytic phenotype.
⚠️ Trap: a stem clustering pulmonary HTN + leg ulcers + priapism is read as unrelated comorbidities. Discriminator: these all share ONE mechanism (intravascular hemolysis → NO scavenging) and segregate with high hemolytic markers and low Hgb — recognizing the shared pathway is the tested concept, and it explains why this phenotype differs from the pain-crisis phenotype.
10. HbAS — trait
Sickle TRAIT (HbAS): one normal + one S allele, HbA > HbS (~60/40), so it is generally ASYMPTOMATIC with normal CBC, smear, and lifespan. Symptoms appear only under extreme stress — severe hypoxia, dehydration, or high altitude. Renal medulla is the exception: painless hematuria, hyposthenuria, and a rare association with renal MEDULLARY carcinoma. Important for genetic counseling.
⚠️ Trap: telling a sickle-trait patient they have 'mild sickle cell disease' or that they are anemic. Discriminator: trait is NOT a disease — normal Hgb, no chronic hemolysis; the only consistent organ effect is renal concentrating defect/hematuria. Watch for sudden death/rhabdomyolysis during extreme exertion and the renal medullary carcinoma association.
11. HbSS — most severe
HbSS = homozygous sickle cell ANEMIA, the classic severe genotype with NO normal HbA produced. Electrophoresis/HPLC: predominant HbS (~85–95%), HbF 1–15%, HbA2 ~2–3%, and ZERO HbA. Disease severity tracks INVERSELY with HbF level — which is why raising HbF (hydroxyurea) helps. Baseline Hgb typically 6–9 g/dL with reticulocytosis.
⚠️ Trap: expecting any HbA on the electrophoresis of an untransfused HbSS patient. Discriminator: HbSS makes NO HbA at all — seeing HbA means either sickle TRAIT, an S/β+ thalassemia (some HbA), or recent transfusion. Conversely, S/β0-thal also shows no HbA and mimics SS.
12. HbS/β0-thal
HbS/β0-thalassemia: the β-thal allele makes NO β-globin, so NO HbA is produced → the patient relies entirely on HbS and behaves clinically like severe HbSS. Clue distinguishing it from true SS: microcytosis (low MCV) and an elevated HbA2 from the thalassemia allele, with HbS > HbF and no HbA.
⚠️ Trap: calling an electrophoresis with 'no HbA, high HbS' definitively HbSS. Discriminator: HbS/β0-thal ALSO shows no HbA and is clinically as severe as SS — distinguish them by MCV (low/microcytic in Sβ0-thal vs normal in SS) and a higher HbA2. Management is the same severe-disease approach.
13. HbS/β+-thal
HbS/β+-thalassemia: the β+ allele still makes SOME β-globin → some HbA is produced (electrophoresis shows HbS > HbA, plus ↑HbA2), so disease is MILDER/intermediate compared with SS. Patients are microcytic and usually have fewer and less severe complications, sometimes nearly asymptomatic depending on the β+ allele's residual output.
⚠️ Trap: assuming any compound-heterozygote sickle syndrome is severe. Discriminator: presence of HbA on electrophoresis (in an untransfused patient) points to S/β+-thal (or trait) and predicts MILDER disease than SS/Sβ0. The amount of HbA produced inversely correlates with severity.
14. HbSC disease
HbSC (compound heterozygote for HbS and HbC): generally MILDER than HbSS with higher baseline Hgb and target cells/HbC crystals on smear, but classically associated with PROLIFERATIVE RETINOPATHY, avascular necrosis of the femoral head, splenic infarcts/sequestration into adulthood, and renal medullary issues. The relatively higher Hgb gives a viscosity-driven phenotype.
⚠️ Trap: 'HbSC is mild, so it needs no surveillance.' Discriminator: despite milder anemia, HbSC has the HIGHEST risk of proliferative sickle retinopathy and prominent AVN — mandating regular dilated eye exams. Don't equate 'milder overall' with 'no end-organ disease.'
