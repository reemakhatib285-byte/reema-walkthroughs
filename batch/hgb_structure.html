⚠️ Board traps 13
  1. Primary structure — Trap: 'quaternary structure is the amino-acid sequence' — WRONG; the sequence is PRIMARY structure. Quaternary refers to assembly of multiple chains (α2β2). Discriminator: primary = sequence; secondary = local folds; tertiary = one chain's 3D shape; quaternary = multi-subunit assembly.
  2. Secondary / Pro wedge — Trap: assuming proline behaves like other residues inside a helix. Discriminator: proline (and glycine) are the classic helix DISRUPTORS — a proline introduced mid-helix breaks it, a frequently tested biochemistry point that explains certain unstable hemoglobin variants.
  3. Tertiary structure — Trap: thinking the sickle mutation changes the heme/iron itself. Discriminator: HbS alters the surface tertiary chemistry (a hydrophobic patch where a charged residue belonged), not iron binding or oxygen chemistry directly—the oxygen problem is secondary to polymerization in the deoxy state.
  4. Quaternary — α2β2 — Trap: confusing the adult composition with fetal/A2 forms. Discriminator: HbA = α2β2; HbA2 = α2δ2; HbF = α2γ2 — ALL contain two alpha chains, so α-globin defects affect every hemoglobin, whereas β-globin defects (sickle, β-thal) spare HbF and HbA2.
  5. LCR — master switch — Trap: assuming each globin gene is regulated alone. Discriminator: the β-cluster genes are co-regulated by ONE master LCR, and chromosomal gene order = developmental expression order—deletion of the LCR shuts down the whole β-cluster. The α-cluster, by contrast, is expressed largely independently of this LCR.
  6. β-cluster on Chr 11 — Trap: 'β-globin genes are on chromosome 16' — WRONG; that is the ALPHA cluster. The β-cluster is on chromosome 11. Discriminator: remember '11 = beta, 16 = alpha' (alpha has more genes/higher number).
  7. α-cluster on Chr 16 — Trap: applying a two-gene model to alpha. Discriminator: there are FOUR α genes (vs two β genes), so α-thal severity depends on how many of the four are lost (1–4 deletions). Because α is in every Hb, severe α-thal has no asymptomatic 'fetal escape'—Hb Barts causes hydrops in utero, unlike β-thal which presents after the γ→β switch.
  8. HbF switching / BCL11A — Trap: thinking BCL11A turns HbF ON. Discriminator: BCL11A is a REPRESSOR—it silences HbF after birth, so inhibiting/editing it RAISES HbF. This is why the sickle/β-thal mutations (β-chain problems) only manifest AFTER the switch, when HbF declines (≈6 months of age).
  9. Adult Hb fractions — Trap: reading an elevated HbA2 as 'normal variant.' Discriminator: HbA2 >3.5% is the hallmark of β-thalassemia MINOR/trait (microcytic anemia with normal/high RBC count and low Mentzer index). Iron deficiency, by contrast, lowers/normalizes HbA2 and is the main mimic to exclude.
  10. Left shift factors — Trap: assuming high O2 affinity is always good. Discriminator: a LEFT shift means Hb won't release O2 to tissues → tissue hypoxia despite high saturation (classic in CO poisoning, where SpO2 reads falsely normal). HbF's left shift is adaptive in utero but the same physiology is harmful with CO/metHb.
  11. Right shift factors — Trap: mixing up which factors shift the curve. Discriminator: use CADET—CO2, Acid, 2,3-DPG, Exercise/Temp all shift RIGHT (more O2 delivery). High altitude RAISES 2,3-BPG → right shift to offload O2; do not confuse with the left shift of stored (old) banked blood, which is 2,3-BPG-depleted.
  12. P50 = 26 mmHg — Trap: reversing the direction—thinking a higher P50 means higher affinity. Discriminator: HIGH P50 = LOW affinity (right shift); LOW P50 = HIGH affinity (left shift). HbF and CO-Hb have LOW P50 (left), exercising muscle has HIGH P50 (right).
  13. HbF & placental O2 grab — Trap: thinking the fetus gets O2 because maternal blood is more oxygenated. Discriminator: the gradient is driven by HbF's HIGHER O2 affinity (reduced 2,3-BPG binding by γ-chains)—fetal Hb 'pulls' O2 from maternal HbA. This same property (HbF can't enter the deoxy-HbS polymer) is why raising HbF protects in sickle cell disease.
📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Primary structure
Primary structure = the linear amino-acid SEQUENCE of a globin chain. Single-residue changes here define the major hemoglobinopathies: β6 Glu→Val = HbS (sickle), β6 Glu→Lys = HbC, and β26 Glu→Lys = HbE. Because these are point substitutions in the primary sequence, they alter charge and migration on electrophoresis/HPLC, the basis of diagnosis.
⚠️ Trap: 'quaternary structure is the amino-acid sequence' — WRONG; the sequence is PRIMARY structure. Quaternary refers to assembly of multiple chains (α2β2). Discriminator: primary = sequence; secondary = local folds; tertiary = one chain's 3D shape; quaternary = multi-subunit assembly.
2. Secondary / Pro wedge
Secondary structure = local repeating folds (α-helices and β-sheets) stabilized by backbone hydrogen bonds; globin chains are highly α-helical. PROLINE acts as a 'helix breaker'—its rigid ring and lack of an amide H kink or terminate a helix, so proline substitutions disrupt secondary structure and can destabilize the protein.
⚠️ Trap: assuming proline behaves like other residues inside a helix. Discriminator: proline (and glycine) are the classic helix DISRUPTORS — a proline introduced mid-helix breaks it, a frequently tested biochemistry point that explains certain unstable hemoglobin variants.
3. Tertiary structure
Tertiary structure = the full 3D fold of a SINGLE globin chain, with a hydrophobic CORE and charged/polar residues facing the aqueous surface, creating the heme pocket that holds the iron-porphyrin. In HbS, the β6 valine places a hydrophobic patch on the SURFACE—abnormal for a charged-surface protein—which is exactly why deoxy-HbS molecules stick together and polymerize.
⚠️ Trap: thinking the sickle mutation changes the heme/iron itself. Discriminator: HbS alters the surface tertiary chemistry (a hydrophobic patch where a charged residue belonged), not iron binding or oxygen chemistry directly—the oxygen problem is secondary to polymerization in the deoxy state.
4. Quaternary — α2β2
Quaternary structure = assembly of the four globin chains into a tetramer; adult HbA = 2 alpha + 2 beta (α2β2), each with one heme/Fe2+ binding one O2 (4 O2 per Hb). The tetramer shifts between the TENSE (T, deoxy, low-affinity) and RELAXED (R, oxy, high-affinity) states; sequential O2 binding stabilizes R, producing COOPERATIVITY and the sigmoidal dissociation curve.
⚠️ Trap: confusing the adult composition with fetal/A2 forms. Discriminator: HbA = α2β2; HbA2 = α2δ2; HbF = α2γ2 — ALL contain two alpha chains, so α-globin defects affect every hemoglobin, whereas β-globin defects (sickle, β-thal) spare HbF and HbA2.
5. LCR — master switch
The Locus Control Region (LCR) is the powerful upstream enhancer that drives high-level, tissue-specific expression of the entire β-globin gene cluster. The physical ORDER of genes along the chromosome mirrors the developmental ORDER of expression (5'→3' = embryo→fetus→adult), as the LCR sequentially activates each gene.
⚠️ Trap: assuming each globin gene is regulated alone. Discriminator: the β-cluster genes are co-regulated by ONE master LCR, and chromosomal gene order = developmental expression order—deletion of the LCR shuts down the whole β-cluster. The α-cluster, by contrast, is expressed largely independently of this LCR.
6. β-cluster on Chr 11
The β-globin gene cluster sits on chromosome 11 and contains ε, Gγ, Aγ, δ, and β. Expression proceeds developmentally in gene order: ε (embryo) → γ (fetus, makes HbF) → δ and β (adult). There are only TWO β-globin genes total (one per chromosome 11), which is why a single point mutation (sickle) or reduced output (β-thal) has a large effect.
⚠️ Trap: 'β-globin genes are on chromosome 16' — WRONG; that is the ALPHA cluster. The β-cluster is on chromosome 11. Discriminator: remember '11 = beta, 16 = alpha' (alpha has more genes/higher number).
7. α-cluster on Chr 16
The α-globin gene cluster is on chromosome 16 and contains ζ, α2, and α1—giving FOUR functional α genes total (two per chromosome). α-globin is needed for EVERY hemoglobin (HbA, HbA2, HbF all use α). This four-gene dosage explains the spectrum of α-thalassemia: 1 deleted = silent carrier, 2 = trait, 3 = HbH disease (β4), 4 = Hb Barts/hydrops fetalis (γ4).
⚠️ Trap: applying a two-gene model to alpha. Discriminator: there are FOUR α genes (vs two β genes), so α-thal severity depends on how many of the four are lost (1–4 deletions). Because α is in every Hb, severe α-thal has no asymptomatic 'fetal escape'—Hb Barts causes hydrops in utero, unlike β-thal which presents after the γ→β switch.
8. HbF switching / BCL11A
The γ→β switch: around birth the repressor BCL11A silences γ-globin, switching HbF (α2γ2) production over to adult HbA (α2β2), so HbF normally falls to <1% by ~6–12 months. Therapeutically, KNOCKING DOWN BCL11A reactivates γ-globin/HbF—the basis of CRISPR gene therapy (exa-cel) and part of how hydroxyurea benefits sickle/β-thal patients.
⚠️ Trap: thinking BCL11A turns HbF ON. Discriminator: BCL11A is a REPRESSOR—it silences HbF after birth, so inhibiting/editing it RAISES HbF. This is why the sickle/β-thal mutations (β-chain problems) only manifest AFTER the switch, when HbF declines (≈6 months of age).
9. Adult Hb fractions
Normal adult hemoglobin fractions: HbA (α2β2) ~96–97%, HbA2 (α2δ2) ~2–3.5%, and HbF (α2γ2) <1%. Diagnostically, HbA2 is ELEVATED (>3.5%) in β-thalassemia TRAIT, while HbF rises in β-thalassemia major, hereditary persistence of HbF, and is induced by hydroxyurea.
⚠️ Trap: reading an elevated HbA2 as 'normal variant.' Discriminator: HbA2 >3.5% is the hallmark of β-thalassemia MINOR/trait (microcytic anemia with normal/high RBC count and low Mentzer index). Iron deficiency, by contrast, lowers/normalizes HbA2 and is the main mimic to exclude.
10. Left shift factors
A LEFT shift = INCREASED O2 affinity (curve moves left, lower P50)—Hb holds O2 tightly and unloads less to tissues. Causes: ↓2,3-BPG, alkalosis (↑pH), hypothermia (↓temp), ↓CO2, and fetal hemoglobin; also carboxyhemoglobin (CO) and methemoglobin shift left/impair delivery. Mnemonic: the opposite of the 'exercise/CADET' state.
⚠️ Trap: assuming high O2 affinity is always good. Discriminator: a LEFT shift means Hb won't release O2 to tissues → tissue hypoxia despite high saturation (classic in CO poisoning, where SpO2 reads falsely normal). HbF's left shift is adaptive in utero but the same physiology is harmful with CO/metHb.
11. Right shift factors
A RIGHT shift = DECREASED O2 affinity (higher P50)—Hb releases O2 more readily to tissues. Causes (mnemonic CADET, face Right): ↑CO2, ↑Acid (↓pH, the Bohr effect), ↑2,3-BPG, ↑Exercise/Temperature. This is the adaptive response in exercising/metabolically active or hypoxic tissue and at high altitude (2,3-BPG rises over days).
⚠️ Trap: mixing up which factors shift the curve. Discriminator: use CADET—CO2, Acid, 2,3-DPG, Exercise/Temp all shift RIGHT (more O2 delivery). High altitude RAISES 2,3-BPG → right shift to offload O2; do not confuse with the left shift of stored (old) banked blood, which is 2,3-BPG-depleted.
12. P50 = 26 mmHg
P50 = the partial pressure of O2 at which hemoglobin is 50% saturated; normal ~26–27 mmHg. A HIGHER P50 = right shift = lower affinity (releases O2 easily); a LOWER P50 = left shift = higher affinity (holds O2 tight). P50 is the single-number summary of the dissociation curve's position.
⚠️ Trap: reversing the direction—thinking a higher P50 means higher affinity. Discriminator: HIGH P50 = LOW affinity (right shift); LOW P50 = HIGH affinity (left shift). HbF and CO-Hb have LOW P50 (left), exercising muscle has HIGH P50 (right).
13. HbF & placental O2 grab
Fetal hemoglobin (HbF, α2γ2) binds 2,3-BPG POORLY because γ-chains lack the cationic β-chain residues that bind it; with less 2,3-BPG bound, HbF has HIGHER O2 affinity (lower P50, left-shifted) than maternal HbA, allowing the fetus to extract O2 across the placenta from mother's blood.
⚠️ Trap: thinking the fetus gets O2 because maternal blood is more oxygenated. Discriminator: the gradient is driven by HbF's HIGHER O2 affinity (reduced 2,3-BPG binding by γ-chains)—fetal Hb 'pulls' O2 from maternal HbA. This same property (HbF can't enter the deoxy-HbS polymer) is why raising HbF protects in sickle cell disease.
