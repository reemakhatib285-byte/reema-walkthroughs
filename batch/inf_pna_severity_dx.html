📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. CURB-65 hand of 5 buttons
CURB-65 is a 5-point mortality/disposition tool, 1 point each for Confusion, Urea >7 mmol/L (BUN >19 mg/dL), Respiratory rate >=30, low Blood pressure (SBP <90 or DBP <=60), and age >=65. It estimates 30-day mortality and is simpler than the PSI/PORT score.
⚠️ "choose antibiotics because CURB-65 is high" -> wrong; the score guides SITE OF CARE, not drug choice — and it can underestimate a young hypoxemic patient (no oxygenation variable).
2. C — Confusion button
New-onset confusion/disorientation scores 1 point, reflecting severe disease and/or hypoperfusion. Especially important in the elderly, in whom altered mental status may be the dominant or only presentation of pneumonia.
⚠️ "baseline dementia" or "just a UTI" for new confusion -> wrong; in an older adult it may be the only clue to pneumonia/sepsis. It must be NEW confusion to count.
3. U — Urea >7 button
Blood urea >7 mmol/L (BUN >19-20 mg/dL) scores 1 point, marking end-organ hypoperfusion/dehydration and worse prognosis — one reason a metabolic panel is in the initial CAP workup.
⚠️ "score the creatinine" -> wrong; it is UREA/BUN — a normal creatinine with elevated BUN still earns the point. The lab-free 'CRB-65' drops this variable.
4. R — RR >=30 button
Respiratory rate >=30 breaths/min scores 1 point — an early, sensitive marker of respiratory compromise; tachypnea often precedes hypoxemia and hypotension. RR >=30 is also an IDSA/ATS minor ICU criterion.
⚠️ "vitals look okay so skip the RR" -> wrong; tachypnea is frequently undercounted at the bedside — don't overlook it.
5. B — BP 90/60 button
Hypotension (SBP <90 OR DBP <=60) scores 1 point, signaling impending septic shock. Fluid-requiring hypotension is a minor ICU criterion; vasopressor-requiring septic shock is a MAJOR criterion (automatic ICU).
⚠️ "routine ward care" for a hypotensive pneumonia patient -> wrong; distinguish fluid-responsive hypotension (minor) from vasopressor-dependent shock (major -> automatic ICU).
6. 65 — age button
Age >=65 scores 1 point, reflecting the steep rise in pneumonia mortality with age from comorbidity, immunosenescence, and atypical presentations.
⚠️ "vitals mild so send home an elder" -> wrong; age alone can tip toward admission, and elders may lack fever/leukocytosis, presenting with confusion or functional decline.
7. Traffic-light score (0-1 / 2 / >=3)
Mortality climbs with score: 0-1 ~0.6-1.5% (outpatient), 2 = consider admission, >=3 ~22% 30-day mortality (admit and assess for ICU). PSI/PORT is a more granular alternative that better identifies low-risk outpatients.
⚠️ "treat a CURB-65 >=3 patient as outpatient" -> wrong; that is clearly unsafe — and clinical judgment/hypoxemia can override a deceptively low score.
8. ICU gate — major levers
The IDSA/ATS severe-CAP definition has 2 MAJOR criteria — invasive mechanical ventilation (respiratory failure needing intubation) and septic shock requiring vasopressors. Either ONE alone mandates ICU admission.
⚠️ "wait to accumulate minor criteria" -> wrong; a single major criterion is sufficient for ICU — never downgrade a ventilated or vasopressor-dependent patient to the ward.
9. ICU minor criteria switches (need >=3)
Nine MINOR criteria: RR >=30, PaO2/FiO2 <=250, multilobar infiltrates, confusion, uremia (BUN >=20), leukopenia (WBC <4000), thrombocytopenia (<100k), hypothermia (<36C), and hypotension requiring aggressive fluids. Meeting >=3 defines severe CAP warranting ICU/high-level monitoring.
⚠️ "no shock or vent so it's not severe" -> wrong; >=3 minor criteria need ICU care. Note the counterintuitive ones: LEUKOPENIA (not leukocytosis), HYPOthermia, and thrombocytopenia all count.
10. Sputum & blood cultures (bench)
Sputum Gram stain/culture (good specimen: >25 neutrophils, <10 epithelial cells/LPF) and two sets of blood cultures (before antibiotics) are for severe disease and risk factors (prior MRSA/Pseudomonas, cavitary, asplenia, leukopenia). Pneumococcus is the most common blood-culture isolate. NOT routine in mild outpatient CAP.
⚠️ "delay antibiotics for culture results" in severe CAP/sepsis -> wrong; collect specimens but treat promptly — time-to-antibiotics matters, and an epithelial-rich specimen is uninterpretable contamination.
11. Legionella & pneumococcal urinary antigen
Urinary antigens give rapid, antibiotic-insensitive results: Legionella antigen detects L. pneumophila serogroup 1; pneumococcal antigen detects S. pneumoniae. Recommended in severe CAP and with Legionella outbreak/travel, allowing targeted de-escalation.
⚠️ "negative Legionella antigen excludes it" -> wrong; it misses non-serogroup-1 strains — pursue BCYE culture or PCR if suspicion is high; antigen can also stay positive for weeks (recent vs current).
12. Procalcitonin (crossed out)
Procalcitonin rises with bacterial and falls with viral/resolving infection and can help GUIDE de-escalation/duration. But IDSA/ATS say PCT should NOT be used alone to decide whether to START antibiotics in suspected CAP — clinical and radiographic diagnosis drives initiation.
⚠️ "low PCT, so withhold antibiotics" -> wrong; a low procalcitonin does not rule out bacterial pneumonia in a patient with a clinical/radiographic CAP diagnosis.
13. Elderly presentation pearl
In older adults pneumonia may present WITHOUT fever, cough, or leukocytosis — altered mental status, functional decline, falls, tachypnea, or decompensation of chronic illness may be the only signs. Keep a low threshold for chest imaging.
⚠️ "anchor on UTI/delirium" in a confused elder -> wrong; include pneumonia for any new confusion, and don't be reassured by absent fever or normal WBC (leukopenia/hypothermia are severity markers).
