⚠️ Board traps 13
  1. Hereditary elliptocytosis (HE) — WRONG answer: "HE is usually a severe transfusion-dependent hemolytic anemia." Discriminator: most HE is clinically silent with little or no hemolysis — only the biallelic HPP variant and a minority of HE patients hemolyze significantly. If a question describes a sick, transfusion-dependent neonate with bizarre RBCs, think HPP or HS, not classic HE.
  2. alpha-spectrin / SPTA1 — WRONG answer: "HE is inherited in an X-linked recessive pattern like G6PD." Discriminator: HE/SPTA1 is autosomal dominant — look for an affected parent across generations and equal sex distribution, not the male-predominant pedigree of G6PD.
  3. % elliptocytes ≠ severity — WRONG answer: "A higher fraction of elliptocytes on the peripheral smear means more severe disease and predicts the need for splenectomy." Discriminator: morphology percentage and clinical severity are decoupled in HE — use hemolysis markers (retics, LDH, haptoglobin), not the elliptocyte count, to gauge severity.
  4. Mostly asymptomatic — WRONG answer: "Every patient with hereditary elliptocytosis should undergo prophylactic splenectomy." Discriminator: splenectomy is only for the severe/HPP minority; the asymptomatic majority needs reassurance and at most folate, and splenectomy carries lifelong encapsulated-organism and thrombosis risk.
  5. HPP severe variant — WRONG answer: "The bizarre fragmented cells and thermal instability mean this is a microangiopathic process like TTP/DIC." Discriminator: HPP is a congenital biallelic spectrin defect with a positive family history of HE and abnormal thermal fragility — there are no schistocytes from fibrin strands, no thrombocytopenia, and the Coombs test is negative.
  6. HPP → splenectomy — WRONG answer: "Splenectomy is contraindicated in HPP because of thrombosis risk, just as in dehydrated stomatocytosis." Discriminator: the thrombosis-related splenectomy contraindication applies to the stomatocytoses (DHS/xerocytosis), NOT to HE/HPP — in severe HE/HPP, splenectomy is beneficial and standard, with vaccination and lifelong OPSI awareness.
  7. SAO malaria protection — WRONG answer: "SAO is a severe congenital hemolytic anemia requiring chronic transfusion." Discriminator: heterozygous SAO is a benign, often hemolysis-free polymorphism selected for malaria protection — the rigid cells are the very feature that blocks parasite invasion, not a marker of severe disease.
  8. SAO homozygous lethal — WRONG answer: "Homozygous SAO produces the most malaria-resistant, healthiest individuals." Discriminator: homozygous SAO is embryonic-lethal — only the heterozygous (carrier) state is viable and malaria-protective, the classic signature of a balanced polymorphism maintained by heterozygote advantage.
  9. Stomatocytosis wing — WRONG answer: "Stomatocytes on the smear are specific for hereditary spherocytosis." Discriminator: spherocytes (no central pallor) point to HS or warm autoimmune hemolysis; stomatocytes (mouth-shaped pallor) point to cation-permeability disorders (DHS/OHS) or acquired causes like alcoholic liver disease.
  10. DHS — dehydrated (PIEZO1/Gardos) — WRONG answer: "Splenectomy is the treatment of choice for dehydrated hereditary stomatocytosis, as it is for hereditary spherocytosis." Discriminator: in DHS/xerocytosis splenectomy is CONTRAINDICATED — post-splenectomy thrombosis and pulmonary hypertension are well documented. Distinguishing DHS from HS before any splenectomy is the entire point of this scene's central red X.
  11. OHS — overhydrated (RHAG) — WRONG answer: "OHS is caused by a band-3 mutation and is safely cured by splenectomy." Discriminator: OHS is an RHAG cation-leak disorder (water IN, low MCHC), not a band-3 defect, and shares the stomatocytosis splenectomy-thrombosis caution — band 3 / SLC4A1 is the SAO and one HS mechanism, not OHS.
  12. Splenectomy DANGER (skull) — WRONG answer: "All hereditary membrane hemolytic anemias respond to and should be treated with splenectomy." Discriminator: splenectomy is beneficial in HS/severe HE/HPP but DANGEROUS in stomatocytosis (DHS/OHS) due to thrombosis — never offer splenectomy until you've excluded a cation-permeability disorder.
  13. Other rare variants — WRONG answer: "Acanthocytes (spur cells) and stomatocytes are interchangeable terms for the same membrane lesion." Discriminator: acanthocytes (irregular spicules — abetalipoproteinemia, severe liver disease, neuroacanthocytosis) are distinct from stomatocytes (mouth-shaped pallor — cation-leak disorders); GLUT1 deficiency uniquely couples a hematologic shape change with neurologic disease.
📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Hereditary elliptocytosis (HE)
Hereditary elliptocytosis is a HORIZONTAL cytoskeleton defect: the spectrin-spectrin self-association contact and the spectrin–actin–protein 4.1 horizontal junction are weakened, so the membrane cannot rebound after shear in the microvasculature and RBCs become permanently cigar/pencil-shaped elliptocytes. Unlike vertical defects (hereditary spherocytosis = ankyrin/band 3/spectrin), the lipid bilayer is not lost, so the MCHC is normal and cells are not spherical. The vast majority (~90%) of patients are asymptomatic with a normal Hb and only incidental elliptocytes on smear. Mnemonic: HE = Horizontal/Elliptical, HS = vertical/spherical.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "HE is usually a severe transfusion-dependent hemolytic anemia." Discriminator: most HE is clinically silent with little or no hemolysis — only the biallelic HPP variant and a minority of HE patients hemolyze significantly. If a question describes a sick, transfusion-dependent neonate with bizarre RBCs, think HPP or HS, not classic HE.
2. alpha-spectrin / SPTA1
HE is autosomal DOMINANT and most commonly caused by mutations in alpha-spectrin (SPTA1), beta-spectrin (SPTB), or protein 4.1R (EPB41), all components of the horizontal membrane skeleton. SPTA1 defects impair spectrin tetramer formation; protein 4.1 defects loosen the spectrin–actin junction. A patient often has an affected parent with the same incidental smear finding. Contrast with the X-linked (G6PD) and autosomal-recessive (pyruvate kinase) enzymopathies on the neighboring scenes.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "HE is inherited in an X-linked recessive pattern like G6PD." Discriminator: HE/SPTA1 is autosomal dominant — look for an affected parent across generations and equal sex distribution, not the male-predominant pedigree of G6PD.
3. % elliptocytes ≠ severity
The PERCENTAGE of elliptocytes on the smear does NOT predict clinical severity — a patient can have >50% elliptocytes and be hemolysis-free, while another with fewer cells hemolyzes. Severity is driven by the functional degree of skeletal destabilization (e.g., biallelic/compound-heterozygous states, the HPP variant) and by spleen function, not by the count. Reticulocyte count, LDH, haptoglobin, and bilirubin assess actual hemolysis far better than morphology alone.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "A higher fraction of elliptocytes on the peripheral smear means more severe disease and predicts the need for splenectomy." Discriminator: morphology percentage and clinical severity are decoupled in HE — use hemolysis markers (retics, LDH, haptoglobin), not the elliptocyte count, to gauge severity.
4. Mostly asymptomatic
Most HE is discovered incidentally on a smear or CBC and requires NO treatment — no folate, no transfusion, no splenectomy. Supportive folic acid (1 mg/day) is reserved for the minority with ongoing hemolysis to prevent megaloblastic crisis; transfusion and splenectomy are reserved for severe symptomatic disease or HPP. Reassurance and family screening are the main interventions for the typical asymptomatic carrier.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "Every patient with hereditary elliptocytosis should undergo prophylactic splenectomy." Discriminator: splenectomy is only for the severe/HPP minority; the asymptomatic majority needs reassurance and at most folate, and splenectomy carries lifelong encapsulated-organism and thrombosis risk.
5. HPP severe variant
Hereditary pyropoikilocytosis (HPP) is the severe BIALLELIC variant on the HE spectrum (homozygous or compound-heterozygous spectrin defects, often combined with the low-expression alpha-LELY allele). The smear shows bizarre microspherocytes, fragments, and poikilocytes that look like a thermal burn — RBCs fragment at 45–46°C (vs the normal ~49°C), hence "pyro" (heat-sensitive). It presents in infancy with marked hemolysis, microcytosis, and a low MCV. HPP is essentially HE that crossed into severe disease.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "The bizarre fragmented cells and thermal instability mean this is a microangiopathic process like TTP/DIC." Discriminator: HPP is a congenital biallelic spectrin defect with a positive family history of HE and abnormal thermal fragility — there are no schistocytes from fibrin strands, no thrombocytopenia, and the Coombs test is negative.
6. HPP → splenectomy
Because HPP and the severe end of HE produce transfusion-dependent hemolysis, splenectomy is the definitive treatment — it removes the principal site of extravascular RBC destruction and usually raises Hb and cuts transfusion need substantially. Pre-splenectomy give pneumococcal, meningococcal, and Hib vaccines (≥2 weeks prior) and ideally defer surgery past age 5–6 to reduce overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI). Note this is the OPPOSITE of the stomatocytosis disorders, where splenectomy is contraindicated.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "Splenectomy is contraindicated in HPP because of thrombosis risk, just as in dehydrated stomatocytosis." Discriminator: the thrombosis-related splenectomy contraindication applies to the stomatocytoses (DHS/xerocytosis), NOT to HE/HPP — in severe HE/HPP, splenectomy is beneficial and standard, with vaccination and lifelong OPSI awareness.
7. SAO malaria protection
Southeast Asian ovalocytosis (SAO) is caused by a 9-amino-acid (27 bp) in-frame deletion in band 3 / SLC4A1, producing rigid, oval, often stomatocytic RBCs. It is a balanced POLYMORPHISM, not a disease — the rigid membrane resists invasion by Plasmodium falciparum and vivax, giving heterozygotes malaria protection in Melanesia/Southeast Asia. Heterozygotes are usually asymptomatic with little or no hemolysis.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "SAO is a severe congenital hemolytic anemia requiring chronic transfusion." Discriminator: heterozygous SAO is a benign, often hemolysis-free polymorphism selected for malaria protection — the rigid cells are the very feature that blocks parasite invasion, not a marker of severe disease.
8. SAO homozygous lethal
The SAO band-3 deletion shows balanced polymorphism genetics: HETEROZYGOTES are benign and malaria-protected, but the HOMOZYGOUS state is lethal in utero (no functional band 3, the major anion exchanger and structural anchor). This is why SAO is essentially never seen in the homozygous form in living patients — selection maintains the allele only in carriers. Conceptually parallel to other balanced polymorphisms (sickle trait, thalassemia, G6PD) where the carrier state confers malaria resistance.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "Homozygous SAO produces the most malaria-resistant, healthiest individuals." Discriminator: homozygous SAO is embryonic-lethal — only the heterozygous (carrier) state is viable and malaria-protective, the classic signature of a balanced polymorphism maintained by heterozygote advantage.
9. Stomatocytosis wing
Stomatocytes are RBCs with a central slit-like (mouth-shaped) zone of pallor, caused by disorders of monovalent CATION (Na+/K+) permeability that shift cell water content. The two hereditary forms are dehydrated (DHS/xerocytosis, water out) and overhydrated (OHS, water in). Stomatocytes also appear as artifact and in acquired settings, classically alcoholic liver disease. The unifying board point: these are channelopathies, and splenectomy is dangerous in them.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "Stomatocytes on the smear are specific for hereditary spherocytosis." Discriminator: spherocytes (no central pallor) point to HS or warm autoimmune hemolysis; stomatocytes (mouth-shaped pallor) point to cation-permeability disorders (DHS/OHS) or acquired causes like alcoholic liver disease.
10. DHS — dehydrated (PIEZO1/Gardos)
Dehydrated hereditary stomatocytosis (DHS = hereditary xerocytosis) is autosomal dominant, caused most often by gain-of-function PIEZO1 mutations (a mechanosensitive cation channel) or KCNN4/Gardos-channel mutations. K+ and water LEAVE the cell, dehydrating it; the MCHC rises and cells appear macrocytic with stomatocytes/target cells. Hemolysis is usually mild, but iron overload is common. Critically, SPLENECTOMY IS CONTRAINDICATED because it markedly increases the risk of life-threatening venous thromboembolism and pulmonary hypertension.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "Splenectomy is the treatment of choice for dehydrated hereditary stomatocytosis, as it is for hereditary spherocytosis." Discriminator: in DHS/xerocytosis splenectomy is CONTRAINDICATED — post-splenectomy thrombosis and pulmonary hypertension are well documented. Distinguishing DHS from HS before any splenectomy is the entire point of this scene's central red X.
11. OHS — overhydrated (RHAG)
Overhydrated hereditary stomatocytosis (OHS) is the mirror image of DHS: mutations in RHAG (Rh-associated glycoprotein) cause excessive cation (Na+) and water INFLUX, overhydrating the cell so the MCHC falls and cells become large, fragile macrocytic stomatocytes with loss of the membrane protein stomatin. Hemolysis tends to be more pronounced than in DHS. Like DHS, OHS carries thrombosis risk and splenectomy is generally avoided.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "OHS is caused by a band-3 mutation and is safely cured by splenectomy." Discriminator: OHS is an RHAG cation-leak disorder (water IN, low MCHC), not a band-3 defect, and shares the stomatocytosis splenectomy-thrombosis caution — band 3 / SLC4A1 is the SAO and one HS mechanism, not OHS.
12. Splenectomy DANGER (skull)
The central skull-and-red-X is the scene's single highest-yield message: you MUST differentiate the cation-leak stomatocytoses (DHS/OHS) from hereditary spherocytosis before recommending splenectomy. In HS/HE/HPP splenectomy helps; in the stomatocytoses it triggers severe post-splenectomy thrombosis (DVT/PE, portal vein thrombosis) and pulmonary hypertension. Use MCHC and the smear (stomatocytes vs spherocytes), osmotic gradient ektacytometry, and family history to tell them apart.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "All hereditary membrane hemolytic anemias respond to and should be treated with splenectomy." Discriminator: splenectomy is beneficial in HS/severe HE/HPP but DANGEROUS in stomatocytosis (DHS/OHS) due to thrombosis — never offer splenectomy until you've excluded a cation-permeability disorder.
13. Other rare variants
Rare stomatocytosis-spectrum and shape disorders include cryohydrocytosis (cold-induced cation leak, SLC4A1), GLUT1 deficiency syndrome (SLC2A1 — stomatocytes PLUS a neurologic phenotype of seizures, developmental delay, low CSF glucose, treated with ketogenic diet), and abetalipoproteinemia (MTTP defect → fat malabsorption, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency, spinocerebellar degeneration, and ACANTHOCYTES, not stomatocytes). These are the "don't forget" tail of the museum.
⚠️ WRONG answer: "Acanthocytes (spur cells) and stomatocytes are interchangeable terms for the same membrane lesion." Discriminator: acanthocytes (irregular spicules — abetalipoproteinemia, severe liver disease, neuroacanthocytosis) are distinct from stomatocytes (mouth-shaped pallor — cation-leak disorders); GLUT1 deficiency uniquely couples a hematologic shape change with neurologic disease.
