📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Cardiac emergency
Hyperkalemia is a cardiac emergency because elevated extracellular K+ destabilizes the cardiac resting membrane potential, risking lethal arrhythmia. The ECG — not the absolute number — drives urgency: any ECG change or K+ >= 6.5 mandates immediate IV calcium. Disposition hinges on rate of rise and ECG, so get a STAT ECG and place the patient on a monitor first.
⚠️ WRONG to wait for a confirmatory repeat K+ before acting when the ECG already shows peaked T waves or wider changes — and equally WRONG to be reassured by a NORMAL ECG in CKD/ESRD, where the myocardium is chronically adapted and the ECG can stay normal even at K+ > 7. The discriminator: treat based on ECG plus clinical context, never on a single number in isolation.
2. Peaked T (5.5-6.5)
The earliest ECG change is tall, peaked/tented, narrow-based T waves, typically appearing at K+ 5.5-6.5 mM. They reflect accelerated repolarization from increased K+ conductance. This is the cue to start treatment even before higher-grade changes appear.
⚠️ WRONG to call any tall T wave hyperkalemia — hyperacute T waves of early STEMI are broad-based and asymmetric, whereas hyperkalemic T waves are narrow, symmetric, and tented. Discriminator: check the K+ and the base of the T wave; STEMI T waves come with reciprocal ST changes and a clinical ischemic story.
3. PR prolong / flat P (6.5-7.5)
At K+ ~6.5-7.5 mM the PR interval prolongs and the P wave flattens as atrial myocardium and AV conduction slow. This marks progression from 'early' to 'moderate-severe' hyperkalemia and increased urgency. IV calcium is indicated at this stage.
⚠️ WRONG to interpret PR prolongation here as primary first-degree AV block needing only observation. Discriminator: in the setting of a high K+, conduction slowing is the metabolic emergency — fix the potassium, and stabilize with calcium; do not simply 'monitor the block.'
4. Wide QRS (7-8)
At K+ ~7-8 mM the P wave disappears entirely (sinoventricular rhythm) and the QRS widens from progressive Na+ channel inactivation and slowed intraventricular conduction. A wide QRS in this setting is pre-arrhythmic — VF/asystole may be imminent. Give IV calcium immediately and prepare for repeat dosing.
⚠️ WRONG to treat a wide-complex rhythm here as a primary bundle branch block or VT and reach for antiarrhythmics. Discriminator: absent P waves + widening QRS + known/likely hyperkalemia = give calcium first; lidocaine/amiodarone will not fix the membrane and can worsen the situation.
5. Sine wave (>8)
At K+ > 8 mM the widened QRS merges with the T wave into a sinusoidal 'sine wave' pattern — the pre-terminal rhythm. This demands IV calcium NOW (calcium gluconate 1-2 g, or chloride via central line) plus simultaneous K+-lowering therapy. Without immediate calcium, this degenerates to VF or asystole within minutes.
⚠️ WRONG to prioritize insulin/dextrose or dialysis access before giving calcium when a sine wave is present. Discriminator: calcium is the only agent that acts in 1-3 min to stabilize the membrane; shift and removal therapies are too slow to prevent imminent arrest.
6. VF / asystole
The terminal rhythm of untreated hyperkalemia is ventricular fibrillation or asystole. In cardiac arrest with suspected/known hyperkalemia, give empiric IV calcium plus high-dose shift therapy during CPR. Hyperkalemia is one of the reversible 'H's of arrest and must be treated empirically.
⚠️ WRONG to run a standard ACLS arrest algorithm without empiric calcium when hyperkalemia is suspected (dialysis patient, crush injury, missed dialysis). Discriminator: in arrest, do not wait for a lab value — give IV calcium and treat the reversible cause concurrently with standard ACLS.
7. Resting membrane up
High extracellular K+ reduces the K+ gradient across the membrane, raising (making less negative) the resting membrane potential toward threshold per the Nernst equation. Initially this makes cells MORE excitable, but sustained depolarization then inactivates voltage-gated Na+ channels. This biphasic effect explains both early excitability and later conduction failure.
⚠️ WRONG to assume that a 'less negative' resting potential means the heart simply fires more easily and is therefore more robust. Discriminator: persistent partial depolarization holds Na+ channels in the inactivated state, so net conduction SLOWS — the dominant danger is failed conduction, not hyperexcitability.
8. Na channel inactivation
Sustained membrane depolarization locks voltage-gated Na+ channels in their inactivated state, reducing phase-0 upstroke velocity and slowing conduction. This is the mechanistic basis for PR prolongation, P-wave loss, and QRS widening. IV calcium works by raising the threshold potential, restoring the gap between resting and threshold and re-enabling Na+ channel availability.
⚠️ WRONG to think calcium lowers serum K+ to fix conduction. Discriminator: calcium acts purely electrophysiologically — it shifts threshold to restore excitability/conduction within minutes, while serum K+ stays elevated until shift/removal therapy works.
9. IV calcium stabilizes
IV calcium is the first drug given in any hyperkalemic emergency: calcium gluconate 1-2 g (10 mL of 10%) IV, or calcium chloride via central access (3x the elemental calcium, more sclerosing). It antagonizes the membrane effects of K+ by raising threshold potential, restoring the resting-to-threshold gap. Onset is 1-3 minutes; redose if ECG changes persist after 5 minutes.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER on boards: 'calcium gluconate lowers potassium.' Calcium does NOT lower K+ at all — it only stabilizes the myocardium (buys time). Discriminator: pair calcium (membrane protection) with insulin+glucose / albuterol (shift) and dialysis / kayexalate-patiromer / loop diuretics (removal) — these three roles are distinct and tested.
10. Onset 1-3 min
Calcium's protective effect begins in 1-3 minutes and lasts only ~30-60 minutes, so it must be repeated if ECG changes recur and bridged with definitive K+-lowering therapy. Because it is transient, calcium alone never constitutes complete treatment. Always co-administer shift agents (insulin/glucose, albuterol) and arrange removal (dialysis, GI binders, diuresis).
⚠️ WRONG to give calcium, see the ECG improve, and then stop active management. Discriminator: the short duration means K+ will re-injure the membrane once calcium wears off unless shift and removal are underway — calcium buys a 30-60 minute window, not a cure.
11. Pseudo-Brugada
Severe hyperkalemia can produce a coved ST-elevation pattern in V1-V2 that mimics Brugada — termed pseudo-Brugada or a Brugada phenocopy. Correcting the potassium fully resolves the ECG pattern. Recognizing this prevents misdiagnosis as the inherited channelopathy.
⚠️ WRONG ANSWER: diagnosing true Brugada syndrome and referring for an ICD based on this ECG. Discriminator: true Brugada is a genetic SCN5A Na-channel disease that persists regardless of electrolytes; pseudo-Brugada normalizes once K+ is corrected — always check and treat the K+ first.
12. Ascending paralysis
Beyond the heart, hyperkalemia can cause secondary hyperkalemic paralysis: ascending muscle weakness from sustained depolarization blocking skeletal Na+ channels. It typically begins in the legs and ascends, sparing cranial nerves and sensation. Weakness can progress to flaccid paralysis and respiratory compromise.
⚠️ WRONG to anchor on Guillain-Barre for an ascending paralysis without checking the potassium. Discriminator: GBS shows areflexia with albuminocytologic dissociation on LP, while hyperkalemic paralysis has a high serum K+ that resolves with treatment — always send electrolytes in acute ascending weakness.
13. Familial HyperKPP
Familial hyperkalemic periodic paralysis is an autosomal dominant SCN4A skeletal Na-channel mutation causing episodic weakness triggered by rest after exercise, potassium-rich meals, or cold. Attacks are short (minutes to hours) and may be aborted with carbohydrate/mild exercise. Serum K+ rises during attacks but may be only mildly elevated or normal.
⚠️ WRONG to confuse this with HYPOkalemic periodic paralysis (CACNA1S/SCN4A), which is triggered by carbohydrate loads and insulin and is treated by giving potassium. Discriminator: in HyperKPP attacks K+ rises and carbohydrates help; in HypoKPP attacks K+ falls and carbohydrates trigger — the trigger and direction of K+ change distinguish them.
14. NAGMA backroom
Hyperkalemia and acidosis are linked: in the distal tubule, K+ secretion competes with H+ secretion, so high K+ impairs acid excretion and can produce a normal-anion-gap metabolic acidosis (classically Type 4 RTA). Hyperkalemia also suppresses renal ammoniagenesis, blunting acid excretion further. This bidirectional relationship is high-yield.
⚠️ WRONG to assume metabolic acidosis with hyperkalemia is always a high-anion-gap process like DKA. Discriminator: hyperkalemia-associated Type 4 RTA is a NORMAL-anion-gap (hyperchloremic) acidosis from hypoaldosteronism — calculate the anion gap and check aldosterone status to separate it from gap acidoses.
15. Treat-now threshold
Definitive treat-now triggers: serum K+ >= 6.5 mM OR ANY ECG change at any K+ level. Treatment proceeds in three parallel arms — stabilize (IV calcium), shift (insulin 10 units + 25 g dextrose, albuterol 10-20 mg neb), and remove (dialysis for refractory/ESRD, loop diuretics if making urine, GI binders patiromer/sodium zirconium). Mnemonic 'C BIG K Drop': Calcium, Bicarb (if acidotic), Insulin/Glucose, Kayexalate/binders, Diuretics/Dialysis.
⚠️ WRONG to defer treatment until the patient becomes symptomatic or until a higher K+ returns. Discriminator: do not wait for symptoms — K+ >= 6.5 OR any ECG change is the action line; symptoms (weakness, arrhythmia) are late and may be the arrest itself.
