The discriminator: SIADH requires normal thyroid/adrenal function and no recent thiazide.
  2. Order of steps — WRONG: starting with volume status (the classic textbook tree) before checking osmolality. If you skip the osmometer you can mistreat isotonic pseudohyponatremia or hypertonic hyperglycemic hyponatremia as if it were true hypotonic hyponatremia.

It will not correct and can precipitate adrenal crisis — the answer is glucocorticoid replacement (hydrocortisone). 📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Work the algorithm
Hyponatremia (Na <135) demands a fixed stepwise workup, because SIADH is a diagnosis of EXCLUSION. The sequence is: serum osmolality → exclude mimics (pseudohyponatremia, hyperglycemia) → urine osmolality → urine sodium → clinical volume status. Each step narrows the differential; jumping straight to SIADH skips treatable mimics like adrenal insufficiency and hypothyroidism.
⚠️ WRONG: labeling a euvolemic hyponatremic patient "SIADH" on first glance. SIADH cannot be diagnosed until hypothyroidism, adrenal/glucocorticoid insufficiency, thiazides, and low-solute states are excluded. The discriminator: SIADH requires normal thyroid/adrenal function and no recent thiazide.
2. Order of steps
Memorize the order: serum OSM → MIMICS → urine OSM → urine Na → VOLUME. Osmolality first sorts hypotonic (true) from isotonic (pseudo) and hypertonic (glucose/mannitol). Urine osm then tells you whether ADH is on (>100) or off (<100), urine Na localizes renal vs extrarenal, and volume status finalizes the category and treatment.
⚠️ WRONG: starting with volume status (the classic textbook tree) before checking osmolality. If you skip the osmometer you can mistreat isotonic pseudohyponatremia or hypertonic hyperglycemic hyponatremia as if it were true hypotonic hyponatremia.
3. Osmometer step 1
Step 1 is always measured serum osmolality (normal ~275–295 mOsm/kg). LOW osm (<275) = true hypotonic hyponatremia, the only category needing the full volume-status algorithm. NORMAL osm = isotonic pseudohyponatremia (lab artifact). HIGH osm (>295) = hypertonic hyponatremia (hyperglycemia, mannitol) where osmotically active solute pulls water out of cells, diluting Na.
⚠️ WRONG: treating every low Na as water excess. If serum osm is normal or high, the problem is not free-water retention — it is a lipid/protein artifact or an osmotic solute. Measure osmolality before committing to any treatment.
4. Pseudo (isotonic)
Isotonic pseudohyponatremia is a laboratory artifact of older flame-photometry/indirect-ISE methods caused by severe hyperlipidemia (high triglycerides/cholesterol) or paraproteinemia (multiple myeloma, Waldenström). The solid phase displaces plasma water; serum osmolality is NORMAL and there is no true sodium or water disturbance.
⚠️ WRONG: aggressively correcting a "sodium of 128" in a myeloma or hypertriglyceridemia patient with a normal serum osm. There is no real hyponatremia to treat — the fix is to recognize the artifact (or use a direct ISE/whole-blood gas analyzer), not to give hypertonic saline.
5. Hyperglycemia +1.6/2.4
Hyperglycemia causes hypertonic (translocational) hyponatremia: glucose draws water into the vascular space, diluting Na. Correct measured Na UP by 1.6 mEq/L (or ~2.4 above glucose ~400) for every 100 mg/dL glucose over 100. Serum osm is HIGH. Treating the hyperglycemia restores Na.
⚠️ WRONG: giving hypertonic saline for the "Na 126" in a DKA patient with glucose 600. The corrected Na is actually ~134 (near normal); the real treatment is insulin and fluids for the glucose. Forgetting the correction leads to dangerous overtreatment.
6. Cortisol mimic
Glucocorticoid (cortisol) deficiency — primary adrenal insufficiency or secondary/central hypopituitarism — causes euvolemic hyponatremia. Low cortisol removes feedback inhibition of CRH/ADH and impairs free-water excretion, mimicking SIADH. Check a morning cortisol and/or cosyntropin stimulation test before diagnosing SIADH.
⚠️ WRONG: managing adrenal-insufficiency hyponatremia with fluid restriction like SIADH. It will not correct and can precipitate adrenal crisis — the answer is glucocorticoid replacement (hydrocortisone). Primary adrenal insufficiency also gives high K + low Na, a clue SIADH never produces.
7. TSH mimic
Severe hypothyroidism (especially myxedema) causes euvolemic hyponatremia via reduced cardiac output and GFR with non-osmotic ADH release. It is a mandatory SIADH mimic to exclude — order TSH and free T4. Unlike adrenal insufficiency, potassium is NORMAL in hypothyroid hyponatremia.
⚠️ WRONG: diagnosing SIADH without thyroid studies. A myxedematous patient's hyponatremia responds to thyroid hormone replacement, not to fluid restriction alone; SIADH requires documented euthyroid status.
8. K high/Na low = adrenal
The combination of LOW sodium + HIGH potassium (± metabolic acidosis, hypotension, hyperpigmentation) points to PRIMARY adrenal insufficiency (Addison) from combined gluco- and mineralocorticoid (aldosterone) loss. SIADH never causes hyperkalemia — it is a pure water problem with normal K.
⚠️ WRONG: calling hyperkalemic hyponatremia SIADH. The hyperkalemia is the giveaway for aldosterone deficiency (Addison's) — order cortisol/cosyntropin and give hydrocortisone, do not fluid-restrict.
9. Low urate = SIADH
SIADH characteristically lowers serum uric acid (<4 mg/dL) through dilution and ADH/volume-mediated uricosuria. A low urate supports SIADH; a HIGH urate (>5–6) argues for volume depletion/hypovolemia, where the kidney avidly reabsorbs urate. This is a high-yield discriminator between euvolemic SIADH and hypovolemic hyponatremia.
⚠️ WRONG: ignoring uric acid when distinguishing SIADH from hypovolemia. An elevated urate in a hyponatremic patient should steer you AWAY from SIADH and toward volume depletion (and away from fluid restriction toward isotonic saline).
10. Urine osm gauge
After confirming hypotonicity, urine osmolality reveals ADH activity. Uosm <100 mOsm/kg = ADH appropriately OFF, dilute urine (primary polydipsia, low-solute beer potomania/tea-and-toast). Uosm >100 = ADH ON, inappropriately concentrated urine (SIADH, hypovolemia, edematous states, thiazides).
⚠️ WRONG: diagnosing SIADH with a maximally dilute urine. If Uosm is <100, ADH is suppressed and the problem is excess water intake or low solute — SIADH requires INAPPROPRIATELY concentrated urine (>100) despite hypotonic plasma.
11. Dry / primary polydipsia
Primary (psychogenic) polydipsia: massive water intake (often >10 L/day, classically in psychiatric patients) overwhelms renal free-water excretion. ADH is correctly suppressed, so urine is maximally dilute (Uosm <100, often <50–60). Treatment is water restriction; correction can be rapid (autocorrection risk).
⚠️ WRONG: fluid restriction is correct, but beware OVER-rapid autocorrection. Once water intake stops, the kidney rapidly dumps free water and Na can rise too fast — risking osmotic demyelination. Monitor closely and consider DDAVP clamp if Na overcorrects.
12. Euvolemic mid Uosm
A clinically euvolemic patient (no edema, no dryness/orthostasis) with inappropriately concentrated urine (Uosm >100), urine Na >30, low uric acid, and normal thyroid/adrenal function = SIADH. It is the endpoint of the euvolemic arm AFTER all mimics are excluded.
⚠️ WRONG: equating "euvolemic + concentrated urine" with SIADH without finishing the exclusion. Hypothyroidism, glucocorticoid deficiency, thiazides, and reset osmostat can all look identical — the diagnosis is only secure once these are ruled out.
13. Urine Na step
Urine sodium localizes the lesion. Una <20 mEq/L = kidney conserving Na = extrarenal/hypovolemic loss (vomiting, diarrhea, third-spacing) or edematous states (HF, cirrhosis). Una >30–40 = renal Na leak (SIADH, diuretics, salt-wasting nephropathy, adrenal insufficiency, RTA).
⚠️ WRONG: interpreting urine Na in a patient on diuretics. Diuretics force renal Na loss and make Una high regardless of the true cause, masking hypovolemia. Always check the medication list and ideally interpret Una off diuretics.
14. Thiazide mimic 1-2wk
Thiazides (not loops) classically cause a euvolemic, SIADH-indistinguishable hyponatremia: they impair urinary dilution at the distal tubule while sparing concentrating ability, and frequently affect elderly women within weeks of starting. Management: STOP the thiazide and recheck Na in 1–2 weeks before labeling SIADH.
⚠️ WRONG: diagnosing SIADH in a patient currently on a thiazide. The thiazide must be discontinued and the patient reassessed — many "SIADH" cases resolve completely once the drug is stopped. Loops, by contrast, impair concentrating ability and rarely cause hyponatremia.
15. Beer / low solute
Low-solute states — beer potomania and tea-and-toast diet — cause hyponatremia from inadequate dietary solute (protein/salt) limiting free-water excretion despite suppressed ADH. Labs show LOW Uosm (<100–200) and LOW Una. Correction is with solute/protein (and saline if depleted); free water dumps rapidly once solute is given.
⚠️ WRONG: confusing low-solute hyponatremia with SIADH and fluid-restricting hard. Uosm is LOW here (ADH off), so the mechanism is solute-limited excretion. Beware rapid autocorrection/overcorrection once the patient eats — restrict the rate of Na rise to avoid osmotic demyelination.
