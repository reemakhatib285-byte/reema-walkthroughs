📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Normal serum protein electrophoresis
Normal SPEP shows a tall albumin peak then a broad, low polyclonal gamma region. Serves as the baseline against which spikes are read.
⚠️ A normal gamma region is broad and low — any tall, narrow gamma peak is the abnormality to chase.
2. Polyclonal gammopathy
A broad-based gamma elevation = polyclonal, from many B-cell clones. Seen in infection, inflammation, chronic liver disease, autoimmune disease — NOT a malignant plasma-cell clone.
⚠️ A broad polyclonal hump is reactive/benign; only a sharp narrow spike (monoclonal) suggests plasma-cell malignancy.
3. Monoclonal spike (M-protein)
A monoclonal spike is a tall, narrow band ('church spire') from a single plasma-cell clone making identical immunoglobulin. Detectable on SPEP when ≥0.5 g/dL and ~10^9 cells.
⚠️ One narrow spike = monoclonal = clonal plasma-cell process; the spire shape, not the height alone, defines monoclonality.
4. Immunofixation — confirms monoclonality
Immunofixation identifies the exact heavy- and light-chain class (e.g., IgG-kappa) and confirms a band is truly monoclonal. More specific than SPEP, which only quantifies.
⚠️ SPEP detects/quantifies the spike; immunofixation TYPES it. You need IFE to confirm clonality and class — SPEP alone isn't enough.
5. 24-hour urine / UPEP (Bence Jones)
Urine studies detect free light chains (Bence Jones protein). A 24-hour UPEP quantifies urinary M-protein; a threshold of ~500 mg/24h is a meaningful marker for light-chain disease.
⚠️ Standard urine dipstick misses Bence Jones light chains — you need UPEP/immunofixation on a 24-hour collection.
6. Free light chain (FLC) assay
The serum free light-chain assay measures kappa and lambda; an abnormal kappa:lambda RATIO signals clonality. Catches 'light-chain only' and oligosecretory/nonsecretory disease missed by SPEP.
⚠️ FLC ratio (not absolute level) defines clonality, and it catches light-chain-only myeloma that SPEP/IFE can miss.
7. Conditions making M-protein without PC tumor
Lymphoid (CLL, lymphoma), non-neoplastic (chronic infection, autoimmune), and other clonal disorders can produce M-components without being a plasma-cell tumor. Context dictates work-up.
⚠️ An M-protein does NOT equal myeloma — lymphoproliferative and reactive conditions also make monoclonal proteins.
8. M-spike threshold for detection
SPEP reliably detects M-proteins at ≥0.5 g/dL, corresponding to roughly 10^9 clonal cells. Smaller clones need the more sensitive FLC assay or immunofixation.
⚠️ A 'negative' SPEP doesn't exclude clonal disease below ~0.5 g/dL — escalate to IFE/FLC when suspicion is high.
9. Tumor burden marker
The M-protein quantity tracks tumor burden and is used serially to monitor disease and therapy response in myeloma and related disorders.
⚠️ Use the SAME measured M-protein (serum spike or involved FLC) consistently for monitoring — don't switch metrics mid-course.
10. Therapy monitoring
Falling M-protein/FLC indicates response; rising values indicate progression. Response criteria depend on serial quantitative measurement.
⚠️ Response is judged on serial quantitative change, not a single value; nonsecretory disease must be followed by FLC or imaging.
11. Screening limitation
These assays are diagnostic/monitoring tools, not population screening tests — they're not specific enough to screen asymptomatic people for plasma-cell disease.
⚠️ Don't use SPEP/FLC to screen asymptomatic patients; order them when there is a clinical indication (CRAB, neuropathy, etc.).
12. IgM threshold context
Specific thresholds (e.g., urinary light chain ≥500 mg/24h, or IgM spike levels) help stratify monoclonal gammopathies and trigger further work-up.
⚠️ Thresholds differ by isotype and fluid — apply the correct cutoff for serum vs 24-hour urine, IgG vs IgM.
13. Kappa vs lambda restriction
A clone overproduces ONE light chain, skewing the kappa:lambda ratio. The skew direction identifies the involved clone and is central to FLC interpretation.
⚠️ It's the RATIO skew (not just a high absolute light chain, which also rises in renal failure) that indicates clonality.
14. Quantitative vs qualitative methods
SPEP gives quantitative spike size; immunofixation and FLC give qualitative/typing and sensitive detection. Together they define presence, type, and burden.
⚠️ Quantitation (SPEP) and characterization (IFE) answer different questions — boards expect you to order both appropriately.
15. One clone with a megaphone
The visual mnemonic: a single clone loudly overproducing identical immunoglobulin = monoclonal gammopathy. Contrasts with the quiet, diverse polyclonal crowd.
⚠️ Monoclonality = one loud identical product; polyclonal = many different quiet ones. The shape on SPEP reflects this.
