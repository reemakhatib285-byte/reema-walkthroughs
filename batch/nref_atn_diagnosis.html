📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Dx = creatinine + sediment, NOT UOP
AKI/ATN is diagnosed by rising creatinine plus the urine sediment (muddy brown granular casts, RTE cells) — NOT by urine output. Both oliguric and non-oliguric ATN exist; non-oliguric carries a better prognosis.
⚠️ Excluding ATN because urine output is normal — non-oliguric ATN is common; rely on creatinine trend and sediment.
2. No oliguria ≠ no ATN
Maintained urine output does NOT rule out tubular necrosis. Non-oliguric ATN (UOP >400–500 mL/day) is frequent with nephrotoxins (aminoglycosides, contrast) and has lower mortality and less need for dialysis.
⚠️ Calling preserved UOP 'prerenal' or 'not AKI' — the sediment and creatinine define ATN regardless of output.
3. Recovery in 24–48h (not always)
Prerenal azotemia reverses within 24–48h of restoring volume; ATN does NOT — it persists for days to weeks. Failure to improve after a fluid challenge points away from prerenal toward established tubular injury.
⚠️ Expecting ATN to bounce back in 1–2 days like prerenal — ATN follows a prolonged maintenance phase before recovery.
4. Left bed: low UO but normal sediment
Low urine output alone, with a bland sediment and a high BUN/Cr ratio, suggests prerenal physiology, not ATN. UOP is a poor discriminator without the sediment context.
⚠️ Diagnosing ATN from oliguria alone — a bland sediment with low UOP is more consistent with prerenal azotemia.
5. Right bed: 'still ATN' with normal UO
A patient with normal urine output can still have ATN — muddy brown casts and a rising creatinine confirm injury. This is the non-oliguric ATN pattern, classically from aminoglycosides.
⚠️ Reassuring on 'normal' urine output — normal UOP with rising Cr and granular casts is non-oliguric ATN.
6. Aminoglycoside — delayed, non-oliguric
Aminoglycosides cause classic non-oliguric ATN via proximal tubular accumulation. Injury is dose/duration related, often appears 5–10 days in, and Cr keeps rising even after the drug is stopped. Also causes hypomagnesemia/hypokalemia.
⚠️ Assuming the kidney recovers as soon as the drug is stopped — aminoglycoside ATN worsens for days after discontinuation.
7. 'Still here even after you stopped me'
Aminoglycoside nephrotoxicity has a delayed, persistent course: tubular cells accumulated the drug and continue to die after cessation, so creatinine peaks late. Recovery follows once tubules regenerate.
⚠️ Misattributing the late Cr rise to a new insult — it is the expected lag of aminoglycoside tubular toxicity.
8. Recovery calendar: onset phase (days 2–3)
ATN evolves through phases: initiation/onset (the insult, days 1–3), then maintenance. The onset phase is when GFR falls and the diagnosis is made; intervention here is limited to removing the insult and supportive care.
⚠️ Expecting reversal during the onset phase — damage is already done; the goal is preventing further injury.
9. Maintenance phase (days ~7–14)
The maintenance phase is the prolonged plateau of low GFR (often 1–2 weeks) where complications occur: hyperkalemia, acidosis, volume overload, uremia. Most dialysis needs arise here. UOP may be low or normal.
⚠️ Mistaking the maintenance plateau for treatment failure — it is the expected protracted course of ATN before recovery.
10. Recovery / polyuric phase
Recovery brings tubular regeneration and a polyuric phase (tubules regain GFR before reabsorptive capacity), risking volume depletion and hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia. Creatinine then trends down toward baseline.
⚠️ Overlooking electrolyte losses in the polyuric recovery phase — patients can become hypokalemic and volume-depleted.
11. Normal monitor framing
A 'normal' vital/output display reinforces that ATN can present without obvious red flags. Diagnosis rests on the laboratory creatinine trajectory and microscopic sediment, which the bedside numbers may not reveal.
⚠️ Trusting a normal-appearing bedside picture — order a creatinine and urine micro to catch non-oliguric ATN.
12. Rising creatinine trend graphs
Serial creatinine rise (KDIGO: ↑≥0.3 mg/dL in 48h or ≥1.5x baseline) defines AKI. Trending Cr — not a single value — establishes the diagnosis and stages severity alongside urine output criteria.
⚠️ Using one creatinine value — AKI staging requires the change over time; a single 'normal-ish' Cr can miss early injury.
