📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Left-sided surgery ~25-40%
Roughly 25–40% of patients with left-sided (mitral/aortic) IE require valve surgery, most often during the index admission rather than after completing antibiotics. The decision is driven by complications, not by a fixed rule, and is made jointly by a multidisciplinary 'endocarditis team' (ID, cardiology, cardiac surgery). Left-sided disease has higher surgical rates and mortality than right-sided.
⚠️ It is tempting to assume all IE is medical and surgery is rare. The discriminator: a SUBSTANTIAL minority (25–40%) of left-sided cases need surgery, and the most common reason is heart failure from valve destruction — recognizing the indication early is what is tested.
2. Red operations board — CHF / shock
Heart failure due to acute valvular regurgitation (or, less often, obstruction) is the STRONGEST and MOST COMMON indication for surgery in IE. Acute severe aortic or mitral regurgitation produces pulmonary edema and cardiogenic shock that medical therapy cannot reverse, so valve repair/replacement is required, sometimes emergently (same day) when there is refractory pulmonary edema or shock.
⚠️ A stem with a large vegetation may tempt you to pick 'vegetation size' as the top surgical indication. The discriminator: HEART FAILURE from valve dysfunction — not vegetation size alone — is the leading and most urgent indication for surgery in IE.
3. Red board — Paravalvular extension / abscess
Locally invasive (perivalvular) infection — annular or aortic-root ABSCESS, fistula, prosthetic dehiscence, or NEW heart block — reflects uncontrolled infection that antibiotics cannot eradicate and is a clear surgical indication. New AV block on ECG suggests root-abscess extension into the conduction system; TEE is the test of choice to detect abscess.
⚠️ New first-degree or higher AV block in an IE patient may be dismissed as benign or managed with a pacemaker alone. The discriminator: new conduction block signals a PARAVALVULAR ABSCESS — it is an indication for TEE and surgery, not just rhythm support.
4. Red board — Persistent infection / No antibiotics work
Persistent infection — ongoing fever and positive blood cultures after 5–7 days of appropriate antibiotics (having excluded metastatic abscess and drug fever) — and organisms not reliably cured by antimicrobials (fungi, multidrug-resistant organisms, Brucella, Coxiella) are surgical indications. Fungal IE (Candida, Aspergillus) essentially ALWAYS needs surgery plus prolonged/lifelong suppressive antifungals.
⚠️ Fungal endocarditis tempts the answer of antifungal therapy alone. The discriminator: fungal IE almost always REQUIRES valve surgery in addition to antifungals — medical therapy alone is inadequate and relapse/embolization is the rule.
5. Vegetation ≥85% embolic / size threshold
Large, mobile vegetations carry the highest embolic risk; vegetation >10 mm, especially on the anterior mitral leaflet or with a prior embolic event ON appropriate antibiotics, supports EARLY surgery to prevent (re-)embolization. Embolic risk is highest in the first days–2 weeks and falls sharply once effective antibiotics are underway, so isolated large vegetation size alone is a softer (relative) indication than HF or abscess.
⚠️ A large vegetation with NO other complication tempts immediate surgery. The discriminator: vegetation size is a RELATIVE indication — strongest when ≥10 mm WITH a recurrent embolic event despite antibiotics; size alone, absent emboli or HF, may still be managed medically as embolic risk drops on therapy.
6. Prosthetic vs native survival comparison
Prosthetic-valve endocarditis (PVE) carries far higher mortality (~20–40%, with some early-PVE figures higher) and a much greater need for surgery than native-valve endocarditis (NVE, ~10–20%). Early PVE (<1 year post-op) is typically nosocomial staphylococcal with biofilm on foreign material; late PVE resembles community-acquired NVE. Both bio- and mechanical prostheses are at risk.
⚠️ A bioprosthetic valve may be assumed 'safer/lower risk' than mechanical for IE. The discriminator: the risk of PVE is similar for bioprosthetic and mechanical valves, and ANY prosthetic-valve IE has substantially worse outcomes than native-valve disease — prosthetic material plus biofilm is the dominant risk driver.
7. Timing chart — Emergent / Urgent / Elective
Surgical urgency is tiered. EMERGENT (same day, <24 h): refractory pulmonary edema or cardiogenic shock from acute regurgitation/valve obstruction. URGENT (within days, 1–2 d to a week): perivalvular abscess, persistent infection, large vegetation with recurrent emboli, prosthetic dehiscence. ELECTIVE/SCHEDULED: stable patients with a firm indication who can complete more antibiotics first.
⚠️ All surgical indications may be treated as equally urgent. The discriminator: only refractory HF/shock mandates EMERGENT same-day surgery; abscess and persistent infection are URGENT (days), and stable indications can be ELECTIVE — matching the indication to the correct urgency tier is the tested point.
8. Rarely-surgery / medical >50% board trap
Despite the long surgical-indication list, the MAJORITY of IE (>50%) is still cured with antibiotics alone; surgery is reserved for the specific complications above. Right-sided (tricuspid, IVDU) IE in particular usually responds to medical therapy without operation unless there is persistent infection, large vegetations with recurrent pulmonary emboli, or right heart failure.
⚠️ After memorizing surgical indications, the trap is reflexively recommending surgery for any IE. The discriminator: most endocarditis — and especially right-sided IVDU tricuspid disease — is managed MEDICALLY; over-calling surgery when no hard indication is present is the error.
9. Neuro timing — ischemic stroke, early OK
After a SMALL or asymptomatic ischemic (non-hemorrhagic) stroke or TIA, valve surgery — if otherwise indicated — can proceed EARLY/promptly without significant added neurologic risk, because cardiopulmonary bypass anticoagulation does not meaningfully worsen a bland infarct. Delaying surgery in these patients risks further embolization and progressive cardiac deterioration.
⚠️ Any stroke may be taken as a blanket reason to postpone heart surgery. The discriminator: a small/non-hemorrhagic ISCHEMIC stroke is NOT a reason to delay otherwise-indicated valve surgery — early operation is appropriate; only hemorrhage (or extensive infarct) mandates delay.
10. Neuro timing — hemorrhage, delay surgery
Intracranial HEMORRHAGE (hemorrhagic stroke or ruptured mycotic aneurysm) warrants DELAYING cardiac surgery, classically by about 3–4 weeks, because heparinization for cardiopulmonary bypass can extend the bleed catastrophically. A large ischemic infarct with hemorrhagic transformation risk is also a reason to delay.
⚠️ An IE patient with HF AND an intracranial bleed tempts immediate combined surgery. The discriminator: after intracranial HEMORRHAGE, DELAY valve surgery ~3–4 weeks (balanced against cardiac urgency) — operating immediately on bypass anticoagulation risks fatal rebleeding.
11. Mycotic aneurysm — treat aneurysm first
A large, enlarging, or symptomatic/ruptured mycotic (infectious) aneurysm should be treated FIRST — by endovascular coiling or surgical clipping — before cardiac surgery that requires bypass anticoagulation, to prevent intraoperative rupture. Unruptured small mycotic aneurysms may regress with antibiotics and can be monitored with serial imaging.
⚠️ The order of operations is the trap: doing valve surgery before securing a symptomatic mycotic aneurysm. The discriminator: secure the ANEURYSM FIRST, THEN proceed to heart surgery — anticoagulating on bypass with an unsecured aneurysm risks rupture.
12. Prophylaxis gatehouse — Dental only
Antibiotic prophylaxis is now restricted to HIGH-RISK patients undergoing DENTAL procedures that manipulate gingival tissue, the periapical region, or perforate the oral mucosa (and certain invasive respiratory-tract procedures). High-risk = prosthetic valve or prosthetic repair material, PRIOR IE, unrepaired cyanotic congenital heart disease (or repaired with residual defects/within 6 months), and cardiac transplant valvulopathy.
⚠️ Mitral valve prolapse or bicuspid aortic valve patients are often wrongly given prophylaxis. The discriminator: prophylaxis is ONLY for the defined high-risk lesions (prosthetic valve, prior IE, specific congenital disease, transplant valvulopathy) and ONLY for dental/oral-mucosal procedures — MVP, bicuspid valve, and rheumatic disease do NOT qualify.
13. Amoxicillin 2g PO / Cephalexin allergy alternative
When indicated, prophylaxis is a SINGLE dose 30–60 minutes before the procedure: amoxicillin 2 g PO (ampicillin 2 g IV/IM if NPO). For penicillin allergy: cephalexin 2 g PO (if no anaphylaxis history) OR azithromycin/clarithromycin 500 mg; doxycycline 100 mg is now a listed alternative in updated AHA guidance.
⚠️ A penicillin-allergic patient may be given cephalexin even with a history of anaphylaxis. The discriminator: cephalosporins are acceptable for MILD penicillin allergy but are AVOIDED with a history of anaphylaxis/angioedema to penicillin — use a macrolide or doxycycline instead.
14. Clindamycin NO LONGER board trap
Clindamycin has been REMOVED from endocarditis prophylaxis recommendations (2021 AHA update) because of its disproportionate risk of adverse effects, particularly Clostridioides difficile colitis, relative to other oral alternatives. The accepted penicillin-allergy options are now cephalexin (non-anaphylactic), azithromycin/clarithromycin, or doxycycline.
⚠️ An older-vintage stem still lists clindamycin as the penicillin-allergy prophylaxis choice. The discriminator: clindamycin is NO LONGER recommended for IE prophylaxis (C. difficile risk) — the current allergy alternatives are cephalexin, a macrolide, or doxycycline.
15. Sports scoreboard — mortality/organism stats
Prognosis stratifies by organism, valve type, and host. BEST: viridans streptococcal native-valve IE (low mortality). WORST: S. aureus IE, prosthetic-valve IE, fungal IE, and IE complicated by heart failure, perivalvular abscess, or stroke. Overall in-hospital mortality is roughly 15–30%, climbing higher for early PVE and S. aureus disease. Poor-prognosis markers drive both surgical urgency and counseling.
⚠️ All IE may be assumed to carry similar risk. The discriminator: outcome is organism- and valve-dependent — viridans-strep NVE does WELL, while S. aureus, fungal, and prosthetic-valve disease (especially with HF or abscess) carry markedly HIGHER mortality and a greater need for surgery.
