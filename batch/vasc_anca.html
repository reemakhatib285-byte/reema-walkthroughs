📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. GPA — c-ANCA/PR3
Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (formerly Wegener's) is a pauci-immune, granulomatous, necrotizing small-vessel vasculitis. It is c-ANCA / anti-PR3 (proteinase-3) positive in ~80-90% of generalized disease, lower in limited disease. The classic board triad is upper airway + lower airway (lung) + kidney (RPGN). Mnemonic: GPA = 'Granulomas, PR3, Airways.'
⚠️ Vignette shows saddle nose + cavitary lung nodules + crescentic GN, and the answer offers 'MPO/p-ANCA' — wrong; PR3/c-ANCA is the GPA serology. MPO/p-ANCA points to MPA or EGPA, which lack granulomas and ENT destruction. The discriminator is granulomatous inflammation + upper-airway destruction = PR3.
2. GPA sinus / nose
GPA upper-airway disease: chronic refractory sinusitis, bloody nasal crusting, cartilage destruction producing saddle-nose deformity, subglottic stenosis (can cause stridor/airway compromise), retro-orbital pseudotumor with proptosis, and serous otitis media with conductive hearing loss. ENT involvement occurs in up to 90% and is often the presenting feature. Subglottic stenosis is treated locally (dilation/steroid injection) because it responds poorly to systemic immunosuppression.
⚠️ ENT destruction with saddle nose can be mistaken for cocaine/levamisole-induced midline destructive lesions, which also give high-titer ANCA. The discriminator: ask about intranasal cocaine and check anti-human neutrophil elastase (HNE) antibodies, which are positive in cocaine-induced disease but not true GPA.
3. GPA lung nodules / cavities
GPA lung disease: bilateral cavitating nodules/masses and fixed infiltrates are characteristic (vs the fleeting/migratory infiltrates of EGPA). Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage from capillaritis can also occur. Biopsy shows necrotizing granulomatous inflammation with geographic necrosis.
⚠️ Cavitary lung nodules in an immunosuppressed-appearing patient can be misread as TB, fungus, or septic emboli. The discriminator is the accompanying triad (sinus + RPGN) and PR3-ANCA — but you must still exclude infection with cultures/stains before immunosuppressing, since steroids worsen a missed infection.
4. GPA kidney — RPGN
GPA renal disease is pauci-immune crescentic (rapidly progressive) glomerulonephritis: rising creatinine over days-weeks, active urinary sediment (dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, proteinuria). 'Pauci-immune' means immunofluorescence shows little/no immune-complex deposition, distinguishing it from immune-complex GN and anti-GBM (linear IgG). Renal involvement defines severe disease requiring aggressive induction.
⚠️ RPGN with linear IgG on immunofluorescence and lung hemorrhage is Goodpasture/anti-GBM, NOT GPA (which is pauci-immune). The discriminator is the IF pattern: pauci-immune = ANCA vasculitis, linear = anti-GBM, granular/'lumpy-bumpy' = immune-complex. Note ~10-30% of anti-GBM patients are also MPO-ANCA positive (double-positive).
5. TMP-SMX (sinus)
TMP-SMX (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) reduces relapses in localized/upper-airway GPA, likely by eradicating nasal Staphylococcus aureus carriage (a relapse driver). It simultaneously serves as Pneumocystis jirovecii (PCP) prophylaxis during induction immunosuppression. Standard prophylactic dosing is one DS tablet 3x/week or one SS tablet daily.
⚠️ TMP-SMX is adjunctive for limited/sinus GPA and for PCP prophylaxis — it is NOT induction therapy for organ-threatening disease. Choosing TMP-SMX alone for a patient with RPGN or alveolar hemorrhage is wrong; that requires steroids + rituximab or cyclophosphamide.
6. MPA — p-ANCA/MPO
Microscopic polyangiitis is a pauci-immune necrotizing small-vessel vasculitis that is p-ANCA / anti-MPO (myeloperoxidase) positive in ~60-75%. Unlike GPA it has NO granulomas and NO destructive upper-airway disease. The dominant presentation is the pulmonary-renal syndrome: rapidly progressive GN plus diffuse alveolar hemorrhage. Mnemonic: MPA = 'MPO, Microscopic, no granulomas.'
⚠️ A pulmonary-renal syndrome with p-ANCA but the stem says 'saddle nose deformity' — that detail makes it GPA, not MPA, because MPA never destroys cartilage. Conversely, choosing GPA for a pulmonary-renal syndrome with NO ENT disease and NO granulomas on biopsy is wrong; absence of granulomas + MPO = MPA.
7. MPA alveolar hemorrhage
MPA causes diffuse alveolar hemorrhage (DAH) from pulmonary capillaritis: hemoptysis, dropping hemoglobin, diffuse ground-glass/alveolar infiltrates, and a rising DLCO (CO is taken up by intra-alveolar blood). This with RPGN constitutes the classic pulmonary-renal syndrome. DAH is life-threatening and may warrant plasma exchange in addition to steroids + cyclophosphamide/rituximab.
⚠️ A rising (not falling) DLCO with diffuse infiltrates and hemoptysis is the buzzword for alveolar hemorrhage, not pneumonia or edema. Attributing it to heart failure/infection and withholding immunosuppression in a confirmed capillaritis is the trap. Differential for pulmonary-renal syndrome: MPA, GPA, and anti-GBM — separate them by ENT/granulomas and IF pattern.
8. MPA — no ENT / no nodules
MPA spares the upper airway (no sinusitis, no saddle nose) and does not form cavitary nodules — those are GPA features. MPA is capillaritis, not granulomatous, so lung disease manifests as hemorrhage/fibrosis rather than cavitating masses. It can cause GI and skin (palpable purpura) involvement plus mononeuritis multiplex. MPO-ANCA-associated interstitial lung disease (fibrosis) may even precede vasculitis by years.
⚠️ Long-standing pulmonary fibrosis later developing RPGN with MPO-ANCA is MPA, not idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF). Calling it IPF and missing the treatable vasculitis is the trap — always check ANCA in 'IPF' patients who develop renal disease or hemoptysis.
9. EGPA — asthma
Eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (Churg-Strauss) classically evolves through three phases: allergic (adult-onset asthma + allergic rhinitis), eosinophilic (peripheral/tissue eosinophilia, eosinophilic pneumonia/gastroenteritis), then vasculitic. Late-onset, often steroid-dependent asthma is nearly universal. ANCA (MPO/p-ANCA) is positive in only ~30-40%.
⚠️ A negative ANCA does NOT exclude EGPA — it is ANCA-positive in a minority. Ruling out EGPA in an asthmatic with eosinophilia and mononeuritis because 'ANCA is negative' is wrong. ANCA-positive EGPA skews toward glomerulonephritis/neuropathy/purpura; ANCA-negative skews toward cardiac and lung disease.
10. EGPA eosinophilia >1000
EGPA features marked peripheral eosinophilia (typically >1500/µL or >10% of WBCs) plus eosinophilic tissue infiltration. Biopsy shows eosinophil-rich necrotizing granulomas and small-vessel vasculitis. Eosinophil count tracks disease activity and falls rapidly with steroids.
⚠️ Hypereosinophilia plus cardiac/lung infiltrates can also be idiopathic hypereosinophilic syndrome (HES) or eosinophilic leukemia. The discriminator for EGPA is the asthma + vasculitis (neuropathy, purpura, glomerulonephritis) backdrop; pure HES lacks vasculitis and may carry the FIP1L1-PDGFRA fusion (treat with imatinib, not steroids alone).
11. EGPA cardiac — leading death
Cardiac involvement (eosinophilic myocarditis, cardiomyopathy, coronary vasculitis, pericardial effusion) is the LEADING cause of death in EGPA and is more common in ANCA-negative disease. Every EGPA patient needs an echocardiogram (and often cardiac MRI/troponin) at diagnosis. Cardiac disease upgrades the patient to severe/poor-prognosis status (a Five-Factor Score item) warranting cyclophosphamide-based induction.
⚠️ In EGPA mortality is driven by the HEART, not the kidney (renal disease is less severe than in GPA/MPA). Picking 'renal failure' as the leading cause of death in EGPA is the trap — screen and treat cardiac involvement aggressively.
12. EGPA mono/nasal/allergy
EGPA neurologic disease: mononeuritis multiplex (e.g., foot drop, wrist drop) from vasculitic ischemia of the vasa nervorum is very common and is a severe-disease (Five-Factor) feature. Upper airway involvement is allergic (rhinitis, nasal polyps, sinusitis) rather than destructive — so no saddle nose. Asymmetric, painful, stepwise sensorimotor deficits are the classic exam pattern.
⚠️ Mononeuritis multiplex is non-specific (also PAN, MPA, GPA, RA, diabetes). The discriminator pointing to EGPA is the asthma + hypereosinophilia context. Attributing asymmetric neuropathy in an asthmatic eosinophilic patient to 'diabetic neuropathy' misses a treatable vasculitis.
13. EGPA — mepo/benra
EGPA treatment: glucocorticoids are the backbone for non-severe disease; severe disease (cardiac, GI, renal, severe neuropathy) needs cyclophosphamide or rituximab induction. Mepolizumab (anti-IL-5) is approved for relapsing/refractory non-severe EGPA and is strongly steroid-sparing; benralizumab (anti-IL-5 receptor) is an alternative. These biologics target the eosinophilic, asthma-predominant phenotype.
⚠️ Mepolizumab/benralizumab control the eosinophilic/asthmatic burden but are NOT adequate sole induction for organ-/life-threatening vasculitis (active myocarditis, RPGN). Choosing mepolizumab alone for severe vasculitic EGPA is wrong — that needs steroids + cyclophosphamide or rituximab.
14. Triptych — must biopsy/echo
Diagnostic workflow for AAV: confirm with tissue biopsy where feasible — lung (necrotizing granuloma in GPA/EGPA vs capillaritis in MPA) and kidney (pauci-immune crescentic GN with negative/scant immunofluorescence). For EGPA specifically, obtain an echocardiogram to screen for the cardiac involvement that drives mortality. Combine biopsy with ANCA serology rather than relying on either alone.
⚠️ Do not anchor on ANCA serology alone — biopsy confirms pauci-immune necrotizing vasculitis and excludes mimics. Treating presumptively without tissue (when tissue is obtainable) risks immunosuppressing an infection or malignancy mimicking vasculitis.
15. ANCA-negative warning
Roughly 10-20% of GPA (especially limited/localized disease confined to the ENT or eye) is ANCA-negative, and ~60% of EGPA is ANCA-negative. ANCA testing should pair indirect immunofluorescence (c-/p-ANCA pattern) with antigen-specific ELISA (PR3/MPO) for accuracy. A negative ANCA in a compatible clinical picture mandates biopsy rather than exclusion of vasculitis.
⚠️ The classic trap: 'ANCA is negative, therefore not vasculitis.' Wrong — diagnosis is clinical + histologic. Excluding GPA in a patient with saddle nose, cavitary nodules, and RPGN solely because ANCA is negative leads to a missed, organ-threatening diagnosis.
