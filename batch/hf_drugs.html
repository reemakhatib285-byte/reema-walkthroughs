⚠️ Board traps 17
  1. ACE inhibitors — WRONG: continue the ACEi and add an ARB or ARNI on top for 'extra RAAS blockade.' Discriminator: never combine ACEi with ARB or ARNI (hyperkalemia, hypotension, renal failure, angioedema) — and require a ≥36-hour washout when switching ACEi→ARNI to avoid angioedema.
  2. Lisinopril target 20-35mg — WRONG: any ACEi dose that lowers blood pressure is good enough. Discriminator: ATLAS showed the higher target dose, not just 'on an ACEi,' drives the outcome benefit — under-titration is a common quality gap, so push to target as BP/renal function allow.
  3. ARBs — WRONG: a patient who had ANGIOEDEMA on an ACEi can safely switch to an ARB. Discriminator: ARBs carry a small cross-reactive angioedema risk and are used cautiously/avoided after ACEi angioedema — cough (not angioedema) is the clean reason to switch ACEi→ARB.
  4. Aldosterone antagonists (MRA) — WRONG: start an MRA in a HFrEF patient with eGFR 25 or K+ 5.4. Discriminator: MRAs are contraindicated when eGFR <30 or K+ >5.0 — and combining an MRA with an ACEi/ARNI plus a potassium supplement or trimethoprim is a classic life-threatening hyperkalemia setup.
  5. Spironolactone vs eplerenone — WRONG: gynecomastia from spironolactone means you must stop all MRA therapy. Discriminator: switch to selective eplerenone rather than abandoning the class — MRAs are mortality-reducing, and the endocrine effects are a spironolactone-specific, fixable problem.
  6. Beta blockers — WRONG: all beta-blockers in this class are interchangeable for HFrEF. Discriminator: only carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, and bisoprolol have mortality evidence — metoprolol TARTRATE, atenolol and others do NOT count, and substituting tartrate for succinate is a frequently tested error.
  7. Metoprolol succinate CR/XL target 200 — WRONG: metoprolol tartrate twice daily is equivalent to metoprolol succinate for HFrEF. Discriminator: only the SUCCINATE (CR/XL) salt was studied and proven in MERIT-HF — tartrate has no HFrEF mortality evidence; the salt/formulation is the whole point of this row.
  8. Carvedilol / bisoprolol dosing — WRONG: avoid all beta-blockers in a HFrEF patient with mild COPD/asthma. Discriminator: a cardioselective agent (bisoprolol or metoprolol succinate) is generally tolerated and the mortality benefit outweighs the small bronchospasm risk — carvedilol (non-selective) is the one to avoid in significant reactive airway disease.
  9. Hydralazine + ISDN — WRONG: hydralazine-ISDN can replace ACEi/ARB/ARNI as routine first-line RAAS therapy in everyone. Discriminator: it is ADD-ON therapy in Black patients on top of standard GDMT (A-HeFT), or a substitute only when RAAS inhibitors can't be used — it does not block the RAAS and is not a universal first-line agent.
  10. ARNI (sacubitril-valsartan) — WRONG: start ARNI the day after stopping an ACEi (or while still on it). Discriminator: you MUST allow a ≥36-hour washout from an ACEi before starting ARNI because overlapping neprilysin + ACE inhibition causes angioedema — also avoid ARNI with eGFR/hyperkalemia issues, and it raises BNP but lowers NT-proBNP.
  11. SGLT2 inhibitor (fixed 10mg) — WRONG: SGLT2i must be up-titrated to a target dose like the other pillars. Discriminator: it is a FIXED 10 mg dose with no titration — and unlike its diabetes indication, it is given for HFrEF outcomes even in non-diabetics; do not withhold it for a normal HbA1c.
  12. Novel: vericiguat — WRONG: vericiguat is a first-line pillar that replaces the SGLT2i or MRA. Discriminator: it is reserved as ADD-ON for high-risk patients with a recent decompensation already on GDMT (VICTORIA) — and don't combine with nitrates/PDE5i (additive hypotension via the same cGMP pathway).
  13. Novel: omecamtiv mecarbil — WRONG: omecamtiv mecarbil works like an inotrope such as milrinone or dobutamine and raises myocardial oxygen demand. Discriminator: it improves contractility WITHOUT increasing oxygen consumption or arrhythmia the way calcium-dependent inotropes do — but its clinical benefit is modest and it is not standard GDMT.
  14. Initiation vs target columns — WRONG: keeping a patient 'on all four pillars' at starting doses is optimized GDMT. Discriminator: outcomes track titration to target doses — but the modern STRONG-HF approach gets all four classes ON first at low doses, then rapidly up-titrates, rather than maxing one drug before adding the next.
  15. Avoid in HFrEF — non-DHP CCB, NSAIDs, TZDs — WRONG: use diltiazem or verapamil for rate control of AF in a HFrEF patient. Discriminator: non-DHP CCBs are negative inotropes contraindicated in HFrEF — use a beta-blocker (or digoxin) for rate control instead; amlodipine/felodipine are the only CCBs considered neutral/safe in HFrEF.

Discriminator: they are for congestion/symptom control only, not survival — the four pillars (ARNI, beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2i) reduce mortality, and diuretics should be titrated to the lowest dose maintaining euvolemia.

📖 Read-through — all 17 symbols
1. ACE inhibitors
ACE inhibitors (lisinopril, enalapril, captopril, trandolapril) block conversion of angiotensin I→II, reducing afterload, aldosterone and adverse remodeling — a foundational mortality-reducing RAAS therapy in HFrEF (SOLVD, CONSENSUS). Start low, up-titrate to target, monitor K+ and creatinine, and accept a modest (~<30%) creatinine rise.
⚠️ WRONG: continue the ACEi and add an ARB or ARNI on top for 'extra RAAS blockade.' Discriminator: never combine ACEi with ARB or ARNI (hyperkalemia, hypotension, renal failure, angioedema) — and require a ≥36-hour washout when switching ACEi→ARNI to avoid angioedema.
2. Lisinopril target 20-35mg
Up-titrate lisinopril (initiation 2.5–5 mg) toward its target 20–35 mg once daily, because the mortality/morbidity benefit of RAAS inhibition is dose-dependent (ATLAS: high-dose lisinopril reduced hospitalizations vs low dose). Don't leave patients stranded at the starting dose.
⚠️ WRONG: any ACEi dose that lowers blood pressure is good enough. Discriminator: ATLAS showed the higher target dose, not just 'on an ACEi,' drives the outcome benefit — under-titration is a common quality gap, so push to target as BP/renal function allow.
3. ARBs
ARBs (losartan target 150 mg, valsartan 160 mg bid, candesartan 32 mg) block the AT1 receptor and are used when an ACEi is not tolerated, classically due to ACEi-induced cough (bradykinin) — CHARM-Alternative, Val-HeFT. They are second-line to ARNI/ACEi for RAAS inhibition in HFrEF.
⚠️ WRONG: a patient who had ANGIOEDEMA on an ACEi can safely switch to an ARB. Discriminator: ARBs carry a small cross-reactive angioedema risk and are used cautiously/avoided after ACEi angioedema — cough (not angioedema) is the clean reason to switch ACEi→ARB.
4. Aldosterone antagonists (MRA)
Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (eplerenone, spironolactone) block aldosterone, reducing fibrosis, sodium retention and mortality in HFrEF (RALES spironolactone, EMPHASIS-HF/EPHESUS eplerenone). Indicated for EF ≤35% on ACEi/ARB + beta-blocker. Require K+ <5.0 and eGFR >30 to start; recheck K+/creatinine within 1 week.
⚠️ WRONG: start an MRA in a HFrEF patient with eGFR 25 or K+ 5.4. Discriminator: MRAs are contraindicated when eGFR <30 or K+ >5.0 — and combining an MRA with an ACEi/ARNI plus a potassium supplement or trimethoprim is a classic life-threatening hyperkalemia setup.
5. Spironolactone vs eplerenone
Spironolactone (target 25–50 mg qd) is non-selective and binds androgen/progesterone receptors, causing gynecomastia, breast tenderness and menstrual changes. Eplerenone (target 50 mg qd) is selective for the mineralocorticoid receptor, so it largely avoids those endocrine side effects — switch to eplerenone if spironolactone causes painful gynecomastia.
⚠️ WRONG: gynecomastia from spironolactone means you must stop all MRA therapy. Discriminator: switch to selective eplerenone rather than abandoning the class — MRAs are mortality-reducing, and the endocrine effects are a spironolactone-specific, fixable problem.
6. Beta blockers
Beta-blockers counter chronic sympathetic toxicity in HFrEF, reducing mortality and producing reverse remodeling — but ONLY three are proven: carvedilol, metoprolol SUCCINATE (CR/XL), and bisoprolol (CIBIS-II, MERIT-HF, COPERNICUS). Start low, go slow, and only when the patient is euvolemic/compensated (they can transiently worsen HF).
⚠️ WRONG: all beta-blockers in this class are interchangeable for HFrEF. Discriminator: only carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, and bisoprolol have mortality evidence — metoprolol TARTRATE, atenolol and others do NOT count, and substituting tartrate for succinate is a frequently tested error.
7. Metoprolol succinate CR/XL target 200
Use metoprolol SUCCINATE CR/XL (extended-release, once daily), NOT metoprolol tartrate (immediate-release, twice daily). Initiation 12.5–25 mg, target 200 mg daily (MERIT-HF). The succinate formulation gives the steady beta-1 blockade that delivered the mortality benefit.
⚠️ WRONG: metoprolol tartrate twice daily is equivalent to metoprolol succinate for HFrEF. Discriminator: only the SUCCINATE (CR/XL) salt was studied and proven in MERIT-HF — tartrate has no HFrEF mortality evidence; the salt/formulation is the whole point of this row.
8. Carvedilol / bisoprolol dosing
Carvedilol (initiation 3.125 mg bid, target 25 mg bid; 50 mg bid if >85 kg) is a non-selective beta- plus alpha-blocker (extra vasodilation, helpful in HTN). Bisoprolol (initiation 1.25 mg, target 10 mg qd) is beta-1 selective (CIBIS-II) — a good choice when alpha-blockade/hypotension or reactive airways are a concern.
⚠️ WRONG: avoid all beta-blockers in a HFrEF patient with mild COPD/asthma. Discriminator: a cardioselective agent (bisoprolol or metoprolol succinate) is generally tolerated and the mortality benefit outweighs the small bronchospasm risk — carvedilol (non-selective) is the one to avoid in significant reactive airway disease.
9. Hydralazine + ISDN
Hydralazine (arterial/afterload reducer) + isosorbide dinitrate (venodilator/preload, nitric-oxide donor) — target ~75/40 mg tid or the fixed-dose combo. Add to GDMT in self-identified BLACK patients with NYHA III–IV HFrEF (A-HeFT), and use as the RAAS alternative when ACEi/ARB/ARNI are contraindicated (renal failure, hyperkalemia, hypotension).
⚠️ WRONG: hydralazine-ISDN can replace ACEi/ARB/ARNI as routine first-line RAAS therapy in everyone. Discriminator: it is ADD-ON therapy in Black patients on top of standard GDMT (A-HeFT), or a substitute only when RAAS inhibitors can't be used — it does not block the RAAS and is not a universal first-line agent.
10. ARNI (sacubitril-valsartan)
ARNI = sacubitril (neprilysin inhibitor → raises beneficial natriuretic peptides) + valsartan (ARB). Initiation 24/26–49/51 mg bid, target 97/103 mg (≈200 mg) bid. PARADIGM-HF showed ARNI SUPERIOR to enalapril for CV death/HF hospitalization, so it is the PREFERRED RAAS agent in HFrEF.
⚠️ WRONG: start ARNI the day after stopping an ACEi (or while still on it). Discriminator: you MUST allow a ≥36-hour washout from an ACEi before starting ARNI because overlapping neprilysin + ACE inhibition causes angioedema — also avoid ARNI with eGFR/hyperkalemia issues, and it raises BNP but lowers NT-proBNP.
11. SGLT2 inhibitor (fixed 10mg)
SGLT2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) reduce CV death and HF hospitalization in HFrEF regardless of diabetes (DAPA-HF, EMPEROR-Reduced) via natriuresis and cardiac-metabolic effects. Fixed 10 mg once daily — NO titration. Sotagliflozin (SGLT1/2, target 200 mg) is an alternative. Watch for euglycemic DKA, volume depletion, and genital mycotic infections.
⚠️ WRONG: SGLT2i must be up-titrated to a target dose like the other pillars. Discriminator: it is a FIXED 10 mg dose with no titration — and unlike its diabetes indication, it is given for HFrEF outcomes even in non-diabetics; do not withhold it for a normal HbA1c.
12. Novel: vericiguat
Vericiguat is a soluble guanylate cyclase (sGC) STIMULATOR that augments the NO–cGMP pathway when endogenous NO is low. Initiation 2.5 mg, target 10 mg qd. VICTORIA: in high-risk HFrEF with a recent worsening event, it modestly reduced CV death/HF hospitalization — an add-on, not a core pillar.
⚠️ WRONG: vericiguat is a first-line pillar that replaces the SGLT2i or MRA. Discriminator: it is reserved as ADD-ON for high-risk patients with a recent decompensation already on GDMT (VICTORIA) — and don't combine with nitrates/PDE5i (additive hypotension via the same cGMP pathway).
13. Novel: omecamtiv mecarbil
Omecamtiv mecarbil is a selective cardiac MYOSIN activator that increases contractility (systolic ejection time) without raising myocardial oxygen demand or intracellular calcium. Dosed by plasma level (up to ~50 mg bid). GALACTIC-HF: small reduction in HF events but no mortality benefit — investigational/limited role.
⚠️ WRONG: omecamtiv mecarbil works like an inotrope such as milrinone or dobutamine and raises myocardial oxygen demand. Discriminator: it improves contractility WITHOUT increasing oxygen consumption or arrhythmia the way calcium-dependent inotropes do — but its clinical benefit is modest and it is not standard GDMT.
14. Initiation vs target columns
The table is built around two columns: the low INITIATION dose (where you start to avoid hypotension/renal hits/hyperkalemia) and the evidence-based TARGET DOSE (where the trial mortality benefit was achieved). The principle: start low, titrate up to target as BP, K+ and renal function allow.
⚠️ WRONG: keeping a patient 'on all four pillars' at starting doses is optimized GDMT. Discriminator: outcomes track titration to target doses — but the modern STRONG-HF approach gets all four classes ON first at low doses, then rapidly up-titrates, rather than maxing one drug before adding the next.
15. Avoid in HFrEF — non-DHP CCB, NSAIDs, TZDs
Drugs that WORSEN HFrEF and should be avoided: non-dihydropyridine CCBs (verapamil, diltiazem) — negative inotropes that increase mortality; NSAIDs — cause sodium/water retention, blunt diuretics/RAAS and precipitate decompensation/AKI; thiazolidinediones (pioglitazone, rosiglitazone) — fluid retention worsening HF; and class I antiarrhythmics/dronedarone (pro-arrhythmic in low EF).
⚠️ WRONG: use diltiazem or verapamil for rate control of AF in a HFrEF patient. Discriminator: non-DHP CCBs are negative inotropes contraindicated in HFrEF — use a beta-blocker (or digoxin) for rate control instead; amlodipine/felodipine are the only CCBs considered neutral/safe in HFrEF.
16. Loop diuretics — symptom control
Loop diuretics (furosemide, torsemide, bumetanide) inhibit the Na-K-2Cl transporter to relieve congestion — they improve SYMPTOMS but have no proven mortality benefit. Torsemide/bumetanide have more reliable oral absorption than furosemide. IV-to-PO furosemide is roughly 2:1; equivalence ≈ furosemide 40 = torsemide 20 = bumetanide 1 mg.
⚠️ WRONG: loop diuretics reduce mortality and are a 'pillar' of HFrEF therapy. Discriminator: they are for congestion/symptom control only, not survival — the four pillars (ARNI, beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2i) reduce mortality, and diuretics should be titrated to the lowest dose maintaining euvolemia.
17. Ivabradine & digoxin — adjuncts
Ivabradine blocks the SA-node funny (If) current to slow rate WITHOUT affecting contractility — add when in SINUS rhythm with HR ≥70 despite a maximally tolerated beta-blocker, EF ≤35% (SHIFT: fewer HF hospitalizations). Digoxin (target trough 0.5–0.9 ng/mL) reduces HF hospitalizations but not mortality (DIG trial) — a late add-on for symptoms or AF rate control.
⚠️ WRONG: ivabradine works in atrial fibrillation and substitutes for a beta-blocker. Discriminator: ivabradine requires SINUS rhythm and is added ON TOP of a beta-blocker (not instead of it). For digoxin, watch toxicity (nausea, visual halos, arrhythmia) — risk rises with hypokalemia and renal failure, and the target trough is LOW (0.5–0.9).
