📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. RVT #1 — renal vein thrombosis
Membranous nephropathy is the nephrotic disease MOST associated with hypercoagulability and renal vein thrombosis, plus DVT/PE. Mechanism: urinary loss of antithrombin III and other anticoagulant proteins. Flank pain + hematuria + worsening proteinuria in a membranous patient = think RVT.
⚠️ RVT/hypercoagulability is the membranous hallmark — not MCD or FSGS. Low albumin amplifies the clotting risk.
2. Primary = subepithelial only
In primary (PLA2R) membranous, immune complexes are exclusively subepithelial — no mesangial or subendothelial deposits. IF shows granular IgG (IgG4-dominant) and C3 along the GBM. EM: subepithelial deposits with GBM spikes between them.
⚠️ Subepithelial-ONLY = primary; extra-membranous (mesangial/subendothelial) deposits point to SECONDARY (lupus, infection, malignancy).
3. Secondary → extra sites + IgG1-3
Secondary membranous (lupus, HBV, syphilis, solid tumors, NSAIDs/gold) shows deposits in extra locations (mesangial/subendothelial), full-house IF in lupus, and IgG1/IgG2/IgG3 subclasses rather than primary's IgG4. PLA2R usually negative.
⚠️ IgG1-3 + extra deposit sites + full-house = SECONDARY/lupus; IgG4 + subepithelial-only + anti-PLA2R = primary.
4. Anti-PLA2R 70–80% / no biopsy
Anti-PLA2R antibodies are positive in ~70–80% of primary membranous and are diagnostic — a positive titer in a nephrotic patient may obviate biopsy. THSD7A accounts for a minority. Titer tracks disease activity and predicts response/relapse.
⚠️ Anti-PLA2R = primary membranous biomarker; it is NOT seen in MCD, FSGS, or lupus membranous.
5. Silver stain: spike-and-dome
Jones methenamine silver stain shows GBM 'spikes' projecting between subepithelial deposits, creating the spike-and-dome appearance. EM confirms electron-dense subepithelial deposits.
⚠️ Spike-and-dome on silver = membranous; tram-tracks (double contour) on silver = MPGN. Don't confuse the two silver patterns.
6. IgG + C3 granular IF
Immunofluorescence shows granular (lumpy-bumpy) IgG and C3 along the capillary loops — classic immune-complex pattern. Primary disease is IgG4-dominant.
⚠️ Granular IgG/C3 along GBM = immune-complex membranous; linear IgG = anti-GBM (Goodpasture), a totally different disease.
7. Rituximab (anti-PLA2R driven)
Rituximab (B-cell/anti-CD20) is first-line immunosuppression for high-risk primary membranous, depleting PLA2R antibody-producing B cells. Anti-PLA2R titer falls before proteinuria improves and is used to monitor response.
⚠️ Rituximab targets the B cells making anti-PLA2R; it's preferred over older Ponticelli cyclophosphamide+steroid in many high-risk patients.
8. Ponticelli: steroids + cyclophosphamide
The Ponticelli regimen alternates monthly corticosteroids with cyclophosphamide for high-risk primary membranous. CNIs (cyclosporine/tacrolimus) are an alternative. Reserved for high-risk (heavy proteinuria, rising anti-PLA2R, declining GFR).
⚠️ Immunosuppression is for HIGH-RISK membranous only — low-risk patients get conservative RAAS therapy first.
9. Risk-stratified: very high / high / mod / low
Treatment is risk-based: low risk (preserved GFR, proteinuria <3.5 g, stable anti-PLA2R) → supportive RAAS and watch (one-third remit spontaneously). High/very-high risk → start immunosuppression.
⚠️ Don't immunosuppress every membranous — ~1/3 remit spontaneously, so low-risk patients are watched on RAAS.
10. C3/C4 normal unless lupus
Primary membranous has NORMAL complement. Low C3/C4 in a 'membranous' picture should redirect you to a secondary cause — especially lupus membranous (ISN/RPS class V), which is full-house with low complement.
⚠️ Low complement + membranous pattern = lupus (secondary), not idiopathic PLA2R membranous.
11. >3.5 g nonselective proteinuria
Membranous produces heavy, NONselective proteinuria (albumin + larger proteins) and bland urinary sediment, often with edema and hyperlipidemia. Contrasts with MCD's highly selective albuminuria.
⚠️ Selective (albumin-predominant) proteinuria = MCD; nonselective heavy proteinuria = membranous/FSGS.
12. White adults — most common primary
Primary membranous is the most common cause of primary nephrotic syndrome in older/middle-aged white adults. In adults >60, screen for an underlying malignancy (solid tumors) as a secondary trigger.
⚠️ Membranous is #1 primary nephrotic in WHITE ADULTS; MCD is #1 in children, FSGS leads in African-ancestry/ESKD.
13. 1/3 remit · 1/3 relapse · 1/3 ESKD
Classic 'rule of thirds' natural history: one-third spontaneous remission, one-third relapsing/persistent, one-third progress to ESKD. Tubulointerstitial fibrosis on biopsy is the best histologic predictor of progression.
⚠️ Interstitial fibrosis — not the glomerular deposit stage — best predicts long-term renal outcome.
