📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. GLP-1 / GIP incretin jar
GLP-1 receptor agonists (the '-tides': liraglutide, semaglutide, dulaglutide, exenatide) and the dual GIP/GLP-1 agonist tirzepatide augment glucose-dependent insulin secretion, suppress glucagon, slow gastric emptying, and increase satiety. The result is potent A1c lowering (~1-2%) with marked weight LOSS and low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk. GLP-1 RAs with CV-outcome data (semaglutide, dulaglutide, liraglutide) also reduce atherosclerotic MACE.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'GLP-1 agonists cause weight gain like sulfonylureas and insulin.' Discriminator — GLP-1 RAs/tirzepatide produce weight loss (a key class advantage); the weight-GAINING diabetes drugs are sulfonylureas, TZDs, and insulin.
2. DPP-4 inhibitor
DPP-4 inhibitors (the '-gliptins': sitagliptin, saxagliptin, linagliptin, alogliptin) block degradation of endogenous incretins (GLP-1/GIP), raising their levels. They are oral, weight-NEUTRAL, modest A1c-lowering (~0.5-0.8%), and carry low hypoglycemia risk. Saxagliptin/alogliptin are associated with increased heart-failure hospitalization, so prefer other gliptins in HF patients.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'add a DPP-4 inhibitor to a patient already on a GLP-1 receptor agonist.' Discriminator — both act on the incretin axis; combining a DPP-4 inhibitor with a GLP-1 RA is redundant (no added benefit) and not recommended. Choose one incretin-based agent.
3. GLP-1 angioedema
Incretin therapies can rarely cause hypersensitivity reactions including angioedema and anaphylaxis. Discontinue the drug if a serious hypersensitivity reaction occurs and treat supportively; do not rechallenge.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'angioedema after starting therapy is just an injection-site reaction — continue the drug.' Discriminator — angioedema is a systemic hypersensitivity reaction that warrants stopping the agent, distinct from a benign local injection-site reaction.
4. GLP-1 GI / pancreatitis & MTC
The dominant GLP-1 RA side effects are GI (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) from slowed gastric emptying — usually transient with slow titration. Class warnings: pancreatitis (stop for severe persistent abdominal pain) and a boxed contraindication with personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) or MEN2. Also avoid in established gastroparesis.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'prescribe a GLP-1 agonist to an obese diabetic with a family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma.' Discriminator — personal or family MTC/MEN2 history is an absolute contraindication to GLP-1 RAs and tirzepatide (not to DPP-4 inhibitors); pick a different class.
5. Joints (DPP-4)
DPP-4 inhibitors can cause severe, sometimes disabling arthralgias (a recognized class adverse effect) and, rarely, acute pancreatitis. If a patient develops severe joint pain after starting a gliptin, consider the drug as the cause and discontinue.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'new severe polyarthralgia on a gliptin must be new inflammatory arthritis — work it up and continue the drug.' Discriminator — DPP-4 inhibitors are a recognized cause of severe arthralgia that resolves on discontinuation; stop the gliptin before an extensive rheumatologic workup.
6. Linagliptin safe in renal disease
Linagliptin is primarily excreted via the bile/enterohepatic route, so it requires NO renal dose adjustment and is usable even in advanced CKD/ESRD. The other gliptins (sitagliptin, saxagliptin, alogliptin) are renally cleared and need dose reduction as eGFR falls.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'use full-dose sitagliptin in a patient with eGFR 20 because all gliptins are renally safe.' Discriminator — only linagliptin needs no renal adjustment; the renally cleared gliptins require dose reduction in CKD to avoid accumulation.
7. Alpha-glucosidase inhibitor soldier
Alpha-glucosidase inhibitors (acarbose, miglitol) block intestinal brush-border enzymes, slowing carbohydrate digestion and blunting POST-PRANDIAL glucose spikes. They are weight-neutral with modest efficacy; main side effects are flatulence, bloating, and diarrhea. Key point: treat any hypoglycemia (when combined with insulin/sulfonylurea) with oral GLUCOSE, not sucrose, because sucrose breakdown is blocked.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'treat hypoglycemia in a patient on acarbose with table sugar (sucrose).' Discriminator — acarbose blocks sucrose hydrolysis, so sucrose won't raise glucose; use pure glucose (dextrose) tablets/gel instead.
8. SGLT2 inhibitor plumber
SGLT2 inhibitors (the '-flozins': empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, canagliflozin, ertugliflozin) block the proximal-tubule SGLT2 cotransporter that reabsorbs ~90% of filtered glucose, causing glucosuria and natriuresis. This lowers glucose insulin-independently (low hypoglycemia risk), produces mild weight loss and BP reduction, and confers cardiorenal protection.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'an SGLT2 inhibitor will cause hypoglycemia like a sulfonylurea.' Discriminator — SGLT2 inhibitors act independently of insulin secretion, so monotherapy rarely causes hypoglycemia; the secretagogues (sulfonylureas, meglitinides) and insulin are the hypoglycemia culprits.
9. SGLT2 heart failure / CKD benefit
SGLT2 inhibitors reduce heart-failure hospitalizations (benefit in HFrEF AND HFpEF, even without diabetes) and slow CKD progression / reduce albuminuria and renal endpoints. They are the preferred add-on (alongside or after metformin) when HF or CKD coexists with T2DM. Cardiorenal benefit persists at lower eGFR even as glucose-lowering wanes.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'pick a DPP-4 inhibitor (or sulfonylurea) for glycemic control in a diabetic with heart failure and CKD.' Discriminator — when HF or CKD is present, choose the agent with proven cardiorenal benefit (SGLT2 inhibitor); DPP-4 inhibitors lack renal/HF outcome benefit (and saxagliptin can worsen HF).
10. Euglycemic DKA
SGLT2 inhibitors can precipitate euglycemic DKA — a high-anion-gap ketoacidosis with glucose that is only mildly elevated or near-normal (often <200-250 mg/dL). Triggers include surgery, fasting, acute illness, low-carb/ketogenic diets, and alcohol. Check ketones/anion gap in any unwell SGLT2-treated patient regardless of glucose.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'this acidotic SGLT2-inhibitor patient can't be in DKA because the glucose is only 180.' Discriminator — SGLT2-associated DKA is euglycemic; a near-normal glucose does NOT exclude DKA. Measure ketones and the anion gap.
11. Sick-day / hold rule
To prevent euglycemic DKA, HOLD SGLT2 inhibitors during acute illness, prolonged fasting, and around surgery — guidance is to stop ~3-4 days before elective surgery (ertugliflozin ~4 days; longer-acting agents per protocol) and resume only when eating and clinically stable. This 'sick-day rule' also applies to severe dehydration or volume depletion.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'continue the SGLT2 inhibitor through the perioperative fasting period to keep glucose controlled.' Discriminator — perioperative fasting plus SGLT2 inhibition is a classic euglycemic-DKA trigger; the drug must be held before surgery, not continued.
12. Fournier gangrene / genital infection
Glucosuria from SGLT2 inhibitors fosters genital mycotic infections (vulvovaginal candidiasis, balanitis) and urinary tract infections, plus the rare but life-threatening Fournier gangrene (necrotizing fasciitis of the perineum). Counsel on genital hygiene; severe perineal pain/erythema/swelling with systemic illness is a surgical emergency.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'severe perineal pain and erythema in an SGLT2-treated patient is just a routine yeast infection — give topical antifungal.' Discriminator — rapidly progressive perineal pain with systemic toxicity suggests Fournier gangrene, a surgical emergency requiring urgent debridement, not outpatient antifungal.
13. SGLT2 not in T1DM
SGLT2 inhibitors are not recommended in type 1 diabetes because the insulinopenic state markedly raises the risk of (euglycemic) DKA. They are agents for T2DM (and have HF/CKD indications); in T1DM, basal-bolus insulin remains the foundation.
⚠️ Wrong answer: 'add an SGLT2 inhibitor to a T1DM patient to reduce insulin needs and aid weight loss.' Discriminator — in T1DM the DKA risk from SGLT2 inhibition is unacceptably high; this is the wrong class for T1DM.
