📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. AV roundabout
AVNRT (atrioventricular nodal re-entrant tachycardia) is the most common paroxysmal SVT (~60%), with the re-entry circuit contained entirely within/around the AV node using two functionally distinct pathways: a slow pathway (short refractory period, slow conduction) and a fast pathway (long refractory period, fast conduction). The roundabout = electricity circling the AV node. Typical rate is 150-250 bpm with a narrow regular QRS.
⚠️ Confusing AVNRT with AVRT is the trap — AVRT (e.g., WPW) uses an accessory pathway OUTSIDE the node creating a macro-re-entrant circuit, whereas AVNRT's circuit is micro-re-entrant within the node itself; this distinction changes the ablation target and the danger of pre-excited AF.
2. Slow down, fast up
Typical ('slow-fast') AVNRT, ~90% of cases, conducts ANTEGRADE down the slow pathway and RETROGRADE up the fast pathway. Because retrograde fast conduction reaches the atria almost simultaneously with ventricular activation, the P wave is buried in or just after the QRS (very short RP interval). Atypical ('fast-slow') AVNRT reverses this with a long RP.
⚠️ Assuming every AVNRT is slow-fast is the trap — atypical fast-slow AVNRT produces a long-RP tachycardia that can be mistaken for atrial tachycardia or AVRT; the buried/short-RP P wave is the marker of the common typical form.
3. Slow pathway
The slow pathway conducts antegrade in typical AVNRT and sits near the tricuspid annulus/coronary sinus os (the inferior 'slow' input to the node). It is the ablation target precisely because eliminating it breaks the circuit while preserving normal fast-pathway AV conduction, giving ~95% cure with low complication risk.
⚠️ Ablating the FAST pathway is the trap — fast-pathway ablation carries a much higher risk of complete heart block (it lies near the compact node/His), so the slow pathway is deliberately targeted instead.
4. Premature beat trigger
A premature atrial complex (PAC) initiates AVNRT: arriving when the fast pathway is still refractory (its long refractory period), the impulse is blocked there and forced down the slow pathway; by the time it reaches the bottom, the fast pathway has recovered and conducts retrograde, closing the re-entry loop. This is why AVNRT starts and stops abruptly ('paroxysmal').
⚠️ Thinking AVNRT needs structural heart disease to start is the trap — it classically occurs in young, otherwise healthy patients (women > men), triggered by a simple PAC, caffeine, or stress, not by ischemia or cardiomyopathy.
5. Pseudo-r' in V1
In typical AVNRT, the retrograde P wave is buried at the end of the QRS and deflects upward in V1, mimicking an incomplete RBBB 'r-prime' (pseudo-r'). Comparing the tachycardia ECG to a baseline sinus ECG reveals this 'extra' deflection is actually the retrograde P, a classic AVNRT clue.
⚠️ Reading the pseudo-r' as genuine RBBB is the trap — it disappears in sinus rhythm because it is a retrograde P, not a true conduction-system finding; missing this misclassifies the tachycardia.
6. Pseudo-S inferior
The same retrograde atrial activation produces a pseudo-S wave (terminal negative deflection) in inferior leads II, III, and aVF, reflecting near-simultaneous atrial and ventricular activation in slow-fast AVNRT. Pseudo-r' in V1 plus pseudo-S inferiorly together strongly support typical AVNRT.
⚠️ Mistaking the pseudo-S for a true inferior conduction abnormality is the trap — like the pseudo-r', it is a retrograde P that vanishes in sinus rhythm; it indicates a short RP interval typical of AVNRT, not inferior pathology.
7. Cannon A waves
Because the atria and ventricles contract almost simultaneously in typical AVNRT, the right atrium contracts against a closed tricuspid valve, producing regular cannon A waves visible in the jugular venous pulse (the 'frog sign'). Patients often report rapid regular neck pounding/palpitations.
⚠️ Interpreting cannon A waves as a sign of complete AV dissociation/3rd-degree block is the trap — in AVNRT they are REGULAR (every beat) from synchronized AV contraction, whereas in AV dissociation/VT they are IRREGULAR/intermittent.
8. Adenosine terminates
AVNRT is AV-node-dependent, so AV-nodal blockade terminates it. After failed vagal maneuvers, give adenosine as a rapid IV push: 6 mg, then 12 mg, then 12 mg if needed, each followed by a saline flush. Adenosine transiently blocks the AV node (half-life <10 seconds), interrupting the circuit. Warn the patient about a brief sense of chest discomfort/impending doom/flushing.
⚠️ Giving adenosine for a wide-complex or irregular tachycardia without caution is the trap — in pre-excited AF (WPW), AV-nodal blockers including adenosine can accelerate conduction down the accessory pathway and precipitate VF; adenosine is for regular narrow-complex SVT.
9. Valsalva terminates
Vagal maneuvers are first-line for stable AVNRT: the Valsalva maneuver and carotid sinus massage increase vagal tone, slowing AV-node conduction enough to break the circuit. The modified Valsalva (strain then lie supine with passive leg raise) significantly improves conversion rates over the standard technique.
⚠️ Performing carotid sinus massage without checking for bruits/recent TIA-stroke is the trap — it risks dislodging plaque and causing a stroke in older patients with carotid disease; the modified Valsalva is the safer universal first move.
10. AV block terminates
Because the AV node is an obligatory part of the AVNRT re-entry circuit, anything that transiently blocks the node (vagal maneuvers, adenosine, IV beta-blockers, or IV non-dihydropyridine CCBs) terminates the arrhythmia. This AV-node dependence is the defining therapeutic vulnerability of AVNRT.
⚠️ Expecting AV-node blockade to terminate every SVT is the trap — atrial tachycardia and atrial flutter continue in the atria despite AV block (you simply see slower ventricular response unmasking flutter waves), whereas AVNRT/AVRT actually stop because the node is part of their circuit.
11. Ablation cure
Slow-pathway catheter ablation is the definitive long-term cure for recurrent or drug-refractory AVNRT, with ~95-97% success and low recurrence. It is preferred for patients who want to avoid lifelong medication. The main procedural risk is inadvertent AV block (~1%) requiring a pacemaker, which is why the slow pathway (not the fast) is targeted.
⚠️ Reserving ablation only for failed drug therapy in every patient is the trap — for frequent or highly symptomatic AVNRT, ablation is a reasonable first-line definitive option given its high cure rate, not merely a last resort.
12. BB / diltiazem / flecainide
For chronic suppression/prophylaxis of recurrent AVNRT in patients declining ablation: AV-nodal blockers first — beta-blockers or non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers (diltiazem, verapamil). Class IC agents (flecainide, propafenone) are second-line and only for structurally normal hearts (no CAD/LVH).
⚠️ Combining a beta-blocker WITH IV verapamil/diltiazem, or using non-DHP CCBs in reduced-EF heart failure, is the trap — the combination causes profound bradycardia/AV block and hypotension, and non-DHP CCBs are negative inotropes contraindicated in HFrEF.
13. Accelerated junctional, no tx
Accelerated junctional rhythm (rate 60-100 bpm, faster than the normal junctional escape of 40-60) arises from enhanced automaticity of the AV junction, often from digoxin toxicity, post-cardiac surgery, ischemia, or myocarditis. It is usually benign — management is to treat the underlying cause (e.g., stop digoxin, correct electrolytes), often with no specific antiarrhythmic.
⚠️ Treating accelerated junctional rhythm like AVNRT with adenosine/ablation is the trap — it is an automatic (not re-entrant) rhythm, so AV-nodal blockade and cardioversion do not fix it; the answer is to find and treat the cause, classically digoxin toxicity.
14. JET — pediatric/post-op
Junctional ectopic tachycardia (JET) is an automatic junctional tachycardia of infants and children, classically appearing after congenital cardiac surgery. Unlike re-entrant AVNRT it does not respond to cardioversion or adenosine; management focuses on cooling/hypothermia, avoiding catecholamines, correcting electrolytes, and amiodarone if needed.
⚠️ Cardioverting or giving adenosine for JET is the trap — being an automatic focus, it ignores both; the post-cardiac-surgery infant with an incessant narrow-complex tachycardia needs supportive cooling and amiodarone, not shock.
15. Diuresis / ANP after SVT
Prolonged SVT raises atrial pressure and stretch, releasing atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP), which produces a characteristic post-tachycardia polyuria/diuresis as the rhythm terminates. This benign 'urinary frequency after palpitations stop' is a clinical clue that the episode was a supraventricular tachycardia.
⚠️ Attributing post-SVT polyuria to a primary renal or endocrine problem is the trap — it is simply ANP-mediated diuresis from atrial stretch during the tachycardia, a recognized harmless sign, not a sign of kidney disease.
