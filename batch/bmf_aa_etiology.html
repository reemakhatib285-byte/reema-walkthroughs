📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Two age peaks
Acquired aplastic anemia has a BIMODAL age distribution — a first peak in adolescents/young adults (~15–25 yr) and a second, larger peak in older adults (>60 yr). Overall it is a rare disorder (~2 per million/year in the West, several-fold higher in East Asia). It presents as pancytopenia from an empty (hypocellular) marrow.
⚠️ Don't picture a single middle-age peak — the classic teaching is two peaks (young AND old). Also distinguish ACQUIRED AA from INHERITED bone-marrow-failure syndromes (Fanconi anemia, dyskeratosis congenita, Shwachman-Diamond) which present in children and have congenital stigmata.
2. Idiopathic/autoimmune
The MOST COMMON cause is IDIOPATHIC (no identifiable exposure) — the majority of cases. The underlying mechanism even in 'idiopathic' disease is immune: autoreactive cytotoxic T cells destroy hematopoietic stem cells, which is why immunosuppression works.
⚠️ Boards want 'idiopathic/immune-mediated' as the #1 cause. The named exposures (drugs, toxins, radiation, viruses) are the classic minority — high-yield to recognize but NOT the most frequent etiology.
3. Pharmacy shelf
Drugs are a classic IDIOSYNCRATIC (non-dose-dependent) cause. CHLORAMPHENICOL is the board archetype. Others: sulfonamides, NSAIDs (phenylbutazone), antithyroid drugs (propylthiouracil, methimazole/carbimazole), antiepileptics (carbamazepine, phenytoin), gold, and ticlopidine.
⚠️ Separate IDIOSYNCRATIC drug aplasia (unpredictable, any dose — e.g., chloramphenicol) from DOSE-DEPENDENT marrow suppression of cytotoxic chemo (predictable, reversible). Chloramphenicol is famous for BOTH: a reversible dose-related suppression AND a rare fatal idiosyncratic aplasia.
4. Syringe cannon
Cytotoxic CHEMOTHERAPY and other dose-dependent agents directly suppress marrow stem cells — this is expected, predictable, and usually reversible after the drug clears.
⚠️ This dose-dependent suppression is distinct from true idiosyncratic aplastic anemia; it is anticipated marrow toxicity, not an immune idiosyncratic reaction.
5. Benzene barrel
BENZENE is the classic industrial/occupational toxin causing aplastic anemia (and later myelodysplasia/AML). Exposure is dose-related (paint, solvents, petrochemicals, glue, rubber, cigarette smoke).
⚠️ Benzene links AA to later clonal evolution — chronic exposure can cause aplastic anemia AND predisposes to MDS/acute myeloid leukemia. Recognize the occupational-history clue on the exam.
6. Green radioactive crater
IONIZING RADIATION damages hematopoietic stem cell DNA and causes dose-dependent marrow aplasia (whole-body irradiation, radiotherapy, nuclear accidents). Like benzene, it also carries later leukemia/MDS risk.
⚠️ Radiation marrow injury is dose-dependent and predictable — contrast with the idiosyncratic, dose-independent drug reactions on the pharmacy shelf.
7. Shattering DNA
Common final pathway: these exposures (chemicals, radiation, some drugs) damage hematopoietic stem-cell DNA, depleting the stem-cell pool and producing a hypocellular 'wasteland' marrow.
⚠️ DNA-damage/short-telomere biology overlaps with INHERITED syndromes — short telomeres (dyskeratosis congenita) and Fanconi-pathway DNA-repair defects can masquerade as acquired AA, so chromosome-breakage and telomere testing are done in young patients.
8. Hepatitis liver
POST-HEPATITIS aplastic anemia: severe AA can follow an episode of acute hepatitis, characteristically SERONEGATIVE (non-A, non-B, non-C) hepatitis, classically in a young male, typically 1–3 months after the hepatitis resolves.
⚠️ The hepatitis is usually NOT one of the identifiable viral serotypes (A/B/C) — it is seronegative. This is an immune-mediated phenomenon and responds to immunosuppression like other AA.
9. Parvovirus / EBV
Viral triggers of marrow failure: EBV, CMV, and HIV can cause aplasia. PARVOVIRUS B19 is the classic erythroid-specific suppressor causing PURE RED CELL APLASIA (transient aplastic crisis in chronic hemolysis; chronic PRCA in immunocompromised).
⚠️ Don't confuse the two: Parvovirus B19 → PURE RED CELL APLASIA (isolated anemia, giant pronormoblasts), NOT pancytopenic aplastic anemia. True pancytopenic AA points more to EBV/seronegative hepatitis/idiopathic immune disease.
10. Immune sentinels tower
Core PATHOGENESIS: autoreactive cytotoxic CD8 T cells, driven by IFN-γ and TNF-α, attack and destroy hematopoietic stem/progenitor cells via Fas-mediated apoptosis. This immune mechanism is why ATG + cyclosporine (immunosuppression) restores hematopoiesis.
⚠️ AA is NOT primarily a stem-cell-intrinsic defect in most acquired cases — it is an IMMUNE attack on the marrow. That distinction underlies the entire treatment strategy.
11. Thymoma flag
Immune-dysregulation associations: THYMOMA (and hypogammaglobulinemia) is classically tied to PURE RED CELL APLASIA, reflecting T-cell-mediated marrow injury; the same autoimmune theme underlies aplastic anemia.
⚠️ Thymoma's strongest hematologic association is PRCA (look for thymoma + isolated anemia → image the mediastinum / consider thymectomy), not full pancytopenic AA — a common distractor.
12. Peaceful Night Hotel >50%
PNH OVERLAP: a small paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria clone is detectable by flow cytometry in well over half of acquired AA patients at diagnosis. Screen every AA patient for a PNH clone (flow for CD55/CD59 loss).
⚠️ A PNH clone in AA does not necessarily mean clinical hemolytic PNH — but it supports the immune pathogenesis (GPI-deficient cells escape T-cell attack) and must be monitored for clonal evolution and thrombosis.
13. PIG-A anchor
The PNH clone arises from an acquired somatic PIGA mutation (X-linked) in a hematopoietic stem cell, which abolishes the GPI anchor and therefore the complement regulators CD55 and CD59, causing complement-mediated intravascular hemolysis.
⚠️ PIGA mutation removes GPI-anchored CD55/CD59 → unchecked complement → intravascular hemolysis, thrombosis, and cytopenias. PNH, AA, and MDS form a clonal-marrow-failure spectrum that can evolve into one another.
