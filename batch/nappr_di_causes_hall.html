📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Know the cause then pick the treatment
Polyuria/polydipsia management hinges on classifying the cause first: AVP deficiency (central), AVP resistance (nephrogenic), or primary polydipsia. Each has a distinct therapy — DDAVP, cause removal, vs water restriction.
⚠️ Treating before classifying is dangerous: DDAVP helps central DI but worsens primary polydipsia (hyponatremia) and is useless in nephrogenic DI.
2. AVP deficiency wing (central)
Central DI causes cluster as trauma/surgery, tumor, infiltrative, infectious, and idiopathic/autoimmune. AVP synthesis or storage fails; serum Na trends high; treat with desmopressin.
⚠️ AVP deficiency = central DI, the DDAVP-responsive type — confusing the wing with resistance leads to the wrong drug.
3. Trauma / surgery
Head trauma or pituitary/hypothalamic surgery is a leading central DI cause; may follow a classic triphasic pattern (DI → SIADH → permanent DI). Monitor Na and urine output closely post-op.
⚠️ Post-neurosurgical patients can swing from DI to transient SIADH (hyponatremia) and back — fixed DDAVP dosing without Na monitoring causes dangerous swings.
4. Tumor
Craniopharyngioma, germinoma, pituitary macroadenoma, and metastases (breast/lung) damage the hypothalamic-stalk axis to cause central DI. MRI localizes the lesion after copeptin confirms central type.
⚠️ A pituitary adenoma itself rarely causes DI; stalk/hypothalamic involvement (or post-op) does — pure anterior adenomas more often cause hormone excess/deficiency, not DI.
5. Infectious / infiltrative
Infiltrative/granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis, Langerhans cell histiocytosis, tuberculosis, hypophysitis) and infections damage the hypothalamic-neurohypophyseal axis. Stalk thickening on MRI is a clue.
⚠️ Stalk thickening + DI in a young patient suggests histiocytosis/germinoma/sarcoid — don't dismiss isolated central DI as idiopathic without imaging.
6. AVP resistance wing (nephrogenic)
Nephrogenic DI = kidney resistance to AVP from drugs, electrolytes, intrinsic renal disease, or congenital channel defects. Treat by removing the cause, low-solute diet, thiazide, and amiloride (for lithium).
⚠️ AVP resistance does NOT respond to DDAVP — the central drug fails here; the diagnostic clue is high baseline copeptin.
7. Lithium — most common acquired nephrogenic cause
Chronic lithium accumulates in principal cells via ENaC, downregulating AQP2 to cause nephrogenic DI (often partial, sometimes irreversible). Amiloride blocks ENaC entry; thiazide reduces volume/urine output.
⚠️ In lithium NDI use amiloride to block ENaC uptake; never water-restrict a polyuric DI patient, and monitor lithium levels when adding a thiazide (toxicity risk).
8. Hypercalcemia / hypokalemia
Hypercalcemia and hypokalemia impair medullary concentrating gradient and AQP2, causing reversible acquired nephrogenic DI. Correcting the electrolyte restores concentrating ability.
⚠️ Electrolyte-induced nephrogenic DI is reversible — labeling it congenital/idiopathic misses an easy fix (normalize Ca/K).
9. Congenital — V2 receptor / AQP2 (sickle, etc.)
Congenital nephrogenic DI: X-linked V2-receptor mutation (most common, boys) or autosomal AQP2 defects. Sickle cell disease damages the medulla causing concentrating defects. Presents in infancy with failure to thrive.
⚠️ X-linked V2 mutation presents in male infants — a congenital cause is unresponsive to DDAVP, unlike acquired electrolyte causes that reverse.
10. Metabolic / intrinsic renal
Intrinsic tubulointerstitial disease, obstruction relief, and amyloid impair the medullary gradient and AVP responsiveness, producing acquired nephrogenic DI. Often partial and accompanied by other tubular defects.
⚠️ Post-obstructive nephrogenic DI is self-limited tubular dysfunction — over-aggressive fluid replacement perpetuates the diuresis.
11. Primary polydipsia wing
Primary polydipsia = excessive water intake (psychogenic, dipsogenic, or iatrogenic) suppressing AVP. Low-normal serum Na, dilute urine that concentrates with deprivation. Treat with fluid restriction, not DDAVP.
⚠️ Primary polydipsia presents with LOW serum Na (vs high-normal in DI) — giving DDAVP here precipitates severe hyponatremia.
12. Dipsogenic lesion
Dipsogenic polydipsia stems from a lowered thirst threshold (hypothalamic lesion, sarcoid, MS) driving excess drinking despite normal/low osmolality. Distinguish from psychogenic and from true DI.
⚠️ A reset thirst threshold mimics DI but the AVP/copeptin axis is intact — fluid restriction (carefully) helps, DDAVP harms.
13. Health-conscious / habitual high intake
Habitual high water intake (fitness 'drink more water' culture, medication regimens) can produce iatrogenic primary polydipsia with dilute polyuria and low-normal Na. History reveals the volume of intake.
⚠️ A well-appearing patient drinking liters 'to stay hydrated' with low-normal Na is primary polydipsia — don't escalate to DDAVP.
14. Compulsive water intake (psychogenic)
Psychogenic polydipsia is common in psychiatric patients (schizophrenia); compulsive water drinking can cause acute hyponatremia/water intoxication if intake outpaces excretion.
⚠️ Acute psychogenic water intoxication is a medical emergency (seizures) — the issue is excess intake, not AVP deficiency; restrict water, never give DDAVP.
15. Diagnostic lab first
Always classify with serum Na, urine osm, and copeptin testing BEFORE selecting therapy. The three wings demand opposite treatments, so misclassification is harmful.
⚠️ Skipping the lab/copeptin step and treating empirically risks giving the exactly wrong therapy for the patient's wing.
