⚠️ Board traps 12
  1. IRP-1 / IRP-2 readers — WRONG: conflating the IRP/IRE system with hepcidin. IRP/IRE is intracellular and post-transcriptional (it tunes ferritin and transferrin-receptor synthesis WITHIN a cell); hepcidin is the SYSTEMIC hormone controlling ferroportin and whole-body iron. Boards test that these are two separate layers of control.
  2. LOW iron page — WRONG: thinking ferritin rises when cellular iron is low. When iron is scarce the IRP blocks ferritin translation, so ferritin FALLS and transferrin receptor RISES — the opposite of storage mode. (IRE position matters: 5' IRE on ferritin = translation block; 3' IREs on TfR1 = mRNA stabilization.)
  3. HIGH iron page — WRONG: expecting transferrin receptor to stay high when the cell is iron-replete. With abundant iron, TfR1 is DOWN (no need to import) while ferritin/ferroportin go UP — the inverse of the low-iron state. Mixing up the direction of these arrows is a classic exam error.
  4. Hepcidin (PHAGY/Ft cart) — WRONG: assuming a high ferritin always means iron overload that needs treatment. Macrophage iron HOARDING in inflammation also raises serum ferritin — distinguish true overload (high TSAT, hemochromatosis) from inflammatory ferritin elevation (low TSAT, low TIBC) before acting.
  5. CANCER / ACD alcove (red) — WRONG: expecting a LOW ferritin in anemia of chronic disease. Ferritin is HIGH/normal in ACD because iron is sequestered and ferritin is an acute-phase reactant; a low ferritin would instead indicate true iron deficiency. Low TIBC is the other tip-off separating ACD from IDA.
  6. ATHLETE alcove (yellow) — WRONG: treating dilutional pseudo-anemia with iron. If iron studies are normal, the low Hb is a benign hemodilution that needs no iron; only treat when ferritin/TSAT confirm true deficiency. Conversely, don't dismiss a fatigued athlete — check ferritin, since real iron loss is common.
  7. MASKED IDA (purple) — WRONG: ruling out iron deficiency because ferritin is 'normal' (e.g., 80) in a sick/inflamed patient. The inflammation-adjusted cutoff is higher, and ferritin can be falsely reassuring — order sTfR or marrow iron when the clinical picture still suggests deficiency.
  8. RACE / baseline (blue) — WRONG: applying a single Hb cutoff to everyone. A 'normal' value may be anemic for one patient (a drop from their baseline) and normal for another; conversely a low-normal value in a person with a constitutionally lower baseline may not be pathologic. Trend against the patient's own prior values.
  9. Insect-bite / hookworm — WRONG: omitting parasite/exposure history in IDA from an endemic region or recent traveler. Hookworm IDA is treated by ERADICATING the parasite plus iron repletion — giving iron alone without deworming lets blood loss continue and the anemia recur.
  10. Transfusion >200 / iron overload — WRONG: giving iron to a chronically transfused or thalassemia-major patient. They are iron-OVERLOADED, not deficient — added iron accelerates cardiac/hepatic damage. These patients need CHELATION, not supplementation; never reflexively replete iron based on anemia alone.
  11. Screen first / 6 wk — WRONG: treating IDA and stopping there without an evaluation for the cause. Correcting the anemia while leaving an undiagnosed colon cancer or ulcer to progress is the classic board pitfall — in these populations, IDA is a colon cancer screen until proven otherwise.
  12. Pregnancy supplementation — WRONG: interpreting the mild physiologic Hb dip of pregnancy as no concern, or conversely treating it as severe disease. Pregnancy causes dilutional pseudo-anemia from plasma-volume expansion, but TRUE iron deficiency is also very common — confirm with ferritin and supplement rather than assuming it is purely dilutional.
📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. IRP-1 / IRP-2 readers
Iron regulatory proteins (IRP-1, IRP-2) bind iron-response elements (IREs) on mRNA to set the cell's iron-handling proteins post-transcriptionally based on intracellular iron. When cell iron is LOW, IRPs are active and bind IREs; when iron is HIGH, IRPs disengage. This is CELLULAR (intracellular) regulation, distinct from systemic hepcidin control.
⚠️ WRONG: conflating the IRP/IRE system with hepcidin. IRP/IRE is intracellular and post-transcriptional (it tunes ferritin and transferrin-receptor synthesis WITHIN a cell); hepcidin is the SYSTEMIC hormone controlling ferroportin and whole-body iron. Boards test that these are two separate layers of control.
2. LOW iron page
LOW cellular iron → active IRP binds IREs → STABILIZES transferrin-receptor (TfR1) mRNA (more receptor → pull iron IN) and BLOCKS translation of ferritin mRNA (less storage). Net effect: the cell maximizes iron uptake and minimizes sequestration.
⚠️ WRONG: thinking ferritin rises when cellular iron is low. When iron is scarce the IRP blocks ferritin translation, so ferritin FALLS and transferrin receptor RISES — the opposite of storage mode. (IRE position matters: 5' IRE on ferritin = translation block; 3' IREs on TfR1 = mRNA stabilization.)
3. HIGH iron page
HIGH cellular iron → IRPs RELEASE the IREs → ferritin and ferroportin mRNAs are translated (store and export iron) while TfR1 mRNA is degraded (stop importing). ALAS2 (erythroid heme-synthesis enzyme) is also translated, channeling iron into heme.
⚠️ WRONG: expecting transferrin receptor to stay high when the cell is iron-replete. With abundant iron, TfR1 is DOWN (no need to import) while ferritin/ferroportin go UP — the inverse of the low-iron state. Mixing up the direction of these arrows is a classic exam error.
4. Hepcidin (PHAGY/Ft cart)
Macrophages recycle iron from senescent RBCs and either store it as ferritin or export it via ferroportin. Hepcidin is the systemic gatekeeper: HIGH hepcidin degrades ferroportin so macrophages HOARD iron (ferritin rises, serum iron falls) — the engine of anemia of chronic disease.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming a high ferritin always means iron overload that needs treatment. Macrophage iron HOARDING in inflammation also raises serum ferritin — distinguish true overload (high TSAT, hemochromatosis) from inflammatory ferritin elevation (low TSAT, low TIBC) before acting.
5. CANCER / ACD alcove (red)
Inflammation and malignancy raise IL-6 → hepcidin → iron trapped in macrophages. Iron studies in ACD: serum iron LOW, TIBC LOW, ferritin HIGH/normal, TSAT low, sTfR normal. Usually normocytic, can be mildly microcytic. The cure is treating the underlying disease.
⚠️ WRONG: expecting a LOW ferritin in anemia of chronic disease. Ferritin is HIGH/normal in ACD because iron is sequestered and ferritin is an acute-phase reactant; a low ferritin would instead indicate true iron deficiency. Low TIBC is the other tip-off separating ACD from IDA.
6. ATHLETE alcove (yellow)
Endurance athletes can show a MILD apparent anemia from two mechanisms: plasma-volume expansion causing dilutional 'sports anemia' (a false low Hb with normal red-cell mass), AND true iron loss (foot-strike hemolysis, GI microbleeding, sweat losses, hematuria) plus exercise-induced hepcidin spikes.
⚠️ WRONG: treating dilutional pseudo-anemia with iron. If iron studies are normal, the low Hb is a benign hemodilution that needs no iron; only treat when ferritin/TSAT confirm true deficiency. Conversely, don't dismiss a fatigued athlete — check ferritin, since real iron loss is common.
7. MASKED IDA (purple)
Concurrent inflammation can falsely NORMALIZE or RAISE ferritin and MASK coexisting iron deficiency. To unmask it, use markers unaffected by inflammation: soluble transferrin receptor (sTfR), the sTfR/log-ferritin index, reticulocyte Hb (CHr), or a bone-marrow iron stain. In inflammation, a ferritin up to ~70–100 may still mean depleted stores.
⚠️ WRONG: ruling out iron deficiency because ferritin is 'normal' (e.g., 80) in a sick/inflamed patient. The inflammation-adjusted cutoff is higher, and ferritin can be falsely reassuring — order sTfR or marrow iron when the clinical picture still suggests deficiency.
8. RACE / baseline (blue)
The reference Hb threshold for 'anemia' varies by sex, age, and ancestry (WHO: <13 g/dL men, <12 g/dL non-pregnant women, <11 in pregnancy). Individuals of African ancestry may run slightly lower baseline Hb. Knowing a patient's own baseline matters as much as the population cutoff.
⚠️ WRONG: applying a single Hb cutoff to everyone. A 'normal' value may be anemic for one patient (a drop from their baseline) and normal for another; conversely a low-normal value in a person with a constitutionally lower baseline may not be pathologic. Trend against the patient's own prior values.
9. Insect-bite / hookworm
Hookworm (Ancylostoma duodenale, Necator americanus) and other GI parasites cause chronic intestinal blood loss and are the leading cause of IDA in the developing world. Look for travel/endemic exposure, eosinophilia, and stool ova/parasites; treat with albendazole/mebendazole plus iron.
⚠️ WRONG: omitting parasite/exposure history in IDA from an endemic region or recent traveler. Hookworm IDA is treated by ERADICATING the parasite plus iron repletion — giving iron alone without deworming lets blood loss continue and the anemia recur.
10. Transfusion >200 / iron overload
Each unit of RBCs delivers ~200–250 mg iron and the body cannot excrete it, so chronic transfusion (and ineffective erythropoiesis in thalassemia/sideroblastic/MDS) causes iron OVERLOAD. Manage with iron chelation (deferasirox, deferoxamine, deferiprone), monitoring ferritin and cardiac/hepatic iron (MRI T2*).
⚠️ WRONG: giving iron to a chronically transfused or thalassemia-major patient. They are iron-OVERLOADED, not deficient — added iron accelerates cardiac/hepatic damage. These patients need CHELATION, not supplementation; never reflexively replete iron based on anemia alone.
11. Screen first / 6 wk
In men and postmenopausal women, newly found IDA mandates a search for the SOURCE first — bidirectional endoscopy (EGD + colonoscopy) for occult GI blood loss/malignancy, since right-sided colon cancer often presents purely as IDA. Repleting iron is secondary to localizing the bleed.
⚠️ WRONG: treating IDA and stopping there without an evaluation for the cause. Correcting the anemia while leaving an undiagnosed colon cancer or ulcer to progress is the classic board pitfall — in these populations, IDA is a colon cancer screen until proven otherwise.
12. Pregnancy supplementation
Pregnancy roughly triples iron demand (fetus, placenta, expanded maternal red-cell mass; ~27 mg/day recommended). Routine screening and prophylactic iron are standard; untreated maternal IDA risks preterm birth, low birth weight, and impaired infant neurodevelopment.
⚠️ WRONG: interpreting the mild physiologic Hb dip of pregnancy as no concern, or conversely treating it as severe disease. Pregnancy causes dilutional pseudo-anemia from plasma-volume expansion, but TRUE iron deficiency is also very common — confirm with ferritin and supplement rather than assuming it is purely dilutional.
