📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Check PTH first
The opening move in every hypercalcemia case: confirm it is real (correct for albumin / ionized Ca), then measure PTH FIRST. High/normal PTH = PTH-mediated (primary hyperPTH, FHH); suppressed PTH = PTH-independent (malignancy via PTHrP, granulomatous 1,25-D, vitamin D, etc.). Primary hyperPTH and malignancy together cause ~90% of cases.
⚠️ WRONG: ordering imaging (sestamibi/neck US) or PTHrP before PTH. PTH is the branch-point that defines the entire algorithm; everything else is downstream and only interpretable once you know whether PTH is suppressed.
2. Primary hyperPTH
Primary hyperparathyroidism = #1 OUTPATIENT cause: an autonomous adenoma (~85%) drives high Ca, LOW phosphate, high/normal PTH, and high urine calcium. Most are asymptomatic incidental findings. Parathyroidectomy is curative; indications include Ca >1 above ULN, age <50, T-score ≤ −2.5/fracture, or eGFR <60/stones. Screen for MEN1/MEN2A if young or multigland.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming it is also the top INPATIENT cause. In a hospitalized/ill patient, hypercalcemia is malignancy until proven otherwise — primary hyperPTH dominates the outpatient/incidental setting.
3. PTHrP squamous/breast
Humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy = #1 INPATIENT cause: tumor PTHrP binds the PTH receptor → high Ca, LOW phosphate, but SUPPRESSED native PTH. Classic tumors: squamous cell (lung, head/neck), renal, bladder, and breast. It indicates advanced cancer and poor prognosis; treat the calcium emergently and the tumor definitively.
⚠️ WRONG: expecting PTH to be elevated 'because PTHrP acts like PTH.' Intact PTH is appropriately SUPPRESSED — high Ca + low PTH + low phosphate + a known cancer = PTHrP-mediated, confirmed by an elevated PTHrP level.
4. Granulomatous 1,25-D
Granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis, TB, fungal) and some lymphomas express unregulated macrophage 1-alpha-hydroxylase → excess 1,25-OH vitamin D independent of PTH → high Ca, suppressed PTH, often hypercalciuria. It responds to GLUCOCORTICOIDS, which shut off macrophage 1-alpha-hydroxylase. Patients should avoid sunlight and vitamin D.
⚠️ WRONG: reaching for bisphosphonates first or supplementing vitamin D. Steroids are the targeted therapy for granulomatous hypercalcemia, and vitamin D/sun exposure worsens it by feeding 1,25-D production.
5. Vitamin D → gut/bone
Active vitamin D (1,25-D) raises calcium by increasing GUT absorption of Ca and phosphate and promoting bone resorption. Excess from intoxication (high 25-D) or granulomatous/lymphoma 1,25-D production causes hypercalcemia WITH hyperphosphatemia and suppressed PTH. Measure 25-D for intoxication, 1,25-D for granulomatous disease.
⚠️ WRONG: confusing the two vitamin D forms. Supplement TOXICITY elevates 25-OH-D; GRANULOMATOUS disease elevates 1,25-D. Ordering the wrong metabolite leads to a missed or misattributed diagnosis.
6. CaSR / suppresses PTH
The calcium-sensing receptor (CaSR) on parathyroid and renal cells senses serum calcium: when Ca is high it SUPPRESSES PTH and increases urinary calcium loss (normal negative feedback). The drug cinacalcet is a CaSR agonist (calcimimetic) that exploits this to lower PTH and Ca in hyperPTH/parathyroid carcinoma.
⚠️ WRONG: forgetting that an INACTIVATING CaSR mutation = FHH, where the receptor under-senses calcium → high Ca, high/normal PTH, and LOW urine calcium (FECa <1%) — benign, do NOT operate.
7. Milk-alkali
Milk-alkali syndrome: ingestion of large amounts of calcium plus ABSORBABLE alkali (classically calcium carbonate antacids/supplements) → the triad of HYPERCALCEMIA, METABOLIC ALKALOSIS, and ACUTE KIDNEY INJURY, with suppressed PTH. It is resurging with OTC calcium carbonate use. Treat with hydration and stopping the calcium/alkali.
⚠️ WRONG: attributing hypercalcemia + alkalosis + AKI to a primary parathyroid or malignant cause. The medication history of heavy calcium carbonate (Tums) use plus this triad points to milk-alkali — stop the supplement.
8. Thiazide
Thiazides INCREASE distal tubular calcium reabsorption → lower urine calcium and modestly higher serum calcium (which is why they prevent calcium stones). They can also unmask underlying primary hyperPTH. Hold the thiazide and recheck calcium before extensive workup.
⚠️ WRONG: swapping thiazide and loop effects. Thiazides RAISE calcium (used for stone prevention); loop diuretics LOWER calcium. 'Loops Lose calcium' — and loops are NOT first-line for hypercalcemia.
9. FHH low urine Ca
Familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia: autosomal-dominant inactivating CaSR mutation → mild lifelong hypercalcemia with high/normal PTH but LOW urinary calcium. The discriminator is the calcium-to-creatinine clearance ratio (FECa <0.01). It is benign — no surgery, no treatment, family screening only.
⚠️ WRONG: sending an FHH patient to parathyroidectomy as if it were primary hyperPTH. Surgery fails to correct the calcium. Check 24-h urine calcium/FECa — <1% says FHH (leave it alone), >2% says primary hyperPTH.
10. Osteolytic / immobilization
Local osteolytic hypercalcemia: bone metastases (breast, myeloma) release calcium via local RANKL/cytokine-driven osteoclast activation — PTH and PTHrP can be normal. Prolonged IMMOBILIZATION (especially with high baseline bone turnover, e.g., Paget, young, or extensive injury) similarly liberates skeletal calcium.
⚠️ WRONG: ruling out malignancy because PTHrP is normal. Lytic mets and myeloma cause hypercalcemia LOCALLY (cytokines/RANKL), not via circulating PTHrP — pursue SPEP/UPEP and skeletal imaging.
11. Stones/bones/groans/psych
Symptom mnemonic: STONES (nephrolithiasis, nephrogenic DI/polyuria), BONES (bone pain, osteitis fibrosa cystica), GROANS (abdominal pain, constipation, pancreatitis, peptic ulcers), and PSYCHIATRIC OVERTONES (fatigue, depression, confusion, coma). ECG shows a SHORT QT interval; severe cases cause bradyarrhythmias.
⚠️ WRONG: pairing hypercalcemia with a LONG QT. Hyper-calcemia SHORTENS the QT; hypo-calcemia prolongs it. Swapping these is a classic ECG distractor.
12. IV normal saline first
Step 1 of acute treatment: aggressive ISOTONIC SALINE (often 200–300 mL/h) to reverse the dehydration of hypercalcemia and promote calciuria. Hydration plus correcting volume is the foundation before any loop diuretic. Monitor for fluid overload, especially in CKD/CHF.
⚠️ WRONG: starting a loop diuretic before volume repletion. Loops in a volume-depleted patient worsen dehydration and calcium; saline comes FIRST, loops only later if the patient becomes volume overloaded.
13. Calcitonin rapid
Calcitonin (4 IU/kg SC/IM) inhibits osteoclasts and increases urinary calcium — the FASTEST agent, lowering Ca within hours. But it is short-lived: TACHYPHYLAXIS develops in 48–72 h. Use it as a BRIDGE alongside saline while the bisphosphonate (slow onset) takes effect.
⚠️ WRONG: relying on calcitonin as monotherapy/durable control. It loses effect within 2–3 days; it must be paired with a bisphosphonate or denosumab for sustained calcium lowering.
14. Bisphosphonate/denosumab
For durable control: IV bisphosphonate (zoledronic acid 4 mg, peak effect 2–4 days) is first-line for malignant hypercalcemia; DENOSUMAB (anti-RANKL) is preferred in renal failure (no renal dosing) or bisphosphonate-refractory disease. Both lower bone resorption; watch for hypocalcemia, jaw osteonecrosis, and (bisphosphonate) renal toxicity.
⚠️ WRONG: giving IV bisphosphonate in significant renal impairment or expecting immediate effect. Onset is 2–4 days (hence the calcitonin bridge), and in renal failure denosumab is the safer antiresorptive.
15. Loop only if overloaded
Loop diuretics (furosemide) promote urinary calcium loss but are reserved for patients who become VOLUME OVERLOADED after saline (e.g., CHF/CKD) — never as initial therapy. Routine loop use in a dehydrated patient is harmful. Saline + antiresorptive is the real backbone.
⚠️ WRONG: choosing 'furosemide' as the first-line treatment for hypercalcemia. This outdated answer is a trap — loops are adjuncts only after volume repletion in fluid-overloaded patients.
