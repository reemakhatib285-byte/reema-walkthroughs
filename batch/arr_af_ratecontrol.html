📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Rate control first (stable)
In a hemodynamically stable patient, rate control is the initial strategy: β-blockers or non-dihydropyridine CCBs (diltiazem/verapamil) first-line, digoxin as add-on (especially with heart failure or hypotension). RACE-II showed lenient (<110) rate control is non-inferior to strict in many patients.
⚠️ WRONG: 'every new AF needs urgent rhythm control.' Stable patients are rate-controlled first; rhythm control is for persistent symptoms or specific indications.
2. Unstable → DC cardioversion
Hemodynamic instability (hypotension, ischemia, pulmonary edema, shock) from AF mandates immediate synchronized DC cardioversion regardless of duration — you don't wait for anticoagulation when the patient is crashing.
⚠️ WRONG: 'rate-control drugs first even if hypotensive.' AV-nodal blockers worsen shock; unstable AF needs electrical cardioversion now.
3. Avoid amiodarone if unknown duration
Amiodarone can chemically cardiovert AF; giving it when AF duration is >48 h or unknown (without anticoagulation/TEE) risks dislodging an atrial thrombus → embolic stroke.
⚠️ WRONG: 'amiodarone is a safe rate-control choice in any AF.' Its cardioverting effect makes it unsafe in unanticoagulated AF of uncertain duration.
4. Amiodarone — effective + SAFE in HF
Amiodarone is the most effective agent for maintaining sinus rhythm and is the one antiarrhythmic considered SAFE in structural heart disease and HFrEF (along with dofetilide). Long-term use carries thyroid, pulmonary, hepatic, and skin toxicity.
⚠️ WRONG: 'amiodarone is contraindicated in heart failure.' It is the preferred rhythm-control drug in HF; class IC and dronedarone are the ones to avoid.
5. Dronedarone — CONTRAINDICATED in HF
Dronedarone is contraindicated in decompensated/NYHA III-IV heart failure and permanent AF — ANDROMEDA and PALLAS trials showed increased mortality. Use only in lower-risk paroxysmal/persistent AF without significant HF.
⚠️ WRONG: 'dronedarone is a safer amiodarone for HF patients.' It increases mortality in HF/permanent AF.
6. Class IC — CONTRAINDICATED in structural HD
Class IC agents (flecainide, propafenone) are only for structurally normal hearts; in ischemic or structural heart disease they are proarrhythmic and increase mortality (CAST trial). Often combined with an AV-nodal blocker to prevent 1:1 flutter conduction.
⚠️ WRONG: 'flecainide is fine post-MI.' Class IC drugs are contraindicated in ischemic/structural heart disease.
7. Non-DHP CCB + BB → CHB
Combining a non-dihydropyridine CCB (verapamil/diltiazem) with a β-blocker stacks AV-nodal suppression → severe bradycardia, high-grade AV block, and hypotension.
⚠️ WRONG: 'verapamil plus metoprolol gives smoother rate control.' The combination risks complete heart block — avoid.
8. HFrEF patient → carvedilol
In AF with HFrEF, a β-blocker (e.g., carvedilol) provides rate control and mortality benefit and helps reverse tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy. Avoid non-DHP CCBs (negative inotropes) in reduced EF.
⚠️ WRONG: 'use diltiazem for rate control in HFrEF.' Non-DHP CCBs are negative inotropes — contraindicated in reduced EF; use a β-blocker.
9. Ablation first-line = young/paroxysmal/normal heart
Catheter (pulmonary-vein isolation) ablation is most effective — and increasingly first-line — in younger, symptomatic patients with paroxysmal AF and a structurally normal heart. In HFrEF, PV isolation improves outcomes (CASTLE-AF).
⚠️ WRONG: 'ablation works best for long-standing persistent AF in a dilated atrium.' Success is highest in paroxysmal AF with a normal-sized atrium.
10. PV isolation
Pulmonary-vein isolation electrically disconnects the PV triggers that initiate AF. Complications to know: PV stenosis, atrio-esophageal fistula, cardiac tamponade, phrenic nerve injury.
⚠️ WRONG: 'ablation cures AF permanently in one procedure for everyone.' Recurrence and repeat procedures are common, especially in persistent AF.
11. Typical flutter → CTI ablation >95%
Typical (cavotricuspid-isthmus-dependent) atrial flutter is cured by CTI ablation with >95% success — definitive therapy preferred over long-term antiarrhythmics. Flutter still carries AF-equivalent stroke risk and needs anticoagulation by CHA₂DS₂-VASc.
⚠️ WRONG: 'atrial flutter doesn't need anticoagulation.' Flutter carries the same thromboembolic risk as AF.
12. Propafenone → 1:1 flutter risk
Class IC drugs can slow the atrial flutter rate enough that the AV node conducts 1:1 → dangerously fast ventricular rates. Co-administer an AV-nodal blocker (β-blocker/CCB) when using class IC for AF/flutter.
⚠️ WRONG: 'slowing the atrial rate with flecainide always slows the ventricles.' Slower flutter can conduct 1:1, paradoxically speeding the ventricular rate.
