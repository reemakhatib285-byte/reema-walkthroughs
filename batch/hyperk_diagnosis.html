📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. K+ >= 5.5 start
Start the workup once serum K+ ≥ 5.5 mM confirms hyperkalemia (mild 5.5–6.0, moderate 6.1–6.9, severe ≥7.0). 98% of total body potassium is intracellular, so even small serum changes reflect large shifts; the staircase then asks: emergency? real? what mechanism?
⚠️ WRONG answer = treat aggressively off a single borderline value without confirming it is real and not pseudohyperkalemia. Confirm with a repeat sample (and check the ECG) before committing to an etiology — unless the patient already has emergency features.
2. Emergency first
Identify the emergency immediately: K+ ≥ 6.5 mM, ANY ECG changes, or a rapidly rising level → STABILIZE and TREAT FIRST (membrane stabilization with IV calcium gluconate/chloride, then shift K+ intracellularly with insulin+glucose and albuterol, then remove it with loop diuretics/dialysis). Don't wait for the full diagnostic workup.
⚠️ WRONG answer = complete the entire diagnostic algorithm (TTKG, fludrocortisone challenge) before treating a patient with K+ 7.2 and peaked T waves. Membrane stabilization and K+ lowering come FIRST; the etiologic workup follows once the patient is safe.
3. Real or fake?
Step 2 — exclude PSEUDOhyperkalemia (a spuriously high lab value from K+ released in vitro): hemolysis of the sample, prolonged tourniquet time or fist clenching, marked thrombocytosis, or leukocytosis. The patient is asymptomatic with a NORMAL ECG.
⚠️ WRONG answer = treat a 'high' potassium in a patient with thrombocytosis/leukocytosis and a normal ECG. If pseudohyperkalemia is suspected, redraw properly (a PLASMA sample, no tourniquet/fist clenching, prompt processing) — no treatment is needed. The discriminator: normal ECG + a clotting/cellular cause = fake.
4. Mechanism?
Once real, classify the mechanism into one of three buckets: transcellular SHIFT (redistribution out of cells), increased LOAD (intake/release), or decreased renal EXCRETION. This framing directs the next test (history/labs for shift and load; urine studies/TTKG for excretion).
⚠️ WRONG answer = anchor on one mechanism and stop. Hyperkalemia is often multifactorial (e.g. a CKD patient on an ACEi who develops acidosis). Work through all three buckets rather than attributing it to a single cause.
5. Shift / load
SHIFT (redistribution): metabolic acidosis (especially mineral/non-anion-gap), insulin deficiency/hyperglycemia, beta-blockers, digoxin toxicity (Na-K-ATPase block), succinylcholine, tumor lysis/rhabdomyolysis, and hyperkalemic periodic paralysis. LOAD: excess oral/IV intake, transfusion of stored blood, GI bleed reabsorption — load alone rarely causes hyperkalemia unless excretion is also impaired.
⚠️ WRONG answer = blame dietary potassium load in a patient with normal kidneys. Healthy kidneys excrete a large potassium load easily; significant hyperkalemia from load almost always requires concurrent impaired excretion (CKD, low aldosterone, K-sparing drugs).
6. Excretion most common
DECREASED RENAL EXCRETION is the most common true mechanism of sustained hyperkalemia. It arises from low GFR/low distal flow (advanced CKD, oliguric AKI) or from impaired distal secretion (hypoaldosteronism, aldosterone resistance, ENaC blockade). Urine studies and the TTKG help localize the defect.
⚠️ WRONG answer = stop at 'renal failure' as the explanation in a patient with only mild CKD. Mild-to-moderate CKD alone rarely causes hyperkalemia until GFR is quite low — look for a superimposed secretory defect (drugs, hypoaldosteronism, type 4 RTA).
7. Urine Na+ >= 25?
Check distal sodium delivery: distal K+ secretion via ENaC requires adequate Na+ reaching the collecting duct. Urine Na+ ≥ 25 mM indicates sufficient distal delivery, making the TTKG interpretable as a measure of aldosterone-driven secretion.
⚠️ WRONG answer = compute and trust a TTKG when urine Na+ < 25. Low distal Na+ delivery (CHF, hypovolemia, cirrhosis) itself limits K+ secretion and INVALIDATES the TTKG; here, restore perfusion/volume rather than chasing an aldosterone defect.
8. TTKG path
TTKG (transtubular potassium gradient) = (urine K+ / plasma K+) ÷ (urine osm / plasma osm). It estimates aldosterone-driven distal K+ secretion. Valid ONLY when urine osmolality > serum osmolality AND urine Na+ > 25 mM. A normal response to hyperkalemia is a TTKG > 8–10.
⚠️ WRONG answer = interpret a TTKG without first confirming its validity conditions (urine osm > serum, urine Na+ > 25). A 'low' TTKG from low flow or dilute urine will be misread as hypoaldosteronism. (Note: TTKG is a teaching/exam tool; it has fallen out of clinical favor but remains board-relevant.)
9. TTKG > 8 = flow
TTKG > 8 during hyperkalemia means the distal nephron IS secreting K+ appropriately (aldosterone and ENaC are working) — so the problem is inadequate distal FLOW/delivery: advanced CKD/ESRD or oliguric AKI. Management centers on flow (diuretics if responsive) or dialysis.
⚠️ WRONG answer = give fludrocortisone for a high-TTKG hyperkalemia. A TTKG > 8 already shows adequate aldosterone effect — more mineralocorticoid won't help; the issue is low flow/GFR, not hormone deficiency.
10. TTKG < 5 = aldo/tubule
TTKG < 5 in the face of hyperkalemia = inappropriately LOW distal K+ secretion → an aldosterone problem (deficiency or resistance), an ENaC defect, or a tubular/tubulointerstitial disorder. The next step is the fludrocortisone challenge to separate hormone deficiency from tubular resistance.
⚠️ WRONG answer = call any low TTKG 'hypoaldosteronism.' A low TTKG could be aldosterone DEFICIENCY (corrects with fludrocortisone) OR tubular RESISTANCE/ENaC blockade (does not correct). The fludrocortisone test is what distinguishes them.
11. Fludrocortisone test
9-alpha-fludrocortisone challenge: give exogenous mineralocorticoid and re-measure TTKG. If secretion (TTKG) corrects/rises, the collecting duct is responsive and the defect was aldosterone deficiency. If TTKG stays low, the tubule is resistant to mineralocorticoid.
⚠️ WRONG answer = use the fludrocortisone challenge before confirming the TTKG was valid (urine Na+ > 25, concentrated urine). Testing on an invalid TTKG gives uninterpretable results.
12. Corrects -> low aldo
If TTKG CORRECTS with fludrocortisone → aldosterone DEFICIENCY. Then check renin: HIGH renin/low aldosterone = primary adrenal failure (Addison disease, isolated hypoaldosteronism); LOW renin/low aldosterone = hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism (type 4 RTA), classically diabetic nephropathy, NSAIDs, or calcineurin inhibitors.
⚠️ WRONG answer = treat all aldosterone deficiency the same. The renin level branches it: low-renin (type 4 RTA, diabetes/NSAIDs) is managed differently (fludrocortisone, loop diuretic, low-K diet) than primary adrenal insufficiency (which also needs glucocorticoid replacement).
13. No correction -> tubule
If TTKG does NOT correct with fludrocortisone → tubular RESISTANCE to aldosterone: pseudohypoaldosteronism (PHA type 1/2), drugs blocking ENaC (amiloride, triamterene, trimethoprim, pentamidine), or tubulointerstitial disease (obstruction, sickle cell, lupus, amyloid).
⚠️ WRONG answer = keep escalating mineralocorticoid when the tubule won't respond. In aldosterone resistance, more fludrocortisone is futile — remove the offending ENaC-blocking drug and treat the underlying tubulointerstitial process; use a loop/thiazide diuretic to enhance K+ loss.
14. Lab evidence wall
Core lab panel for the workup: CBC (thrombocytosis/leukocytosis → pseudohyperkalemia), urine Na+/K+/osmolality (distal delivery and TTKG), BUN/creatinine (GFR/renal cause), bicarbonate (metabolic acidosis driving shift or type 4 RTA), and glucose (hyperglycemia/insulin-deficiency shift). Plus an ECG in every case.
⚠️ WRONG answer = order potassium-lowering treatment without an ECG and without these confirmatory labs. The ECG dictates urgency (and may be the only sign of life-threatening hyperkalemia), while the labs distinguish pseudo, shift, load, and excretion mechanisms.
