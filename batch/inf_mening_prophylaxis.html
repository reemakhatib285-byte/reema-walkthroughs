📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Close contact = gold star (VIP line)
Meningococcal chemoprophylaxis goes ONLY to CLOSE contacts: household members, intimate/kissing contacts, those sharing food/drink/utensils, dorm roommates, barracks-mates, and healthcare workers with unprotected exposure to respiratory secretions (intubation/suctioning without mask) in the 7 days before symptom onset. Goal: eradicate nasopharyngeal carriage and prevent secondary cases.
2. Casual contacts turned away (red X)
Casual contact does NOT warrant prophylaxis: sharing a classroom/office, riding the same bus, or brief bedside care under droplet precautions. Over-prescribing wastes resources and adds resistance/side-effect risk. Eligible agents are rifampin, ciprofloxacin, ceftriaxone (azithromycin as alternative).
⚠️ "give prophylaxis to the whole classroom/office/all hospital staff" -> wrong; casual contact doesn't qualify — restrict to true close contacts within the 7-day window.
3. Index case eradication on discharge
The index patient needs an eradication agent (rifampin, cipro, or ceftriaxone) before discharge UNLESS already treated with ceftriaxone/cefotaxime (which clear carriage). IV penicillin or ampicillin treats invasive disease but does NOT reliably eliminate nasopharyngeal carriage, so the patient could re-seed contacts.
⚠️ "recovered on IV penicillin, so no longer a carrier" -> wrong; penicillin doesn't eradicate carriage — without a dedicated eradication agent (or a 3rd-gen cephalosporin), the index case can still transmit on discharge.
4. Rifampin
Rifampin: adults 600mg PO q12h x2 days (4 doses); children ≥1mo 10 mg/kg q12h x2d. Counsel about orange-red discoloration of urine/tears/sweat (stains contact lenses) and CYP450 INDUCTION reducing efficacy of oral contraceptives, warfarin, and many drugs.
⚠️ "rifampin for a pregnant contact or someone on OCPs" -> wrong; avoided in pregnancy (use ceftriaxone) and its CYP450 induction can cause OCP failure.
5. Ciprofloxacin (one-and-done)
Ciprofloxacin 500mg PO single dose is convenient adult prophylaxis (no multi-dose course); azithromycin is the alternative where fluoroquinolone-resistant meningococcus circulates. Avoid fluoroquinolones in pregnancy and generally in young children (cartilage/tendon concerns).
⚠️ "default to single-dose cipro in a fluoroquinolone-resistant area or for a pregnant/pediatric contact" -> wrong; use azithromycin or ceftriaxone in resistant regions, and ceftriaxone in pregnancy — blanket cipro can fail.
6. Ceftriaxone (single IM, pregnancy)
Ceftriaxone 250mg IM single dose (125mg for children <15) is the PREFERRED prophylaxis in PREGNANCY and a reliable single-dose option for anyone. As a 3rd-gen cephalosporin it both treats and eradicates carriage, useful when rifampin or fluoroquinolones are contraindicated.
⚠️ "rifampin or cipro for a pregnant close contact" -> wrong; rifampin and fluoroquinolones are avoided in pregnancy — use single-dose IM ceftriaxone.
7. Penicillin trap (no carriage eradication)
Penicillin and amoxicillin do NOT reliably eradicate meningococcal nasopharyngeal carriage (poor secretion levels), so they are NOT used for chemoprophylaxis. Acceptable agents: rifampin, ciprofloxacin, ceftriaxone (azithromycin alternative).
⚠️ "oral penicillin or amoxicillin for contact prophylaxis" -> wrong; penicillin treats invasive disease but leaves carriage intact — pick rifampin, cipro, or ceftriaxone.
8. Prophylaxis timing (past 24h still OK)
Give prophylaxis ASAP, ideally within 24h of index-case identification, but it remains worthwhile for close contacts up to ~14 days after exposure. The benefit is not confined to the first 24 hours.
⚠️ "decline prophylaxis because >24h has passed" -> wrong; prophylaxis still reduces secondary cases when given within ~2 weeks — withholding it past day 1 misapplies the rule.
9. Enteroviruses #1 viral cause
Enteroviruses (coxsackie, echovirus) cause >85% of viral (aseptic) meningitis, peaking SUMMER/FALL via fecal-oral spread, sometimes with hand-foot-mouth, herpangina, or myopericarditis. CSF is lymphocytic with normal glucose; diagnose by CSF RT-PCR. Management is SUPPORTIVE — no specific antiviral.
⚠️ "start antibiotics or acyclovir for a well patient with summertime lymphocytic/normal-glucose CSF and enterovirus PCR" -> wrong; enteroviral meningitis is self-limited — supportive care suffices (empiric antibiotics reasonable only until bacterial excluded).
10. HSV-2 = meningitis (Mollaret)
HSV-2 typically causes (often recurrent) aseptic MENINGITIS — recurrent lymphocytic 'Mollaret' meningitis is the classic association, frequently with genital herpes. Diagnose by CSF HSV PCR; treat with IV acyclovir.
⚠️ "HSV-1 causes meningitis, HSV-2 causes encephalitis" -> wrong; the boards test the reverse — HSV-2 -> recurrent meningitis (Mollaret), HSV-1 -> temporal-lobe encephalitis.
11. HSV-1 = temporal-lobe encephalitis
HSV-1 typically causes ENCEPHALITIS with temporal-lobe involvement (hemorrhagic; MRI/EEG temporal changes; RBCs in CSF). Treat with IV acyclovir; start empirically when suspected because delay risks irreversible damage.
⚠️ "wait for HSV PCR before treating suspected HSV-1 encephalitis" -> wrong; start empiric acyclovir immediately — waiting risks irreversible temporal-lobe damage.
12. VZV — zoster sine herpete
VZV can cause meningitis, encephalitis, vasculitis/stroke, and myelitis, and may occur WITHOUT rash (zoster sine herpete), especially in older/immunocompromised patients. Diagnose by CSF VZV PCR and/or CSF VZV IgM; treat with IV acyclovir.
⚠️ "no vesicular rash, so exclude VZV CNS disease" -> wrong; zoster sine herpete means VZV meningitis/encephalitis can present with no rash — send CSF VZV PCR.
13. West Nile — CSF IgM, flaccid paralysis
West Nile is mosquito-borne (Culex) with birds as reservoir, peaks late summer, hits OLDER adults, and classically causes acute asymmetric FLACCID paralysis (anterior horn, polio-like). CSF is lymphocytic with normal glucose; diagnose by CSF (and serum) IgM.
⚠️ "order CSF viral PCR alone to diagnose West Nile" -> wrong; WNV PCR has LOW CSF sensitivity (viremia clears before neuroinvasion) — CSF IgM antibody is the test of choice.
14. CMV in immunocompromised — ganciclovir
CMV meningoencephalitis (also ventriculoencephalitis, polyradiculopathy, retinitis) occurs almost exclusively in the IMMUNOSUPPRESSED — advanced HIV (CD4 <50), transplant recipients. Diagnose by CSF CMV PCR; treat with ganciclovir (often + foscarnet) plus immune reconstitution. Acyclovir and ganciclovir are NOT interchangeable: acyclovir for HSV/VZV, ganciclovir for CMV (acyclovir fails because CMV lacks thymidine kinase).
⚠️ "acyclovir for CMV (or CMV high on the differential in a healthy host)" -> wrong; acyclovir has no meaningful CMV activity (CMV lacks thymidine kinase), and CMV CNS disease is essentially limited to severe immunosuppression — in a healthy host think enterovirus or HSV-2.
