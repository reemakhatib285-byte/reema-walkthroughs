📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Cold-air snowball fight
Cold, dry air is a classic asthma trigger: rapid heat and water loss from the airway mucosa during hyperventilation activates mast cells and sensory nerves, causing reflex bronchoconstriction. It frequently combines with exercise and viral season in winter to provoke attacks.
2. Exercise / running kids
Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB) peaks 5–15 minutes after exertion (worse in cold/dry air) from airway dehydration and osmotic mast-cell activation, then resolves over 30–60 minutes. Prevention: a SABA 10–15 min before exercise, or — preferred long-term — regular ICS or ICS-formoterol, plus warm-up and an LTRA option.
⚠️ Treat EIB by optimizing CONTROLLER therapy (ICS/ICS-formoterol) rather than relying on daily/chronic SABA — frequent SABA need before exercise signals inadequately controlled underlying asthma. Isolated EIB in an athlete may need confirmation by exercise/eucapnic challenge.
3. Fireball / shortness of breath
Smoke (tobacco and wood/biomass), fumes, occupational irritants, and ambient air pollution (ozone, particulates) are environmental triggers that inflame airways and precipitate acute dyspnea and exacerbations. Pollution spikes correlate with ED visits for asthma.
4. Snowman with cannon (airway obstruction)
The central event of an exacerbation is acute, largely reversible airflow obstruction from bronchial smooth-muscle spasm, mucosal edema, and mucus plugging — producing wheeze, prolonged expiration, air-trapping/hyperinflation, and a falling PEF/FEV1.
5. Blue monster (hypoxia / cyanosis)
Severe exacerbation causes ventilation-perfusion mismatch and hypoxemia (cyanosis, falling SpO2). Early attacks show a respiratory ALKALOSIS from hyperventilation (low pCO2); as the patient tires, pCO2 rises toward and above normal — a normal/rising pCO2 is a red flag for impending respiratory failure.
⚠️ Cyanosis, silent chest, exhaustion, drowsiness, and a 'normalizing' or rising pCO2 are LATE, life-threatening signs — escalate to high-flow O2, continuous bronchodilators, IV steroids/magnesium, and ICU/intubation rather than reassuring on a 'normal' gas.
6. Spilled bricks/blocks (inflammation)
Beyond bronchospasm, exacerbations involve airway inflammation with mucosal edema and tenacious mucus plugging (sometimes Curschmann's spirals and Charcot-Leyden crystals on sputum), which is why systemic corticosteroids — not bronchodilators alone — are essential to resolve an attack.
7. Snowballs as allergens/triggers
Aeroallergens — house dust mite, pollen, pet dander, cockroach, and mold — are common provoking triggers in atopic asthma, driving IgE-mediated mast-cell degranulation. Allergen avoidance and, in selected cases, immunotherapy or anti-IgE therapy reduce exacerbations.
8. Heart / red gift (cardiac mimic)
'Cardiac asthma' — wheezing from pulmonary congestion in left heart failure — can mimic an asthma exacerbation, especially in older patients. Clues favoring HF: orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, peripheral edema, raised JVP, S3, bibasilar crackles, and elevated BNP.
⚠️ New-onset wheeze in an ELDERLY patient with cardiac risk factors may be heart failure, not asthma — treating it with bronchodilators alone (and missing diuresis) is the wrong move.
9. Green sled / toy (viral trigger)
Viral upper-respiratory infections (rhinovirus most commonly, also RSV, influenza) are the single most frequent trigger of asthma exacerbations across all ages, accounting for the majority of attacks. This drives the winter peak in asthma hospitalizations.
⚠️ Most exacerbations are VIRAL — antibiotics are not routinely indicated and should be reserved for clear evidence of bacterial infection (e.g., fever with purulent sputum and consolidation). Annual influenza vaccination is recommended.
10. Snowmen building (chronic remodeling)
Repeated or chronically uncontrolled inflammation leads to airway remodeling — subepithelial fibrosis, smooth-muscle hypertrophy, goblet-cell hyperplasia, and angiogenesis — producing a partially fixed obstructive component that does not fully reverse with bronchodilators.
11. Warm tent / shelter (treatment refuge)
Acute exacerbation management: controlled O2 to target SpO2 93–95% (88–92% if at COPD-overlap risk), repeated/continuous inhaled SABA (± ipratropium for severe), and early SYSTEMIC corticosteroids (oral prednisolone ~40–50 mg ×5–7 days, equal to IV). Add IV magnesium sulfate for severe/refractory attacks; consider escalation to NIV/intubation if failing.
12. Person resting indoors (recovery)
Recovery is not just symptom relief: every exacerbation mandates a review of inhaler technique, adherence, triggers, and a written action plan, and a STEP-UP of controller therapy (e.g., start or increase ICS / move to ICS-formoterol MART) to reduce future risk. Arrange follow-up within 2–7 days.
⚠️ Don't treat the acute attack and discharge on SABA alone — failing to start/intensify ICS controller therapy after an exacerbation is a key board error and a driver of recurrent attacks and asthma death.
