📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. G6PD → NADPH shield
G6PD is the rate-limiting enzyme of the pentose phosphate pathway, generating NADPH. NADPH regenerates reduced glutathione (GSH), the RBC's only defense against oxidative stress. RBCs have no other NADPH source, so G6PD deficiency leaves them defenseless against oxidants.
⚠️ Thinking RBCs have backup NADPH sources; the HMP shunt via G6PD is the sole RBC NADPH supply.
2. Glutathione / catalase
GSH and catalase neutralize H2O2/ROS. Without NADPH, GSH cannot be regenerated, oxidants accumulate, and hemoglobin is oxidized and precipitates. The pathway: G6PD → NADPH → GSH → detoxify peroxide. Loss of G6PD breaks this chain.
⚠️ Confusing the defect site; G6PD deficiency fails NADPH regeneration upstream, not catalase itself.
3. Heinz bodies
Oxidized denatured hemoglobin precipitates as Heinz bodies (seen with crystal/methyl violet supravital stain). Splenic macrophages pluck them out, leaving membrane defects. Heinz bodies are the upstream lesion preceding bite cells.
⚠️ Looking for Heinz bodies on a routine Wright stain; they require a SUPRAVITAL stain (crystal violet), not standard smear.
4. Bite cells
After the spleen removes Heinz bodies, the RBC has a scooped-out 'bite cell' (degmacyte). Bite cells and blister cells on smear during an oxidative crisis are the classic G6PD finding. They appear acutely with the trigger.
⚠️ Expecting bite cells on a baseline smear; they appear during acute oxidative hemolysis, not at steady state.
5. Triggers: infection
INFECTION is the most common precipitant of G6PD hemolysis (oxidant burst from neutrophils). Other triggers: fava beans, drugs, and metabolic stress. Episodes are acute, self-limited as old cells are cleared and young cells take over.
⚠️ Naming drugs as the top trigger; infection is the single most common precipitant of acute G6PD hemolysis.
6. Fava beans
Favism: ingestion of fava beans (vicine/convicine oxidants) triggers severe hemolysis, especially in the Mediterranean variant. Classic exam vignette is dark urine after a fava bean meal. Severity is variant-dependent.
⚠️ Expecting favism in all variants; severe favism is typical of the more severe Mediterranean variant, not mild A-.
7. Drug triggers
Oxidant DRUGS: primaquine/tafenoquine, dapsone, rasburicase, nitrofurantoin, sulfonamides, methylene blue. Rasburicase and methylene blue are absolutely contraindicated and can cause methemoglobinemia/hemolysis. Screen G6PD before rasburicase/primaquine.
⚠️ Giving rasburicase for tumor lysis or methylene blue for methemoglobinemia in a G6PD patient — both are contraindicated and worsen hemolysis.
8. X-linked recessive genetics
G6PD deficiency is X-LINKED RECESSIVE — males (hemizygous) are fully affected; heterozygous females are mosaic and usually mild. Contrast with PK deficiency, which is autosomal recessive. Predominantly affects males.
⚠️ Confusing inheritance with PK deficiency; G6PD is X-linked recessive, PK deficiency is autosomal recessive.
9. Malaria protection
G6PD deficiency is the most common human enzymopathy (~400-500 million), concentrated where malaria is endemic (Mediterranean, Africa, Asia) because it confers partial malaria resistance — convergent/balanced selection like sickle and thalassemia.
⚠️ Forgetting the malaria link; G6PD geographic distribution mirrors malaria endemicity due to selective advantage.
10. Blood smear timing
During crisis: bite cells, blister cells, Heinz bodies (supravital), hemighosts. The picture evolves over 2-3 days. Self-limited because only the older, most enzyme-deficient cells lyse; reticulocytes are enzyme-replete.
⚠️ Expecting persistent hemolysis; G6PD crisis is self-limited as young high-enzyme cells replace lysed old cells.
11. Diagnosis timing trap
Quantitative G6PD enzyme assay (gold standard) can be FALSELY NORMAL during/just after acute hemolysis because deficient old cells are gone and young reticulocytes have higher G6PD. Repeat testing 2-3 months later (steady state).
⚠️ Excluding G6PD deficiency on a normal assay drawn during the acute episode; retest at steady state weeks later.
12. Treatment / prevention
Management is mainly avoidance of triggers (drugs, fava beans) and supportive care; severe cases need transfusion. Screen before high-risk drugs (rasburicase, primaquine, dapsone). Hydration protects kidneys during hemoglobinuria.
⚠️ Reaching for steroids or splenectomy; G6PD crisis is managed by removing the trigger and supportive care, not immunosuppression.
13. Green tents = mild majority
Class III (A-) variants cause episodic hemolysis only with triggers and are the vast majority — asymptomatic between episodes. Most G6PD-deficient people are well unless oxidatively stressed.
⚠️ Assuming all G6PD patients are chronically anemic; most are asymptomatic between trigger-induced episodes.
14. Neonatal jaundice (NNJ)
G6PD deficiency is a major cause of neonatal jaundice and can lead to kernicterus; naphthalene (mothballs) is a classic neonatal trigger. Treat with phototherapy and exchange transfusion when severe. Screen at-risk newborns.
⚠️ Overlooking G6PD as a cause of severe neonatal jaundice; it is a leading cause requiring phototherapy/exchange.
15. CNSHA severe variant
Class I (CNSHA) variants cause chronic non-spherocytic hemolytic anemia even WITHOUT triggers — rare, severe, transfusion-dependent. Contrast with the common A- variant that hemolyzes only on exposure. Severity is variant-defined.
⚠️ Assuming G6PD only hemolyzes with triggers; severe Class I variants cause chronic ongoing hemolysis.
