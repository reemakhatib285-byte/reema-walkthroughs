📖 Read-through — all 16 symbols
1. AV node tollbooth (normal PR gate)
The AV node is the normal single gate for atrium-to-ventricle conduction and sets the PR interval, deliberately delaying impulses (~decremental conduction) so ventricles fill before they fire. In atrial flutter/AF it also rate-limits the ventricle. Most regular narrow-complex SVTs (AVNRT, orthodromic AVRT) are reentry circuits that depend on the AV node, which is why AV-nodal blockade (vagal, adenosine) terminates them. The PR interval is the electrocardiographic readout of this tollbooth delay.
⚠️ Assuming every narrow-complex tachycardia is sinus tach is the trap — a regular ~150 bpm narrow tachycardia with no visible P waves is AVNRT (or flutter with 2:1 block) until adenosine proves otherwise.
2. Accessory pathway / bypass road (Bundle of Kent)
WPW is caused by a congenital accessory pathway (Bundle of Kent) that directly connects atrium to ventricle, bypassing the AV node's protective delay. Because the bypass conducts fast and without decrement, it pre-excites part of the ventricle. This pathway is the substrate for AVRT and the lethal pre-excited AF. Catheter ablation of the accessory pathway is curative and definitive therapy for symptomatic or high-risk patients.
⚠️ Calling WPW a structural heart disease is wrong — it is an electrical accessory connection; the heart is structurally normal, and ablation cures it.
3. Delta wave + short PR (sinus WPW)
In sinus rhythm WPW shows a short PR interval (<120 ms) plus a delta wave — a slurred upstroke at the start of the QRS — because the accessory pathway pre-excites the ventricle before the AV node impulse arrives, widening the QRS. This is the resting ECG signature of pre-excitation. The combination short PR + delta wave + wide QRS = WPW pattern.
⚠️ A short PR with a delta wave is NOT a bundle branch block or old infarct (the wide QRS mimics one) — recognize pre-excitation, not ischemia.
4. Asymptomatic WPW = not an emergency
Incidental WPW pattern on ECG in an asymptomatic patient requires no acute treatment. Management is observation; risk stratification (exercise testing or EP study) and ablation are reserved for symptomatic patients or those with high-risk features (e.g., shortest pre-excited RR <250 ms in AF). The mere presence of a delta wave is not a reason to treat emergently.
⚠️ 'WPW on ECG → admit and treat now' is the trap — asymptomatic pre-excitation alone is not an emergency; do not over-treat.
5. Orthodromic AVRT — narrow complex
Orthodromic AVRT is the common form: the reentry circuit goes DOWN the AV node (antegrade) and back UP the accessory pathway (retrograde). Because the ventricle is depolarized normally through the His-Purkinje system, the QRS is NARROW and regular (~150-250 bpm). It accounts for ~90-95% of AVRT. It behaves like other AV-nodal-dependent SVT.
⚠️ Confusing orthodromic AVRT with AVNRT matters less acutely (both narrow, both adenosine-responsive) — the trap is mislabeling a narrow regular tachycardia as VT.
6. AV nodal blockers OK in orthodromic AVRT
For orthodromic AVRT (and AVNRT), treat like SVT: vagal maneuvers first, then adenosine (6 mg → 12 mg IV push), then AV-nodal blockers such as verapamil or beta-blockers. These are safe here because conduction to the ventricle is going through the AV node, so blocking it breaks the circuit. There is no antegrade pre-excitation to accelerate.
⚠️ The safety of AV-nodal blockers applies ONLY when there is no pre-excited AF — do not generalize this to AF+WPW, where the same drugs are lethal.
7. Adenosine (caution: asthma, transient AF)
Adenosine causes transient complete AV block (half-life ~10 sec) and terminates AV-nodal-dependent SVT; it also unmasks atrial flutter by transiently slowing conduction to reveal flutter waves. Cautions: it can provoke bronchospasm (avoid/use caution in asthma — give verapamil instead), and it can briefly precipitate atrial fibrillation. Patients feel a few seconds of chest pressure/flushing/impending doom. Give as a rapid push followed by a saline flush.
⚠️ Giving adenosine to a patient with known pre-excited AF (irregular wide tachycardia) is the trap — the transient AV block forces all conduction down the accessory pathway and can trigger VF.
8. Antidromic AVRT — wide complex (looks like VT)
Antidromic AVRT (rare, ~5%) reverses the circuit: DOWN the accessory pathway (antegrade) and UP the AV node (retrograde). The ventricle is activated abnormally from the pathway insertion, producing a WIDE, bizarre, regular QRS that looks identical to VT. A regular wide-complex tachycardia in a patient with known WPW is antidromic AVRT until proven otherwise. Treat stable patients with procainamide; cardiovert if unstable.
⚠️ Defaulting to an AV-nodal blocker for this 'SVT' is dangerous — a wide regular tachycardia should be treated as VT/pre-excitation (procainamide), never with adenosine/verapamil empirically.
9. AF + WPW — irregular wide tachycardia (most dangerous)
Pre-excited AF is the most dangerous WPW rhythm. In normal AF the AV node caps ventricular rate near ~150; with an accessory pathway, impulses bypass that filter and can drive ventricular rates >250-300 bpm. The ECG shows an IRREGULAR, WIDE-complex tachycardia with varying QRS morphology. This can degenerate into ventricular fibrillation and hemodynamic collapse.
⚠️ Reading an irregular wide-complex tachycardia as polymorphic VT and reaching for an AV-nodal blocker is the fatal trap — irregular + wide + very fast = AF with WPW; use procainamide or cardioversion.
10. Procainamide / ibutilide for stable AF+WPW
For STABLE pre-excited AF, the correct drug slows conduction in the accessory pathway: procainamide (Class IA) is first-line; ibutilide is an alternative. These prolong the refractory period of the Bundle of Kent, reducing the dangerous ventricular rate. They do the opposite of AV-nodal blockers, which would shunt more traffic to the pathway. Procainamide also treats antidromic AVRT.
⚠️ Choosing amiodarone reflexively for pre-excited AF is a trap on boards — IV amiodarone can paradoxically enhance accessory-pathway conduction; procainamide/ibutilide are the preferred answers.
11. Synchronized cardioversion if unstable
Any unstable patient (hypotension, ischemia, altered mental status, shock) with AF+WPW, antidromic AVRT, or pre-excited tachycardia gets immediate synchronized DC cardioversion regardless of the rhythm label. Synchronization avoids delivering the shock on the T wave. This is the universal escape hatch when pharmacology is too slow or the patient is crashing.
⚠️ Delaying cardioversion to 'try one more drug' in an unstable pre-excited patient is the trap — instability mandates immediate electrical cardioversion.
12. Four forbidden AV-nodal blockers → VF
The four forbidden drugs in pre-excited AF are Adenosine, Beta-blockers, Calcium-channel blockers (diltiazem/verapamil), and Digoxin — remember 'ABCD.' By blocking the AV node they force all atrial impulses down the fast accessory pathway, accelerating the ventricular rate toward VF. Digoxin additionally shortens the accessory pathway's refractory period directly. This is the single highest-stakes wrong-answer cluster in the chapter.
⚠️ Picking ANY of adenosine/beta-blocker/CCB/digoxin for an irregular wide tachycardia is the killer wrong answer — in AF+WPW these precipitate ventricular fibrillation.
13. Digoxin (foxglove) shortens pathway refractoriness
Digoxin (derived from foxglove, Digitalis) is doubly bad in WPW: it both blocks the AV node and directly shortens the refractory period of the accessory pathway, allowing even faster antegrade conduction during AF. It is therefore contraindicated in pre-excited AF. This mechanistic detail is a favorite board distractor among the four forbidden drugs.
⚠️ Treating a foxglove/digoxin choice as 'just another rate-control agent' is the trap — in WPW it uniquely shortens pathway refractoriness and accelerates conduction toward VF.
14. Atrial flutter — sawtooth, ~150 with 2:1 block
Atrial flutter is a macro-reentrant atrial circuit (atrial rate ~300) producing sawtooth flutter waves, usually conducting 2:1 to give a regular ventricular rate near 150. Unlike AVNRT/AVRT it does not depend on the AV node for the circuit, so AV-nodal blockade (or adenosine) slows the ventricular response and unmasks flutter waves rather than terminating it. Definitive therapy is cavotricuspid isthmus ablation.
⚠️ Calling a regular 150 bpm narrow tachycardia 'sinus tachycardia' is the trap — a rate parked exactly at ~150 should prompt suspicion of 2:1 atrial flutter; slow the node to reveal the sawtooth.
15. Atrial tachycardia (focal) vs reentrant SVT
Focal atrial tachycardia arises from an ectopic atrial focus (not the AV node or an accessory pathway), giving a regular narrow tachycardia with abnormal P-wave morphology and often a 'warm-up' onset. Because the AV node is not part of the circuit, adenosine typically only transiently slows the rate (producing AV block) rather than terminating the tachycardia — a key way to distinguish AT from AVNRT/AVRT.
⚠️ Assuming adenosine failure means the drug was given wrong is the trap — failure to terminate (only transient block) points to atrial tachycardia or flutter rather than AV-nodal-dependent reentry.
16. Ablation = curative for symptomatic WPW
Radiofrequency catheter ablation of the accessory pathway is the definitive, curative treatment for symptomatic WPW (recurrent AVRT or pre-excited AF) and for high-risk asymptomatic patients found on EP study. Success rates exceed ~95% with low complication rates. After successful ablation the delta wave disappears and the arrhythmia substrate is gone.
⚠️ Choosing lifelong antiarrhythmic drugs over ablation for a symptomatic young WPW patient is the trap — ablation is curative and preferred, not just rate/rhythm suppression.
