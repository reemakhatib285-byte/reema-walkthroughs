📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Plasma CMV PCR — best initial test
Quantitative plasma CMV PCR (viral load) is the best initial test to diagnose and monitor CMV in transplant recipients — it detects viremia, guides preemptive therapy, and tracks treatment response. Rising viral load signals active disease.
⚠️ Relying on CMV serology to diagnose active disease post-transplant — serology shows prior exposure; quantitative PCR detects active replication.
2. Tissue biopsy for invasive/GI CMV
In suspected tissue-invasive CMV (e.g., colitis) plasma PCR may be negative; tissue biopsy showing characteristic owl-eye inclusions / immunostaining confirms invasive disease. Biopsy is required when PCR is negative but clinical suspicion is high.
⚠️ Excluding CMV colitis because plasma PCR is negative — tissue-invasive GI disease can have low/absent viremia; biopsy is needed.
3. Ganciclovir IV — severe disease
IV ganciclovir is first-line for severe or tissue-invasive CMV disease. It is a guanosine analog activated by viral UL97 kinase, inhibiting CMV DNA polymerase. Main toxicity is bone-marrow suppression (neutropenia). Treat until viral clearance.
⚠️ Using oral valganciclovir for severe/invasive CMV — severe disease needs IV ganciclovir; oral valgan is for non-severe/maintenance.
4. Valganciclovir PO — stable disease
Oral valganciclovir (prodrug of ganciclovir) treats non-severe CMV disease and is the standard agent for prophylaxis. Same mechanism and marrow toxicity as ganciclovir. Treat until viral load is undetectable.
⚠️ Choosing valganciclovir for life-threatening/invasive CMV — reserve oral valgan for mild disease and prophylaxis; use IV ganciclovir if severe.
5. Preemptive vs universal prophylaxis
Two prevention strategies: universal prophylaxis (antiviral for all at-risk for a fixed period, e.g., D+/R– kidney) versus preemptive therapy (serial PCR monitoring, treat only when viral load crosses a threshold). High-risk D+/R– favors universal prophylaxis.
⚠️ Treating D+/R– (donor-pos, recipient-neg) with preemptive monitoring alone — highest-risk serostatus warrants universal prophylaxis.
6. Preemptive = weekly PCR, treat at threshold
Preemptive therapy uses weekly quantitative CMV PCR; antiviral starts once viral load reaches a defined threshold, avoiding drug exposure in those who never reactivate. Requires reliable, frequent monitoring to catch early replication.
⚠️ Choosing preemptive strategy when reliable weekly PCR isn't feasible — without close monitoring, universal prophylaxis is safer.
7. Letermovir — HCT prophylaxis, no HSV/VZV
Letermovir inhibits the CMV terminase complex and is used for CMV prophylaxis (notably in HCT). It is CMV-specific — it does NOT cover HSV or VZV, so acyclovir/valacyclovir must be added for herpes prophylaxis.
⚠️ Assuming letermovir covers HSV/VZV — it is CMV-only; pair it with acyclovir/valacyclovir to cover herpes simplex and varicella.
8. Boards trap — letermovir leaves HSV/VZV uncovered
Key board point: ganciclovir/valganciclovir cover CMV plus HSV/VZV, but letermovir's narrow CMV-terminase mechanism leaves a herpesvirus coverage gap. Recognize when a second agent is required for HSV/VZV prophylaxis.
⚠️ Stopping all acyclovir when switching to letermovir — letermovir's coverage gap leaves HSV/VZV unprotected.
9. Resistance — UL97 / UL54 mutations
CMV refractory after ~2 weeks of appropriate therapy raises concern for resistance — UL97 mutations confer ganciclovir resistance, UL54 (DNA polymerase) confers broader resistance. Send genotypic resistance testing to guide salvage therapy.
⚠️ Continuing ganciclovir despite a rising viral load on treatment — refractory CMV at ~2 weeks needs resistance testing, not dose persistence.
10. Foscarnet — resistant CMV salvage
Foscarnet directly inhibits CMV DNA polymerase without requiring UL97 activation, so it treats UL97-mediated ganciclovir-resistant CMV. Major toxicity is nephrotoxicity and electrolyte wasting (hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia, hypokalemia).
⚠️ Picking another guanosine analog for UL97-resistant CMV — foscarnet (no kinase activation needed) is the salvage; watch nephro/electrolyte toxicity.
11. 4-virus comparison — BK, EBV, HHV
Post-transplant DNA viruses behave differently: CMV (colitis/pneumonitis, PCR-guided antivirals), BK polyomavirus (nephropathy — reduce immunosuppression, no good antiviral), EBV (PTLD — reduce immunosuppression ± rituximab). Distinguish their management.
⚠️ Treating BK nephropathy with antivirals like CMV — BK is managed by lowering immunosuppression; antivirals don't reliably work.
12. Reduce immunosuppression as adjunct
Reducing net immunosuppression is a key adjunct in CMV (and essential in BK/EBV PTLD) because over-immunosuppression drives viral reactivation. Balance against rejection risk while antiviral therapy controls active CMV.
⚠️ Maximizing antivirals while leaving heavy immunosuppression untouched — failure to reduce immunosuppression perpetuates viral reactivation.
