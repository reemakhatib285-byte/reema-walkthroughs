📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Imaging war room
Cross-sectional imaging answers the malignancy question for an adrenal mass; UNENHANCED (non-contrast) CT is the first and most important test because intracytoplasmic LIPID in benign adenomas lowers attenuation. If non-contrast CT is indeterminate, add a CT washout study or MRI chemical-shift imaging.
2. Radiographic differential panel
The radiographic differential ranks lesions: small (<4 cm) low-HU homogeneous = adenoma; large (>4–6 cm), heterogeneous, irregular = ACC; macroscopic fat = myelolipoma; markedly T2-bright/'light-bulb' on MRI or very vascular = pheochromocytoma — which must ALWAYS be excluded biochemically before any intervention.
⚠️ A markedly T2-hyperintense or hypervascular mass is pheo until proven otherwise — don't catalog it as a generic adenoma and don't proceed to biopsy.
3. HU thermometer
Non-contrast attenuation is the single most useful number: ≤10 HU = LIPID-RICH benign adenoma (high specificity, no further workup). 10–20 HU is indeterminate (lipid-poor adenoma vs other) and needs washout/MRI; >20 HU is suspicious for malignancy/ACC, pheo, or metastasis. On CT WASHOUT, an adenoma shows absolute washout >60% or relative washout >40% even if lipid-poor.
⚠️ LOW HU is the reassuring one. The common reversal trap is treating high attenuation as benign — >20 HU raises malignancy concern, not lowers it.
4. Size ruler
Size correlates with ACC risk: <4 cm overwhelmingly benign; >4 cm raises concern; >6 cm carries a substantial malignancy probability and generally warrants resection regardless of function. Size plus attenuation (and growth >0.8–1 cm over follow-up) drive the surgical decision.
⚠️ Size alone is not diagnostic — combine with HU/phenotype. But a >4 cm lesion should never be dismissed without characterization, and rapid interval growth is a red flag.
5. >4cm + >20 HU = ACC
The classic ACC imaging signature: >4 cm, non-contrast >20 HU (often >40), HETEROGENEOUS with central necrosis/hemorrhage and calcification, irregular margins, and delayed/poor contrast washout. Many ACCs are hormonally active (cortisol ± androgens → virilization, or mixed). Such a lesion should go to a multidisciplinary team for resection, not biopsy.
⚠️ Virilization + a large heterogeneous adrenal mass = ACC until proven otherwise — don't mislabel as a benign androgen-secreting adenoma.
6. Local/vascular invasion
Invasion of adjacent organs (kidney, liver) or tumor thrombus in the adrenal/renal vein or IVC is a definitive sign of MALIGNANCY (ACC) and upstages disease. It also dictates an OPEN operation by an experienced surgeon to avoid capsule violation and tumor spillage.
7. FDG-PET avid
On FDG-PET, malignant lesions (ACC, metastases) are FDG-avid with adrenal-to-liver SUV ratio above background, whereas benign adenomas are typically not. PET is most useful for indeterminate lesions, staging ACC, and detecting metastases; (18F-FDG for ACC/mets; 18F-FDOPA or MIBG for pheo/paraganglioma).
8. Biopsy rules — never biopsy
Adrenal biopsy is rarely indicated and is CONTRAINDICATED for suspected ACC or pheo: cytology cannot reliably distinguish adenoma from carcinoma (that requires the whole resected specimen and Weiss score), and needling risks NEEDLE-TRACK SEEDING and capsule violation that worsens prognosis. Biopsy is reserved for a suspected METASTASIS in a patient with known extra-adrenal cancer, and only AFTER pheo is biochemically excluded.
⚠️ Don't biopsy a suspected ACC (downstages/seeds, doesn't even diagnose). And never biopsy any adrenal mass before excluding pheo — needling can trigger a hypertensive crisis.
9. Always exclude pheo first
Before ANY procedure (biopsy or surgery) on an adrenal mass, biochemically exclude pheochromocytoma with plasma free or 24-h urinary fractionated metanephrines. An unsuspected pheo can release catecholamines during manipulation → lethal hypertensive crisis; if confirmed, pretreat with alpha-blockade (phenoxybenzamine/doxazosin) before beta-blockade.
⚠️ The number-one safety trap: manipulating/biopsying/operating on an adrenal mass without first ruling out pheo — phenoxybenzamine alpha-blockade must precede any beta-blocker.
10. Weiss score ≥3 = malignant
The Weiss system is the HISTOLOGIC criterion for adrenocortical malignancy (assessable only on the resected tumor): 9 features including high nuclear grade, >5 mitoses/50 HPF, atypical mitoses, <25% clear cells, diffuse architecture, necrosis, and venous/sinusoidal/capsular invasion. A score ≥3 indicates carcinoma.
⚠️ Weiss requires the WHOLE specimen — this is exactly why biopsy/FNA cannot diagnose ACC.
11. Ki-67 prognostic
Ki-67 is a proliferation index in ACC: it guides adjuvant treatment and prognosis — roughly <10% lower risk, ≥10% poor prognosis (some thresholds use >20% for high risk). Higher Ki-67 favors adjuvant mitotane ± chemotherapy.
12. ENSAT/TNM staging tower
ACC is staged by ENSAT/TNM (I–IV): I ≤5 cm confined, II >5 cm confined, III local invasion/regional nodes/vein thrombus, IV distant METASTASES. Stage drives therapy and prognosis — complete R0 resection is the only curative option; adjuvant mitotane for high-risk; advanced disease gets EDP-mitotane (etoposide, doxorubicin, cisplatin).
13. Metastatic map
ACC metastasizes hematogenously most often to LIVER and LUNG, then bone; this defines stage IV (ENSAT IV). Conversely, the adrenal is a frequent recipient of metastases FROM lung, breast, melanoma, renal and GI primaries — so in a known-cancer patient a new adrenal lesion is more likely a metastasis than a primary ACC.
⚠️ Don't confuse direction: a solitary adrenal mass in a patient with known lung/breast cancer is usually a metastasis (biopsy-able after pheo exclusion), not a new ACC.
14. Clinical pearl
Lock the bottom line: >20 HU + >4 cm + heterogeneous = ACC; never biopsy a suspected ACC or any mass before excluding pheo; Weiss ≥3 confirms malignancy on resection; Ki-67 ≥10% portends poor prognosis; ACC mets favor liver and lung.
