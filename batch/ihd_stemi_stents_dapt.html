📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. 12 months calendar
After ACS (including STEMI) with stenting, the default duration of dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) is ~12 months: aspirin 81 mg daily lifelong PLUS a P2Y12 inhibitor for 12 months. Preferred P2Y12 agents after ACS are ticagrelor (90 mg BID) or prasugrel (10 mg daily); clopidogrel (75 mg daily after a 300–600 mg load) is the alternative when potent agents are contraindicated. Duration can be shortened (to 1–6 months) if high bleeding risk, or extended if high ischemic/low bleeding risk.
⚠️ WRONG: DAPT for only 1 month after ACS. Discriminator: 1 month is a short course reserved for high-bleeding-risk patients with a modern DES — the standard after ACS is 12 months; calling 1 month the default is the trap.
2. NOT DAPT bottle (lone aspirin)
A single antiplatelet agent alone is inadequate in the early period after stenting — aspirin monotherapy does not prevent stent thrombosis while the struts are re-endothelializing. You need DUAL therapy (aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor). Only after the DAPT period (≥12 months post-ACS) do you de-escalate to a single agent for life.
⚠️ WRONG: aspirin monotherapy suffices after stenting. Discriminator: in the vulnerable window aspirin alone leaves the patient unprotected against stent thrombosis — dual therapy is mandatory until endothelialization is complete.
3. Thrombosis bench
Premature discontinuation of DAPT is the strongest precipitant of acute stent thrombosis — an abrupt, catastrophic thrombotic occlusion of the stented segment presenting as a new STEMI/sudden death. This is why patients must NOT stop DAPT early (e.g., for elective surgery) without cardiology input; elective non-cardiac surgery should be deferred until the minimum DAPT period is met.
⚠️ WRONG: stent thrombosis risk is unrelated to DAPT. Discriminator: stopping DAPT early is the single biggest modifiable cause of stent thrombosis — the link is causal and heavily tested in perioperative-management questions.
4. 3–9x death/MI risk
Stent thrombosis is highly lethal: it raises the risk of death/MI several-fold (~3–9x), with mortality roughly 10–40% and a very high rate of MI (30–70%) when it occurs. It is an emergency requiring immediate repeat angiography/PCI. Contrast this acute, dramatic event with restenosis, which is gradual and far less lethal.
⚠️ WRONG: stent thrombosis is usually benign. Discriminator: stent thrombosis is a medical emergency with high mortality (unlike restenosis) — treating it as a minor event is the error; it presents as acute MI.
5. Coronary anatomy first horse
Before committing to PCI, define the full coronary anatomy and disease burden — this determines whether PCI or CABG is the better revascularization. CABG is generally favored for left main disease, complex multivessel disease (high SYNTAX score), and diabetics with multivessel disease; PCI suits focal/single-vessel disease and the acute STEMI culprit. Heart-team discussion is standard for complex anatomy.
⚠️ WRONG: stent immediately without assessing the full anatomy. Discriminator: in complex/left-main/diabetic multivessel disease, CABG can be superior — reflexively stenting without weighing the anatomy (SYNTAX) misses the PCI-vs-CABG decision.
6. Stroke-brain (load only ≤?)
Choose the P2Y12 agent by bleeding/stroke risk. Prasugrel is CONTRAINDICATED in any prior stroke/TIA (net harm from intracranial bleeding) and is avoided in age ≥75 or weight <60 kg. Ticagrelor is the potent alternative there (but causes dyspnea and requires BID dosing and low-dose aspirin). Clopidogrel is the gentlest option for the highest bleeding-risk patients.
⚠️ WRONG: load every patient with the same potent P2Y12 inhibitor regardless of stroke risk. Discriminator: prasugrel is absolutely contraindicated with prior stroke/TIA — picking it for a patient with cerebrovascular history is a classic harm trap.
7. BVS / bioresorbable (crossed)
Older bare scaffolds / balloon angioplasty alone produce high restenosis (~20–50%) from elastic recoil and neointimal hyperplasia. Bioresorbable vascular scaffolds (BVS) were withdrawn from routine use after showing higher rates of scaffold thrombosis and target-lesion failure than modern DES. They are not first-line.
⚠️ WRONG: bioresorbable scaffolds are first-line. Discriminator: BVS were largely abandoned for higher thrombosis/failure — current first-line is a second-generation drug-eluting stent, not a bioresorbable scaffold.
8. BMS 10–30% restenosis
Bare-metal stents have higher restenosis (~10–30%) than DES but historically allowed a SHORTER mandatory DAPT duration because they re-endothelialize faster. Their main niche today is patients who cannot tolerate prolonged DAPT (imminent surgery, very high bleeding risk), though modern DES now also permit short DAPT, narrowing this advantage.
⚠️ WRONG: BMS has lower restenosis than DES. Discriminator: BMS has MORE restenosis than DES — its only relative edge was shorter required DAPT, not better patency.
9. DES 5–15% restenosis
Drug-eluting stents elute an antiproliferative agent (e.g., everolimus, zotarolimus, sirolimus) that suppresses neointimal hyperplasia, giving the lowest restenosis rate (~5–15%). They are the preferred stent for nearly all PCI today. Second-generation DES also have low thrombosis rates and now permit shortened DAPT when bleeding risk is high.
⚠️ WRONG: DES has the highest restenosis rate. Discriminator: DES has the LOWEST restenosis (the drug blocks neointimal growth) — it is preferred precisely for that; claiming the opposite reverses the whole rationale.
10. DES late thrombosis clock
First-generation DES delayed endothelialization, raising LATE/very-late stent thrombosis risk — which is why prolonged DAPT became important with DES. Second-generation DES (everolimus/zotarolimus) markedly reduced this, so late thrombosis with modern stents is now low and DAPT can sometimes be shortened.
⚠️ WRONG: DES never cause late thrombosis. Discriminator: the delayed-healing of (especially first-gen) DES is exactly why late stent thrombosis was a concern and DAPT was prolonged — denying any late thrombosis risk is the trap.
11. Restenosis bench
Restenosis is gradual re-narrowing of the stented segment from neointimal hyperplasia (smooth-muscle proliferation), evolving over months. It is a distinct process from stent thrombosis: restenosis is a slow re-stenosis presenting as recurrent stable angina, whereas thrombosis is an abrupt clot causing acute MI. DAPT prevents thrombosis, not restenosis.
⚠️ WRONG: restenosis and stent thrombosis are the same process. Discriminator: restenosis = gradual neointimal regrowth (recurrent angina); thrombosis = acute clot (STEMI) — different mechanism, timeline, and lethality.
12. Gradual 12 months sign
Restenosis typically presents GRADUALLY over months (commonly 3–12 months post-PCI) as recurrent exertional/stable angina, not as sudden collapse. The slow tempo (vs the abrupt presentation of stent thrombosis) is the clinical discriminator on exam questions.
⚠️ WRONG: restenosis presents as sudden total occlusion / acute MI. Discriminator: a gradual return of angina over months = restenosis; a sudden STEMI = stent thrombosis — matching the tempo to the diagnosis is the tested point.
