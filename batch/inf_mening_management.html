📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Step 1: blood cultures + clock <60 min
Sequence: draw 2 sets of blood cultures → give dexamethasone with/just before the first antibiotic → give empiric antibiotics, all within 60 minutes (door-to-antibiotic time drives mortality). Blood cultures are positive in ~50-70% and preserve a microbiologic diagnosis even when CSF is sterilized by antibiotics.
⚠️ "wait until CT and LP are done before antibiotics" -> wrong; every hour of delay increases death/disability — give empiric therapy right after blood cultures; CSF yield drops only modestly in the first few hours.
2. Decision fork: who needs CT first
Obtain CT head BEFORE LP only with an IDSA red flag: focal neurologic deficit, papilledema, new-onset seizure (within 1 week), moderate-severe altered consciousness, immunocompromise (HIV, transplant, immunosuppressants), or known CNS disease/mass. These raise concern for a mass lesion and herniation risk from LP.
⚠️ "CT every suspected meningitis to be safe" -> wrong; universal pre-LP CT delays low-risk patients who can be tapped immediately — reserve CT for the specific IDSA features, and never let it delay antibiotics.
3. Left path: direct LP (gold star)
Immunocompetent, normal mentation, no focal deficit/papilledema/recent seizure → go DIRECTLY to LP without CT, then start dexamethasone + empiric antibiotics. Sending CSF before antibiotics (when feasible without delay) maximizes Gram stain and culture yield.
⚠️ "normal neuro exam means you can delay antibiotics for the LP" -> wrong; even in the direct-LP path, if obtaining CSF is held up, give antibiotics immediately after blood cultures.
4. Right path: CT-first red flags
Concerning features → blood cultures + dexamethasone + empiric antibiotics FIRST, THEN CT to exclude a mass/herniation risk, THEN LP if CT is clear. The antibiotics-before-CT order is the high-yield point: imaging must never push back the first dose.
⚠️ "CT -> LP -> then antibiotics" -> wrong; correct order when CT is indicated is blood cultures + steroids + antibiotics -> CT -> LP. Waiting for imaging before treating is the classic error.
5. CT scanner
CT head before LP excludes a space-occupying lesion / herniation risk in red-flag patients. It is NOT required for low-risk patients and must never delay the first antibiotic dose. Most herniation in meningitis is from diffuse cerebral edema, so even a normal CT does not fully eliminate risk.
6. Empiric: ceftriaxone + vancomycin + acyclovir
Standard empiric regimen for immunocompetent adults: ceftriaxone 2g IV q12h (S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, H. influenzae) PLUS vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h (ceftriaxone-resistant pneumococcus). Add acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h if HSV encephalitis is on the differential (altered mentation, temporal-lobe signs, seizures).
⚠️ "ceftriaxone monotherapy" -> wrong; up to ~25% of pneumococci have reduced cephalosporin susceptibility, so vancomycin is mandatory empirically until susceptibilities return.
7. Add ampicillin >50 / immunocompromised
Add AMPICILLIN 2g IV q4h for Listeria in age >50, neonates, pregnancy, alcoholism, and immunocompromise (steroids, transplant, HIV, malignancy). Cephalosporins have NO Listeria activity, so ampicillin (or penicillin G) is the dedicated agent for this gram-positive rod.
⚠️ "ceftriaxone + vancomycin alone for a 65-year-old" -> wrong; cephalosporins miss Listeria — omitting ampicillin in a Listeria-risk host leaves a fatal gap.
8. PCN allergy: TMP-SMX (red X)
For Listeria coverage in true penicillin allergy, use TMP-SMX (Bactrim) IV; meropenem is an alternative covering Listeria plus gram-negatives (low cross-reactivity except in severe/anaphylactic histories).
⚠️ "swap ampicillin for cefepime in a PCN-allergic Listeria-risk patient" -> wrong; NO cephalosporin covers Listeria — the correct swap is TMP-SMX or meropenem, not another beta-lactam in the cephalosporin class.
9. Nosocomial / post-neurosurgery: vanc + cefepime
Healthcare-associated meningitis (post-neurosurgery, CSF shunt/drain, penetrating trauma, neutropenic) needs vancomycin (MRSA, coag-negative staph) PLUS an antipseudomonal beta-lactam: cefepime, ceftazidime, or meropenem. These hosts harbor staphylococci and Pseudomonas not covered by ceftriaxone.
⚠️ "community ceftriaxone-based therapy after neurosurgery/shunt" -> wrong; ceftriaxone misses Pseudomonas and under-covers staph — the healthcare setting mandates antipseudomonal + MRSA coverage.
10. Dexamethasone BEFORE antibiotics
Give dexamethasone 10mg IV 15-20 minutes BEFORE, or concurrently with, the first antibiotic in suspected bacterial meningitis. Antibiotics lyse bacteria releasing cell-wall fragments that trigger a cytokine surge; pre-dosing blunts this storm and reduces death and sequelae (notably pneumococcal).
⚠️ "give dexamethasone after antibiotics are already running" -> wrong; late steroid offers little benefit once bacteriolysis/cytokines are underway — but also do NOT delay antibiotics for steroid; give together if steroid isn't ready.
11. Mechanism: TNF-a / IL-1B from lysis
Mechanism: antibiotics kill bacteria, releasing cell-wall components (pneumococcal peptidoglycan, LPS) that activate macrophages/microglia to release TNF-alpha and IL-1beta, driving meningeal inflammation, cerebral edema, vasculitis, and raised ICP. Dexamethasone suppresses this host response, limiting secondary CNS injury.
⚠️ "the steroid treats the infection" -> wrong; dexamethasone is a purely anti-inflammatory adjunct — it does nothing to the organism and must accompany, never replace, antibiotics.
12. Dexa benefit greatest in pneumococcus
Dexamethasone's proven mortality benefit is greatest in PNEUMOCOCCAL meningitis in adults; in children it reduces sensorineural HEARING LOSS in Hib meningitis. Dose: 10mg IV q6h x4 days (peds 0.15 mg/kg q6h x4d). Continue only if cultures confirm pneumococcus (adults) or Hib (children); stop otherwise. Benefit is established mainly in high-income settings.
⚠️ "finish the full 4-day steroid course regardless of organism" -> wrong; for meningococcal, Listeria, gram-negative, or viral disease benefit is unproven — STOP dexamethasone once those are confirmed.
13. Vancomycin sub-gag: raise the dose
Dexamethasone reduces meningeal inflammation and BBB permeability, lowering vancomycin CSF penetration. When giving dexamethasone WITH vancomycin for resistant pneumococcus, use higher dosing (trough 15-20, ~45-60 mg/kg/day) and consider adding rifampin if response is poor.
⚠️ "give vancomycin intrathecally to overcome poor penetration" -> wrong; the fix is to RAISE IV vancomycin and follow troughs (or add rifampin) — routine intrathecal vancomycin isn't standard for community-acquired meningitis and is toxic.
14. Acyclovir vs ganciclovir
Add acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h empirically when HSV/VZV encephalitis is suspected (fever + altered mentation, focal/temporal-lobe signs, seizures, CSF lymphocytosis with RBCs). Acyclovir covers HSV-1/2 and VZV; CMV requires ganciclovir/foscarnet. Hydrate to prevent acyclovir crystalline nephropathy.
⚠️ "acyclovir for CMV encephalitis" -> wrong; acyclovir has essentially no CMV activity (CMV lacks thymidine kinase) — use ganciclovir/foscarnet for CMV, acyclovir for HSV/VZV.
