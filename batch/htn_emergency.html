⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. Urgency ≥180/100 — WRONG answer = admit to ICU and start an IV nitroprusside drip. In urgency there is no end-organ damage, so rapid IV lowering only risks hypoperfusion (stroke, MI, AKI). Oral therapy with close follow-up is correct; the discriminator is the ABSENCE of acute target-organ injury.
  2. Emergency ≥180 + organ — WRONG answer = define emergency by the BP number alone (e.g. 'BP 220/120 with no symptoms = emergency'). It is the presence of ACUTE end-organ damage — encephalopathy, ACS, pulmonary edema, AKI, dissection, eclampsia — not the absolute number, that defines an emergency. The same 220/120 with no organ injury is urgency.
  3. Do not normalize fast — WRONG answer = drive BP rapidly to <140/90 to 'protect the brain.' Overshoot below the autoregulatory threshold precipitates watershed (border-zone) cerebral infarction, AKI, and MI. Exceptions that DO warrant faster/lower targets are aortic dissection (SBP <120, HR <60 within ~20 min) — the discriminator is the diagnosis, not the BP value.
  4. End-organ targets — WRONG answer = call asymptomatic severe HTN an 'emergency' because of an elevated creatinine that is actually chronic. Distinguish ACUTE injury (new rise in Cr, new neuro deficit, fresh retinal hemorrhages) from chronic baseline changes; only acute, evolving damage upgrades urgency to emergency.
  5. Aortic dissection — WRONG answer = start nitroprusside (a pure vasodilator) first. Vasodilation alone triggers reflex tachycardia and increased dP/dt, propagating the dissection. Beta-blockade MUST precede the vasodilator. The discriminator: control heart rate/contractility before pressure.
  6. Pheo crisis — WRONG answer = give a beta-blocker (e.g. propranolol/labetalol) up front. Unopposed alpha stimulation from beta-blockade causes severe vasoconstriction and a hypertensive crisis. Alpha BEFORE beta — same rule as elective pheo surgical prep (phenoxybenzamine first).
  7. Eclampsia / pregnancy — WRONG answer = use an ACEi/ARB or nitroprusside. ACEi/ARBs are teratogenic (renal dysgenesis, oligohydramnios) and contraindicated; nitroprusside risks fetal cyanide toxicity. Also WRONG = treat seizures with standard antiepileptics — magnesium is the agent of choice in eclampsia.
  8. Resistant HTN definition — WRONG answer = label as resistant when the patient is on three drugs but no diuretic, or on submaximal doses. A diuretic at adequate dose is mandatory to meet the definition; otherwise it's undertreated, not resistant.
  9. Also monitor K+ and creatinine after starting an MRA — hyperkalemia is the key adverse effect, particularly with concurrent ACEi/ARB or CKD.
  10. Resistant workup — WRONG answer = jump straight to renal denervation or a 5th drug. Most apparent resistance is pseudoresistance (nonadherence, white-coat, bad technique, or interfering drugs); these must be excluded first. Primary aldosteronism is the most common identifiable secondary cause in resistant HTN.
  11. Single-pill combo — WRONG answer = combine an ACEi WITH an ARB (or add a direct renin inhibitor to either). Dual RAAS blockade increases hyperkalemia, hypotension, and AKI without added benefit (ONTARGET/ALTITUDE). Combine across mechanisms, not within the same pathway.

📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Urgency ≥180/100
Hypertensive URGENCY: BP ≥180/120 with NO acute end-organ damage. Manage with ORAL agents (restart/uptitrate the patient's regimen — e.g. amlodipine, an ACEi/ARB, or a long-acting agent) and arrange outpatient follow-up in 1–7 days; the goal is gradual reduction over days, not minutes. No ICU, no IV drips, no continuous monitoring required.
⚠️ WRONG answer = admit to ICU and start an IV nitroprusside drip. In urgency there is no end-organ damage, so rapid IV lowering only risks hypoperfusion (stroke, MI, AKI). Oral therapy with close follow-up is correct; the discriminator is the ABSENCE of acute target-organ injury.
2. Emergency ≥180 + organ
Hypertensive EMERGENCY: severe BP (usually >180/120) WITH acute, ongoing end-organ damage. Requires ICU admission, an arterial line, and titratable IV agents — IV nicardipine or clevidipine (CCBs), labetalol (combined α/β), or nitroprusside. General target: lower MAP by ~10–20% in the first hour (max ~25%), then toward 160/100–110 over the next 2–6 hours.
⚠️ WRONG answer = define emergency by the BP number alone (e.g. 'BP 220/120 with no symptoms = emergency'). It is the presence of ACUTE end-organ damage — encephalopathy, ACS, pulmonary edema, AKI, dissection, eclampsia — not the absolute number, that defines an emergency. The same 220/120 with no organ injury is urgency.
3. Do not normalize fast
Do NOT normalize BP acutely. Lower MAP by ~10–20% (never more than ~25%) in the first hour. Chronic HTN shifts cerebral/renal autoregulation curves rightward, so a 'normal' BP can fall below the autoregulatory floor and cause watershed cerebral, renal, and coronary ischemia.
⚠️ WRONG answer = drive BP rapidly to <140/90 to 'protect the brain.' Overshoot below the autoregulatory threshold precipitates watershed (border-zone) cerebral infarction, AKI, and MI. Exceptions that DO warrant faster/lower targets are aortic dissection (SBP <120, HR <60 within ~20 min) — the discriminator is the diagnosis, not the BP value.
4. End-organ targets
Acute end-organ targets in hypertensive emergency: hypertensive encephalopathy (headache, confusion, seizures, papilledema), acute pulmonary edema/HF, acute kidney injury, grade III/IV retinopathy, ACS/MI, aortic dissection, and eclampsia. Tailor the IV agent to the organ — e.g. nitroglycerin/clevidipine for pulmonary edema, labetalol/nicardipine for encephalopathy.
⚠️ WRONG answer = call asymptomatic severe HTN an 'emergency' because of an elevated creatinine that is actually chronic. Distinguish ACUTE injury (new rise in Cr, new neuro deficit, fresh retinal hemorrhages) from chronic baseline changes; only acute, evolving damage upgrades urgency to emergency.
5. Aortic dissection
Aortic dissection is the exception requiring FAST, AGGRESSIVE lowering: target SBP <120 mmHg and HR <60 within ~20 minutes. Give an IV beta-blocker FIRST (esmolol or labetalol) to blunt dP/dt (the shear/pulsatile force on the aortic wall), THEN add a vasodilator (nitroprusside) if SBP remains high.
⚠️ WRONG answer = start nitroprusside (a pure vasodilator) first. Vasodilation alone triggers reflex tachycardia and increased dP/dt, propagating the dissection. Beta-blockade MUST precede the vasodilator. The discriminator: control heart rate/contractility before pressure.
6. Pheo crisis
Pheochromocytoma crisis: catecholamine surge → give IV alpha-blockade FIRST (phentolamine, or nicardipine); beta-blockade may be added only AFTER adequate alpha-blockade to control reflex tachycardia. Suspect with paroxysmal HTN plus headache, palpitations, and diaphoresis.
⚠️ WRONG answer = give a beta-blocker (e.g. propranolol/labetalol) up front. Unopposed alpha stimulation from beta-blockade causes severe vasoconstriction and a hypertensive crisis. Alpha BEFORE beta — same rule as elective pheo surgical prep (phenoxybenzamine first).
7. Eclampsia / pregnancy
Severe preeclampsia/eclampsia: treat BP with IV labetalol, IV hydralazine, or oral immediate-release nifedipine (target <160/110), and give IV magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis/treatment. Definitive treatment is delivery of the fetus/placenta.
⚠️ WRONG answer = use an ACEi/ARB or nitroprusside. ACEi/ARBs are teratogenic (renal dysgenesis, oligohydramnios) and contraindicated; nitroprusside risks fetal cyanide toxicity. Also WRONG = treat seizures with standard antiepileptics — magnesium is the agent of choice in eclampsia.
8. Resistant HTN definition
Resistant HTN: BP above goal despite adherence to ≥3 antihypertensives at optimal/max doses from different classes, ONE of which is a diuretic — OR BP controlled but requiring ≥4 agents. Prompts evaluation for secondary causes and pseudoresistance.
⚠️ WRONG answer = label as resistant when the patient is on three drugs but no diuretic, or on submaximal doses. A diuretic at adequate dose is mandatory to meet the definition; otherwise it's undertreated, not resistant.
9. Add spironolactone
For true resistant HTN, the preferred 4th-line add-on is a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist — spironolactone 25–50 mg/day (eplerenone if antiandrogen effects like gynecomastia occur). The PATHWAY-2 trial showed spironolactone superior to bisoprolol/doxazosin as the 4th agent.
⚠️ WRONG answer = escalate to a 4th agent before confirming adherence, proper BP technique, white-coat effect, and excluding secondary causes (especially primary aldosteronism). Also monitor K+ and creatinine after starting an MRA — hyperkalemia is the key adverse effect, particularly with concurrent ACEi/ARB or CKD.
10. Resistant workup
Resistant-HTN workup before escalating: confirm correct BP measurement technique, obtain out-of-office (home/ambulatory) readings to exclude white-coat effect, verify medication adherence, review interfering substances (NSAIDs, decongestants, OCPs, alcohol, stimulants, licorice), and screen for secondary causes (primary aldosteronism, OSA, renovascular, CKD).
⚠️ WRONG answer = jump straight to renal denervation or a 5th drug. Most apparent resistance is pseudoresistance (nonadherence, white-coat, bad technique, or interfering drugs); these must be excluded first. Primary aldosteronism is the most common identifiable secondary cause in resistant HTN.
11. Single-pill combo
Single-pill (fixed-dose) combinations are preferred to improve adherence and reach goal faster. General ACC/AHA target is <130/80 for most adults. Rational pairings: ACEi/ARB + thiazide, or ACEi/ARB + dihydropyridine CCB (the ACCOMPLISH-trial combo, benazepril+amlodipine, outperformed ACEi+HCTZ).
⚠️ WRONG answer = combine an ACEi WITH an ARB (or add a direct renin inhibitor to either). Dual RAAS blockade increases hyperkalemia, hypotension, and AKI without added benefit (ONTARGET/ALTITUDE). Combine across mechanisms, not within the same pathway.
12. Diabetes / CKD
In diabetes and CKD with albuminuria, an ACEi or ARB is the compelling first-line choice for renoprotection (reduces intraglomerular pressure and slows progression of proteinuric nephropathy). Target generally <130/80.
⚠️ WRONG answer = combine ACEi + ARB for 'extra' kidney protection in diabetic nephropathy. Dual blockade worsens hyperkalemia and AKI without slowing CKD. Use ONE RAAS blocker; monitor K+ and creatinine (a rise up to ~30% is acceptable and expected).
13. Black adults
In Black adults WITHOUT CKD or albuminuria, initial therapy should be a thiazide diuretic or a calcium channel blocker, which are more effective than ACEi/ARB as monotherapy in this group (lower-renin physiology). Target <130/80.
⚠️ WRONG answer = start an ACEi/ARB as monotherapy. It is less effective for BP lowering and carries a higher risk of angioedema in Black patients. Exception: if CKD with albuminuria is present, an ACEi/ARB IS indicated for renoprotection regardless of race.
14. Elderly SBP
In older adults, treat isolated systolic hypertension (SBP-driven); the SPRINT trial supported intensive control (SBP ~120 by automated office measurement) even in fit elderly. Start low, go slow, and check standing BP at each visit.
⚠️ WRONG answer = push aggressively to a low SBP in a frail elder while ignoring symptoms. Watch for orthostatic hypotension, dizziness, and falls; an excessively low DBP may compromise coronary perfusion. Individualize the target by frailty and tolerance.
