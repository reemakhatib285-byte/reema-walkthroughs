📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Octopus pot = LV shape
The octopus-trap jar is the end-systolic LV silhouette in Takotsubo: wide hypokinetic bottom + narrow contractile neck. 'Takotsubo' is Japanese for 'octopus pot,' named for the LV shape on ventriculography. The stunned octopus with limp tentacles = apical hypokinesis. This shape is the single most recognizable board image for the disease.
⚠️ It is NOT a structural aneurysm — the wall-motion abnormality is reversible stunning, not infarcted scar, so the pot 'reinflates' over weeks.
2. Apical ballooning
The hallmark is apical ballooning: the LV apex balloons out and is akinetic/hypokinetic at end-systole. The apex has the highest density of sympathetic nerve terminals, making it most vulnerable to catecholamine stunning. This is the classic mid/apical variant of Takotsubo.
⚠️ Apical hypokinesis here mimics an anterior (LAD) STEMI wall-motion pattern — but coronaries are clean, distinguishing stunning from true infarction.
3. Basal hypercontractility
While the apex balloons, the basal segments are hyperdynamic (hypercontractility). This base-apex mismatch produces the characteristic pot shape and can cause dynamic LVOT obstruction in some patients. The combination apical ballooning + basal hypercontractility is the echo signature.
⚠️ Dynamic LVOT obstruction from basal hypercontractility can mimic HOCM physiology — but the trigger and reversibility point to Takotsubo.
4. Emotional/physical stress trigger
Takotsubo is triggered by an acute emotional stressor (death of a loved one, bad news, fear) or a physical stressor (surgery, critical illness, acute medical events). The stressor immediately precedes presentation and is a key history clue. Classic vignette: chest pain after a death in the family.
⚠️ Absence of a remembered emotional trigger does not exclude it — physical stressors (post-op, sepsis, asthma attack) cause Takotsubo just as often.
5. Catecholamine surge mechanism
Mechanism is a massive catecholamine surge that directly stuns myocardial cells, preferentially at the apex (densest sympathetic innervation). It is neurohormonal stunning, not atherosclerotic ischemia. This explains why the apex fails despite patent coronaries.
⚠️ Do not attribute the troponin rise to plaque rupture — the injury is catecholamine-mediated stunning, so anticoagulation/thrombolysis targets the wrong mechanism.
6. Postmenopausal woman
Board demographic: postmenopausal women are disproportionately affected. Estrogen normally buffers catecholamine signaling; its loss after menopause increases catecholamine sensitivity. The stereotyped vignette is an older woman after acute emotional stress.
⚠️ A young man with the same ECG is far more likely true STEMI — the postmenopausal-female demographic is a strong (not absolute) discriminator favoring Takotsubo.
7. Normal coronary angiogram
The diagnostic linchpin: catheterization shows NO obstructive CAD. The presentation mimics STEMI (ST elevation, troponin) but the coronaries are clean. Normal coronaries + apical ballooning = Takotsubo. This is the finding that seals the diagnosis.
⚠️ You cannot diagnose Takotsubo clinically before angiography — clean coronaries are required; assuming Takotsubo and skipping cath risks missing a true STEMI.
8. Apical ballooning on echo
Echocardiography (or ventriculography) shows apical ballooning with basal hypercontractility — distinguishing Takotsubo from anterior STEMI. In anterior STEMI the LAD territory is infarcted; in Takotsubo the apex is stunned despite normal vessels. The wall-motion abnormality typically extends beyond a single coronary territory.
⚠️ Wall motion crossing multiple coronary territories argues AGAINST a single-vessel STEMI and FOR Takotsubo — a key echo discriminator.
9. STEMI impostor — emergent cath
Core scenario: ST elevation + troponin elevation CAN occur, but coronaries are clean. A 68-year-old woman with chest pain and ST elevation after her husband's death has elevated troponin. Correct management is EMERGENT coronary angiography to exclude true STEMI — you cannot tell them apart clinically.
⚠️ Wrong answer = empiric thrombolytics without angiography. Lysing a Takotsubo patient gives no benefit and adds bleeding risk; always cath first.
10. Supportive care + ACEi/BB
There is no specific Takotsubo therapy. Management is supportive: ACE inhibitor and beta-blocker for hemodynamic support during the acute phase, plus treating complications (heart failure, arrhythmia, LVOT obstruction). The goal is to bridge the patient until spontaneous recovery.
⚠️ Beta-blockade is reasonable acutely, but if dynamic LVOT obstruction is present, avoid inotropes/vasodilators that worsen the gradient — manage like obstructive physiology.
11. Recovery in 1–4 weeks
Expected course: spontaneous recovery to normal EF in roughly 1–4 weeks (days to weeks). Most patients recover completely with excellent prognosis. Board rule — when asked the expected course of Takotsubo, the answer is spontaneous normalization of EF.
⚠️ Board trap: 'start aggressive long-term HFrEF therapy.' Wrong — EF normalizes within weeks, so chronic GDMT is not indicated; this is a reversible cardiomyopathy.
12. Spironolactone red X
Spironolactone (MRA) is a classic wrong-answer distractor. While MRAs help chronic HFrEF, they are NOT a primary treatment for Takotsubo. The disease is self-limited, so adding an MRA does not change its course. Recognizing this distractor is a frequently tested point.
⚠️ Picking spironolactone is choosing chronic-HFrEF logic for a reversible, self-limited condition — the discriminator is that Takotsubo needs supportive care, not long-term neurohormonal blockade.
13. QT prolongation / TdP risk
Takotsubo prolongs the QT interval during the acute phase, creating a risk of torsades de pointes. Monitor the QT and correct electrolytes (K, Mg). Avoid antiarrhythmics that further prolong QT — Class IA and Class III agents — acutely.
⚠️ Reaching for a Class III antiarrhythmic (e.g., sotalol/amiodarone) in acute Takotsubo can precipitate torsades — the QT is already prolonged, so QT-prolonging drugs are the wrong choice.
14. STE + troponin but clean vessels
Summary discriminator: ST elevation AND troponin elevation can both occur, yet the coronary arteries are clean. STE + troponin + normal coronaries + apical ballooning = Takotsubo. This triad is the high-yield take-home that separates it from acute MI.
⚠️ Elevated troponin does NOT confirm MI here — biomarker rise is from stunning; the clean angiogram, not the troponin, defines the diagnosis.
15. Japanese fishing village theme
The Japanese fishing-village backdrop anchors the octopus-pot mnemonic and the disease's origin (described in Japan). It ties together the trigger (stress), the mechanism (catecholamines), the shape (pot), the diagnosis (clean coronaries), and the course (reversible) into one memorable scene.
⚠️ The theme is mnemonic only — there is no geographic/ethnic restriction; Takotsubo occurs worldwide, classically in postmenopausal women after acute stress.
