📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. PEAL mnemonic
PEAL captures the nephrotic tetrad: Proteinuria >3.5 g/day, Edema, hypoAlbuminemia (<3 g/dL), and hyperLipidemia. The unifying mechanism is loss of glomerular barrier charge/size selectivity, so proteinuria is the primary event and the other three follow from low oncotic pressure. Add the complications layer — hypercoagulability (antithrombin III loss) and infection risk (Ig loss) — for the full picture.
⚠️ WRONG answer: adding hematuria or RBC casts to the nephrotic syndrome definition. Those are NEPHRITIC features (glomerular inflammation/bleeding); the discriminator is that PEAL is a barrier-permeability problem with a bland sediment, not an inflammatory one.
2. Proteinuria >3.5 g/day
Nephrotic-range proteinuria is >3.5 g/24h, reflecting failure of the size/charge barrier (GBM heparan sulfate + podocyte slit diaphragm). It is quantified by 24h collection or spot urine protein:creatinine ratio (>3-3.5 g/g). 'Selective' proteinuria (mostly albumin) suggests MCD, whereas 'non-selective' loss of larger globulins suggests more structural barrier damage (FSGS/membranous).
⚠️ WRONG answer: classifying 1-3 g/day as nephrotic. That is sub-nephrotic; nephritic syndrome typically runs <3.5 g/day WITH hematuria and RBC casts — the discriminator is magnitude of protein loss plus sediment activity.
3. Albumin lost
Massive transglomerular albumin escape exceeds hepatic compensation, producing hypoalbuminemia (<3 g/dL) and the fall in oncotic pressure that drives edema and reactive hyperlipidemia. Loss of albumin also reduces carrier capacity for protein-bound drugs, hormones, and vitamin D-binding protein. In MCD the loss is highly selective for albumin, reflecting predominantly a charge-barrier defect.
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming all nephrotic albumin loss is equally selective. Highly SELECTIVE albuminuria points specifically to MCD; non-selective loss of larger proteins suggests FSGS or membranous — the discriminator is the protein-selectivity profile on the biopsy/urine.
4. Thrombosis — antithrombin III loss
Urinary loss of antithrombin III, along with increased hepatic procoagulants (fibrinogen) and platelet hyperactivity, creates a hypercoagulable state. The signature complication is renal vein thrombosis — flank pain, hematuria, sudden worsening proteinuria, and possible left varicocele — most associated with membranous nephropathy; DVT/PE also occur. Anticoagulate when albumin is very low (<2-2.5 g/dL) or risk is high.
⚠️ WRONG answer: blaming thrombocytosis/platelet excess. The driver is ANTITHROMBIN III loss in the urine (a loss-of-anticoagulant state), not a primary platelet disorder — the discriminator is low antithrombin activity and the membranous association for RVT.
5. Dyslipidemia
Reduced plasma oncotic pressure upregulates hepatic lipoprotein synthesis and impairs lipid clearance (reduced LCAT and LDL-receptor activity), raising LDL, total cholesterol, and triglycerides. The resulting atherogenic profile increases cardiovascular risk and contributes to lipiduria/fatty casts and Maltese-cross bodies on polarized microscopy. Treat with a statin (e.g., atorvastatin 10-40 mg daily).
⚠️ WRONG answer: predicting low cholesterol/triglycerides. Lipids RISE in nephrotic syndrome via hepatic overproduction — the discriminator from a starvation/malabsorption picture is the coexisting proteinuria, edema, and hypoalbuminemia.
6. Infection / altered drug binding
Urinary loss of immunoglobulins (IgG) and complement factor B raises susceptibility to ENCAPSULATED organisms — classic pneumococcal spontaneous bacterial peritonitis in a nephrotic child with ascites. Concurrent loss of albumin and binding globulins alters pharmacokinetics: the free fraction of highly protein-bound drugs rises and dosing/half-life shift, and vitamin D-binding protein and thyroxine-binding globulin losses can cause functional deficiencies.
⚠️ WRONG answer: treating altered protein binding as a clinically trivial footnote, or assuming infection risk reflects a separate immunodeficiency. The mechanism is acquired urinary protein loss — the discriminator is hypoalbuminemia altering free drug levels and Ig loss driving encapsulated-organism infection.
7. MCD — 70-90% pediatric nephrotic
Minimal change ('nil') disease causes 70-90% of pediatric nephrotic syndrome: normal light microscopy, negative immunofluorescence, and diffuse foot-process effacement only on EM. It is highly steroid-responsive — children are usually treated empirically with prednisone (e.g., 60 mg/m^2/day) without biopsy. Triggers include recent viral URI, atopy, NSAIDs, and Hodgkin lymphoma.
⚠️ WRONG answer: treating MCD as a common ADULT cause. In adults it is only ~10-15% of nephrotic syndrome; FSGS leads in adults — the discriminator is the pediatric predominance and brisk steroid responsiveness of MCD.
8. Foot-process effacement
Effacement (flattening/fusion) of podocyte foot processes seen only on electron microscopy is the unifying ultrastructural lesion of MCD and a feature of FSGS. The podocyte slit diaphragm proteins — nephrin and podocin — normally form the final size barrier; their disruption causes the foot processes to retract and the barrier to leak protein. In pure MCD the light microscopy and IF are normal, so EM is required.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing foot-process effacement to subepithelial spikes/deposits. Subepithelial 'spike-and-dome' deposits with granular IgG/C3 define MEMBRANOUS nephropathy; foot-process effacement without deposits points to MCD/FSGS — the discriminator is the presence or absence of immune deposits on EM/IF.
9. Steroids first-line (MCD)
In children, MCD is treated empirically with corticosteroids (prednisone ~60 mg/m^2/day, tapered over weeks) and biopsy is typically deferred because steroid response is the norm; relapses may be managed with calcineurin inhibitors, cyclophosphamide, or rituximab. The brisk response reflects the reversible, immune-mediated podocyte injury without structural scarring.
⚠️ WRONG answer: applying empiric-steroids-without-biopsy to adults. That approach is appropriate in children with classic MCD, but adults with nephrotic syndrome generally need a biopsy first because FSGS, membranous, and secondary causes differ in treatment — the discriminator is patient age and atypical features.
10. FSGS variants
FSGS histologic variants carry distinct prognoses: NOS (classic, default), tip lesion (BEST prognosis, most steroid-responsive), cellular, perihilar (linked to obesity/hyperfiltration and reduced nephron mass), and collapsing (WORST prognosis, associated with HIV and APOL1 risk alleles). All share focal/segmental sclerosis on light microscopy and foot-process effacement on EM, and many are steroid-resistant.
⚠️ WRONG answer: reversing the prognostic poles. Collapsing variant has the WORST outcome and tip lesion the BEST — flipping them is the classic distractor; the discriminator is variant histology plus the HIV/APOL1 link for collapsing.
11. APOL1 / African ancestry risk
Two APOL1 high-risk variants (G1/G2), common in people of West African ancestry, markedly increase risk of FSGS — particularly the collapsing variant and HIV-associated nephropathy (HIVAN). The same alleles accelerate progression of nondiabetic CKD to ESKD. They confer ancestral protection against trypanosomiasis, explaining their high frequency.
⚠️ WRONG answer: linking APOL1 to MCD or membranous nephropathy. APOL1 risk variants are tied specifically to FSGS/collapsing/HIVAN — the discriminator is the FSGS histology and African ancestry, not a podocyte-effacement-only lesion or immune-deposit disease.
12. FSGS — ESKD #1 primary cause
FSGS is a leading primary glomerular cause of progression to end-stage kidney disease, with roughly half of patients reaching ESKD within about 10 years, especially when nephrotic-range and steroid-resistant. Progression reflects irreversible segmental scarring and nephron loss, contrasting with the generally benign, non-progressive course of MCD. It can also recur in renal allografts, sometimes within hours to days of transplant.
⚠️ WRONG answer: predicting frequent ESKD progression from MCD. MCD rarely scars or progresses to ESKD; sustained, steroid-resistant nephrotic syndrome that marches toward ESKD is FSGS — the discriminator is segmental sclerosis on biopsy and steroid resistance.
