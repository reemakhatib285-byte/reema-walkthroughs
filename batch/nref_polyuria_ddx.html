⚠️ Board traps 10
  1. Post-obstructive diuresis (WITH AKI) — WRONG: chasing every mL of urine 1:1 with IV fluid after relieving obstruction. Aggressive matched replacement perpetuates a diuresis that should resolve on its own. Discriminator: separate appropriate (clears retained solute, self-limits) from pathologic (true tubular defect) — replace partially and trend weight, Na, K, Mg, and volume status.
  2. Recovery phase of ATN (WITH AKI) — WRONG: easing off monitoring once creatinine starts dropping. The polyuric recovery phase can cause severe volume and K/Mg/PO4 depletion exactly when the team assumes recovery is complete. Discriminator: rising output + falling creatinine after ATN = recovery diuresis — keep replacing fluids/electrolytes and rechecking labs.
  3. Nephrotoxic nonoliguric ATN (WITH AKI) — WRONG: AKI must be oliguric, so brisk output excludes tubular injury. Nephrotoxic ATN is frequently NON-oliguric — high urine output does not exclude necrosis. Discriminator: granular casts/RTE cells + a nephrotoxin + rising creatinine confirm ATN regardless of output.
  4. AIN / tubulointerstitial (WITH AKI) — WRONG: ruling out AIN because there is no fever, rash, or eosinophilia. The classic triad is INCOMPLETE/absent in the large majority of cases (especially PPI- and checkpoint-inhibitor-induced). Discriminator: subacute AKI + sterile pyuria + WBC casts + a new drug is sufficient to suspect AIN and stop the agent.
  5. Osmotic diuresis (WITH or WITHOUT AKI) — WRONG: treating osmotic-diuresis polyuria as a primary ADH/water-handling defect (chasing DI). The driver is solute, not ADH. Discriminator: high urine osm/solute excretion with an obvious solute source (glucose, mannitol, urea) = osmotic diuresis; correct the solute and replace volume rather than working up DI.
  6. Diabetes insipidus (WITHOUT AKI) — WRONG: confusing DI with primary polydipsia by sodium. DI gives dilute urine with HIGH/high-normal serum sodium, whereas primary polydipsia gives dilute urine with LOW/low-normal sodium. Discriminator: a water-deprivation test then DDAVP separates central DI (concentrates after DDAVP), nephrogenic DI (no response to DDAVP), and polydipsia (concentrates with deprivation).
  7. Primary polydipsia (WITHOUT AKI) — WRONG: starting DDAVP empirically for dilute polyuria. In primary polydipsia, DDAVP plus continued heavy drinking risks acute hyponatremia. Discriminator: a water-deprivation test concentrates the urine in polydipsia (kidney works) but fails to in DI — confirm before treating.
  8. Diuretics (WITHOUT AKI) — WRONG: launching a full DI/polyuria workup before reviewing the medication list. Diuretics (including OTC/herbal and surreptitious use) reproduce many polyuria patterns. Discriminator: a urine diuretic screen + careful med reconciliation can short-circuit an expensive workup.
  9. Hypercalcemia / hypokalemia (WITHOUT AKI) — WRONG: treating the polyuria itself instead of the electrolyte. These cause a REVERSIBLE acquired nephrogenic DI — correct the calcium or potassium and the concentrating defect/polyuria resolves. Discriminator: dilute polyuria + hypercalcemia or hypokalemia → fix the electrolyte first, don't reach for DDAVP.
  10. CKD concentrating defect (WITHOUT AKI) — WRONG: dismissing new nocturia/polyuria as benign aging or BPH because the creatinine is still normal. Loss of concentrating ability is an EARLY CKD sign that PRECEDES a measurable creatinine rise. Discriminator: persistent nocturia with iso-osthenuric urine warrants checking for underlying CKD/tubulointerstitial disease.
📖 Read-through — all 10 symbols
1. Post-obstructive diuresis (WITH AKI)
After relief of obstruction (Foley for BPH, ureteral stent, percutaneous nephrostomy, stone passage), the kidney offloads retained salt, water, and urea (appropriate component) on top of a transient tubular concentrating defect (pathologic component). Output may exceed 200 mL/h. Risks: dehydration, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hypotension. Replace only ~half to two-thirds of output with isotonic fluid so an appropriate diuresis can self-limit.
⚠️ WRONG: chasing every mL of urine 1:1 with IV fluid after relieving obstruction. Aggressive matched replacement perpetuates a diuresis that should resolve on its own. Discriminator: separate appropriate (clears retained solute, self-limits) from pathologic (true tubular defect) — replace partially and trend weight, Na, K, Mg, and volume status.
2. Recovery phase of ATN (WITH AKI)
In recovering ATN the GFR (filtration) returns before the tubules regain reabsorptive/concentrating capacity, and accumulated urea adds an osmotic load → a brisk polyuric phase. Creatinine stabilizes then falls as urine output rises. This phase carries genuine risk of hypovolemia and hypokalemia/hypophosphatemia/hypomagnesemia.
⚠️ WRONG: easing off monitoring once creatinine starts dropping. The polyuric recovery phase can cause severe volume and K/Mg/PO4 depletion exactly when the team assumes recovery is complete. Discriminator: rising output + falling creatinine after ATN = recovery diuresis — keep replacing fluids/electrolytes and rechecking labs.
3. Nephrotoxic nonoliguric ATN (WITH AKI)
Direct tubular toxins necrose cells while urine flow stays preserved/increased. Aminoglycosides are the classic culprit (non-oliguric, Mg/K wasting, onset ~5–10 days, dose once-daily and follow troughs); also cisplatin (Mg wasting), amphotericin B (distal RTA + K/Mg wasting; lipid formulations less toxic), ifosfamide (Fanconi), and tenofovir (proximal/Fanconi). Sediment: muddy-brown granular casts + renal tubular epithelial cells.
⚠️ WRONG: AKI must be oliguric, so brisk output excludes tubular injury. Nephrotoxic ATN is frequently NON-oliguric — high urine output does not exclude necrosis. Discriminator: granular casts/RTE cells + a nephrotoxin + rising creatinine confirm ATN regardless of output.
4. AIN / tubulointerstitial (WITH AKI)
Interstitial inflammation impairs the medullary gradient and tubular ADH responsiveness → concentrating defect and polyuria, with subacute AKI. Top drug triggers: PPIs, NSAIDs, beta-lactams, sulfa, allopurinol, rifampin, and immune checkpoint inhibitors. Sediment: WBCs, WBC casts, sterile pyuria; eosinophiluria is unreliable. First step: stop the culprit drug; add steroids if no recovery within ~5–7 days.
⚠️ WRONG: ruling out AIN because there is no fever, rash, or eosinophilia. The classic triad is INCOMPLETE/absent in the large majority of cases (especially PPI- and checkpoint-inhibitor-induced). Discriminator: subacute AKI + sterile pyuria + WBC casts + a new drug is sufficient to suspect AIN and stop the agent.
5. Osmotic diuresis (WITH or WITHOUT AKI)
A non-reabsorbed solute load (hyperglycemia in DKA/HHS once filtered glucose exceeds the reabsorptive threshold, mannitol, or a high urea load from catabolism/GI bleed/TPN) holds water in the tubule → solute-rich diuresis. It straddles both columns: mild = polyuria without AKI; severe = volume depletion → prerenal AKI → ATN. Urine osm is not maximally dilute and daily solute excretion is high.
⚠️ WRONG: treating osmotic-diuresis polyuria as a primary ADH/water-handling defect (chasing DI). The driver is solute, not ADH. Discriminator: high urine osm/solute excretion with an obvious solute source (glucose, mannitol, urea) = osmotic diuresis; correct the solute and replace volume rather than working up DI.
6. Diabetes insipidus (WITHOUT AKI)
DI is a free-WATER diuresis from failure of ADH signaling. Central DI = deficient ADH (pituitary surgery, trauma, tumor, infiltrative disease) → responds to desmopressin/DDAVP. Nephrogenic DI = collecting-duct resistance to ADH (lithium, hypercalcemia, hypokalemia, congenital) → does NOT respond to DDAVP; manage with low-salt diet + thiazide ± amiloride (for lithium), and treat the cause. Urine is very dilute (osm often <300, sometimes <100); serum Na rises if water access is restricted.
⚠️ WRONG: confusing DI with primary polydipsia by sodium. DI gives dilute urine with HIGH/high-normal serum sodium, whereas primary polydipsia gives dilute urine with LOW/low-normal sodium. Discriminator: a water-deprivation test then DDAVP separates central DI (concentrates after DDAVP), nephrogenic DI (no response to DDAVP), and polydipsia (concentrates with deprivation).
7. Primary polydipsia (WITHOUT AKI)
Compulsive/psychogenic water intake (or a low-solute 'tea-and-toast'/beer-potomania pattern) suppresses ADH appropriately → dilute polyuria. Serum sodium is low/low-normal because the excess water dilutes plasma. The kidney is normal and CAN concentrate urine once water intake is restricted.
⚠️ WRONG: starting DDAVP empirically for dilute polyuria. In primary polydipsia, DDAVP plus continued heavy drinking risks acute hyponatremia. Discriminator: a water-deprivation test concentrates the urine in polydipsia (kidney works) but fails to in DI — confirm before treating.
8. Diuretics (WITHOUT AKI)
Loop (furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide) and thiazide diuretics force a Na+/water diuresis. They also invalidate the FENa for AKI workup (forced natriuresis falsely elevates FENa) — use FEUrea <35% instead when diuretics are on board. Surreptitious or unlisted diuretic use is a classic exam twist.
⚠️ WRONG: launching a full DI/polyuria workup before reviewing the medication list. Diuretics (including OTC/herbal and surreptitious use) reproduce many polyuria patterns. Discriminator: a urine diuretic screen + careful med reconciliation can short-circuit an expensive workup.
9. Hypercalcemia / hypokalemia (WITHOUT AKI)
Both electrolyte disturbances cause an ACQUIRED nephrogenic DI by impairing collecting-duct ADH responsiveness/aquaporin-2 trafficking and the medullary gradient → dilute polyuria. Hypercalcemia (think malignancy, primary hyperparathyroidism) and chronic hypokalemia are the testable culprits.
⚠️ WRONG: treating the polyuria itself instead of the electrolyte. These cause a REVERSIBLE acquired nephrogenic DI — correct the calcium or potassium and the concentrating defect/polyuria resolves. Discriminator: dilute polyuria + hypercalcemia or hypokalemia → fix the electrolyte first, don't reach for DDAVP.
10. CKD concentrating defect (WITHOUT AKI)
Tubulointerstitial scarring and nephron loss in CKD impair urinary concentration EARLY — often presenting as nocturia/polyuria with a fixed iso-osthenuric urine osm (~300) before the serum creatinine rises appreciably. Loss of concentrating ability is one of the earliest functional signs of chronic kidney damage.
⚠️ WRONG: dismissing new nocturia/polyuria as benign aging or BPH because the creatinine is still normal. Loss of concentrating ability is an EARLY CKD sign that PRECEDES a measurable creatinine rise. Discriminator: persistent nocturia with iso-osthenuric urine warrants checking for underlying CKD/tubulointerstitial disease.
