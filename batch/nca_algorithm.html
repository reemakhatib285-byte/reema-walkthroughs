📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. PTH → PTHrP → 1,25-D
The single most important board reflex for any hypercalcemia: measure PTH FIRST. PTH cleanly splits the differential into PTH-mediated (high/inappropriately-normal) vs PTH-independent (suppressed). Only if PTH is suppressed do you proceed to PTHrP (humoral malignancy) and then 1,25-OH vitamin D (granulomatous/lymphoma). Roughly 90% of all hypercalcemia is either primary hyperparathyroidism or malignancy, and PTH distinguishes them in one test.
⚠️ WRONG: order a serum calcium-only panel or jump to a parathyroid sestamibi scan before checking PTH. Imaging localizes an adenoma but never establishes the diagnosis — PTH is the branch-point. Ionized calcium confirms true hypercalcemia but does not tell you the cause.
2. High serum Ca trunk
Confirmed high serum calcium is the trunk that splits into two branches. Before working it up, confirm it is REAL: correct for albumin (corrected Ca = measured + 0.8 × [4 − albumin]) or get an ionized calcium, since dehydration and high albumin/paraprotein can spuriously raise total calcium. Normal total Ca is ~8.5–10.5 mg/dL.
⚠️ WRONG: treating an elevated TOTAL calcium in a patient with high albumin (hemoconcentration) or a paraprotein (myeloma) as true hypercalcemia. Ionized calcium is normal in pseudohypercalcemia — no workup or treatment needed.
3. High PTH branch
A high OR inappropriately-normal PTH in the face of hypercalcemia = PTH-mediated disease. Normally high calcium should suppress PTH via the CaSR, so any non-suppressed PTH is pathologic. This branch holds primary (and tertiary) hyperparathyroidism and FHH. Confirm with 24-hour urine calcium to separate FHH (low) from primary hyperPTH (high/normal).
⚠️ WRONG: calling a PTH value that sits in the normal range 'normal.' With elevated calcium, a mid-normal PTH is INAPPROPRIATELY normal and still indicates PTH-mediated hypercalcemia.
4. Suppressed PTH branch
A low/suppressed PTH means the parathyroids are appropriately responding — the calcium is being driven from elsewhere (PTH-independent). Next steps: PTHrP (humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy), 1,25-D (granulomatous disease/lymphoma), 25-D (vitamin D intoxication), and SPEP/imaging for myeloma or lytic mets. Malignancy dominates this branch in inpatients.
⚠️ WRONG: ordering a PTHrP level FIRST in every hypercalcemia. PTHrP is only useful once PTH is confirmed suppressed; checking it up front wastes a send-out test and confuses the algorithm.
5. Primary hyperPTH
Primary hyperparathyroidism is the #1 OUTPATIENT cause — usually a single autonomous adenoma (~85%), often found incidentally as mild asymptomatic hypercalcemia with high/normal PTH and low phosphate. Surgical cure (parathyroidectomy) is indicated for Ca >1 mg/dL above normal, age <50, osteoporosis/fragility fracture, or eGFR <60/nephrolithiasis. Consider MEN1 and MEN2A in young/multigland disease.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming the most common cause of hypercalcemia OVERALL is also the most common in a sick hospitalized patient. Inpatient hypercalcemia is malignancy until proven otherwise — primary hyperPTH is the outpatient/incidental winner.
6. CDC73 / jaw tumor
Hyperparathyroidism–jaw tumor syndrome is caused by germline CDC73 (HRPT2, parafibromin) mutations. It carries a notably HIGH risk of parathyroid CARCINOMA (markedly elevated Ca and very high PTH) plus ossifying fibromas of the jaw and renal lesions. Think of it in a young patient with severe hyperPTH and a jaw mass.
⚠️ WRONG: lumping it with sporadic adenoma. A young patient with very high PTH, markedly elevated calcium, and a palpable neck mass should raise parathyroid carcinoma / CDC73, not benign adenoma.
7. FHH
Familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia is an autosomal-dominant INACTIVATING CaSR mutation: the receptor under-senses calcium, so PTH and Ca run mildly high but the kidney avidly reabsorbs calcium → LOW urine calcium. The discriminator is the fractional excretion of calcium <0.01 (1%); 24-h urine Ca is low. It is benign and lifelong — no surgery, no treatment.
⚠️ WRONG: sending a patient with high Ca and high/normal PTH to parathyroidectomy without checking urine calcium. Operating on FHH does nothing (hypercalcemia persists) — the FECa <1% (vs >2% in primary hyperPTH) prevents the needless surgery.
8. Malignancy PTHrP
Humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy is the #1 INPATIENT cause: tumor-secreted PTHrP mimics PTH (binds the PTH receptor) → high Ca, LOW phosphate, but SUPPRESSED native PTH. Classic primaries: squamous cell (lung, head/neck), renal, bladder, and breast carcinoma. It signals advanced disease and poor prognosis; treat the calcium AND the tumor.
⚠️ WRONG: expecting PTH to be HIGH because PTHrP 'acts like PTH.' The assay measures intact PTH, which is appropriately SUPPRESSED — high calcium with low PTH and low phosphate plus a known cancer = PTHrP-mediated.
9. Check PTHrP node
This is the second-tier test on the suppressed-PTH branch. After PTH comes back low, an elevated PTHrP nails humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy. If PTHrP is also normal/low, pivot to 1,25-D (granulomatous/lymphoma), 25-D (vitamin D excess), SPEP/UPEP and skeletal survey (myeloma), and TSH/cortisol for endocrine mimics.
⚠️ WRONG: stopping the workup at a normal PTHrP and declaring no malignancy. Lytic bone metastases and myeloma cause hypercalcemia via local cytokines (RANKL/IL-6), NOT PTHrP — PTHrP can be normal while cancer is the cause.
10. Granulomatous 1,25-D
In sarcoidosis, TB, and lymphoma, activated macrophages express UNREGULATED 1-alpha-hydroxylase, converting 25-D to active 1,25-D independent of PTH. Result: high 1,25-D, suppressed PTH, high calcium and often hypercalciuria. It responds dramatically to GLUCOCORTICOIDS (prednisone), which shut down macrophage 1-alpha-hydroxylase.
⚠️ WRONG: treating granulomatous hypercalcemia primarily with bisphosphonates or heavy sun exposure/vitamin D. Steroids are the targeted therapy, and these patients should AVOID vitamin D and sunlight, which fuel the conversion.
11. Low 1,25-D group
PTH-independent causes with LOW or normal 1,25-D: vitamin D INTOXICATION raises 25-D (not 1,25-D), Paget disease + immobilization releases skeletal calcium, and milk-alkali syndrome (calcium + absorbable alkali) gives the triad of hypercalcemia, metabolic alkalosis, and AKI. Thiazides and lithium also live on the drug side of this branch.
⚠️ WRONG: ordering 1,25-D to diagnose vitamin D TOXICITY. Exogenous vitamin D excess raises 25-OH-D (the storage form measured for toxicity); 1,25-D is often normal/low. Check 25-D for intoxication, 1,25-D for granulomatous disease.
12. Calcium severity scale
Severity guides urgency: MILD 10.5–11.9 (often asymptomatic, outpatient workup), MODERATE 12–14 (symptomatic, treat), SEVERE >14 mg/dL or symptomatic = hypercalcemic emergency needing aggressive IV saline + calcitonin + bisphosphonate. Above ~14, neurologic changes and arrhythmia risk rise sharply.
⚠️ WRONG: discharging a patient with calcium >14 because they 'look okay.' Severe hypercalcemia is an emergency regardless of how mild symptoms seem — treat immediately, don't wait for the workup.
13. Hungry bone syndrome
After parathyroidectomy for severe/long-standing hyperPTH, the abrupt drop in PTH lets demineralized bone avidly reabsorb minerals → profound, prolonged HYPOcalcemia, hypophosphatemia, and hypomagnesemia. It is distinguished from transient post-op hypoparathyroidism by LOW phosphate (hypoPTH gives HIGH phosphate). Treat with aggressive calcium + calcitriol.
⚠️ WRONG: confusing hungry bone with surgical hypoparathyroidism. Both give post-op hypocalcemia, but hungry bone has LOW phosphate and elevated/normal PTH, whereas hypoparathyroidism has HIGH phosphate and low PTH — the phosphate level discriminates.
14. Vitamins / alkalosis trap
Alkalosis (respiratory or metabolic) increases calcium binding to albumin, dropping IONIZED (active) calcium while total calcium stays the same — this is why acute hyperventilation can trigger perioral tingling and carpopedal spasm with a 'normal' total Ca. Conversely, acidosis frees ionized calcium. Always interpret calcium alongside pH and albumin.
⚠️ WRONG: reassuring a hyperventilating patient with tetany because total calcium is normal. The ionized fraction has fallen due to alkalosis — symptomatic hypocalcemia can exist with a perfectly normal total calcium.
