📖 Read-through — all 16 symbols
1. Brugada — coved ST elevation V1-V2 + RBBB
Brugada syndrome shows a Type 1 ECG: coved (downsloping, tombstone-shaped) ST elevation ≥2 mm in ≥1 of leads V1-V3 with an RBBB-like morphology. The pattern can be spontaneous or unmasked by sodium-channel blockers, fever, or vagal tone. It reflects a sodium-channelopathy causing abnormal right-ventricular epicardial repolarization. Type 1 (coved) is diagnostic; the saddleback Type 2 is only suggestive.
⚠️ Mistaking coved ST elevation in V1-V2 for an anteroseptal STEMI is the trap — Brugada has no reciprocal changes, no troponin rise, and a structurally normal heart.
2. Brugada genetics — SCN5A loss of function
Brugada is most classically a LOSS-of-function mutation in SCN5A, the cardiac sodium channel gene (reduced INa). The same gene with a GAIN-of-function produces LQT3 — one gene, opposite physiology. Inheritance is autosomal dominant with variable penetrance, and the heart is structurally normal on echo. Penetrance is higher in males.
⚠️ Thinking SCN5A always means one disease is the trap — SCN5A loss = Brugada, SCN5A gain = LQT3; the direction of the mutation determines the syndrome.
3. Brugada — classic patient (young SE Asian male, death at sleep)
The board vignette is a young Southeast Asian male with syncope or sudden cardiac death occurring at REST or during SLEEP (often nocturnal, agonal respirations), with no structural heart disease on echocardiography. A family history of sudden death is common. Events are vagally/bradycardia-mediated, explaining the nighttime predominance.
⚠️ Attributing nocturnal sudden death in a young man to seizure or sleep apnea alone is the trap — rest/sleep arrest with a normal heart should prompt a Brugada ECG (and provocation testing).
4. Brugada triggers — fever & Na-channel blockers (avoid)
Fever is a major precipitant of Brugada arrhythmias — treat fever aggressively with antipyretics and maintain normothermia. Sodium-channel blockers (Class IC: flecainide, propafenone; Class IA: procainamide, and also tricyclics, cocaine) unmask or worsen the Brugada pattern and are contraindicated. These same agents are used diagnostically (ajmaline/procainamide challenge) to provoke a Type 1 pattern.
⚠️ Starting flecainide for AF in a patient with a Brugada pattern is the classic trap — Class IC/IA sodium-channel blockers are contraindicated and can precipitate fatal arrhythmia.
5. Brugada treatment — ICD (quinidine adjunct)
The only proven therapy that prevents sudden death in symptomatic Brugada (prior arrest, syncope, or spontaneous Type 1 with risk features) is an implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD). Quinidine can pharmacologically suppress arrhythmic storms and is useful when ICD shocks recur or as a bridge. Avoid all sodium-channel blockers and treat fevers promptly.
⚠️ Choosing a beta-blocker or antiarrhythmic instead of an ICD for symptomatic Brugada is the trap — only the ICD prevents sudden death; drugs do not.
6. ARVC — LBBB-morphology VT in young athlete
Arrhythmogenic RV cardiomyopathy causes exercise-triggered VT/VF in young athletes. Because the arrhythmia originates in the right ventricle, ventricular activation spreads right-to-left, producing a LEFT bundle branch block (LBBB) morphology VT. It is a desmosomal disease (e.g., plakophilin-2) with autosomal dominant inheritance. Exercise accelerates disease progression, so athletes are advised to restrict.
⚠️ Expecting RBBB-morphology VT is the trap — ARVC arises in the RV, so the VT has LBBB morphology; RBBB-pattern VT instead suggests a left-ventricular origin.
7. ARVC — fatty RV replacement (Swiss-cheese)
The pathologic hallmark of ARVC is fibrofatty replacement of right-ventricular myocardium, giving a Swiss-cheese appearance and predisposing to reentrant VT. The process is often patchy and begins in the RV free wall (the 'triangle of dysplasia'). This substrate explains both the arrhythmias and progressive RV dysfunction/failure in advanced disease.
⚠️ Calling RV fatty infiltration a normal variant or simple lipomatosis is the trap — in a young athlete with VT and epsilon waves it represents ARVC, not benign fat.
8. ARVC — epsilon wave & T-inversions V1-V3
The epsilon wave is the ARVC hallmark: a small terminal deflection just after the QRS in V1-V3, representing delayed RV activation. Accompanying findings include T-wave inversions in V1-V3 (beyond age 14, without RBBB). These are part of the Task Force diagnostic criteria along with imaging, arrhythmia, and family-history terms.
⚠️ Dismissing the small post-QRS bump as a notched J wave or artifact is the trap — in this context the epsilon wave is the specific ARVC marker.
9. ARVC — normal echo does NOT exclude; MRI is superior
Early ARVC can have a completely normal echocardiogram. Cardiac MRI is the superior modality — it detects RV fatty infiltration, wall thinning/dyskinesis, and late gadolinium enhancement (fibrosis). MRI plus the Task Force criteria establish the diagnosis. Management includes exercise restriction, beta-blockers/antiarrhythmics, and an ICD for high-risk patients.
⚠️ 'Echo was normal, so ARVC is ruled out' is the trap — a normal echo does not exclude ARVC; order cardiac MRI when ARVC is suspected.
10. LQT1 — exertion/swimming trigger (KCNQ1, IKs)
LQT1 is a loss-of-function mutation in KCNQ1 reducing the slow delayed-rectifier potassium current IKs, the channel needed to shorten QT during sympathetic/exertional drive. It is the most common congenital LQTS, and events are triggered by EXERTION — classically swimming/diving. Beta-blockers are highly effective here, and competitive swimming may need restriction.
⚠️ Attributing an unexplained drowning/near-drowning in a young swimmer to accident is the trap — exertion- or swimming-triggered syncope/arrest should raise LQT1.
11. LQT2 — startle/noise trigger (KCNH2, IKr)
LQT2 is loss-of-function in KCNH2 (hERG) reducing the rapid delayed-rectifier potassium current IKr, the second most common subtype. Events are triggered by sudden AUDITORY startle — alarm clock, doorbell, telephone. IKr is also the channel blocked by most QT-prolonging drugs, so LQT2 carriers are exquisitely sensitive to acquired long QT; postpartum is a high-risk window. Beta-blockers are partially effective.
⚠️ Labeling startle-provoked syncope as vasovagal is the trap — auditory/startle-triggered syncope points to LQT2, and these patients are especially vulnerable to QT-prolonging drugs.
12. LQT3 — sleep/rest trigger (SCN5A gain)
LQT3 is a GAIN-of-function mutation in SCN5A causing a persistent (late) inward sodium current that prolongs repolarization. Events occur at REST or during SLEEP (bradycardia-dependent) and it carries the worst prognosis per event. Because the defect is excess late sodium current, beta-blockers are LESS effective than in LQT1/2. Note: SCN5A loss of function instead causes Brugada — same gene, opposite direction.
⚠️ Assuming beta-blockers are equally protective across all LQTS subtypes is the trap — LQT3 is the exception; it is the rest/sleep, worst-prognosis subtype responding less to beta-blockade.
13. LQT3 treatment — mexiletine + ICD
Because LQT3 is driven by a persistent late sodium current, mexiletine (Class IB sodium-channel blocker) is specifically useful — it blocks the late INa and shortens the QT. Beta-blockers are added but are less protective than in LQT1/2. An ICD is recommended for symptomatic LQT3 (prior arrest/syncope on therapy) given the high event severity.
⚠️ Picking a sodium-channel blocker for Brugada vs LQT3 is the discriminator — sodium-channel blockers are contraindicated in Brugada but therapeutic (mexiletine) in LQT3.
14. CPVT — RyR2, bidirectional VT, normal resting ECG, beta-blockers
CPVT is caused by RyR2 (ryanodine receptor) or calsequestrin (CASQ2) mutations causing excess diastolic calcium release. The resting ECG and QT are NORMAL; exercise or emotional stress provokes bidirectional VT (beat-to-beat QRS axis alternation) or polymorphic VT, often with syncope in children. Treatment is high-dose beta-blockers (nadolol), flecainide as add-on, exercise restriction, and ICD for high-risk patients.
⚠️ Calling exertional syncope with a normal resting ECG 'benign vasovagal' is the trap — exercise/emotion-triggered VT with a normal baseline ECG is CPVT; the diagnosis is made on stress/exercise testing, not the resting strip.
15. Short QT syndrome — gain-of-function K+ channels
Short QT syndrome is a rare channelopathy from GAIN-of-function mutations in potassium channels (e.g., KCNH2), giving an abnormally short QT interval (often QTc <340-360 ms) with tall, peaked, symmetric T waves. It predisposes to AF and sudden cardiac death/VF. Treatment is an ICD for symptomatic patients; quinidine can prolong the QT pharmacologically.
⚠️ Treating a strikingly short QT as a normal or benign finding is the trap — a very short QTc with peaked T waves and syncope/AF is short QT syndrome and carries SCD risk.
16. Genetics museum — structurally normal hearts, AD inheritance
Most inherited channelopathies (Brugada, LQTS, CPVT, short QT) share a structurally NORMAL heart with autosomal dominant inheritance, variable penetrance, and a family history of unexplained sudden death or drowning. ARVC is the structural exception (fibrofatty RV). Across the group, an ICD is the unifying definitive therapy for high-risk/symptomatic patients, and first-degree relatives warrant ECG screening and genetic counseling.
⚠️ Ordering only an echo to 'rule out' inherited sudden-death syndromes is the trap — with normal-heart channelopathies the diagnosis rests on the ECG pattern, triggers, provocation testing, and genetics, not structural imaging.
