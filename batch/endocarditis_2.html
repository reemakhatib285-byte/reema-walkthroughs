⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. Major evidence chute — Micro (positive cultures) — A single positive culture, especially of a skin contaminant like coagulase-negative staph or diphtheroids, tempts an IE diagnosis. The discriminator: one isolated positive set of a common contaminant does NOT meet the major criterion — you need typical organisms in 2 separate sets or persistently positive cultures.
  2. Major evidence — Coxiella / Q-fever serology — Culture-negative IE tempts the answer 'cannot diagnose without positive cultures.' The discriminator: Coxiella phase I IgG >1:800 is itself a MAJOR Duke criterion — serology can clinch culture-negative endocarditis.
  3. Major evidence — Imaging (echo vegetation/abscess) — Ordering PET/CT early after valve surgery tempts over-reading inflammation as infection. The discriminator: 18F-FDG PET/CT is UNRELIABLE within ~3 months of prosthetic valve implantation because of post-surgical inflammation, yielding false positives; it becomes a useful major-criterion tool only beyond that window.
  4. Minor — Predisposition (heart condition / IVDU) — Mitral valve prolapse is over-feared as a strong IE risk and is sometimes flagged for prophylaxis. The discriminator: MVP raises baseline risk only modestly and is NOT a high-risk lesion warranting antibiotic prophylaxis; only the highest-risk lesions (prosthetic valve, prior IE, certain congenital disease) qualify for prophylaxis.
  5. Minor — Fever ≥38°C — An afebrile patient tempts you to exclude IE. The discriminator: fever may be ABSENT or blunted in the elderly, those with renal/heart failure, immunocompromise, or after recent antibiotics — absence of fever does not rule out endocarditis.
  6. Minor — Vascular & immunologic phenomena — Janeway and Osler are easily lumped together. The discriminator: Janeway lesions are a VASCULAR (embolic, painless) minor criterion, whereas Osler nodes are an IMMUNOLOGIC (immune-complex, painful) minor criterion — they fall in different criterion categories, not the same one.
  7. Balance scale — Definite vs Possible vs Reject — The thresholds are deliberately swappable on exams (e.g., calling 1 major + 1 minor 'definite'). The discriminator: lock in 2/1+3/5 for DEFINITE and 1+1/3 for POSSIBLE — transposing the counts is the single most common Duke-criteria trap.
  8. Echo station — TTE misses, escalate to TEE — A negative TTE tempts the answer 'endocarditis excluded.' The discriminator: a negative TTE does NOT exclude IE — escalate to TEE (and repeat TEE if still negative but clinically suspected), especially with prosthetic valves or possible abscess.
  9. Native valve short-course aminoglycoside — Adding gentamicin to a staph native-valve regimen 'for synergy' is the trap. The discriminator: routine aminoglycoside is NOT recommended for native-valve S. aureus IE; its value is in strep dose-shortening and in enterococcal synergy, where the cell wall must be breached for the drug to enter.
  10. Streptococcus — Penicillin/Ceftriaxone arrow — Because gentamicin is mentioned for synergy, the trap is using an aminoglycoside as the backbone. The discriminator: aminoglycosides are NOT bactericidal as monotherapy against strep — they only work synergistically alongside a cell-wall agent; the backbone must be a beta-lactam (or vancomycin).
  11. MSSA native valve — Nafcillin — After empiric vancomycin, the trap is continuing vancomycin even after MSSA is confirmed. The discriminator: for MSSA, DE-ESCALATE to nafcillin/oxacillin/cefazolin — vancomycin has slower kill and higher failure rates against MSSA; cefazolin is the alternative if beta-lactam allergy is mild.
  12. MRSA native valve — Vancomycin + dart — If vancomycin fails or the MIC creeps up, the trap is simply pushing higher vancomycin doses. The discriminator: switch to daptomycin (or consider ceftaroline-based salvage) for vancomycin MIC ≥2 or persistent MRSA bacteremia — and remember daptomycin is useless for pneumonia (surfactant inactivation).
  13. Prosthetic valve — Nafcillin/Vancomycin + Rifampin + Gentamicin — Starting rifampin first or alone, or using it for native-valve staph, is the trap. The discriminator: rifampin is reserved for PROSTHETIC/foreign-material (biofilm) infection, must be added AFTER the bacteremia is controlled by the backbone (to avoid selecting rifampin resistance), and has no role in routine native-valve staph IE.
  14. Early surgery STOP sign — The trap is delaying surgery to 'finish the antibiotic course' before operating on complicated IE. The discriminator: in complicated IE (HF, abscess, persistent infection, large embolizing vegetation), surgery should NOT wait for antibiotic completion — early operation improves survival; remaining antibiotics are given postoperatively.
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Major evidence chute — Micro (positive cultures)
The microbiologic MAJOR criterion: (1) a TYPICAL IE organism from two SEPARATE blood cultures — viridans strep, S. gallolyticus, HACEK, S. aureus, or community-acquired enterococcus without a primary focus; OR (2) PERSISTENTLY positive cultures with any organism (≥2 sets drawn >12 h apart, or 3 of 3 / majority of ≥4 sets). Obtain 3 sets from separate venipunctures BEFORE antibiotics; the bacteremia of IE is continuous, so the timing relative to fever does not matter.
⚠️ A single positive culture, especially of a skin contaminant like coagulase-negative staph or diphtheroids, tempts an IE diagnosis. The discriminator: one isolated positive set of a common contaminant does NOT meet the major criterion — you need typical organisms in 2 separate sets or persistently positive cultures.
2. Major evidence — Coxiella / Q-fever serology
A serologic MAJOR criterion exists for the classic culture-negative cause: a single positive blood culture for Coxiella burnetii OR a phase I IgG antibody titer >1:800 counts as a stand-alone major criterion. This is why Q fever is singled out among fastidious organisms. Bartonella and other zoonotic causes are pursued with serology/PCR but are weighed within the overall clinical picture.
⚠️ Culture-negative IE tempts the answer 'cannot diagnose without positive cultures.' The discriminator: Coxiella phase I IgG >1:800 is itself a MAJOR Duke criterion — serology can clinch culture-negative endocarditis.
3. Major evidence — Imaging (echo vegetation/abscess)
The imaging MAJOR criterion: echocardiographic evidence of endocardial involvement — a VEGETATION, ABSCESS, or NEW partial dehiscence of a prosthetic valve (or a new valvular regurgitant murmur). The 2023 Duke-ISCVID update added abnormal activity around a prosthetic valve on PET/CT (>3 months post-op) and paravalvular lesions on cardiac CT as major criteria.
⚠️ Ordering PET/CT early after valve surgery tempts over-reading inflammation as infection. The discriminator: 18F-FDG PET/CT is UNRELIABLE within ~3 months of prosthetic valve implantation because of post-surgical inflammation, yielding false positives; it becomes a useful major-criterion tool only beyond that window.
4. Minor — Predisposition (heart condition / IVDU)
MINOR criterion #1 — predisposition: either a predisposing cardiac condition (prior IE, prosthetic valve, unrepaired cyanotic congenital disease, significant regurgitation/stenosis, prior valve repair) OR injection drug use. This identifies the at-risk host.
⚠️ Mitral valve prolapse is over-feared as a strong IE risk and is sometimes flagged for prophylaxis. The discriminator: MVP raises baseline risk only modestly and is NOT a high-risk lesion warranting antibiotic prophylaxis; only the highest-risk lesions (prosthetic valve, prior IE, certain congenital disease) qualify for prophylaxis.
5. Minor — Fever ≥38°C
MINOR criterion #2 — fever, defined as temperature ≥38.0°C (100.4°F). It is the most common presenting symptom of IE (present in ~90%) but, as a single minor criterion, is far from diagnostic on its own.
⚠️ An afebrile patient tempts you to exclude IE. The discriminator: fever may be ABSENT or blunted in the elderly, those with renal/heart failure, immunocompromise, or after recent antibiotics — absence of fever does not rule out endocarditis.
6. Minor — Vascular & immunologic phenomena
Two SEPARATE minor criteria. VASCULAR phenomena: arterial emboli, septic pulmonary infarcts, mycotic aneurysm, intracranial/conjunctival hemorrhage, JANEWAY lesions. IMMUNOLOGIC phenomena: glomerulonephritis, OSLER nodes, ROTH spots, rheumatoid factor. (A sixth minor criterion is microbiologic evidence not meeting a major criterion.)
⚠️ Janeway and Osler are easily lumped together. The discriminator: Janeway lesions are a VASCULAR (embolic, painless) minor criterion, whereas Osler nodes are an IMMUNOLOGIC (immune-complex, painful) minor criterion — they fall in different criterion categories, not the same one.
7. Balance scale — Definite vs Possible vs Reject
The Duke tally. CLINICALLY DEFINITE IE = 2 major, OR 1 major + 3 minor, OR 5 minor (pathologic definite = organisms/active endocarditis on the valve specimen). POSSIBLE IE = 1 major + 1 minor, OR 3 minor. REJECTED = firm alternative diagnosis, resolution with ≤4 days of antibiotics, or criteria not met.
⚠️ The thresholds are deliberately swappable on exams (e.g., calling 1 major + 1 minor 'definite'). The discriminator: lock in 2/1+3/5 for DEFINITE and 1+1/3 for POSSIBLE — transposing the counts is the single most common Duke-criteria trap.
8. Echo station — TTE misses, escalate to TEE
TTE is the first-line, noninvasive test but has limited sensitivity (~60–70% for native valves, far lower for prosthetic valves and small vegetations). TEE sensitivity is ~90–96% and is the test of choice when suspicion is high, when TTE is negative/nondiagnostic, and for prosthetic valves, suspected perivalvular abscess, or device leads. A negative TEE in a still-suspicious patient should be repeated in 5–7 days.
⚠️ A negative TTE tempts the answer 'endocarditis excluded.' The discriminator: a negative TTE does NOT exclude IE — escalate to TEE (and repeat TEE if still negative but clinically suspected), especially with prosthetic valves or possible abscess.
9. Native valve short-course aminoglycoside
Aminoglycosides (gentamicin) are used only for synergy, never as monotherapy. For susceptible viridans strep, low-dose gentamicin added to penicillin/ceftriaxone can SHORTEN therapy to 2 weeks. For S. aureus native-valve disease, routine gentamicin is NO LONGER recommended (added nephrotoxicity without clear benefit). For enterococcal IE, dual cell-wall + aminoglycoside (or ampicillin + ceftriaxone) synergy IS required for cure.
⚠️ Adding gentamicin to a staph native-valve regimen 'for synergy' is the trap. The discriminator: routine aminoglycoside is NOT recommended for native-valve S. aureus IE; its value is in strep dose-shortening and in enterococcal synergy, where the cell wall must be breached for the drug to enter.
10. Streptococcus — Penicillin/Ceftriaxone arrow
Penicillin-susceptible viridans strep / S. gallolyticus IE is treated with penicillin G or ceftriaxone (2 g IV daily) for 4 weeks, or a 2-week course with added gentamicin synergy for uncomplicated native-valve disease. Ceftriaxone's once-daily dosing makes it ideal for outpatient parenteral therapy. Penicillin allergy → vancomycin.
⚠️ Because gentamicin is mentioned for synergy, the trap is using an aminoglycoside as the backbone. The discriminator: aminoglycosides are NOT bactericidal as monotherapy against strep — they only work synergistically alongside a cell-wall agent; the backbone must be a beta-lactam (or vancomycin).
11. MSSA native valve — Nafcillin
Methicillin-SUSCEPTIBLE S. aureus on a native valve is treated with an antistaphylococcal beta-lactam — nafcillin or oxacillin 2 g IV every 4 h (or cefazolin 2 g IV every 8 h) for 6 weeks. Beta-lactams are bactericidal and clinically SUPERIOR to vancomycin for MSSA (faster clearance, lower relapse), so empiric vancomycin should be narrowed to nafcillin/cefazolin once susceptibility returns.
⚠️ After empiric vancomycin, the trap is continuing vancomycin even after MSSA is confirmed. The discriminator: for MSSA, DE-ESCALATE to nafcillin/oxacillin/cefazolin — vancomycin has slower kill and higher failure rates against MSSA; cefazolin is the alternative if beta-lactam allergy is mild.
12. MRSA native valve — Vancomycin + dart
Methicillin-RESISTANT S. aureus native-valve IE is treated with vancomycin (target trough 15–20 mg/L or AUC/MIC ≥400) OR daptomycin (≥8–10 mg/kg IV daily) for 6 weeks. Daptomycin is preferred when the vancomycin MIC is high (≥2) or the patient is failing; it is inactivated by surfactant in the lung, so do NOT use it for pneumonia, but it is fine for right-sided IE with pulmonary emboli.
⚠️ If vancomycin fails or the MIC creeps up, the trap is simply pushing higher vancomycin doses. The discriminator: switch to daptomycin (or consider ceftaroline-based salvage) for vancomycin MIC ≥2 or persistent MRSA bacteremia — and remember daptomycin is useless for pneumonia (surfactant inactivation).
13. Prosthetic valve — Nafcillin/Vancomycin + Rifampin + Gentamicin
Staphylococcal PROSTHETIC-valve endocarditis requires triple therapy: the backbone (nafcillin/cefazolin for MSSA, or vancomycin for MRSA) PLUS rifampin (penetrates and sterilizes biofilm on foreign material) PLUS gentamicin for the first 2 weeks (added bactericidal synergy), with the backbone + rifampin continued for ≥6 weeks. PVE within 1 year of surgery is usually staphylococcal/nosocomial.
⚠️ Starting rifampin first or alone, or using it for native-valve staph, is the trap. The discriminator: rifampin is reserved for PROSTHETIC/foreign-material (biofilm) infection, must be added AFTER the bacteremia is controlled by the backbone (to avoid selecting rifampin resistance), and has no role in routine native-valve staph IE.
14. Early surgery STOP sign
Antibiotics alone fail in complicated IE; EARLY surgery (during the index hospitalization, often within days) is indicated for: refractory heart failure from valve dysfunction (the leading indication), perivalvular abscess/fistula/new heart block, large mobile vegetations (>10 mm) with recurrent emboli, persistent bacteremia/fever despite ≥5–7 days of appropriate antibiotics, prosthetic valve dehiscence, and fungal or highly resistant organisms.
⚠️ The trap is delaying surgery to 'finish the antibiotic course' before operating on complicated IE. The discriminator: in complicated IE (HF, abscess, persistent infection, large embolizing vegetation), surgery should NOT wait for antibiotic completion — early operation improves survival; remaining antibiotics are given postoperatively.
