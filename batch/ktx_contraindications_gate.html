📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. ACTIVE = block (the gate rule)
Core principle: ACTIVE problems (active infection, active malignancy, active substance abuse, untreated/active severe psychiatric or non-adherence) are contraindications. Once controlled, most are no longer absolute barriers.
⚠️ Most contraindications are relative once disease is CONTROLLED — 'has cancer history' alone is not an absolute no-go.
2. Active infection — blocks transplant
Untreated active infection (e.g., active TB, untreated sepsis, active hepatitis with viremia) must be controlled before transplant, since immunosuppression will worsen it. Treat and clear first.
⚠️ Active infection delays, not permanently bars, transplant — contrast with positive cytotoxic crossmatch, which is an absolute donor-specific no-go.
3. Active malignancy — blocks; treated cancer maybe
Active cancer is a contraindication. After curative treatment, a waiting period (commonly ~2–5 years depending on tumor type/stage) is observed before listing because immunosuppression promotes recurrence.
⚠️ Remote, treated cancer is often acceptable — it is not an absolute lifelong contraindication; the answer is 'wait, then list.'
4. Recent cancer → wait 2–5 years
Wait time after cancer cure depends on type and stage — low-risk skin/in-situ may need little wait, while aggressive solid tumors need years of disease-free interval before transplant.
⚠️ There is no single universal wait — high-stage aggressive tumors need a longer disease-free interval than a treated low-grade lesion.
5. Hyperacute rejection — preformed antibody
Hyperacute rejection occurs within minutes–hours due to PREFORMED donor-specific (often anti-ABO or anti-HLA) antibodies that fix complement and thrombose the graft. It is prevented by crossmatching, not treated.
⚠️ Hyperacute rejection is immediate and untreatable — distinguish from acute cellular rejection (days–weeks, T-cell, steroid-responsive).
6. Positive cytotoxic crossmatch — absolute no-go
A positive complement-dependent cytotoxicity crossmatch means preformed anti-donor antibody — an ABSOLUTE contraindication to THAT specific donor (predicts hyperacute rejection). It is donor-specific, not a universal bar.
⚠️ Positive crossmatch blocks only THAT donor pairing — the patient can still receive a different compatible kidney.
7. ABO mismatch
ABO incompatibility risks hyperacute rejection but can be overcome with desensitization (plasmapheresis, IVIG, rituximab) or paired donation, unlike an unmodifiable positive crossmatch.
⚠️ ABO incompatibility is not absolute — desensitization or paired exchange can enable transplant; positive cytotoxic crossmatch is the harder barrier.
8. Paired donation / desensitization
For sensitized or ABO-incompatible pairs, options include kidney paired exchange (swap donors) and desensitization protocols (plasmapheresis + IVIG ± rituximab) to lower antibody before transplant.
⚠️ Sensitization is a barrier to be engineered around (paired exchange/desensitize), not an automatic disqualification from transplant entirely.
9. HIV — controlled is acceptable
Well-controlled HIV (suppressed viral load, adequate CD4, on ART) is NOT a contraindication; HIV+ to HIV+ transplantation is performed. Uncontrolled/AIDS-defining illness is the barrier.
⚠️ HIV itself is no longer an absolute contraindication — controlled HIV with suppressed VL can be transplanted.
10. Hepatitis C — treatable, transplantable
With direct-acting antivirals, HCV is curable; HCV+ donors can even be used for recipients with planned DAA treatment. Active untreated cirrhosis/decompensation is the real concern.
⚠️ HCV is no longer a bar — DAAs cure it, and HCV+ organs are deliberately used; don't reflexively exclude HCV recipients.
11. Controlled chronic disease — ADMIT
Stable, controlled chronic conditions (well-managed diabetes, controlled HTN, compensated cardiac disease) and good functional status favor candidacy. Most ESRD patients benefit from transplant over dialysis.
⚠️ Controlled comorbidity does NOT block transplant — most candidates benefit; few contraindications are absolute but high-yield.
12. Active substance abuse / non-adherence
Active substance use disorder and documented non-adherence predict graft loss and are contraindications until addressed, since immunosuppression demands strict adherence.
⚠️ Non-adherence/active addiction is a behavioral contraindication — modifiable; abstinence and adherence support can restore candidacy.
13. Strong functional status favors listing
Adequate functional status and life expectancy to benefit from the graft are required; severe frailty or limited life expectancy may preclude transplant on a risk/benefit basis.
⚠️ Frailty/limited life expectancy can preclude transplant even with no 'disease' contraindication — benefit must outweigh surgical risk.
14. Boards decision band
Algorithm: ACTIVE infection or malignancy → control first; recent cancer → wait 2–5 yr; positive cytotoxic crossmatch → absolute no-go for THAT donor only; controlled chronic disease/HIV/HCV → eligible.
⚠️ Single most-tested fact: positive crossmatch = absolute donor-specific contraindication; nearly everything else is relative once controlled.
