📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. AVP foreman, doors stay locked
In nephrogenic DI the collecting duct fails to respond to AVP (ADH): the aquaporin-2 'doors' don't insert despite present hormone. Result is impaired water reabsorption and inappropriately dilute urine despite hyperosmolar plasma.
⚠️ Treating NDI with DDAVP — the defect is renal resistance, so vasopressin/DDAVP fails to concentrate the urine.
2. AQP2 channels stay shut
AVP normally binds V2 receptors → cAMP → insertion of aquaporin-2 water channels in the apical collecting duct membrane. In NDI this signaling/insertion is blocked, so water can't be reabsorbed and urine stays dilute.
⚠️ Localizing the lesion to the hypothalamus/pituitary — NDI is a collecting-duct (kidney) defect, not a central AVP deficiency.
3. Lithium is the classic cause
Lithium enters principal cells via ENaC and disrupts AVP-stimulated aquaporin-2 trafficking, the most common acquired NDI. Other causes: hypercalcemia, hypokalemia, chronic obstruction, demeclocycline.
⚠️ Blaming primary polydipsia for the polyuria in a lithium patient — lithium-induced NDI is the real cause; polydipsia is the red herring.
4. Amiloride blocks ENaC (lithium NDI Rx)
Amiloride blocks the ENaC channel that lithium uses to enter principal cells, reducing intracellular lithium and partially reversing lithium-induced NDI while allowing lithium to be continued.
⚠️ Stopping lithium as the only option — amiloride can mitigate lithium NDI by blocking its ENaC entry route.
5. Thiazide: paradoxical antidiuresis
Thiazides paradoxically reduce urine output in NDI by inducing mild volume contraction → enhanced proximal Na/water reabsorption → less water delivered distally. First-line symptomatic therapy along with low-salt, low-protein diet.
⚠️ Avoiding diuretics in a polyuric patient — thiazides actually decrease urine volume in NDI via proximal reabsorption.
6. Furosemide worsens depletion
Loop diuretics impair the medullary concentration gradient and worsen volume/electrolyte depletion, aggravating NDI polyuria. They are the wrong diuretic class here.
⚠️ Reaching for furosemide to 'treat' polyuria — it worsens NDI; the thiazide is the counterintuitive correct choice.
7. DDAVP fails in NDI
DDAVP (desmopressin) is a V2 agonist that works in CENTRAL DI but fails in nephrogenic DI because the tubule cannot respond. The DDAVP response is the key test separating central from nephrogenic DI.
⚠️ A normal urine-concentrating response to DDAVP — that excludes NDI and indicates central DI instead.
8. Hypercalcemia causes acquired NDI
Hypercalcemia activates the calcium-sensing receptor in the collecting duct, downregulating aquaporin-2 and causing reversible NDI with polyuria. Correcting calcium reverses the concentrating defect.
⚠️ Missing hypercalcemia as a reversible NDI trigger — work up Ca (and K) before labeling NDI idiopathic.
9. Hypokalemia causes acquired NDI
Hypokalemia also downregulates aquaporin-2 and impairs the medullary gradient, producing a reversible nephrogenic concentrating defect. Repleting potassium restores urinary concentration.
⚠️ Overlooking low K as a cause of polyuria — correcting hypokalemia can resolve the NDI.
10. Acquired NDI label
Acquired NDI (lithium, hypercalcemia, hypokalemia, obstruction, demeclocycline) is far more common than congenital (V2 receptor or AQP2 gene mutations). Identify and reverse the offending cause first.
⚠️ Assuming a genetic cause in an adult — acquired/drug-electrolyte NDI dominates and is often reversible.
11. Water deprivation: high Osm, no concentration
In DI a water deprivation test fails to concentrate urine (urine stays dilute, Osm low) while serum Na/Osm rise. Then DDAVP distinguishes: no response = nephrogenic; concentrates = central.
⚠️ Confusing primary polydipsia (low plasma Osm, low Na) with DI — in DI plasma Osm/Na are HIGH; polydipsia gives LOW values.
12. Tubule can't concentrate → dilute urine
The net result of failed AVP action is persistently dilute urine (low urine osmolality, often <300) despite a hyperosmolar plasma — the hallmark of diabetes insipidus, here nephrogenic in origin.
⚠️ Reading low urine osmolality as 'overhydration' — with high plasma Osm it signals a concentrating defect (DI), not water excess.
