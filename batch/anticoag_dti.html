📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Direct thrombin inhibitors
Direct thrombin inhibitors (DTIs) bind and inactivate thrombin (factor IIa) DIRECTLY, with no need for the antithrombin cofactor that heparins require ('No Intruding' = no middleman). Because they act at the final common enzyme of coagulation, they block fibrinogen→fibrin conversion, thrombin-mediated activation of factors V/VIII/XI/XIII, and thrombin-driven platelet activation. The parenteral DTIs are argatroban and bivalirudin (also desirudin/lepirudin historically); the oral DTI is dabigatran.
⚠️ Classic board contrast: heparin/LMWH/fondaparinux work INDIRECTLY by potentiating antithrombin, so they fail in antithrombin deficiency and in HIT (where heparin is the culprit). DTIs work independently of antithrombin, which is exactly why they are the agents of choice in HIT.
2. Inhibit free + clot-bound
DTIs inhibit BOTH circulating (free) thrombin AND thrombin already bound to fibrin within an existing clot. This is a key pharmacologic advantage: the heparin–antithrombin complex is too bulky to reach thrombin trapped in a clot, so heparin only neutralizes free thrombin and leaves clot-bound thrombin to keep propagating the thrombus.
⚠️ Board favorite: 'Which anticoagulant inactivates clot-bound thrombin?' → a DTI. Heparin/LMWH inactivate only free (fluid-phase) thrombin, allowing rebound thrombin generation from the clot surface.
3. Argatroban univalent
Argatroban is a small synthetic UNIVALENT DTI ('one hand') that binds only the catalytic active site of thrombin. It is a reversible competitive inhibitor. Univalent binding means it does not occupy exosite 1.
⚠️ Distinguish from bivalirudin (bivalent, binds active site + exosite 1). Hirudin-derived agents are bivalent; argatroban and dabigatran are univalent active-site binders.
4. Argatroban hepatic clearance
Argatroban is cleared HEPATICALLY (metabolized by the liver, minimal renal excretion). This makes it the DTI of choice in patients with renal failure/dialysis, because the dose does not need adjustment for kidney function. Standard infusion ~2 µg/kg/min, titrated to aPTT 1.5–3× baseline.
⚠️ REDUCE or avoid argatroban in significant hepatic impairment (start ~0.5 µg/kg/min in liver disease) — accumulation causes bleeding. Mirror trap: bivalirudin/dabigatran depend on renal clearance, so the rule is 'bad kidneys → argatroban, bad liver → not argatroban.'
5. Argatroban for HIT
Argatroban is licensed for treatment and prophylaxis of thrombosis in heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT), and is the preferred DTI when the HIT patient ALSO has renal failure (hepatic clearance is unaffected by kidneys).
⚠️ In HIT you must stop ALL heparin (including LMWH and heparin flushes) and start a non-heparin anticoagulant (DTI such as argatroban, or fondaparinux/DOAC per scenario). Do NOT start warfarin alone in acute HIT — give a parenteral agent first and overlap, because warfarin-induced protein C drop can precipitate venous limb gangrene.
6. Argatroban short half-life
Argatroban has a short half-life of roughly 45 minutes, so anticoagulation reverses quickly on stopping the infusion. Monitored and titrated by aPTT (target 1.5–3× baseline; ACT used during PCI).
⚠️ There is NO specific antidote for argatroban or bivalirudin — management of bleeding relies on stopping the drug and waiting out the short half-life (± supportive measures). Only dabigatran has a reversal agent (idarucizumab).
7. Bivalirudin bivalent
Bivalirudin is a synthetic BIVALENT DTI ('two hands') — a hirudin analog that simultaneously binds thrombin's catalytic active site AND exosite 1 (the fibrinogen-binding/substrate-recognition site). Its inhibition is potent but transient because thrombin slowly cleaves the bound bivalirudin, restoring some enzyme activity.
⚠️ Bivalent vs univalent is testable: bivalirudin and the hirudins are bivalent; argatroban and dabigatran are univalent active-site-only inhibitors.
8. Bivalirudin renal clearance
Bivalirudin is cleared mostly by enzymatic proteolysis (thrombin-mediated cleavage), with a significant PARTIAL RENAL component (~20%). Dose reduction is required in renal impairment, and it accumulates in severe renal failure/dialysis.
⚠️ Reverse of argatroban: in a HIT patient with renal failure choose argatroban (hepatic), not bivalirudin. Bivalirudin is favored in the cath lab/PCI setting rather than for renal-failure inpatients.
9. Bivalirudin shortest half-life
Bivalirudin has the shortest half-life of the DTIs (~25 minutes), driven mainly by enzymatic degradation. This rapid offset is ideal for procedural anticoagulation during PCI, where quick on/off control limits bleeding.
⚠️ Short half-life is an advantage, but remember there is still no specific reversal agent — you simply stop the infusion and wait.
10. Bivalirudin for PCI
Bivalirudin is the DTI of choice for percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI), including PCI in patients with HIT or at high bleeding risk, given via IV during the procedure (ACT-guided). It is used as an alternative to heparin ± GP IIb/IIIa inhibitors.
⚠️ Board association: 'HIT patient who needs cardiac catheterization/PCI' → bivalirudin. 'HIT patient with renal failure on the ward' → argatroban.
11. Comparison scoreboard
Scoreboard summary — Argatroban: univalent, MW ~527, hepatic clearance, t½ ~45 min, the bigger INR offender. Bivalirudin: bivalent, MW ~1980, partial renal/enzymatic clearance, t½ ~25 min (shortest), used in PCI. Both are parenteral, both monitored by aPTT (ACT in cath lab), both lack a reversal agent.
⚠️ Don't confuse with dabigatran (oral DTI): dabigatran is renally cleared, dosed orally, and IS reversible with idarucizumab — the only DTI with a specific antidote.
12. INR trap with argatroban
Argatroban prolongs the PT/INR more than other DTIs because it inhibits thrombin in the PT reagent, FALSELY elevating the INR. This is a major pitfall when transitioning a HIT patient from argatroban to warfarin: the combined INR overestimates the true warfarin effect, and stopping argatroban too early leaves the patient under-anticoagulated.
⚠️ Practical board rule: during argatroban→warfarin overlap, target a HIGHER combined INR (often >4) per protocol, then STOP the argatroban infusion and RE-CHECK the INR ~2 hours later; only continue warfarin alone once the off-argatroban INR is therapeutic (2–3).
13. Both monitored by aPTT
Both argatroban and bivalirudin are titrated to the aPTT (typically 1.5–2.5× baseline) for systemic anticoagulation; during PCI the activated clotting time (ACT) is used instead because of the much higher doses and shorter time frame.
⚠️ Unlike heparin, DTIs require no antithrombin and are not reversed by protamine. Monitoring is aPTT/ACT — anti-Xa assays are for heparin/LMWH/fondaparinux, NOT for DTIs.
