📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Osmolality + volume → AVP release
AVP secretion is driven by two inputs: plasma osmolality (sensed by hypothalamic osmoreceptors, exquisitely sensitive) and effective blood volume/pressure (baroreceptors, less sensitive but dominant when severe). Both converge on the supraoptic/paraventricular neurons.
⚠️ Osmotic control is the fine-tuner; a large volume/BP drop overrides it and releases AVP even when the patient is hypo-osmolar — the basis of hypovolemic hyponatremia.
2. Magnocellular neuron / hypothalamus
AVP is synthesized in magnocellular neurons of the supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei of the hypothalamus, then transported down axons. Damage here (not the gland) causes central DI.
⚠️ Central DI is a hypothalamic/stalk problem — a lesion sparing the magnocellular neurons but hitting only the gland tip may cause only transient DI.
3. Posterior pituitary storage
AVP travels along the supraopticohypophyseal tract and is STORED/released from the posterior pituitary (neurohypophysis). The stored hormone produces the T1 MRI 'bright spot'.
⚠️ The posterior pituitary stores but does not synthesize AVP — high stalk-section lesions abolish the bright spot; low ones may spare it.
4. Osmoreceptor — senses Na, not glucose/urea
Hypothalamic osmoreceptors respond to EFFECTIVE osmoles (sodium). Urea and glucose (in the absence of insulin deficiency) cross cell membranes and are ineffective osmoles that don't trigger AVP.
⚠️ A high BUN raises measured osmolality but is an INEFFECTIVE osmole — it doesn't stimulate AVP or shift water, so don't treat urea-driven hyperosmolality like hypernatremia.
5. Chromosome 20 — AVP-neurophysin II-copeptin
The AVP gene on chromosome 20 makes a preprohormone cleaved into AVP, neurophysin II (carrier), and copeptin. Copeptin's 1:1 co-secretion underlies its use as an AVP surrogate.
⚠️ Copeptin is co-released stoichiometrically with AVP — that is exactly why a copeptin assay can stand in for the unmeasurable AVP.
6. Osmotic threshold ~280–285
AVP secretion begins around plasma osmolality 280–285 mOsm/kg; thirst is triggered slightly higher (~290–295). This narrow range keeps osmolality tightly regulated.
⚠️ Thirst threshold sits ABOVE the AVP threshold — losing thirst (e.g., adipsic/hypothalamic lesions) is more dangerous because the last defense against hypernatremia is gone.
7. Blood pressure / volume — 5–10% drop
Baroreceptor-mediated AVP release requires a larger stimulus (roughly >5–10% volume/BP fall) but then becomes powerful, overriding osmotic suppression. Signals via vagal/glossopharyngeal afferents.
⚠️ Small volume changes don't move AVP, but large hypovolemia massively stimulates it — explaining ADH-mediated water retention and hyponatremia in heart failure/cirrhosis.
8. V2 receptor → cAMP → AQP2
AVP binds the basolateral V2 receptor on collecting-duct principal cells, activating Gs/cAMP/PKA, which inserts aquaporin-2 (AQP2) channels into the apical membrane to reabsorb water. Nephrogenic DI is a defect here.
⚠️ Nephrogenic DI = V2/AQP2 defect downstream of normal AVP — so DDAVP (a V2 agonist) fails, distinguishing it from central DI.
9. Bright spot present vs absent
The posterior pituitary T1 bright spot represents stored AVP-neurophysin; it is present normally and absent in central (AVP-deficient) DI. Used to localize central causes on MRI.
⚠️ Absence of the bright spot supports central DI but can also fade with normal aging — interpret alongside copeptin, not in isolation.
10. Oxytocin — sister hormone
Oxytocin is co-produced in the same magnocellular system; it drives uterine contraction, milk letdown, and social bonding. Structurally similar to AVP, explaining minor cross-reactivity.
⚠️ High-dose oxytocin has weak antidiuretic (V2) activity and can cause water intoxication/hyponatremia during labor inductions.
11. V1 effects — vasoconstriction / nausea / hypoglycemia
Beyond osmolality/volume, AVP release is stimulated by nausea (most potent), pain, hypoglycemia, angiotensin II, and nicotine. V1 receptors mediate vasoconstriction and glycogenolysis.
⚠️ Nausea is one of the strongest non-osmotic AVP stimuli — post-op nausea/pain drives SIADH-like hyponatremia even with normal osmoreceptors.
12. Desmopressin (DDAVP) — V2-selective, long half-life
Desmopressin is a synthetic V2-selective analog with minimal V1 pressor effect, resistance to vasopressinase, and a longer half-life — making it the treatment for central DI and a tool in the DDAVP concentration test.
⚠️ DDAVP's V2 selectivity means it won't help nephrogenic DI (receptor/channel resistance) — and overuse risks dilutional hyponatremia.
13. Vasopressinase — pregnancy / placenta
Placental vasopressinase degrades native AVP, increasing clearance 3–4x and causing transient gestational DI in the 3rd trimester. DDAVP (vasopressinase-resistant) is the treatment.
⚠️ Gestational DI is treated with DDAVP, not native vasopressin, because placental vasopressinase rapidly destroys endogenous/native AVP.
