⚠️ Board traps 19

Discriminator: viral/NAFLD give ALT>AST (ratio <1); the >2:1 pattern points to alcohol, but NAFLD that has progressed to cirrhosis can also flip the ratio above 1 — interpret with the clinical context, not the number in isolation.

Discriminator: AST/ALT >1000 are NOT alcohol — think ischemic ('shock') hepatitis, acute viral hepatitis, or toxin (acetaminophen); reconsider the diagnosis when an apparent drinker has very high values (look for coingested acetaminophen, hypotension, viral cause).

📖 Read-through — all 19 symbols
1. ALT tube (liver-specific)
ALT (alanine aminotransferase) is the LIVER-specific transaminase — it is concentrated in hepatocytes, so its elevation reliably localizes injury to the liver. An ALT-predominant (ALT > AST) hepatocellular pattern classically reflects viral hepatitis or NAFLD/MASLD (fatty liver). In chronic HCV and steatohepatitis ALT can be only mildly elevated yet still mark ongoing injury, so it anchors the noninvasive fibrosis workup.
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming a normal ALT excludes significant liver disease/fibrosis. Discriminator: ALT can be normal in advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis and in NAFLD — staging relies on FIB-4 and elastography, not on the ALT value alone.
2. AST tube (multi-organ)
AST (aspartate aminotransferase) is MULTI-organ: it comes from liver, skeletal muscle, cardiac muscle, kidney and red blood cells, so it is NOT liver-specific. An isolated AST rise should prompt a non-hepatic search (CK for muscle/rhabdomyolysis, hemolysis, recent MI, strenuous exercise) before blaming the liver.
⚠️ WRONG answer: diagnosing liver disease from an isolated AST elevation with a normal ALT. Discriminator: check CK and a hemolysis panel — muscle injury (rhabdo, myositis, macro-AST) or hemolysis raises AST without liver disease; a truly hepatic process usually moves ALT too.
3. AST:ALT dial >2:1
The AST:ALT (De Ritis) ratio triages hepatocellular injury: ALT > AST (ratio <1) suggests viral hepatitis or NAFLD; AST:ALT >2:1 suggests alcoholic liver disease; >3:1 strongly supports established ALD. A rising ratio toward/above 1 in known NAFLD also signals progression to advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis, and any cause plus a low albumin/high INR signals cirrhosis.
⚠️ WRONG answer: using an AST:ALT >2:1 to call viral hepatitis or to diagnose ALD by ratio alone. Discriminator: viral/NAFLD give ALT>AST (ratio <1); the >2:1 pattern points to alcohol, but NAFLD that has progressed to cirrhosis can also flip the ratio above 1 — interpret with the clinical context, not the number in isolation.
4. AST>300 in alcohol rare
In pure alcoholic hepatitis the AST rarely exceeds ~300-400 IU/L despite a sick patient; the injury is ratio-defined (AST:ALT >2) rather than magnitude-defined. The relatively modest AST cap is a key board discriminator from the >1000 causes.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing transaminases in the thousands to alcohol. Discriminator: AST/ALT >1000 are NOT alcohol — think ischemic ('shock') hepatitis, acute viral hepatitis, or toxin (acetaminophen); reconsider the diagnosis when an apparent drinker has very high values (look for coingested acetaminophen, hypotension, viral cause).
5. B6 deficiency / low ALT
ALT synthesis requires pyridoxal-5'-phosphate (vitamin B6). Chronic alcoholics are B6-deficient, so they make less ALT — this is the mechanism that DROPS the ALT and raises the AST:ALT ratio above 2 in alcoholic liver disease (it is the AST:ALT ratio's underlying explanation, not just a footnote).
⚠️ WRONG answer: interpreting the low ALT in an alcoholic as evidence of mild/improving liver disease. Discriminator: the low ALT is a B6-deficiency artifact inflating the ratio — it does not mean less injury; severity is judged by Maddrey's discriminant function/MELD, not by the falsely low ALT.
6. Gilbert range bar
Unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia from impaired UGT1A1 conjugation grades by bilirubin level: Gilbert syndrome typically <3 mg/dL (reduced UGT1A1 activity), Crigler-Najjar type II ~6-25 mg/dL (severe partial deficiency, responds to phenobarbital), Crigler-Najjar type I ~20-45 mg/dL (near-absent enzyme, kernicterus risk, needs phototherapy/transplant).
⚠️ WRONG answer: treating a mildly elevated INDIRECT bilirubin (<3) as a cholestatic/obstructive problem warranting imaging. Discriminator: isolated unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia with normal ALP/transaminases and no hemolysis is Gilbert — benign, no imaging or biopsy; cholestasis is CONJUGATED with high ALP.
7. Gilbert no jaundice face
Gilbert syndrome is a benign, common (~5-10%) inherited reduction in UGT1A1 conjugation producing mild, fluctuating, often subclinical indirect hyperbilirubinemia. It is unmasked by FASTING, illness, stress, dehydration or exertion, and is otherwise asymptomatic with a completely normal exam — no treatment needed.
⚠️ WRONG answer: launching a hepatitis/cholestasis workup or biopsy in a young patient with intermittent mild jaundice during illness/fasting. Discriminator: normal CBC, normal LFTs except isolated indirect bilirubin, no hemolysis = Gilbert; the trigger (fasting/stress) and otherwise normal labs clinch it without further testing.
8. Normal CBC, no hemolysis
For isolated unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia, a NORMAL CBC with no hemolysis markers (normal reticulocytes, LDH, haptoglobin, no schistocytes) effectively confirms Gilbert and ends the workup — no imaging, no biopsy.
⚠️ WRONG answer: ordering a hemolysis panel/imaging anyway when the CBC is normal and bilirubin is isolated indirect. Discriminator: with a normal CBC and no hemolysis the cause is Gilbert by default; pursuing hemolysis labs or biliary imaging is the over-testing trap.
9. Abnormal CBC → hemolysis
If the CBC is ABNORMAL (anemia) alongside unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia, pursue a hemolysis workup: reticulocyte count (high), LDH (high), haptoglobin (low), peripheral smear (schistocytes/spherocytes), and a direct Coombs/DAT. Hemolysis overproduces bilirubin faster than the liver can conjugate it.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing the indirect hyperbilirubinemia to Gilbert when the patient is anemic. Discriminator: anemia + reticulocytosis + high LDH + low haptoglobin = hemolysis, not Gilbert — Gilbert has a NORMAL CBC and no hemolysis markers.
10. IgG / AIH jar
A high IgG (hypergammaglobulinemia) points to autoimmune hepatitis — a hepatocellular, interface-hepatitis disease, classically in women, with positive ANA and anti-smooth-muscle antibody (type 1) or anti-LKM-1 (type 2). Treatment is corticosteroids (prednisone) +/- azathioprine.
⚠️ WRONG answer: mistaking the high-IgG, AMA-negative cholestatic/hepatocellular picture for PBC. Discriminator: AIH = high IgG + ANA/ASMA + hepatocellular (high ALT) responding to steroids; PBC = high IgM + AMA + cholestatic (high ALP) treated with ursodeoxycholic acid — the immunoglobulin class and the LFT pattern separate them.
11. IgM / PBC jar
A high IgM together with a positive anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA) points to primary biliary cholangitis — autoimmune destruction of small bile ducts in a middle-aged woman, presenting with pruritus, fatigue and a cholestatic (high ALP) profile. First-line therapy is ursodeoxycholic acid 13-15 mg/kg/day.
⚠️ WRONG answer: confirming PBC by cholangiography or confusing its IgM with the IgG of AIH. Discriminator: PBC is IgM + AMA + cholestatic with a NORMAL cholangiogram (small-duct disease); AIH is IgG + ANA/ASMA + hepatocellular — the antibody and Ig class decide it, not imaging.
12. IgA alcoholic jar
A high IgA points to alcoholic liver disease (the 'A for Alcohol' mnemonic: IgA-Alcohol, IgM-PBC/Mitochondrial, IgG-autoimmune hepatitis). It complements the AST:ALT >2:1 pattern and the alcohol-related peripheral stigmata.
⚠️ WRONG answer: forgetting the Ig-to-disease mapping and pairing IgA with an autoimmune liver disease. Discriminator: IgA = alcohol, IgM = PBC, IgG = AIH — use this triad to assign the immunoglobulin to the correct etiology.
13. FIB-4 calculator
FIB-4 = (age x AST) / (platelets x sqrt(ALT)) — a free, four-variable index that is the recommended FIRST-LINE noninvasive fibrosis estimate in NAFLD/MASLD and other chronic liver disease. It risk-stratifies who can stay in primary care versus who needs elastography or hepatology referral, avoiding biopsy in most patients.
⚠️ WRONG answer: going straight to liver biopsy to stage fibrosis in NAFLD. Discriminator: the validated pathway is FIB-4 first, then elastography (FibroScan) for indeterminate/high values, and biopsy only when noninvasive tests are discordant or inconclusive.
14. FIB-4 <1.3 community
A FIB-4 <1.3 (or <2.0 if age >65) has a high negative predictive value for advanced fibrosis — these patients are LOW risk and can be managed in primary/community care with lifestyle modification and periodic reassessment, no specialist or biopsy needed.
⚠️ WRONG answer: referring every NAFLD patient to hepatology regardless of FIB-4. Discriminator: FIB-4 <1.3 rules out advanced fibrosis well — referral/elastography is reserved for FIB-4 >1.3 (indeterminate) or >2.67 (high risk).
15. FIB-4 >2.67 referral
A FIB-4 >2.67 indicates HIGH probability of advanced fibrosis — these patients need hepatology referral and further staging (transient elastography/FibroScan, and biopsy if discordant). Values between 1.3 and 2.67 are indeterminate and go to elastography next.
⚠️ WRONG answer: reassuring/discharging a patient with FIB-4 >2.67. Discriminator: a high FIB-4 mandates referral and elastography — treating it as low-risk misses advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis that needs varices and HCC surveillance.
16. FibroScan first
Transient elastography (FibroScan) measures liver stiffness in kilopascals (kPa) as a noninvasive surrogate for fibrosis stage — roughly <7 kPa low/none, ~8-12 kPa significant fibrosis, and >12-15 kPa suggestive of advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis (cutoffs vary by etiology). It is the second step after an elevated/indeterminate FIB-4 and stages fibrosis BEFORE biopsy is considered.
⚠️ WRONG answer: choosing liver biopsy as the first-line test to stage fibrosis. Discriminator: biopsy is NOT first-line — noninvasive FIB-4 then FibroScan come first; biopsy (with its bleeding risk and sampling error) is reserved for discordant or inconclusive noninvasive results.
17. Platelets low = fibrosis
A falling platelet count is one of the earliest clues to advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis: portal hypertension causes splenic sequestration (hypersplenism) and reduced hepatic thrombopoietin lowers platelets. Low platelets is also why FIB-4 (which divides by platelets) rises with worsening fibrosis.
⚠️ WRONG answer: working up isolated thrombocytopenia as a primary hematologic problem (ITP, marrow) while ignoring the liver. Discriminator: in chronic liver disease, low platelets reflect portal hypertension/hypersplenism — check spleen size, FIB-4 and synthetic function before assuming a marrow/immune cause.
18. HBV flag
Always screen for hepatitis B (HBsAg, anti-HBc, anti-HBs) and hepatitis C (anti-HCV) as treatable, fibrosis-driving causes in any chronic liver/fibrosis workup. Chronic HBV independently raises HCC risk (even without cirrhosis), mandating surveillance and antiviral therapy (entecavir/tenofovir) when indicated.
⚠️ WRONG answer: omitting viral hepatitis serologies and attributing all fibrosis to NAFLD/alcohol. Discriminator: HBV/HCV are reversible drivers — missing them forgoes curative DAA therapy (HCV) or HCC surveillance and antivirals (HBV); chronic HBV needs HCC screening even without cirrhosis.
19. MRCP / biopsy crossed
MRCP and liver biopsy are NOT part of the routine evaluation of isolated unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia (Gilbert pattern) — they are reserved for unexplained, abnormal, or discordant cases (e.g., abnormal CBC suggesting hemolysis, cholestatic biochemistry, or noninvasive tests that disagree).
⚠️ WRONG answer: ordering MRCP or biopsy for benign Gilbert-pattern isolated indirect hyperbilirubinemia. Discriminator: with normal CBC, no hemolysis and normal ALP/transaminases the answer is reassurance — invasive/advanced imaging is over-testing; escalate only when something is genuinely abnormal.
