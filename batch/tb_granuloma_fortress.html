📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. CD4+ Th1 → IFN-γ
Containment of TB is a CD4+ Th1 cell-mediated response. Th1 cells secrete IFN-γ which activates macrophages to kill intracellular bacilli. This is the immunologic basis of both the granuloma and the IGRA/TST tests.
⚠️ It is NOT a humoral/antibody response — antibodies are useless against intracellular mycobacteria. Loss of CD4 (HIV) is what reactivates TB.
2. Activates macrophage
IFN-γ–activated macrophages fuse into epithelioid/giant cells forming the granuloma. Activated macrophages restrain but rarely sterilize bacilli, producing latent infection.
⚠️ TNF-α (not just IFN-γ) maintains the granuloma — anti-TNF biologics (infliximab) cause reactivation, so screen for LTBI before starting them.
3. Caseous necrosis
Caseating (cheese-like) granuloma is the histologic hallmark of TB. Central caseous necrosis surrounded by epithelioid macrophages, Langhans giant cells, and lymphocytes.
⚠️ NON-caseating granulomas suggest sarcoidosis (or Crohn). Caseation + acid-fast bacilli = TB.
4. Post-primary upper lobe cavitation
Reactivation (post-primary) TB localizes to the high-oxygen apical/posterior upper lobes and cavitates. Cavitary disease = high bacillary load and high infectivity.
⚠️ Primary TB in adults often involves LOWER lobes without cavitation (Ghon focus); upper-lobe cavitation = reactivation.
5. Primary lower lobe — children
Primary infection seeds the mid/lower lung zones (Ghon focus) plus hilar nodes (Ghon/Ranke complex). Usually asymptomatic and self-limited in immunocompetent hosts.
⚠️ Don't expect cavities in primary TB — that is the reactivation pattern.
6. 10% lifetime reactivation risk
Immunocompetent latent TB carries ~10% LIFETIME reactivation risk, highest in the first 2 years. HIV co-infection raises this to ~10% PER YEAR.
⚠️ The 10% figure is lifetime for normal hosts; in HIV it becomes ~10%/year — a classic swapped-number trap.
7. Silicosis RR 30
Silicosis impairs macrophage function and is one of the strongest reactivation risk factors (relative risk ~30). Silica-laden macrophages cannot kill bacilli.
⚠️ Silicosis specifically predisposes to TB (and to upper-lobe progressive massive fibrosis).
8. HIV / CD4 <200 — highest risk
Advanced HIV (CD4 <200) is the top reactivation risk and causes atypical presentations: lower-lobe/disseminated/miliary disease, anergic TST. Start TB treatment, then ART.
⚠️ In HIV the TST may be falsely negative (anergy); use IGRA and a lower induration cutoff (≥5 mm).
9. Gastrectomy RR 2–5
Gastrectomy, chronic kidney disease/dialysis, diabetes, and malnutrition are moderate reactivation risk factors (RR 2–5).
⚠️ These are moderate (RR 2–5), not the extreme risks of HIV or silicosis — match the magnitude to the factor.
10. TST induration cutoffs
TST cutoffs depend on risk: ≥5 mm = HIV, recent contact, immunosuppressed/anti-TNF, fibrotic CXR; ≥10 mm = recent immigrants, IVDU, healthcare/prison, DM; ≥15 mm = no risk factors.
⚠️ A 6 mm reaction is POSITIVE in an HIV patient but NEGATIVE in a healthy low-risk person — interpret cutoff by risk group, not absolute size.
11. PPD reads at 48–72 hrs
TST is read at 48–72 hours measuring INDURATION (not erythema) transversely. It is a delayed-type (type IV) hypersensitivity reaction.
⚠️ Measure induration, NOT redness; reading before 48 h or after 72 h invalidates the test.
12. IGRA — IFN-γ release
IGRA measures IFN-γ released by T cells exposed to TB-specific antigens (ESAT-6/CFP-10). One visit, objective, and not affected by BCG.
⚠️ Prefer IGRA over TST in BCG-vaccinated patients to avoid false positives; IGRA cannot distinguish latent from active TB.
13. BCG → false-positive TST
BCG vaccination can cause a false-positive TST. IGRA antigens are absent from BCG, so IGRA is 'NOT AFFECTED BY BCG'.
⚠️ A positive TST in a BCG-vaccinated immigrant may be BCG, not LTBI — confirm with IGRA before treating.
14. Screen first / no prior exposure
Before immunosuppression (anti-TNF, transplant, dialysis) screen for LTBI and treat to prevent reactivation. A truly unexposed host (negative IGRA/TST) needs no prophylaxis.
⚠️ Screening identifies latent infection to treat BEFORE biologics — don't start anti-TNF without LTBI screening.
