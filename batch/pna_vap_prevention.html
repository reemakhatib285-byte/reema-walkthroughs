📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Avoid intubation
The single most effective VAP-prevention strategy is to avoid the endotracheal tube entirely or remove it as early as safely possible — VAP risk is roughly 1-3% per ventilator-day, highest in the first week. The ETT bypasses upper-airway defenses, props the glottis open, and lets a biofilm of secretions pool above the cuff and leak into the lower airway.
⚠️ VAP prevention is mostly about ventilator days, not antibiotics — prophylactic systemic antibiotics do NOT prevent VAP and breed resistance.
2. NIV / non-invasive
Non-invasive ventilation (CPAP/BiPAP) avoids the ETT and its VAP risk; it is first-line in COPD exacerbation and cardiogenic pulmonary edema. By keeping the natural airway defenses intact, NIV markedly lowers nosocomial pneumonia versus invasive ventilation.
⚠️ NIV is contraindicated with depressed consciousness, copious secretions, hemodynamic instability, or facial trauma — forcing NIV in a patient who needs intubation risks aspiration and delayed definitive airway.
3. Daily sedation hold
Daily "sedation vacations" (spontaneous awakening trials) paired with spontaneous breathing trials are core ABCDE/ventilator-bundle elements that shorten ventilation, ICU stay, and therefore VAP. Lighter sedation lets patients clear secretions and pass extubation readiness sooner.
4. Subglottic drainage
Specialized ETTs with a dorsal lumen for continuous/intermittent subglottic secretion drainage remove the pooled, bacteria-laden secretions above the cuff that would otherwise micro-aspirate past it — proven to reduce VAP incidence and recommended for patients expected to be ventilated >48-72h.
5. Head of bed >30°
Semi-recumbent positioning at 30-45° (vs supine) uses gravity to reduce gastroesophageal reflux and micro-aspiration of gastric/oropharyngeal contents — a simple, free, evidence-based bundle element.
⚠️ Flat/supine positioning increases aspiration and VAP — being supine is the wrong answer when asked how to position a ventilated patient.
6. Hand hygiene
Hand hygiene is the single most effective infection-control measure for preventing healthcare-associated transmission of MDR pathogens, including those causing VAP. Alcohol-based rubs before/after every patient contact interrupt cross-contamination from staff hands.
⚠️ When a question asks the most effective measure to prevent nosocomial infection spread overall, the answer is hand hygiene — not gloves, gowns, or antibiotics.
7. Avoid prolonged ETT
Prolonged intubation selects for resistant flora and biofilm; daily readiness assessment and prompt extubation cut cumulative VAP risk. Reintubation, however, also raises VAP — so balance early extubation against a high failure risk.
⚠️ Don't pursue early tracheostomy purely to prevent VAP — routine early trach has not been shown to reduce VAP or mortality versus standard extubation timing.
8. Severe CAP — major/minor
IDSA/ATS severe-CAP criteria: either of TWO MAJOR criteria mandates ICU — (1) need for invasive mechanical ventilation, or (2) septic shock requiring vasopressors. Either lever alone opens the ICU gate directly.
⚠️ A single major criterion (mechanical ventilation OR septic shock) is enough for ICU — you do not also need minor criteria when a major one is present.
9. ≥3 minor criteria
ICU is also indicated with ≥3 of 9 MINOR criteria: RR ≥30, PaO2/FiO2 ≤250, multilobar infiltrates, confusion/disorientation, uremia (BUN ≥20), leukopenia (WBC <4), thrombocytopenia (plt <100k), hypothermia (<36°C), and hypotension needing aggressive fluids. (Distinct from CURB-65, which is a 0-5 mortality/disposition score: Confusion, Urea >7, RR ≥30, BP <90/60, age ≥65.)
⚠️ Don't confuse the IDSA/ATS minor criteria (≥3 → ICU) with CURB-65 (≥2 → admit, ≥3 → consider ICU) — they are different tools and boards test mixing them up.
10. CRP / inflammation
Markedly elevated inflammatory markers (CRP) flag severe disease. Adjunctive corticosteroids (e.g., hydrocortisone) reduce mortality in severe CAP and are recommended in refractory septic shock; in COVID-19 pneumonia requiring oxygen, dexamethasone is standard.
⚠️ Steroids help severe CAP but are HARMFUL in influenza pneumonia (increased mortality/secondary infection) — don't give routine steroids for flu-driven pneumonia.
11. CHF mimic trap
Acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema (CHF) produces bilateral infiltrates, dyspnea, and crackles that mimic pneumonia — but features cardiomegaly, cephalization, Kerley B lines, effusions, elevated BNP, and a response to diuresis rather than antibiotics. Always confirm infection before committing to CAP therapy.
⚠️ Bilateral infiltrate + dyspnea isn't always pneumonia — consider CHF and ARDS; treating volume-overload edema with antibiotics alone misses the diagnosis.
12. Pleural effusion drained
A parapneumonic effusion needs thoracentesis to distinguish uncomplicated (sterile, pH >7.2, glucose >60, no organisms) from complicated effusion/empyema (pH <7.2, glucose <60, LDH high, positive Gram stain/culture, or frank pus). Complicated effusions and empyema require chest-tube drainage — antibiotics alone will not clear them.
⚠️ Pleural fluid pH <7.2 (or pus/positive Gram stain) mandates chest-tube drainage — managing an empyema with antibiotics alone is a classic wrong answer.
