⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. M = monoclonal protein — WRONG answer: diagnosing myeloma from the M-protein alone without a defining event. The discriminator is that an M-protein with no CRAB and no SLiM biomarker is MGUS or smoldering myeloma — you need a CRAB feature or a SLiM criterion (60% plasma cells, FLC ratio ≥100, >1 MRI lesion) to call it active myeloma.
  2. Claw C — Calcium greater than 11 — WRONG answer: managing myeloma hypercalcemia with loop diuretics (or thiazides) up front. The discriminator is volume status — give IV saline FIRST; thiazides RAISE calcium, and loops are only added after the patient is volume-replete (not as primary therapy).
  3. Claw R — Renal — WRONG answer: ordering a contrast CT/IVP or giving NSAIDs to evaluate/treat the renal failure. The discriminator is light-chain cast nephropathy — IV contrast and NSAIDs precipitate casts and worsen AKI; avoid them and instead hydrate and reduce light-chain production.
  4. Claw A — Anemia — WRONG answer: working it up as iron-deficiency anemia and giving iron. The discriminator is a normocytic anemia with rouleaux, high ESR/total protein, and an M-spike — this is anemia of marrow infiltration/chronic disease, not iron deficiency; pursue SPEP/marrow rather than iron studies alone.
  5. Claw B — Bone smashing — WRONG answer: ordering a technetium-99m bone scan to find myeloma lesions. The discriminator is that myeloma is purely osteolytic with little osteoblastic activity, so bone scans are falsely negative — use skeletal survey/whole-body CT, PET-CT, or MRI instead.
  6. Punched-out lytic lesions — WRONG answer: attributing acute back pain with leg weakness to musculoskeletal strain and ordering plain films/physical therapy. The discriminator is cord compression — it requires urgent MRI of the whole spine and steroids/radiation; delay risks permanent paralysis.
  7. Cast nephropathy / myeloma kidney — WRONG answer: assuming the proteinuria is glomerular/albumin and that the urine dipstick reflects it. The discriminator is that cast nephropathy is tubulointerstitial from light chains (dipstick-negative) — quantify with UPEP/FLC; predominant ALBUMINURIA instead points to AL amyloid or light-chain deposition disease, not cast nephropathy.
  8. Dipstick negative for light chains — WRONG answer: excluding significant proteinuria/myeloma kidney because the dipstick is negative or trace. The discriminator is that dipstick misses light chains — order a 24-hour UPEP with immunofixation (or serum FLC) when you suspect myeloma.
  9. AL amyloidosis — WRONG answer: confusing AL amyloid kidney (heavy albuminuria, nephrotic syndrome) with cast nephropathy (light-chain casts, minimal albumin). The discriminator is the proteinuria type and organ pattern — albuminuria/nephrotic + restrictive cardiomyopathy + macroglossia means AL amyloidosis (confirm with Congo-red apple-green birefringence).
  10. Infection susceptibility — WRONG answer: assuming a high total immunoglobulin/M-protein means the patient is immunologically protected. The discriminator is that the M-protein is non-functional and normal Ig is suppressed — these patients are functionally immunodeficient and need infection prophylaxis and vaccination.
  11. Hyperviscosity — WRONG answer: expecting hyperviscosity to be a common, typical feature of ordinary IgG myeloma. The discriminator is that hyperviscosity is classically Waldenström macroglobulinemia (IgM pentamer); in myeloma it is uncommon and points to IgA or markedly elevated IgG — treat with plasmapheresis regardless of subtype.
  12. Clotting / coagulation hazard — WRONG answer: starting lenalidomide-based therapy without any VTE prophylaxis. The discriminator is the high thrombosis risk of IMiDs — all patients on IMiD regimens need risk-adapted prophylaxis (aspirin vs anticoagulation), not just routine monitoring.
  13. Neurologic compression — WRONG answer: attributing new leg weakness purely to chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy. The discriminator is a myelopathic pattern (bilateral leg weakness, sensory level, bladder dysfunction) signaling CORD COMPRESSION — that needs emergent MRI, steroids, and radiation/surgery, not just dose-reducing bortezomib.
  14. Pseudohyponatremia trap — WRONG answer: treating the low sodium with hypertonic saline or fluid restriction. The discriminator is a NORMAL measured serum osmolality with a normal osmolar gap and high paraprotein — it is an artifact (pseudohyponatremia), so correct the measurement method, not the patient.
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. M = monoclonal protein
The monoclonal protein from clonal plasma cells is the underlying driver; CRAB are its end-organ consequences (hyperCalcemia, Renal failure, Anemia, Bone lesions). Active multiple myeloma = clonal marrow plasma cells (usually ≥10%) PLUS ≥1 myeloma-defining event (CRAB or a SLiM biomarker). Mnemonic: the M-protein is the engine; CRAB is the wreckage.
⚠️ WRONG answer: diagnosing myeloma from the M-protein alone without a defining event. The discriminator is that an M-protein with no CRAB and no SLiM biomarker is MGUS or smoldering myeloma — you need a CRAB feature or a SLiM criterion (60% plasma cells, FLC ratio ≥100, >1 MRI lesion) to call it active myeloma.
2. Claw C — Calcium greater than 11
CRAB 'C' = hyperCalcemia: serum calcium >11 mg/dL (or >1 mg/dL above the upper normal), driven by osteoclast-activating factors (RANKL) causing bone resorption. Symptoms are the classic 'stones, bones, groans, psychiatric overtones.' Treat with aggressive IV normal saline first, then a bisphosphonate (zoledronic acid) and calcitonin for rapid effect.
⚠️ WRONG answer: managing myeloma hypercalcemia with loop diuretics (or thiazides) up front. The discriminator is volume status — give IV saline FIRST; thiazides RAISE calcium, and loops are only added after the patient is volume-replete (not as primary therapy).
3. Claw R — Renal
CRAB 'R' = Renal insufficiency: creatinine >2 mg/dL or creatinine clearance <40 mL/min, most often from light-chain cast nephropathy ('myeloma kidney'). Hypercalcemia, dehydration, hyperuricemia, NSAIDs, and IV contrast all worsen it. Management: hydration, treat hypercalcemia, rapid anti-myeloma therapy (bortezomib-based) to lower light chains.
⚠️ WRONG answer: ordering a contrast CT/IVP or giving NSAIDs to evaluate/treat the renal failure. The discriminator is light-chain cast nephropathy — IV contrast and NSAIDs precipitate casts and worsen AKI; avoid them and instead hydrate and reduce light-chain production.
4. Claw A — Anemia
CRAB 'A' = Anemia: hemoglobin <10 g/dL or >2 g/dL below normal, typically normocytic-normochromic from marrow replacement by plasma cells plus relative erythropoietin deficiency (worse with renal failure). Rouleaux on smear and a high ESR are classic clues. Address with treating myeloma; EPO/transfusion as supportive care.
⚠️ WRONG answer: working it up as iron-deficiency anemia and giving iron. The discriminator is a normocytic anemia with rouleaux, high ESR/total protein, and an M-spike — this is anemia of marrow infiltration/chronic disease, not iron deficiency; pursue SPEP/marrow rather than iron studies alone.
5. Claw B — Bone smashing
CRAB 'B' = Bone disease: lytic ('punched-out') lesions, osteopenia, or pathologic fractures from RANKL-driven osteoclast activation with suppressed osteoblasts. Best detected by whole-body low-dose CT, PET-CT, or MRI — NOT bone scan. Bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid) or denosumab reduce skeletal events.
⚠️ WRONG answer: ordering a technetium-99m bone scan to find myeloma lesions. The discriminator is that myeloma is purely osteolytic with little osteoblastic activity, so bone scans are falsely negative — use skeletal survey/whole-body CT, PET-CT, or MRI instead.
6. Punched-out lytic lesions
Lytic lesions cause bone pain, pathologic fractures, and vertebral compression that can produce spinal cord compression — an oncologic emergency presenting with back pain, leg weakness, sensory level, and bladder/bowel dysfunction. Immediate management: emergent MRI plus high-dose IV dexamethasone, then radiation or surgical decompression. Mnemonic: new back pain + weak legs in myeloma = image the cord now.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing acute back pain with leg weakness to musculoskeletal strain and ordering plain films/physical therapy. The discriminator is cord compression — it requires urgent MRI of the whole spine and steroids/radiation; delay risks permanent paralysis.
7. Cast nephropathy / myeloma kidney
Filtered monoclonal free light chains bind Tamm-Horsfall (uromodulin) protein in the distal tubule, forming obstructing waxy casts = cast nephropathy, the most common cause of myeloma renal failure. Reducing light-chain load fast (bortezomib-based therapy) and hydration are key. Mnemonic: light chains + Tamm-Horsfall = tubular traffic jam.
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming the proteinuria is glomerular/albumin and that the urine dipstick reflects it. The discriminator is that cast nephropathy is tubulointerstitial from light chains (dipstick-negative) — quantify with UPEP/FLC; predominant ALBUMINURIA instead points to AL amyloid or light-chain deposition disease, not cast nephropathy.
8. Dipstick negative for light chains
The urine dipstick detects ALBUMIN only, so monoclonal free light chains (Bence-Jones protein) read NEGATIVE despite heavy light-chain excretion — a classic discordance with a high 24-hour protein. Detect light chains with UPEP/immunofixation or serum FLC. (Proximal tubular injury can add Fanconi-type glucosuria with normal glucose.) Mnemonic: dipstick is blind to light chains.
⚠️ WRONG answer: excluding significant proteinuria/myeloma kidney because the dipstick is negative or trace. The discriminator is that dipstick misses light chains — order a 24-hour UPEP with immunofixation (or serum FLC) when you suspect myeloma.
9. AL amyloidosis
Monoclonal light chains (usually lambda) can misfold and deposit as AL amyloid, causing nephrotic-range ALBUMINURIA, restrictive/infiltrative cardiomyopathy (low-voltage ECG with thick walls on echo), peripheral/autonomic neuropathy, macroglossia, and easy bruising. Diagnose with a Congo-red biopsy (apple-green birefringence) plus typing. Mnemonic: AL = Amyloid Light-chain.
⚠️ WRONG answer: confusing AL amyloid kidney (heavy albuminuria, nephrotic syndrome) with cast nephropathy (light-chain casts, minimal albumin). The discriminator is the proteinuria type and organ pattern — albuminuria/nephrotic + restrictive cardiomyopathy + macroglossia means AL amyloidosis (confirm with Congo-red apple-green birefringence).
10. Infection susceptibility
Despite a high total Ig, functional hypogammaglobulinemia (the malignant clone suppresses normal polyclonal antibody) plus neutropenia/treatment effects leaves patients vulnerable to encapsulated organisms (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae) and gram-negatives like E. coli — infection is a leading cause of death in myeloma. Vaccinate (pneumococcal, influenza) and consider prophylaxis. Mnemonic: many antibodies, none that work.
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming a high total immunoglobulin/M-protein means the patient is immunologically protected. The discriminator is that the M-protein is non-functional and normal Ig is suppressed — these patients are functionally immunodeficient and need infection prophylaxis and vaccination.
11. Hyperviscosity
Hyperviscosity from a high paraprotein causes headache, blurred/visual changes, mucosal bleeding, and confusion; in myeloma it is most associated with IgA (which polymerizes) or very high IgG. Emergency treatment is plasmapheresis to remove the paraprotein, then systemic therapy. Mnemonic: bleeding + blurring + blundering = check serum viscosity.
⚠️ WRONG answer: expecting hyperviscosity to be a common, typical feature of ordinary IgG myeloma. The discriminator is that hyperviscosity is classically Waldenström macroglobulinemia (IgM pentamer); in myeloma it is uncommon and points to IgA or markedly elevated IgG — treat with plasmapheresis regardless of subtype.
12. Clotting / coagulation hazard
The paraprotein can coat platelets and interfere with fibrin/coagulation factors (acquired bleeding tendency), while the disease itself plus immunomodulatory drugs (IMiDs — thalidomide, lenalidomide, pomalidomide) markedly raise VTE risk. IMiD regimens require thromboprophylaxis (aspirin for low risk; LMWH/DOAC for higher risk). Mnemonic: myeloma can both bleed and clot.
⚠️ WRONG answer: starting lenalidomide-based therapy without any VTE prophylaxis. The discriminator is the high thrombosis risk of IMiDs — all patients on IMiD regimens need risk-adapted prophylaxis (aspirin vs anticoagulation), not just routine monitoring.
13. Neurologic compression
Neurologic problems in myeloma include spinal cord/nerve root compression from vertebral lesions (weak legs, sensory level — an emergency), confusion from hypercalcemia or hyperviscosity, and peripheral neuropathy from AL amyloid or bortezomib toxicity. New focal deficits demand urgent spinal MRI. Mnemonic: numb, confused, weak legs = image the spine and check calcium/viscosity.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing new leg weakness purely to chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy. The discriminator is a myelopathic pattern (bilateral leg weakness, sensory level, bladder dysfunction) signaling CORD COMPRESSION — that needs emergent MRI, steroids, and radiation/surgery, not just dose-reducing bortezomib.
14. Pseudohyponatremia trap
A very high paraprotein increases the non-aqueous (solid) fraction of plasma, so flame-photometry/indirect-ISE sodium reads falsely LOW — pseudohyponatremia — while measured serum OSMOLALITY is normal. There is no true sodium deficit and no symptoms; confirm with direct ion-selective electrode and serum osmolality. Mnemonic: 'pseudo, no salt' — the lab is fooled, the patient is fine.
⚠️ WRONG answer: treating the low sodium with hypertonic saline or fluid restriction. The discriminator is a NORMAL measured serum osmolality with a normal osmolar gap and high paraprotein — it is an artifact (pseudohyponatremia), so correct the measurement method, not the patient.
