⚠️ Board traps 15
  1. ODS from rapid correction — Stem: chronic hyponatremia (Na 112 for a week), and a choice says 'correct quickly to normal to prevent complications.' WRONG — speed is the danger; a fast rise causes ODS. Slow, controlled correction (<8/24h) is protective. Only acute (<48h) or actively seizing patients get a deliberate rapid initial bump (4–6 mEq) with hypertonic saline.
  2. Limits protect / overcorrection kills — A choice prioritizes 'getting the number normal' over the correction rate. WRONG — exceeding the rate limit converts an effective therapy into a brain injury. The board-safe posture is: define the 24h ceiling, check Na q2–4h, and actively re-lower if the trajectory exceeds target.
  3. Correction limit 8-10/24h — A choice sets a 24h goal of '12 mEq' or 'normalize Na to 140.' WRONG — even an otherwise correct therapy becomes harmful past ~8 mEq/24h (ODS risk). The maximum is a CEILING, not a target; aim well under it and use DDAVP/D5W to stay within bounds.
  4. High-risk for ODS — Applying the standard <8/24h limit to a high-risk patient (e.g., malnourished alcoholic). WRONG — they need an even slower target (4–6/24h) and pre-emptive control; the usual ceiling is too aggressive and these are precisely the brains most prone to ODS.
  5. Central pontine myelinolysis — Ordering an early MRI to 'rule out ODS' and reassuring the patient when it's normal. WRONG — MRI changes lag the clinical syndrome by days, so a normal early scan does NOT exclude ODS. Diagnosis rests on the overcorrection history plus delayed neuro signs; prevention (rate limits) is the only reliable safeguard.
  6. ODS clinical signs — A biphasic course (initial improvement, then new dysarthria/quadriparesis days later) misread as a new stroke or seizure. WRONG framing — in the setting of recent rapid Na correction, this delayed neurologic decline is ODS. Recognize the pattern and, if overcorrection is caught early, re-lower Na (D5W + DDAVP) to mitigate.
  7. Delayed 1 plus days — Stopping vigilance because 'the patient is fine right after correction.' WRONG — ODS is delayed; a good immediate appearance does not mean you're safe. If the 24h rise was excessive, actively re-lower with D5W + DDAVP NOW rather than waiting for symptoms.
  8. Severe/seizing: 3% hypertonic — Withholding 3% saline in a seizing hyponatremic patient 'for fear of ODS,' or using slow 0.9% NS instead. WRONG — in acute symptomatic hyponatremia the immediate threat is cerebral edema/herniation, and the initial 4–6 mEq emergent rise with 3% saline is appropriate and lifesaving. ODS comes from the TOTAL 24h overshoot, which you then cap — not from the emergent bolus itself.
  9. NS for dry / hypovolemic — Giving 0.9% NS to a SIADH or hypervolemic (CHF/cirrhosis) patient. WRONG — in SIADH, NS can paradoxically WORSEN hyponatremia (the kidney excretes the Na but retains the water due to high Uosm); in hypervolemia it worsens edema. NS is for the DRY patient; euvolemic/wet patients get restriction (± vaptan/loop).
  10. Fluid restrict SIADH/hypervol — Fluid-restricting a HYPOVOLEMIC patient. WRONG — the dry patient needs VOLUME (NS); restriction deepens hypovolemia and the hyponatremia. Restriction is reserved for euvolemic (SIADH) and hypervolemic states. Also note vaptans are useless in low-solute/primary polydipsia where ADH is already suppressed.
  11. Overcorrection rescue DDAVP — Letting an overcorrection 'ride' or only stopping further saline. WRONG — passive stopping isn't enough once you've overshot; actively re-lower with DDAVP + D5W to get back under the 24h ceiling and prevent ODS. The mantra: overcorrection is a reversible emergency if you act.
  12. DDAVP clamp — Assuming DDAVP is only a rescue after overshoot. Refinement — it's also a PROACTIVE strategy: clamping the diuresis up front in high-risk patients prevents the wild autocorrection that causes ODS. Pairing a DDAVP clamp with controlled 3% saline gives the most predictable, board-favored correction.
  13. Fluid restriction ratio clue — Prescribing the same fluid restriction to every SIADH patient. WRONG — if the urine:serum electrolyte ratio is >1, ordinary restriction fails (the kidney retains free water regardless) and you need strict <500 mL/day ± salt/urea/vaptan; if <1, modest restriction suffices. Tailor the restriction to the ratio.
  14. K replacement raises Na — Ignoring potassium's contribution and giving full-dose saline PLUS aggressive K repletion. WRONG — the combined effect can overcorrect Na well past the limit unexpectedly (a classic in hypokalemic, malnourished, high-risk patients). Tally K replacement into your correction math and reduce the saline accordingly.
  15. Goal-direction compass — Applying one therapy to all hyponatremia (e.g., 'always give saline' or 'always restrict'). WRONG — the right move depends on symptoms and volume status: hypertonic for emergencies, NS for dry, restriction for euvolemic/wet. Match the quadrant first, then cap the correction rate.
📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. ODS from rapid correction
Osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS), including central pontine myelinolysis, is caused by OVERLY FAST correction of CHRONIC (>48h) hyponatremia, NOT by the low Na itself. In chronic hyponatremia, brain cells extrude organic osmolytes to avoid swelling; a rapid Na rise then draws water OUT and shrinks/demyelinates neurons. The cure is preventive: respect the rate limit (<8 mEq/L/24h; 4–6 if high-risk).
⚠️ Stem: chronic hyponatremia (Na 112 for a week), and a choice says 'correct quickly to normal to prevent complications.' WRONG — speed is the danger; a fast rise causes ODS. Slow, controlled correction (<8/24h) is protective. Only acute (<48h) or actively seizing patients get a deliberate rapid initial bump (4–6 mEq) with hypertonic saline.
2. Limits protect / overcorrection kills
Central principle: the brain tolerates a chronically LOW Na far better than a rapid RISE. Therapy is governed by RATE limits, not by how low the Na is. Always pick the 24h target, monitor frequently, and be ready to re-lower (D5W + DDAVP) if you overshoot. Count ALL contributors to the rise — hypertonic saline, NS, autocorrection after volume repletion, and K replacement.
⚠️ A choice prioritizes 'getting the number normal' over the correction rate. WRONG — exceeding the rate limit converts an effective therapy into a brain injury. The board-safe posture is: define the 24h ceiling, check Na q2–4h, and actively re-lower if the trajectory exceeds target.
3. Correction limit 8-10/24h
THE board number: limit serum Na rise to <8 mEq/L per 24h (some references allow up to 10) in average patients, and to ~4–6 mEq/L/24h in high-risk patients (alcoholic, malnourished, hypokalemic, Na <105, liver disease). A useful estimate: 1 mL/kg of 3% saline raises Na ~1 mEq/L. Recheck Na q2–4h during active correction so you can slow or reverse before overshooting.
⚠️ A choice sets a 24h goal of '12 mEq' or 'normalize Na to 140.' WRONG — even an otherwise correct therapy becomes harmful past ~8 mEq/24h (ODS risk). The maximum is a CEILING, not a target; aim well under it and use DDAVP/D5W to stay within bounds.
4. High-risk for ODS
High-risk groups needing the stricter 4–6 mEq/L/24h ceiling: chronic ALCOHOLISM, MALNUTRITION/frailty, HYPOKALEMIA, advanced LIVER disease / liver transplant, and very low starting Na (<105). Beer-potomania and tea-and-toast patients are classic members. In these patients, plan a proactive DDAVP clamp and correct K cautiously (K repletion itself raises Na).
⚠️ Applying the standard <8/24h limit to a high-risk patient (e.g., malnourished alcoholic). WRONG — they need an even slower target (4–6/24h) and pre-emptive control; the usual ceiling is too aggressive and these are precisely the brains most prone to ODS.
5. Central pontine myelinolysis
ODS classically targets the PONS (central pontine myelinolysis), but extrapontine sites (basal ganglia, thalamus, cerebellum) can be involved too. Pathology: non-inflammatory demyelination from osmotic shrinkage of neurons during a too-rapid Na rise. MRI shows the lesion but is often delayed/normal early; diagnosis is clinical + history of overcorrection.
⚠️ Ordering an early MRI to 'rule out ODS' and reassuring the patient when it's normal. WRONG — MRI changes lag the clinical syndrome by days, so a normal early scan does NOT exclude ODS. Diagnosis rests on the overcorrection history plus delayed neuro signs; prevention (rate limits) is the only reliable safeguard.
6. ODS clinical signs
ODS presents DAYS after overcorrection with dysarthria, dysphagia, spastic quadriparesis, pseudobulbar palsy, and in severe cases 'locked-in' syndrome (awake but unable to move/speak). It is often permanent. The classic clue: a patient improves transiently after Na correction, then deteriorates neurologically 2–6 days later.
⚠️ A biphasic course (initial improvement, then new dysarthria/quadriparesis days later) misread as a new stroke or seizure. WRONG framing — in the setting of recent rapid Na correction, this delayed neurologic decline is ODS. Recognize the pattern and, if overcorrection is caught early, re-lower Na (D5W + DDAVP) to mitigate.
7. Delayed 1 plus days
ODS symptoms are DELAYED — typically 1 day to ~1 week after the overcorrection — so the patient can look perfectly fine immediately after Na normalizes. This delay is exactly why frequent post-correction monitoring and willingness to re-lower Na matter even in an asymptomatic patient.
⚠️ Stopping vigilance because 'the patient is fine right after correction.' WRONG — ODS is delayed; a good immediate appearance does not mean you're safe. If the 24h rise was excessive, actively re-lower with D5W + DDAVP NOW rather than waiting for symptoms.
8. Severe/seizing: 3% hypertonic
SEVERE symptomatic hyponatremia (seizures, coma, obtundation) is a neuro-emergency from cerebral edema: give 3% hypertonic saline as a BOLUS — 100–150 mL over ~10 min, repeat up to 2–3 times until symptoms abate — to raise Na ~4–6 mEq/L quickly. That initial bump reverses herniation risk and is enough to stop seizures; after it, revert to the slow <8 mEq/24h plan (the emergent rise counts toward the daily total).
⚠️ Withholding 3% saline in a seizing hyponatremic patient 'for fear of ODS,' or using slow 0.9% NS instead. WRONG — in acute symptomatic hyponatremia the immediate threat is cerebral edema/herniation, and the initial 4–6 mEq emergent rise with 3% saline is appropriate and lifesaving. ODS comes from the TOTAL 24h overshoot, which you then cap — not from the emergent bolus itself.
9. NS for dry / hypovolemic
Hypovolemic (dry) hyponatremia: 0.9% normal saline (154 mEq/L Na) restores effective circulating volume, which removes the baroreceptor stimulus and shuts OFF ADH. Watch closely afterward — as ADH falls, a brisk water diuresis (autocorrection) can raise Na faster than intended, so cap the rise and be ready with D5W ± DDAVP.
⚠️ Giving 0.9% NS to a SIADH or hypervolemic (CHF/cirrhosis) patient. WRONG — in SIADH, NS can paradoxically WORSEN hyponatremia (the kidney excretes the Na but retains the water due to high Uosm); in hypervolemia it worsens edema. NS is for the DRY patient; euvolemic/wet patients get restriction (± vaptan/loop).
10. Fluid restrict SIADH/hypervol
SIADH and hypervolemic states: FLUID RESTRICTION (often <800–1000 mL/day) is first-line. Adjuncts for SIADH: oral salt tablets, urea, loop diuretic (to spoil the medullary gradient), or a vaptan (V2 antagonist, e.g., tolvaptan) for refractory cases. Hypervolemic states (HF, cirrhosis): water/salt restriction + loop diuretic + treat the underlying organ failure.
⚠️ Fluid-restricting a HYPOVOLEMIC patient. WRONG — the dry patient needs VOLUME (NS); restriction deepens hypovolemia and the hyponatremia. Restriction is reserved for euvolemic (SIADH) and hypervolemic states. Also note vaptans are useless in low-solute/primary polydipsia where ADH is already suppressed.
11. Overcorrection rescue DDAVP
If Na rises too fast, RESCUE by re-lowering: give DDAVP (desmopressin) to CLAMP the renal free-water diuresis plus D5W (electrolyte-free water, e.g., ~3 mL/kg drops Na ~1 mEq/L) to bring Na back to a safe trajectory. This is indicated whenever the 24h rise exceeds target, even before symptoms appear.
⚠️ Letting an overcorrection 'ride' or only stopping further saline. WRONG — passive stopping isn't enough once you've overshot; actively re-lower with DDAVP + D5W to get back under the 24h ceiling and prevent ODS. The mantra: overcorrection is a reversible emergency if you act.
12. DDAVP clamp
The 'DDAVP clamp' = give DDAVP proactively/continuously (e.g., 1–2 mcg IV q6–8h) to FIX urine output at a maximally concentrated state, eliminating unpredictable free-water diuresis, then raise Na in a controlled way with 3% saline. This is especially useful in high-risk patients (alcoholic, malnourished, very low Na, post-volume-repletion) to keep the rise within 4–6 mEq/24h.
⚠️ Assuming DDAVP is only a rescue after overshoot. Refinement — it's also a PROACTIVE strategy: clamping the diuresis up front in high-risk patients prevents the wild autocorrection that causes ODS. Pairing a DDAVP clamp with controlled 3% saline gives the most predictable, board-favored correction.
13. Fluid restriction ratio clue
The urine-to-serum electrolyte ratio = (urine Na + urine K) / serum Na predicts how aggressively to restrict free water in SIADH-type hyponatremia: >1 = electrolyte-free water is being RETAINED → strict restriction (<500 mL/day); ~1 = moderate restriction (~500–700 mL/day); <1 = the kidney is excreting free water → fluid restriction will work readily (liberal). It estimates whether the patient can actually clear ingested water.
⚠️ Prescribing the same fluid restriction to every SIADH patient. WRONG — if the urine:serum electrolyte ratio is >1, ordinary restriction fails (the kidney retains free water regardless) and you need strict <500 mL/day ± salt/urea/vaptan; if <1, modest restriction suffices. Tailor the restriction to the ratio.
14. K replacement raises Na
Repleting POTASSIUM raises serum Na: to stay electroneutral, infused K+ moves INTO cells while Na+ shifts OUT into the extracellular fluid (and K is osmotically active intracellularly, drawing water in). So giving KCl behaves like giving Na for the purpose of correction — COUNT the K toward the 24h Na-rise total to avoid an unplanned overshoot.
⚠️ Ignoring potassium's contribution and giving full-dose saline PLUS aggressive K repletion. WRONG — the combined effect can overcorrect Na well past the limit unexpectedly (a classic in hypokalemic, malnourished, high-risk patients). Tally K replacement into your correction math and reduce the saline accordingly.
15. Goal-direction compass
Treatment compass by status: EMERGENT (seizing/comatose) → 3% hypertonic saline bolus (raise 4–6 mEq); DRY (hypovolemic) → 0.9% NS; EUVOLEMIC SIADH → fluid restriction ± salt/urea/loop/vaptan; WET (hypervolemic HF/cirrhosis) → fluid + salt restriction + loop diuretic. Overarching all four: respect the <8 mEq/24h (4–6 high-risk) correction limit and re-lower with D5W+DDAVP if you overshoot.
⚠️ Applying one therapy to all hyponatremia (e.g., 'always give saline' or 'always restrict'). WRONG — the right move depends on symptoms and volume status: hypertonic for emergencies, NS for dry, restriction for euvolemic/wet. Match the quadrant first, then cap the correction rate.
