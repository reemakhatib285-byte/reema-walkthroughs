📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Hyperglycemia + immune + perfusion banner
Diabetic foot disease arises from a triad: NEUROPATHY (loss of protective sensation → unnoticed trauma), poor PERFUSION (PAD → impaired healing), and impaired IMMUNITY/hyperglycemia (blunted neutrophil function → infection). All three converge to turn minor trauma into non-healing, infected ulcers and amputation. Mnemonic: 'numb foot + no blood + no defense = the diabetic foot.'
⚠️ WRONG to attribute diabetic foot ulcers to PAD/ischemia alone — the dominant initiating factor is NEUROPATHY (loss of protective sensation). Discriminator: most diabetic foot ulcers are neuropathic (warm foot, palpable or reduced pulses) at pressure points, whereas purely ischemic ulcers are painful, on the toes/margins, with absent pulses.
2. Sensory ninja
Sensory neuropathy causes LOSS OF PROTECTIVE SENSATION in a stocking-glove distribution, so repetitive trauma, foreign bodies, and pressure go unnoticed until an ulcer forms — the dominant mechanism of diabetic foot ulceration. Screen annually with the 10-g monofilament plus a 128-Hz tuning fork (vibration). Mnemonic: 'can't feel it = can't protect it.'
⚠️ WRONG to rely on the patient reporting pain or symptoms to detect the at-risk foot — loss of protective sensation is usually ASYMPTOMATIC. Discriminator: an insensate foot on monofilament testing identifies ulcer risk even when the patient feels 'fine,' so screening (not symptoms) drives prevention.
3. Motor ninja / hammer toes
Motor neuropathy weakens the intrinsic foot muscles, causing imbalance that produces deformities — hammer/claw toes, high arch, prominent metatarsal heads — which create new abnormal PRESSURE POINTS where neuropathic ulcers form under callus. Mnemonic: 'wasted intrinsics → clawed toes → new pressure ulcers.'
⚠️ WRONG to treat hammer/claw-toe deformities as purely orthopedic/cosmetic — in a diabetic they create high-pressure sites for ulceration. Discriminator: deformity-driven pressure points warrant offloading and protective/custom footwear, not just observation, because they predict ulcer location.
4. Autonomic ninja / anhidrosis
Autonomic neuropathy in the foot causes ANHIDROSIS (loss of sweating) → dry, cracked, fissured skin that becomes a portal of entry for bacteria. It also opens arteriovenous shunts, giving a warm, often bounding-pulse foot despite microvascular dysfunction. Manage with emollients and meticulous skin/foot care. Mnemonic: 'dry, cracked, warm autonomic foot lets infection in.'
⚠️ WRONG to interpret a warm foot with palpable/bounding pulses as proof of good tissue perfusion — autonomic AV shunting can mask significant PAD. Discriminator: still assess ABI/pulses and dryness/fissuring; warmth from autonomic shunting does not exclude underlying ischemia or infection risk.
5. Charcot joint
Charcot neuroarthropathy = progressive bone/joint destruction in an insensate foot from repetitive unperceived trauma, producing a warm, swollen, erythematous, often deformed 'rocker-bottom' midfoot. Acute Charcot can closely MIMIC infection. Treatment is prolonged OFFLOADING (total-contact cast) and immobilization. Mnemonic: 'hot swollen deformed foot with intact skin = think Charcot.'
⚠️ WRONG to treat a red, hot, swollen diabetic foot WITHOUT an ulcer or break in the skin as cellulitis/osteomyelitis and start antibiotics — acute Charcot is the key mimic. Discriminator: no skin breach, midfoot collapse/'rocker-bottom,' and often improvement when the limb is elevated favor Charcot over infection; X-ray/MRI and clinical context distinguish them.
6. Met head pressure / ulcer / callus
Neuropathic ulcers classically form at plantar PRESSURE POINTS — most often under the METATARSAL HEADS — where callus builds up over the high-pressure area and underlying tissue breaks down. They are typically painless, surrounded by callus, with a 'punched-out' appearance. Treatment centers on OFFLOADING (total-contact cast), debridement of callus, and glycemic control. Mnemonic: 'callus over the met head hides the neuropathic ulcer.'
⚠️ WRONG to debride callus/ulcer and dress it while leaving the patient weight-bearing — without OFFLOADING the ulcer will not heal. Discriminator: the single most important intervention for a plantar neuropathic ulcer is pressure offloading (total-contact cast), not just topical wound care or antibiotics.
7. PAD wall
Peripheral arterial disease impairs perfusion and wound HEALING and raises amputation risk; diabetic PAD tends to be distal (tibial/peroneal) and is often clinically silent due to coexisting neuropathy. Assess pulses and the ankle-brachial index (ABI) in any foot ulcer; calcified non-compressible vessels can falsely ELEVATE the ABI (>1.3), so use toe-brachial index or waveforms if suspected. Revascularize ischemic, non-healing wounds. Mnemonic: 'no blood, no healing — check the ABI.'
⚠️ WRONG to accept a 'normal' or high ABI as ruling out ischemia in a diabetic — medial arterial calcification makes vessels non-compressible and falsely elevates the ABI. Discriminator: an ABI >1.3 (or non-healing ulcer with a 'normal' ABI) should prompt toe pressures/waveform studies rather than reassurance.
8. Probe-to-bone
A positive PROBE-TO-BONE test (a sterile blunt probe reaches bone through the ulcer) strongly raises the likelihood of underlying OSTEOMYELITIS, especially in deep or long-standing ulcers; combined with an elevated ESR/CRP it is a useful bedside predictor. Confirm with MRI and bone culture/biopsy. Mnemonic: 'touch bone, suspect bone infection.'
⚠️ WRONG to rule OUT osteomyelitis just because a plain X-ray is normal — early osteomyelitis is X-ray-occult for ~2 weeks. Discriminator: a positive probe-to-bone, ulcer >2 cm/>1 cm deep, or a markedly elevated ESR should prompt MRI and bone biopsy rather than relying on a normal radiograph.
9. MRI best for osteo
MRI is the most sensitive and specific IMAGING for diabetic foot OSTEOMYELITIS (detects marrow edema before bone destruction and defines abscess/soft-tissue extent). Plain X-ray is the initial test but is insensitive early; the definitive diagnosis is BONE biopsy with histology and culture to guide targeted antibiotics. Mnemonic: 'X-ray first, MRI to confirm, bone biopsy to prove.'
⚠️ WRONG to choose plain X-ray as the best test for early osteomyelitis — it is insensitive for the first ~2 weeks. Discriminator: MRI is the imaging of choice; when MRI is contraindicated, use a tagged WBC scan, but the gold standard remains bone biopsy/culture.
10. Polymicrobial / culture
Chronic/deep diabetic foot infections are often POLYMICROBIAL (Gram-positive cocci like S. aureus/streptococci plus Gram-negatives and anaerobes); MRSA and Pseudomonas matter with prior antibiotics or maceration. Obtain DEEP tissue or bone cultures (after debridement) — NOT superficial swabs — to guide therapy; empiric coverage is broad then narrowed. Mnemonic: 'deep cultures for polymicrobial feet.'
⚠️ A superficial wound SWAB is the WRONG specimen — it grows surface colonizers and misleads therapy. Discriminator: send DEEP tissue or bone culture (and treat based on it), rather than tailoring antibiotics to a superficial swab result.
11. Prevent (daily inspection / footwear)
Prevention is the highest-yield intervention: DAILY foot self-inspection (use a mirror), proper protective/therapeutic footwear and custom orthotics for high-risk feet, regular podiatry/callus care, smoking cessation, and glycemic control. Annual comprehensive foot exams stratify risk; the strongest predictor of a future ulcer is a PRIOR ulcer. Mnemonic: 'look every day, the right shoes every step.'
⚠️ WRONG to defer foot screening to only symptomatic patients — neuropathy is silent and prior ulceration predicts recurrence. Discriminator: high-risk feet (neuropathy, PAD, deformity, prior ulcer) need structured prevention and protective footwear, not reactive care only after a wound appears.
12. Mucor (rhino-cerebral)
Rhinocerebral mucormycosis (Mucor/Rhizopus) classically strikes patients in DKA/uncontrolled diabetes — acidosis and high glucose free up iron and impair phagocytes. It presents with facial pain/swelling, black necrotic nasal/palatal eschar, and rapid orbital/cerebral invasion. Emergency treatment is aggressive surgical DEBRIDEMENT plus IV liposomal amphotericin B and correction of the acidosis. Mnemonic: 'DKA + black eschar = Mucor — cut and ampho now.'
⚠️ WRONG to start antibacterials and observe a diabetic with facial/sinus pain and a black nasal eschar — this is a surgical fungal emergency. Discriminator: black necrotic eschar with non-septate, broad, ribbon-like, right-angle-branching hyphae in a DKA patient is mucormycosis (needs urgent surgery + amphotericin), not bacterial sinusitis; Aspergillus, by contrast, has septate acute-angle hyphae.
13. Emphysematous cholecystitis / pyelonephritis
Diabetics are prone to gas-forming (EMPHYSEMATOUS) infections: emphysematous cholecystitis (often Clostridium/E. coli, frequently acalculous, high gangrene/perforation risk → urgent cholecystectomy) and emphysematous pyelonephritis (gas in renal parenchyma, usually E. coli/Klebsiella → broad antibiotics, drainage, sometimes nephrectomy). Mnemonic: 'gas in the gallbladder or kidney = think diabetic + go aggressive.'
⚠️ WRONG to manage emphysematous cholecystitis/pyelonephritis conservatively like routine cholecystitis/UTI — they are life-threatening, gas-forming infections needing urgent source control. Discriminator: GAS in the organ wall/parenchyma on imaging mandates emergent surgery/drainage plus broad-spectrum antibiotics, not antibiotics alone.
14. Malignant otitis externa
Malignant (necrotizing) otitis externa is a PSEUDOMONAS aeruginosa infection of the external canal/skull base in ELDERLY DIABETICS, presenting with severe ear pain (often out of proportion, worse at night), otorrhea, and granulation tissue at the bony-cartilaginous junction; it can extend to skull-base osteomyelitis and cranial nerve palsies (especially CN VII). Treat with prolonged anti-pseudomonal therapy (e.g., ciprofloxacin) ± surgical debridement. Mnemonic: 'elderly diabetic + severe ear pain + granulation = Pseudomonas skull base.'
⚠️ WRONG to treat severe ear pain with granulation tissue in an elderly diabetic as ordinary otitis externa with topical drops — this is a skull-base osteomyelitis emergency. Discriminator: pain out of proportion, granulation at the canal floor, and an elevated ESR in a diabetic warrant CT/MRI and prolonged systemic anti-pseudomonal antibiotics, not topical therapy alone.
15. Asymptomatic bacteriuria sign
Asymptomatic bacteriuria (positive culture without urinary symptoms) should NOT be treated in diabetics — antibiotics do not reduce complications and promote resistance. The ONLY routine indications to treat asymptomatic bacteriuria are PREGNANCY and BEFORE a urologic procedure with anticipated mucosal bleeding. Mnemonic: 'no symptoms, no treatment — except pregnancy or pre-urologic.'
⚠️ WRONG to treat asymptomatic bacteriuria in a diabetic 'because diabetes' — diabetes is NOT an indication to treat. Discriminator: treat only symptomatic UTIs (or asymptomatic bacteriuria in pregnancy / before urologic procedures); a positive culture without symptoms in a non-pregnant diabetic should be left alone.
