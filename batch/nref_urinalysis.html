⚠️ Board traps 13
  1. Exam algorithm header — WRONG answer: jumping straight to a diagnosis from one dipstick square. Discriminator: dipstick is a screen, microscopy + casts localize. Always pair them — e.g., protein-positive dipstick alone cannot tell you glomerular vs tubular vs overflow proteinuria without the sediment and a UPCR/UACR.
  2. Step 1 — pigment vs hematuria — WRONG answer: calling any heme-positive dipstick 'hematuria.' Discriminator: dipstick detects heme, not cells. Heme+ with absent RBCs on micro = pigmenturia (rhabdo/hemolysis), not bleeding — the supernatant is also pink in hemoglobinuria but clear in hematuria once RBCs are spun down.
  3. Blood+ / no RBCs = pigment — WRONG answer: attributing heme+/no-RBC urine to glomerulonephritis or 'microscopic hematuria.' Discriminator: GN gives RBCs/dysmorphic RBCs/RBC casts; an empty sediment with heme positivity is pigment, so pursue CK (rhabdo) and LDH/haptoglobin/smear (hemolysis) — not a renal biopsy for GN.
  4. Blood+ / RBC casts = glomerular — WRONG answer: assuming visible blood means a bladder/urologic source or stone. Discriminator: RBC casts and dysmorphic RBCs = glomerular and mandate a nephritis workup (C3/C4, ANCA, anti-GBM, ANA, ASO); isomorphic (normal-shaped) RBCs with no casts suggest a urologic source (stone, tumor, UTI) needing imaging/cystoscopy.
  5. Step 2 — rhabdo vs hemolysis — WRONG answer: ordering a urine myoglobin assay to confirm rhabdo. Discriminator: serum CK is the test (myoglobin clears too fast for reliable urine testing); the clue cluster of CK >5,000 + hyperK + hyperphos + hypoCa + heme+/no-RBC urine after immobilization/crush nails rhabdo over hemolysis.
  6. Rhabdo clues — WRONG answer: blaming the urine findings on 'opiate/cocaine nephrotoxicity' or simple dehydration in a found-down patient. Discriminator: prolonged immobilization (overdose, stroke, long lie) → compression rhabdomyolysis with CK>5,000 and heme+/no-RBC urine; the drug itself is the trigger via immobility, and the mechanism of AKI is myoglobinuric ATN.
  7. Hemolysis clues — WRONG answer: calling tea/cola-colored urine with anemia 'hematuria' and chasing a urologic source. Discriminator: low haptoglobin + high LDH + spherocytes/schistocytes = hemolysis; the urine is heme+ from free hemoglobin, not intact RBCs — confirm with the hemolysis panel and smear, not cystoscopy.
  8. Step 3 — RBC casts = GN — WRONG answer: confusing nephritic (RBC casts, mild proteinuria) with nephrotic (>3.5 g/day proteinuria, edema, hypoalbuminemia, hyperlipidemia, oval fat bodies/maltese-cross lipiduria, bland sediment). Discriminator: RBC casts/active sediment = nephritic GN; heavy proteinuria with FATTY casts and no RBC casts = nephrotic — different differentials and workup.
  9. Step 4 — WBC casts = AIN — WRONG answer: treating WBC casts/pyuria reflexively as a bladder UTI and prescribing antibiotics. Discriminator: AIN gives sterile pyuria + WBC casts + drug exposure ± eosinophiluria with a NEGATIVE culture; pyelonephritis gives WBC casts WITH bacteriuria/positive nitrites/leukocyte esterase and systemic infection — context (offending drug vs fever/flank pain + positive culture) separates them.
  10. Step 5 — muddy brown casts = ATN — WRONG answer: calling muddy brown granular casts 'prerenal' and giving more fluids expecting full reversal. Discriminator: prerenal = BLAND sediment/hyaline casts, FENa<1%, BUN/Cr>20, urine osm>500; established ATN shows muddy brown granular casts, FENa>2%, isosthenuria — it will not correct with a fluid bolus the way prerenal does.
  11. Trap — 'opiate abuse' is rhabdo — WRONG answer: attributing the AKI to direct opioid nephrotoxicity, contrast, or 'just dehydration.' Discriminator: the mechanism is immobilization → rhabdo → pigment ATN; the giveaway is a sky-high CK with heme-positive but RBC-negative urine. Treat the rhabdo, not a phantom direct drug toxicity.
  12. Trap — 'blood in urine' = GN only if real RBCs — WRONG answer: launching a GN serology workup (or biopsy) off a positive dipstick alone. 📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Exam algorithm header
Approach every dipstick/sediment vignette in a fixed order: (1) read the dipstick panel — protein, blood/heme, leukocyte esterase, nitrites, glucose, ketones, specific gravity, pH, bilirubin; (2) correlate with microscopy (RBCs/dysmorphic RBCs, WBCs, casts, crystals, epithelial cells); (3) localize by cast type. Casts are the localizing key: RBC=glomerular, WBC=AIN/pyelo, muddy brown granular=ATN, hyaline=prerenal, broad waxy=CKD, fatty/oval fat bodies (maltese cross)=nephrotic.
⚠️ WRONG answer: jumping straight to a diagnosis from one dipstick square. Discriminator: dipstick is a screen, microscopy + casts localize. Always pair them — e.g., protein-positive dipstick alone cannot tell you glomerular vs tubular vs overflow proteinuria without the sediment and a UPCR/UACR.
2. Step 1 — pigment vs hematuria
A 'blood-positive' dipstick reacts to the peroxidase activity of HEME, so it lights up for intact RBCs, free hemoglobin (intravascular hemolysis), OR myoglobin (rhabdomyolysis). The first fork is therefore: is this real hematuria or pigment? Resolve it by spinning the urine and looking — RBCs on micro = true hematuria; heme-positive dipstick with NO RBCs = pigment (myoglobin or hemoglobin).
⚠️ WRONG answer: calling any heme-positive dipstick 'hematuria.' Discriminator: dipstick detects heme, not cells. Heme+ with absent RBCs on micro = pigmenturia (rhabdo/hemolysis), not bleeding — the supernatant is also pink in hemoglobinuria but clear in hematuria once RBCs are spun down.
3. Blood+ / no RBCs = pigment
Positive heme with an empty microscopy field means a heme-containing pigment is in the urine without intact red cells: myoglobin (rhabdomyolysis) or free hemoglobin (intravascular hemolysis). Both can cause pigment-nephropathy ATN via tubular obstruction, direct toxicity, and vasoconstriction; aggressive IV fluids are the cornerstone of prevention.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing heme+/no-RBC urine to glomerulonephritis or 'microscopic hematuria.' Discriminator: GN gives RBCs/dysmorphic RBCs/RBC casts; an empty sediment with heme positivity is pigment, so pursue CK (rhabdo) and LDH/haptoglobin/smear (hemolysis) — not a renal biopsy for GN.
4. Blood+ / RBC casts = glomerular
RBC casts and dysmorphic (acanthocytic) RBCs prove the bleeding is GLOMERULAR — red cells squeezed through a damaged glomerular basement membrane get deformed and molded into Tamm-Horsfall casts. This defines a nephritic picture: hematuria + RBC casts + variable proteinuria + HTN + AKI (e.g., IgA nephropathy, post-strep GN, ANCA/anti-GBM vasculitis, lupus nephritis).
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming visible blood means a bladder/urologic source or stone. Discriminator: RBC casts and dysmorphic RBCs = glomerular and mandate a nephritis workup (C3/C4, ANCA, anti-GBM, ANA, ASO); isomorphic (normal-shaped) RBCs with no casts suggest a urologic source (stone, tumor, UTI) needing imaging/cystoscopy.
5. Step 2 — rhabdo vs hemolysis
Once you've established pigmenturia (heme+/no RBCs), separate the two sources with labs and history. Rhabdomyolysis: massively elevated CK (often >5,000–10,000), muscle pain/weakness, dark urine, plus its electrolyte signature — hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia (early), and a high anion gap. Hemolysis: anemia with elevated LDH and indirect bilirubin and LOW haptoglobin, ± schistocytes.
⚠️ WRONG answer: ordering a urine myoglobin assay to confirm rhabdo. Discriminator: serum CK is the test (myoglobin clears too fast for reliable urine testing); the clue cluster of CK >5,000 + hyperK + hyperphos + hypoCa + heme+/no-RBC urine after immobilization/crush nails rhabdo over hemolysis.
6. Rhabdo clues
Rhabdomyolysis releases myoglobin, K+, phosphate, and CK from damaged muscle. Triggers: crush/trauma, prolonged immobilization ('found down', overdose), seizures, extreme exertion, statins (esp. with fibrates), and certain toxins/infections. Treat with early aggressive isotonic IV fluids titrated to urine output ~200–300 mL/hr; correct hyperkalemia (the life-threatening complication); urinary alkalinization/mannitol are not routinely beneficial.
⚠️ WRONG answer: blaming the urine findings on 'opiate/cocaine nephrotoxicity' or simple dehydration in a found-down patient. Discriminator: prolonged immobilization (overdose, stroke, long lie) → compression rhabdomyolysis with CK>5,000 and heme+/no-RBC urine; the drug itself is the trigger via immobility, and the mechanism of AKI is myoglobinuric ATN.
7. Hemolysis clues
Intravascular hemolysis releases free hemoglobin that overwhelms haptoglobin and spills into urine (heme+/no RBCs). Lab triad: anemia + high LDH + LOW haptoglobin, with elevated indirect bilirubin and reticulocytosis; a peripheral smear and DAT/Coombs localize the cause (autoimmune, MAHA/TTP-HUS with schistocytes, transfusion reaction, G6PD, mechanical valve, PNH).
⚠️ WRONG answer: calling tea/cola-colored urine with anemia 'hematuria' and chasing a urologic source. Discriminator: low haptoglobin + high LDH + spherocytes/schistocytes = hemolysis; the urine is heme+ from free hemoglobin, not intact RBCs — confirm with the hemolysis panel and smear, not cystoscopy.
8. Step 3 — RBC casts = GN
RBC casts = glomerulonephritis (nephritic syndrome). The pattern is hematuria + dysmorphic RBCs/RBC casts + subnephrotic proteinuria + HTN + rising creatinine. Workup is driven by complement and serologies: low complement (post-strep GN, lupus, MPGN, cryoglobulinemia, endocarditis) vs normal complement, pauci-immune (ANCA vasculitis), anti-GBM (Goodpasture), IgA nephropathy.
⚠️ WRONG answer: confusing nephritic (RBC casts, mild proteinuria) with nephrotic (>3.5 g/day proteinuria, edema, hypoalbuminemia, hyperlipidemia, oval fat bodies/maltese-cross lipiduria, bland sediment). Discriminator: RBC casts/active sediment = nephritic GN; heavy proteinuria with FATTY casts and no RBC casts = nephrotic — different differentials and workup.
9. Step 4 — WBC casts = AIN
WBC casts with sterile pyuria and white cell casts point to acute interstitial nephritis — a hypersensitivity reaction in the interstitium. Classic causes: drugs (NSAIDs, beta-lactams/penicillins, cephalosporins, sulfonamides, PPIs, rifampin), infections, and autoimmune disease. Classic (but only ~10–30% present) triad: fever, rash, eosinophilia, often with eosinophiluria. Treat by stopping the offending drug; steroids if no recovery.
⚠️ WRONG answer: treating WBC casts/pyuria reflexively as a bladder UTI and prescribing antibiotics. Discriminator: AIN gives sterile pyuria + WBC casts + drug exposure ± eosinophiluria with a NEGATIVE culture; pyelonephritis gives WBC casts WITH bacteriuria/positive nitrites/leukocyte esterase and systemic infection — context (offending drug vs fever/flank pain + positive culture) separates them.
10. Step 5 — muddy brown casts = ATN
Muddy brown granular casts and renal tubular epithelial cell casts = acute tubular necrosis (ischemic or nephrotoxic). Supporting labs: FENa >2% (FEurea >50% if on diuretics), urine osmolality ~300 (isosthenuria). Causes: prolonged hypoperfusion/sepsis (ischemic) and contrast, aminoglycosides, amphotericin, cisplatin, pigments (nephrotoxic). Management is supportive with dialysis for refractory hyperK, acidosis, volume overload, or uremia.
⚠️ WRONG answer: calling muddy brown granular casts 'prerenal' and giving more fluids expecting full reversal. Discriminator: prerenal = BLAND sediment/hyaline casts, FENa<1%, BUN/Cr>20, urine osm>500; established ATN shows muddy brown granular casts, FENa>2%, isosthenuria — it will not correct with a fluid bolus the way prerenal does.
11. Trap — 'opiate abuse' is rhabdo
The unresponsive/'found down' patient (opioid or other overdose, stroke, alcohol) lies immobile for hours, causing pressure-induced compression rhabdomyolysis → myoglobinuric ATN. Expect CK >5,000, heme+/no-RBC urine, hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia, and an anion-gap acidosis. Early aggressive IV fluids are key.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing the AKI to direct opioid nephrotoxicity, contrast, or 'just dehydration.' Discriminator: the mechanism is immobilization → rhabdo → pigment ATN; the giveaway is a sky-high CK with heme-positive but RBC-negative urine. Treat the rhabdo, not a phantom direct drug toxicity.
12. Trap — 'blood in urine' = GN only if real RBCs
Do not equate dipstick heme positivity with glomerulonephritis. GN requires evidence of glomerular bleeding: dysmorphic (acanthocytic) RBCs and/or RBC casts on microscopy. Isolated heme positivity could be pigment (rhabdo/hemolysis), and isomorphic RBCs without casts suggest a urologic source needing imaging/cystoscopy rather than a nephritis workup.
⚠️ WRONG answer: launching a GN serology workup (or biopsy) off a positive dipstick alone. Discriminator: confirm with microscopy first — only dysmorphic RBCs/RBC casts justify the nephritic workup; isomorphic RBCs → urologic source; heme+/no RBCs → pigment workup (CK, LDH/haptoglobin).
13. Trap — dipstick sees heme not RBCs
Core principle: the blood pad reacts to heme peroxidase activity, so it cannot distinguish RBCs from hemoglobin or myoglobin — microscopy is mandatory to interpret it. A parallel blind spot exists for protein: the dipstick protein pad detects mainly ALBUMIN and is poorly sensitive to immunoglobulin light chains (Bence-Jones protein).
⚠️ WRONG answer in myeloma vignettes: 'rule out significant proteinuria because the dipstick protein is negative/trace.' Discriminator: in multiple myeloma, heavy light-chain (Bence-Jones) proteinuria can give a NEGATIVE/trace dipstick because the pad reads albumin, not light chains — order a 24-hour urine protein, urine protein electrophoresis (UPEP), and serum/urine immunofixation to catch it. The dipstick blood pad likewise misses nothing about heme source, so always confirm with micro.
