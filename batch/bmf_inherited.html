📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Constitutional archway
Inherited (constitutional) bone marrow failure syndromes are germline-driven defects in DNA repair, telomere maintenance, or ribosome biogenesis that classically present in childhood with cytopenias plus characteristic physical anomalies. Unlike acquired aplastic anemia, they carry a lifelong predisposition to MDS/AML and solid tumors. Suspect them in any young patient with cytopenias plus dysmorphic features, or in an adult with unexplained marrow failure and a suggestive family history.
⚠️ Do NOT default to acquired aplastic anemia in a child or young adult with cytopenias — a constitutional syndrome must be excluded first, because it changes cancer surveillance, transplant conditioning (these patients are exquisitely sensitive to chemo/radiation), and donor selection (an affected sibling cannot donate).
2. Fanconi child
Fanconi anemia is the most common inherited BMF syndrome — autosomal recessive (rarely X-linked FANCB), with mutations in FANC genes impairing the DNA interstrand crosslink repair pathway. Phenotype mnemonic: short stature, café-au-lait macules (hyperpigmentation), thumb/radial ray anomalies, microcephaly, and hypogonadism. Progressive marrow failure typically emerges by age 5-10, and the lifetime risk of AML and squamous cell carcinomas (head/neck, gynecologic) is markedly elevated.
⚠️ Up to 30% of Fanconi patients have NO congenital anomalies — a normal-looking child with aplastic anemia still needs a chromosome breakage test before being labeled idiopathic aplastic anemia.
3. Thumb / radial anomaly
Absent or hypoplastic thumbs and radii are the classic skeletal signature of Fanconi anemia (the thumb is involved, distinguishing it from TAR syndrome where thumbs are spared). Renal anomalies — horseshoe kidney, ectopic or absent kidney — accompany the radial-ray defect (think VACTERL-like overlap).
⚠️ Thumb anomaly + thrombocytopenia is NOT automatically Fanconi: in TAR syndrome (thrombocytopenia–absent radius) the thumbs are PRESENT, whereas Fanconi involves the thumb itself. Use thumb status to separate them.
4. FANCA / FANCD2
The FA core complex (FANCA is the most common subtype, ~60-70% of cases) mono-ubiquitinates FANCD2/FANCI, which then recruits downstream repair proteins (including BRCA2/FANCD1) to resolve DNA interstrand crosslinks at stalled replication forks. Loss of this pathway leaves cells unable to repair crosslinks, producing genomic instability and cancer predisposition.
⚠️ FANCD1 is BRCA2 — biallelic BRCA2 mutations cause a severe Fanconi phenotype with early childhood solid tumors (medulloblastoma, Wilms). Don't think of BRCA2 only as adult breast/ovarian cancer.
5. Chromosome fragility
The diagnostic test for Fanconi anemia is the chromosome breakage / fragility test: patient lymphocytes are exposed to DNA crosslinking agents — diepoxybutane (DEB) or mitomycin C (MMC) — and show dramatically increased chromosomal breaks and radial figures versus controls. This is the gold-standard screen before confirmatory germline gene panel.
⚠️ The provocative agents are DEB and mitomycin C specifically — not generic radiation. A normal spontaneous karyotype does NOT exclude Fanconi; breakage only appears after crosslinker challenge.
6. Dyskeratosis triad
Dyskeratosis congenita classic mucocutaneous triad: (1) abnormal skin pigmentation (reticular/lacy hyperpigmentation of neck and upper chest), (2) nail dystrophy, (3) oral leukoplakia. Marrow failure is the leading cause of death, but patients also develop pulmonary fibrosis, hepatic fibrosis/cirrhosis, and a high risk of MDS/AML and squamous cell carcinoma (oral leukoplakia is premalignant).
⚠️ The leukoplakia is premalignant — don't dismiss oral white patches as candidiasis. And don't confuse DC's reticular pigmentation with the discrete café-au-lait spots of Fanconi.
7. DKC1 telomere genes
Dyskeratosis congenita is a telomere maintenance disorder. The most severe X-linked form is caused by DKC1 (encodes dyskerin, which stabilizes the telomerase RNA component); autosomal forms involve TERC (telomerase RNA), TERT (telomerase reverse transcriptase), and TINF2 (shelterin). The result is critically short telomeres, measurable by flow-FISH of leukocytes (very short telomeres for age is the screening test).
⚠️ Telomere disease shows genetic anticipation — successive generations present earlier and more severely because shortened telomeres are inherited. A mild adult parent can have a severely affected child.
8. Telomere bench
Short-telomere syndromes produce a premature-aging phenotype: early greying, marrow failure, and progressive fibrosis of lung (idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis) and liver. In adults, a telomere defect (TERT/TERC) may present as familial pulmonary fibrosis or unexplained cirrhosis with subtle cytopenias and macrocytosis — the marrow failure can be mild or late.
⚠️ Telomere biology disorders are a recognized cause of adult-onset idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis and cryptogenic cirrhosis — these patients tolerate reduced-intensity transplant conditioning poorly and are prone to fatal pulmonary complications.
9. Adults too tree
Constitutional BMF — especially telomere disorders and some Fanconi/GATA2 cases — is NOT exclusively pediatric. Adults can present de novo with aplastic anemia, MDS, pulmonary/hepatic fibrosis, or unexplained macrocytic cytopenias, and germline testing in adults with 'idiopathic' marrow failure increasingly uncovers these syndromes.
⚠️ Assuming inherited BMF 'only presents in children' is a classic exam trap — missing an adult telomere or GATA2 syndrome leads to wrong transplant conditioning and use of an affected related donor.
10. Diamond-Blackfan crib
Diamond-Blackfan anemia is a congenital PURE RED CELL aplasia (a ribosomopathy) presenting in the first year of life with macrocytic anemia, reticulocytopenia, and absent marrow erythroid precursors. Caused by ribosomal protein gene mutations (RPS19 most common). Labs: elevated HbF, elevated erythrocyte adenosine deaminase (eADA). Anomalies include craniofacial defects and the triphalangeal thumb. First-line treatment is corticosteroids; transfusion-dependent or steroid-refractory cases go to transplant.
⚠️ Diamond-Blackfan is RED-CELL-ONLY (anemia with normal WBC/platelets) — it does NOT cause pancytopenia. Distinguish from transient erythroblastopenia of childhood (TEC): DBA presents <1yr with macrocytosis, ↑HbF, ↑eADA and congenital anomalies, whereas TEC occurs in older toddlers, is normocytic, and self-resolves.
11. Shwachman-Diamond
Shwachman-Diamond syndrome: autosomal recessive SBDS gene mutation (a ribosome-assembly defect) producing the triad of neutropenia (the dominant cytopenia, with recurrent infections), exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (steatorrhea, failure to thrive, low fecal elastase), and skeletal abnormalities (metaphyseal dysostosis). Carries a significant risk of progression to MDS/AML.
⚠️ The marrow problem here is primarily NEUTROPENIA, not anemia — and the pancreatic insufficiency can mimic cystic fibrosis, but sweat chloride is normal and there is neutropenia. Don't confuse with cystic fibrosis.
12. GATA2 family
GATA2 deficiency is an autosomal dominant transcription-factor disorder causing inherited predisposition to MDS/AML, profound monocytopenia, B/NK lymphopenia, and severe susceptibility to disseminated mycobacterial, fungal, and HPV infections (the 'MonoMAC' syndrome). Pulmonary alveolar proteinosis and lymphedema (Emberger syndrome) are associated. Often presents in adolescence/young adulthood.
⚠️ Persistent monocytopenia with atypical/disseminated infections (NTM, HPV warts) plus a family history of MDS = think GATA2 — it is a frequently overlooked adult/adolescent cause of familial MDS and is curable only by transplant.
13. Germline panel
Workup of suspected constitutional BMF combines targeted functional screens (chromosome breakage for Fanconi, leukocyte telomere length by flow-FISH for dyskeratosis congenita) with a comprehensive germline next-generation sequencing panel that pins the exact syndrome and gene. This drives cancer surveillance, transplant conditioning intensity, and — critically — exclusion of affected relatives as stem-cell donors.
⚠️ Always send germline (not just tumor/marrow) testing and screen potential RELATED donors — an unaffected-appearing sibling may carry the same germline mutation, making them an unsafe donor and risking donor-derived disease.
