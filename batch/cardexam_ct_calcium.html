📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. HIGH NPV shield
Coronary CT angiography (CCTA) has a very high NEGATIVE predictive value: a normal CCTA reliably excludes obstructive coronary artery disease. That makes CCTA the best non-invasive test to RULE OUT CAD. Its strength is exclusion, not confirmation, of disease.
⚠️ Treating CCTA as the test to CONFIRM significant stenosis is wrong — its power is the negative result that rules disease OUT.
2. Low-intermediate pretest
CCTA is best used in SYMPTOMATIC patients with LOW-to-INTERMEDIATE pretest probability of CAD. In that probability band a normal study confidently excludes disease and avoids invasive testing. The pretest probability determines which test is appropriate.
3. Low-risk symptomatic patient
The ideal CCTA candidate is the lower-risk symptomatic patient in whom you want to safely exclude CAD. A negative scan ends the workup; a positive scan can prompt functional or invasive evaluation.
4. HIGH PRETEST — crossed out
Do NOT order CCTA in patients with HIGH pretest probability of CAD. High-probability patients are likely to have disease and should proceed to a test that can both diagnose and treat. CCTA in this group adds a step without changing the destination.
⚠️ Ordering CCTA in a high-pretest-probability patient is a classic trap — they belong in the invasive-angiography pathway.
5. Invasive angiography arrow
High pretest-probability or high-risk symptomatic patients go directly to INVASIVE coronary angiography. Invasive angiography is both diagnostic and therapeutic, allowing immediate PCI of significant lesions. This is the destination for patients in whom obstructive CAD is already likely.
6. Agatston calcium gauge
The coronary artery calcium (CAC) score uses the Agatston method to QUANTIFY the burden of CALCIFIED plaque on non-contrast cardiac CT. It is a measure of total atherosclerotic burden, not of any single stenosis. Higher scores indicate more calcified plaque and higher cardiovascular risk.
⚠️ The Agatston score measures calcium BURDEN, not stenosis severity — a high score does not localize or prove an obstructive lesion.
7. Calcified plaque rubble
The white rubble represents CALCIFIED atherosclerotic plaque within the coronary arteries. Calcium scoring detects and tallies this calcification. Calcified plaque reflects long-standing atherosclerosis but does not itself indicate the degree of luminal narrowing.
8. Asymptomatic intermediate door
CAC scoring is used for RISK STRATIFICATION in ASYMPTOMATIC patients at INTERMEDIATE cardiovascular risk. It refines risk when the decision to start preventive therapy (e.g., a statin) is uncertain. It is a tool for primary prevention, not for evaluating acute chest pain.
⚠️ CAC scoring is not for symptomatic chest-pain workup — that is CCTA or functional testing; CAC is an asymptomatic risk-refinement tool.
9. Gold star — score 0
A calcium score of ZERO indicates a very low burden of calcified plaque and a very low short-to-intermediate-term cardiovascular risk. A zero score can DE-RISK the patient and may justify withholding or deferring statin therapy. It is one of the strongest negative prognostic markers in prevention.
⚠️ A score of 0 does not exclude soft (non-calcified) plaque, but for risk stratification it strongly de-risks the asymptomatic intermediate patient.
10. Up-classify risk arrow
A HIGH calcium score UP-CLASSIFIES the patient's cardiovascular risk. This reclassification supports initiating or intensifying preventive therapy, particularly statins. The CAC result thus directly changes the management decision in intermediate-risk patients.
11. Statin bottle
A high CAC score is an indication to START a statin in an asymptomatic intermediate-risk patient, while a zero score may allow withholding it. The score therefore guides primary-prevention pharmacotherapy. This is the practical payoff of risk reclassification.
12. AV calcium score thumbnail
Aortic valve calcium scoring on CT helps adjudicate aortic stenosis SEVERITY when echocardiography is equivocal. It is especially useful in low-flow, low-gradient AS where Doppler gradients may underestimate true severity. A high valvular calcium score supports severe AS.
13. Low-flow low-gradient gauge
In LOW-FLOW, LOW-GRADIENT aortic stenosis, echo gradients can be misleadingly low and severity uncertain. CT aortic valve calcium scoring provides a flow-independent measure to confirm or refute severe AS. This resolves the diagnostic ambiguity that pure Doppler cannot.
14. TAVI sizing label
Cardiac CT is also used to size the aortic annulus and assess access vessels for TAVI (transcatheter aortic valve implantation) planning. Accurate annular measurement determines prosthesis size and reduces paravalvular leak. CT is the standard imaging modality for pre-TAVI workup.
15. Burden not stenosis
A positive calcium score proves the PRESENCE of atherosclerotic burden but does NOT prove obstructive stenosis. Many patients with high calcium scores have no flow-limiting lesion, and some obstructive plaques are non-calcified. Burden and stenosis severity are distinct concepts.
⚠️ Treating any positive calcium score as proof of OBSTRUCTIVE coronary stenosis is a core exam trap — calcium is burden, not a measure of luminal narrowing.
