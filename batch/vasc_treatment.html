📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Induction banner
Induction therapy for severe/organ-threatening ANCA-associated vasculitis (RPGN, alveolar hemorrhage, severe neuropathy) = high-dose glucocorticoids PLUS a second agent — either cyclophosphamide or rituximab. Rituximab is non-inferior to cyclophosphamide (RAVE, RITUXVAS) and superior for relapsing PR3 disease. Non-severe disease (e.g., limited GPA) may use methotrexate or mycophenolate with steroids instead.
⚠️ Steroids ALONE are insufficient for severe AAV — choosing glucocorticoid monotherapy for RPGN or alveolar hemorrhage is the trap. You must add cyclophosphamide or rituximab for organ-threatening disease.
2. Cyclophosphamide warrior
Cyclophosphamide is an alkylating agent and a classic induction option for severe AAV: oral 2 mg/kg/day or IV pulse ~15 mg/kg every 2-3 weeks. IV pulse gives a lower cumulative dose with less leukopenia but slightly higher relapse than daily oral (CYCLOPS trial). Dose-reduce in renal impairment and the elderly; switch to a maintenance agent after ~3-6 months of induction.
⚠️ Cyclophosphamide is for INDUCTION only — continuing it long-term as maintenance is the trap because cumulative dose drives bladder cancer and infertility. Transition to rituximab or azathioprine once remission is achieved.
3. CYC toxicities
Cyclophosphamide toxicities: hemorrhagic cystitis and bladder cancer (from the metabolite acrolein — mitigate with MESNA + aggressive hydration and morning dosing so the drug isn't retained in the bladder overnight), gonadal failure/infertility (offer fertility preservation), myelosuppression, and infection (give PCP prophylaxis). Long-term bladder cancer surveillance is needed even after stopping.
⚠️ Forgetting MESNA/hydration and fertility counseling before cyclophosphamide is the trap. In young patients wishing to preserve fertility, rituximab is the better induction choice — defaulting to cyclophosphamide without considering this is a board error.
4. Rituximab — top rung
Rituximab (anti-CD20 B-cell depletion) is preferred induction for relapsing disease, PR3-ANCA disease, and patients wanting to preserve fertility; it is equally effective as cyclophosphamide for induction (RAVE) and SUPERIOR for maintenance/relapse prevention (MAINRITSAN), dosed roughly 500 mg every 6 months. Screen for hepatitis B (risk of reactivation) and give PCP prophylaxis.
⚠️ Prefer rituximab over cyclophosphamide in young patients desiring fertility and in relapsing disease — choosing cyclophosphamide there is the trap. Also screen HBV before rituximab: B-cell depletion can reactivate hepatitis B (a board safety point).
5. Prednisone moon
Glucocorticoids are the backbone of induction across vasculitides: IV methylprednisolone pulses 1000 mg/day x3 for severe/organ-threatening disease, followed by oral prednisone ~1 mg/kg/day with a rapid taper. Modern protocols (PEXIVAS) favor REDUCED-dose steroid tapers — equally effective with fewer infections. Avacopan further enables steroid sparing.
⚠️ Prolonged high-dose steroids cause major toxicity (infection, osteoporosis, diabetes, AVN) — the modern trap is over-tapering too slowly/high. Reduced-dose regimens are non-inferior and safer; steroids alone are still inadequate for severe AAV (need a second agent).
6. MESNA / bladder protection
MESNA (2-mercaptoethane sulfonate) binds acrolein, the toxic cyclophosphamide metabolite, in the bladder, and combined with aggressive hydration prevents hemorrhagic cystitis. Morning dosing of oral cyclophosphamide plus fluids avoids overnight bladder exposure. These measures reduce, but do not eliminate, long-term bladder cancer risk.
⚠️ MESNA protects the bladder but does NOT protect fertility or prevent bladder MALIGNANCY long-term — assuming MESNA makes cyclophosphamide 'safe' is the trap. Separate counseling on infertility and ongoing bladder cancer surveillance is still required.
7. Plasma exchange — limited
Plasma exchange (PLEX) has a LIMITED role in AAV: the PEXIVAS trial showed it does NOT improve death or ESRD overall. Reserve it for selected severe cases — rapidly rising creatinine/dialysis-dependent severe RPGN, possibly diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, and concomitant anti-GBM (double-positive) disease. PLEX is clearly indicated in anti-GBM/Goodpasture disease.
⚠️ Routine PLEX for ALL severe AAV is NOT recommended (PEXIVAS) — adding it reflexively for any severe ANCA vasculitis is the trap. It is, however, standard in anti-GBM disease and reasonable in selected dialysis-dependent RPGN/DAH.
8. Prophylaxis (PCP)
Patients on induction immunosuppression (high-dose steroids + cyclophosphamide or rituximab) require Pneumocystis jirovecii (PCP) prophylaxis, typically TMP-SMX (one DS tab 3x/week or SS daily). TMP-SMX additionally reduces relapses in upper-airway GPA. Also update vaccinations and screen for latent infections (TB, HBV) before/during immunosuppression.
⚠️ Omitting PCP prophylaxis in patients on steroids + cyclophosphamide/rituximab is a classic safety trap — Pneumocystis pneumonia is a preventable, life-threatening complication. The risk is highest with high-dose steroids plus a steroid-sparing agent and lymphopenia.
9. Bisphosphonate
Glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis prophylaxis: calcium + vitamin D for everyone on chronic steroids, plus a bisphosphonate for those at moderate/high fracture risk or on prolonged high-dose steroids. Bone loss is fastest in the first 3-6 months, so start prevention EARLY rather than waiting for a low DXA.
⚠️ Waiting for an abnormal DXA before starting bone protection is the trap — initiate prophylaxis when committing a patient to prolonged steroids, because the fastest bone loss occurs early.
10. Avacopan — steroid sparing
Avacopan is an oral C5a-RECEPTOR antagonist (blocks neutrophil activation via complement) approved as an adjunct in severe AAV. The ADVOCATE trial showed it enables marked glucocorticoid reduction with superior sustained remission at 52 weeks and less steroid toxicity. Monitor liver enzymes (hepatotoxicity reported).
⚠️ Avacopan is a steroid-SPARING add-on to standard induction (rituximab/cyclophosphamide), NOT a replacement for the induction immunosuppressant. Using avacopan + steroids alone without a B-cell-depleting/alkylating agent for severe AAV is the trap.
11. Maintenance ladder
Maintenance therapy (after remission) prevents relapse, ranked by efficacy: rituximab (best — lowest relapse, MAINRITSAN) > azathioprine ≈ methotrexate > mycophenolate mofetil (worst — highest relapse, IMPROVE trial). Continue maintenance for at least ~18-24 months, often longer in PR3/relapsing disease. Glucocorticoids are tapered to low dose or off.
⚠️ MMF is the LEAST effective maintenance agent in AAV (higher relapse than azathioprine) — selecting MMF as first-line maintenance is the trap. Rituximab is the preferred maintenance agent, especially for relapsing/PR3 disease.
12. AZA / MTX rungs
Oral maintenance options below rituximab: azathioprine (check TPMT activity first to avoid life-threatening myelosuppression; avoid with allopurinol) and methotrexate (weekly, with folate supplementation; monitor LFTs/CBC). Both are stepped down from cyclophosphamide induction once remission is achieved.
⚠️ Methotrexate is contraindicated in significant renal impairment (accumulation → toxicity) and in active severe disease — using MTX for a patient with reduced GFR or organ-threatening AAV is the trap. Likewise, starting azathioprine without checking TPMT (or alongside allopurinol) risks severe marrow suppression.
13. Caution circle — not for GPA
Combining cyclophosphamide AND rituximab for induction adds no proven benefit while increasing infection and toxicity — choose ONE induction agent. Standard severe-AAV induction is glucocorticoids + (rituximab OR cyclophosphamide), not both together.
⚠️ Stacking cyclophosphamide + rituximab to be 'more aggressive' is the trap — it raises infection risk without improving remission. Pick a single induction immunosuppressant alongside steroids (± avacopan).
14. Duration ~12-24 mo
Maintenance immunosuppression continues for at least ~18-24 months after achieving remission, and longer (sometimes indefinitely) for PR3-ANCA or relapsing disease given high relapse rates. Stopping too early is a major cause of relapse. Periodically reassess organ function, drug toxicity, and ANCA/clinical status.
⚠️ Discontinuing maintenance at 6-12 months — especially in PR3/relapsing disease — is the trap, as relapse risk is high. Extend maintenance (often 2+ years) and taper cautiously based on serotype and relapse history.
15. ANCA rise = relapse risk
A rising ANCA titer (especially PR3-ANCA) and reappearance of ANCA are associated with INCREASED relapse risk, but a titer change alone is not sufficient to diagnose relapse or restart therapy. Treat clinical/biopsy-proven relapse; use rising titers to heighten surveillance, not to drive blind escalation. (GCA/PMR are treated with glucocorticoids ± tocilizumab/methotrexate, a separate pathway from AAV.)
⚠️ Escalating immunosuppression on a rising ANCA alone — without clinical evidence of relapse — is the trap (risks over-treatment/infection). Increase monitoring instead and treat when clinical or histologic relapse appears.
