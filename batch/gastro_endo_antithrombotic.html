📖 Read-through — all 11 symbols
1. LOW-RISK procedure sign
Low-risk (diagnostic ± biopsy, ERCP without sphincterotomy, stent without dilation) → CONTINUE anticoagulation. Bleeding risk is minimal so the thrombotic risk of stopping outweighs it. Decide hold/continue by procedure bleeding risk first, then by patient thrombotic risk.
2. HIGH-RISK procedure sign
High-bleeding-risk procedures = polypectomy, sphincterotomy, EUS-FNA, dilation, ampullectomy, PEG, variceal therapy → HOLD anticoagulation. Aspirin is the exception and is still continued even here.
3. Aspirin knight — ALWAYS CONTINUE
GOLD STAR: aspirin is continued through virtually all endoscopy, including high-risk procedures, in patients with established cardiovascular/cerebrovascular disease. Stopping it raises thrombotic events without a meaningful drop in clinically significant GI bleeding. Only consider holding for the very highest-risk resections, and even then case-by-case.
⚠️ 'Stop aspirin before any polypectomy' → wrong; aspirin is continued — stopping increases cardiovascular/thrombotic events.
4. Warfarin barrel — STOP 5 DAYS
For high-risk procedures stop warfarin ~5 days before; confirm INR ≤1.5 on the day. Resume the evening of the procedure once hemostasis is secure. Whether to bridge depends entirely on thrombotic risk, not on the warfarin itself.
5. Mechanical valve — BRIDGE
HIGH thrombotic risk → bridge with LMWH (or UFH) while INR is sub-therapeutic. High-risk = mechanical mitral valve, any caged-ball/older mechanical valve, AF with prior stroke, VTE within 3 months. Bridge LMWH stopped ~24h pre-procedure.
6. Tissue valve — NO BRIDGE (red X)
A tissue/bioprosthetic valve is LOW thrombotic risk and does NOT need bridging — just hold and resume warfarin. Bridging here only adds bleeding risk with no thrombotic benefit. Same 'no bridge' logic applies to non-valvular AF without prior stroke.
⚠️ 'Bridge a bioprosthetic valve patient with LMWH' → wrong; tissue valves are low-risk — no bridging, bridging only causes bleeding.
7. P2Y12 (clopidogrel) pills
P2Y12 inhibitors (clopidogrel, prasugrel, ticagrelor) for high-bleeding procedures may be held ~5–7 days — but ONLY if the coronary-stent window allows and after cardiology input. Aspirin is continued throughout when on dual therapy.
8. Call cardiology phone booth
Never stop a P2Y12 inhibitor in the early post-stent window without consulting cardiology; defer elective endoscopy until the window passes. Premature interruption → stent thrombosis, which carries high mortality.
9. Bare-metal stent — 1 month
Bare-metal stent (BMS): minimum ~1 month of dual antiplatelet therapy before P2Y12 can be safely interrupted for elective endoscopy. Delay elective high-risk procedures until then.
10. Drug-eluting stent — 12 mo (red X)
Drug-eluting stent (DES): continue dual antiplatelet ~6–12 months; do NOT stop P2Y12 within this window for an elective procedure. If endoscopy is truly urgent within the window, manage with cardiology and continue aspirin.
⚠️ 'Stop clopidogrel for elective polypectomy 2 months after a drug-eluting stent' → wrong; within the ~12-month window stopping risks fatal stent thrombosis — delay or consult cardiology.
11. DOAC — HOLD, no bridge
DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) → HOLD 1–2 days before high-risk procedures, longer if renal impairment (especially dabigatran with low CrCl). Their short half-life means NO bridging is needed. Resume ~24–48h post-procedure once hemostasis is confirmed.
⚠️ 'Bridge a DOAC patient with LMWH around endoscopy' → wrong; DOACs have short half-lives — just hold and resume, never bridge.
