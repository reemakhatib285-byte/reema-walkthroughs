⚠️ Board traps 13
  1. Needle-shaped crystals — WRONG answer: calling rhomboid/positively birefringent crystals 'gout.' Those are CPPD (calcium pyrophosphate) = pseudogout, which is positively birefringent and BLUE when parallel to the compensator. Discriminator: needle + negative + yellow-when-parallel = gout; rhomboid + positive + blue-when-parallel = CPPD (and CPPD favors knees/wrists, chondrocalcinosis on X-ray).
  2. Purines barrel — WRONG answer: assuming strict purine-restricted diet alone will control gout. Diet changes lower serum urate only ~1 mg/dL — inadequate for tophaceous or recurrent disease. Discriminator: diet is adjunctive; definitive control requires urate-lowering pharmacotherapy to target <6 mg/dL.
  3. Urate vat / 6.8 — WRONG answer: treating asymptomatic hyperuricemia with urate-lowering therapy. Most people with elevated urate never develop gout, and trials show no benefit treating asymptomatic levels. Discriminator: pharmacologic ULT is indicated only with clinical gout (≥2 flares/yr, tophi, urate stones, CKD), not for a high lab value alone.
  4. PRPP — WRONG answer: attributing the majority of gout to overproduction. Overproduction (PRPP excess, HGPRT defect, tumor lysis, psoriasis) accounts for only ~10% of cases. Discriminator: a 24-hr urine urate >800 mg/day on a regular diet suggests overproducer (and contraindicates uricosurics like probenecid); the other ~90% are underexcretors.
  5. Uricase lizard / grave — WRONG answer: using rasburicase for chronic gout. Rasburicase is for tumor lysis syndrome prophylaxis/treatment of acute hyperuricemia, not chronic gout (it's pegloticase that treats refractory tophaceous gout). Discriminator: both are uricase, but BOTH are contraindicated in G6PD deficiency because the H2O2 byproduct triggers hemolysis/methemoglobinemia.
  6. Under-excretion (kidney) — WRONG answer: choosing high-dose aspirin to lower urate or assuming all aspirin raises it. Aspirin is biphasic — LOW dose causes retention (hyperuricemia), HIGH dose is uricosuric. Discriminator: the boards expect low-dose ASA → ↑urate; do not stop cardioprotective aspirin for gout.
  7. MSU crystal fuel furnace — WRONG answer: thinking a NORMAL serum urate during an acute attack rules out gout. Urate often drops during a flare (acute-phase uricosuria/cytokine effect), so a normal level does not exclude it. Discriminator: diagnose on synovial crystals, not on the urate value drawn during the attack — recheck urate 2 weeks after resolution.
  8. IL-1β pipe — WRONG answer: reaching for anti-IL-1 biologics as first-line flare therapy. They are last-line/refractory agents. Discriminator: standard acute flare ladder is NSAIDs OR colchicine OR glucocorticoids first; IL-1 inhibitors only when those fail or are contraindicated.
  9. IL-6 / TNF — WRONG answer: diagnosing 'sepsis/septic arthritis' from fever + leukocytosis + a hot joint without aspiration. A florid gout flare produces the same systemic picture. Discriminator: arthrocentesis distinguishes them — gout shows negatively birefringent crystals with WBC typically 20k–50k, whereas septic arthritis often shows >50k–100k with positive Gram stain/culture; the two can also coexist.
  10. Hot red MTP joint — WRONG answer: skipping joint aspiration because the presentation 'looks like classic gout.' Septic arthritis is the can't-miss mimic and a surgical emergency. Discriminator: always aspirate an acutely hot, swollen joint to send cell count, Gram stain/culture, AND crystal analysis before committing to a gout diagnosis.
  11. 1st MTP / podagra — WRONG answer: assuming gout is always monoarticular at the great toe. Long-standing or polyarticular gout (especially in elderly women on diuretics) can present in the hands/DIPs and mimic osteoarthritis or RA. Discriminator: tophi over Heberden nodes/olecranon and crystal-positive aspirate confirm gout despite an atypical, polyarticular distribution.
  12. Alcohol / beer trigger — WRONG answer: stopping urate-lowering therapy when a flare occurs during the first weeks of treatment. The flare is expected crystal mobilization, not drug failure. Discriminator: CONTINUE the ULT through the flare, treat the flare symptomatically, and co-prescribe colchicine/NSAID prophylaxis for 3–6 months when starting ULT.
  13. Beer mugs (mob) — WRONG answer: telling a gout patient that switching from beer to wine fully resolves risk. All alcohol contributes via lactate-mediated underexcretion; wine is only relatively safer. Discriminator: the high-yield protective foods are coffee, low-fat dairy, and cherries — not 'any alcohol in moderation.'
📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Needle-shaped crystals
MSU (monosodium urate) crystals are needle-shaped and NEGATIVELY birefringent under polarized light. They appear YELLOW when aligned PARALLEL to the slow axis of the red compensator and blue when perpendicular. Synovial aspirate is the gold-standard diagnostic test, showing intracellular crystals within neutrophils during a flare. Mnemonic: 'urAte = pArallel = yellow, Negative birefringence.'
⚠️ WRONG answer: calling rhomboid/positively birefringent crystals 'gout.' Those are CPPD (calcium pyrophosphate) = pseudogout, which is positively birefringent and BLUE when parallel to the compensator. Discriminator: needle + negative + yellow-when-parallel = gout; rhomboid + positive + blue-when-parallel = CPPD (and CPPD favors knees/wrists, chondrocalcinosis on X-ray).
2. Purines barrel
Purine catabolism is the substrate stream feeding uric acid: hypoxanthine → xanthine → uric acid, both steps catalyzed by xanthine oxidase (the drug target of allopurinol/febuxostat). Endogenous sources are high cell turnover (psoriasis, hemolysis, tumor lysis); dietary sources are red/organ meats, seafood, beer, and fructose. Fructose is unique because its metabolism consumes ATP and generates AMP, accelerating purine degradation to urate.
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming strict purine-restricted diet alone will control gout. Diet changes lower serum urate only ~1 mg/dL — inadequate for tophaceous or recurrent disease. Discriminator: diet is adjunctive; definitive control requires urate-lowering pharmacotherapy to target <6 mg/dL.
3. Urate vat / 6.8
The urate saturation/solubility limit in plasma is ~6.8 mg/dL at 37°C; above this, MSU supersaturates and precipitates in cool peripheral/avascular tissue (toes, ears, joints). Hyperuricemia is therefore defined as >6.8 mg/dL. The TREATMENT target is <6 mg/dL (and <5 mg/dL for tophaceous disease) to dissolve existing crystals. Cooler distal joints precipitate first — explaining podagra.
⚠️ WRONG answer: treating asymptomatic hyperuricemia with urate-lowering therapy. Most people with elevated urate never develop gout, and trials show no benefit treating asymptomatic levels. Discriminator: pharmacologic ULT is indicated only with clinical gout (≥2 flares/yr, tophi, urate stones, CKD), not for a high lab value alone.
4. PRPP
PRPP (phosphoribosyl pyrophosphate) synthetase OVERACTIVITY drives purine OVERPRODUCTION and is an X-linked cause of early hyperuricemia. The salvage enzyme HGPRT normally recycles hypoxanthine/guanine; its deficiency wastes PRPP toward de novo synthesis. Complete HGPRT deficiency = Lesch-Nyhan syndrome (X-linked): hyperuricemia, gout, self-mutilation, dystonia, intellectual disability.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing the majority of gout to overproduction. Overproduction (PRPP excess, HGPRT defect, tumor lysis, psoriasis) accounts for only ~10% of cases. Discriminator: a 24-hr urine urate >800 mg/day on a regular diet suggests overproducer (and contraindicates uricosurics like probenecid); the other ~90% are underexcretors.
5. Uricase lizard / grave
Humans and higher primates LACK uricase (urate oxidase) — the gene was silenced in evolution — so we cannot convert relatively insoluble uric acid into highly soluble allantoin. This is why humans get gout but most mammals do not. Recombinant uricase (rasburicase, pegloticase) exploits this missing pathway therapeutically.
⚠️ WRONG answer: using rasburicase for chronic gout. Rasburicase is for tumor lysis syndrome prophylaxis/treatment of acute hyperuricemia, not chronic gout (it's pegloticase that treats refractory tophaceous gout). Discriminator: both are uricase, but BOTH are contraindicated in G6PD deficiency because the H2O2 byproduct triggers hemolysis/methemoglobinemia.
6. Under-excretion (kidney)
~90% of hyperuricemia is renal UNDER-excretion, not overproduction. Urate is freely filtered then largely reabsorbed via URAT1; net excretion is only ~10%. Drugs that reduce excretion (low-dose aspirin, thiazide/loop diuretics, cyclosporine, pyrazinamide, ethambutol), plus CKD, lactic acidosis, and ketoacidosis compete at the tubule and raise urate. Mnemonic for urate-raising drugs: 'CAN'T LEAP' (Cyclosporine, Alcohol, Nicotinic acid, Thiazides, Lasix/loops, Ethambutol, Aspirin-low-dose, Pyrazinamide).
⚠️ WRONG answer: choosing high-dose aspirin to lower urate or assuming all aspirin raises it. Aspirin is biphasic — LOW dose causes retention (hyperuricemia), HIGH dose is uricosuric. Discriminator: the boards expect low-dose ASA → ↑urate; do not stop cardioprotective aspirin for gout.
7. MSU crystal fuel furnace
Deposited MSU crystals are phagocytosed by resident macrophages and act as a danger signal that assembles and activates the NLRP3 inflammasome → caspase-1 cleaves pro-IL-1β into active IL-1β. IL-1β recruits neutrophils that ingest more crystals, perpetuating the acute flare. This crystal→NLRP3→caspase-1→IL-1β axis is the molecular core of gout and the rationale for IL-1 blockade.
⚠️ WRONG answer: thinking a NORMAL serum urate during an acute attack rules out gout. Urate often drops during a flare (acute-phase uricosuria/cytokine effect), so a normal level does not exclude it. Discriminator: diagnose on synovial crystals, not on the urate value drawn during the attack — recheck urate 2 weeks after resolution.
8. IL-1β pipe
IL-1β is THE pivotal cytokine of the acute gout flare downstream of NLRP3/caspase-1. This is the basis of anti-IL-1 therapy — anakinra (IL-1 receptor antagonist) and canakinumab (anti-IL-1β monoclonal) — reserved for flares when NSAIDs, colchicine, and steroids are all contraindicated (e.g., advanced CKD with diabetes/heart failure).
⚠️ WRONG answer: reaching for anti-IL-1 biologics as first-line flare therapy. They are last-line/refractory agents. Discriminator: standard acute flare ladder is NSAIDs OR colchicine OR glucocorticoids first; IL-1 inhibitors only when those fail or are contraindicated.
9. IL-6 / TNF
IL-6 and TNF-α are amplifying cytokines released alongside IL-1β, driving systemic inflammatory features of severe flares (fever, leukocytosis, elevated CRP/ESR). They explain why a polyarticular gout flare can mimic systemic infection or sepsis with marked acute-phase reactants.
⚠️ WRONG answer: diagnosing 'sepsis/septic arthritis' from fever + leukocytosis + a hot joint without aspiration. A florid gout flare produces the same systemic picture. Discriminator: arthrocentesis distinguishes them — gout shows negatively birefringent crystals with WBC typically 20k–50k, whereas septic arthritis often shows >50k–100k with positive Gram stain/culture; the two can also coexist.
10. Hot red MTP joint
Acute gout = abrupt monoarthritis with severe pain, erythema, warmth, and swelling peaking within 12–24 hours, often waking the patient at night and so tender even a bedsheet hurts. Untreated flares self-resolve over days to weeks. Over time, untreated disease progresses to chronic tophaceous gout with destructive 'rat-bite'/punched-out erosions with overhanging edges on X-ray.
⚠️ WRONG answer: skipping joint aspiration because the presentation 'looks like classic gout.' Septic arthritis is the can't-miss mimic and a surgical emergency. Discriminator: always aspirate an acutely hot, swollen joint to send cell count, Gram stain/culture, AND crystal analysis before committing to a gout diagnosis.
11. 1st MTP / podagra
The first metatarsophalangeal joint (podagra) is the classic initial site because it is the coolest, most peripheral, weight-bearing joint where urate precipitates most readily. It is the inaugural joint in ~50% and is involved in up to 90% of patients over the disease course. Other early sites: midfoot, ankle, knee.
⚠️ WRONG answer: assuming gout is always monoarticular at the great toe. Long-standing or polyarticular gout (especially in elderly women on diuretics) can present in the hands/DIPs and mimic osteoarthritis or RA. Discriminator: tophi over Heberden nodes/olecranon and crystal-positive aspirate confirm gout despite an atypical, polyarticular distribution.
12. Alcohol / beer trigger
Flare triggers cause rapid urate fluctuation in either direction: alcohol (esp. beer), purine-rich feasts, dehydration, new diuretics, trauma, surgery, acute illness, and — importantly — INITIATION of urate-lowering therapy. Rapid lowering mobilizes crystals from tophi and can paradoxically precipitate a flare.
⚠️ WRONG answer: stopping urate-lowering therapy when a flare occurs during the first weeks of treatment. The flare is expected crystal mobilization, not drug failure. Discriminator: CONTINUE the ULT through the flare, treat the flare symptomatically, and co-prescribe colchicine/NSAID prophylaxis for 3–6 months when starting ULT.
13. Beer mugs (mob)
Beer is the worst alcoholic trigger (high purine/guanosine content PLUS ethanol, which increases ATP turnover and lactate that blocks urate excretion), spirits are intermediate, and wine is least associated. Fructose-sweetened sodas independently raise urate. Protective dietary factors: coffee, low-fat dairy, vitamin C, and cherries.
⚠️ WRONG answer: telling a gout patient that switching from beer to wine fully resolves risk. All alcohol contributes via lactate-mediated underexcretion; wine is only relatively safer. Discriminator: the high-yield protective foods are coffee, low-fat dairy, and cherries — not 'any alcohol in moderation.'
