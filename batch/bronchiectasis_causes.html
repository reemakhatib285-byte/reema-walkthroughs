📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Upper lobes sign
Lobar distribution is a high-yield board discriminator for the underlying cause. UPPER-lobe-predominant bronchiectasis points to cystic fibrosis first, and also to ABPA and post-primary TB or sarcoid. The 'vicious cycle' mechanism is shared: impaired clearance → infection → neutrophilic inflammation → airway-wall destruction and permanent dilation. Mnemonic: think 'A' diseases up top — Aspergillus (ABPA), Acid-fast (TB), and CF.
⚠️ Don't reflexively assign ALL bronchiectasis to the upper lobes — idiopathic and post-infectious disease favor the LOWER lobes, so distribution actively narrows the differential rather than confirming a single cause.
2. Parker / CF planes
Cystic fibrosis (CFTR mutation, autosomal recessive, ΔF508 most common) is the classic cause of UPPER-lobe bronchiectasis. Suspect it in a young patient with chronic sinopulmonary infection (Pseudomonas/Staph), pancreatic insufficiency with malabsorption, and male infertility from congenital absence of the vas deferens. Confirm with a sweat chloride >60 mmol/L and CFTR genetics.
⚠️ Don't default to CF in an OLDER adult with LOWER-lobe disease — that picture is post-infectious or idiopathic; conversely, missing CF in a young adult with upper-lobe disease and infertility/sinus disease is the under-diagnosis trap (mild CFTR variants can present in adulthood).
3. MAC plane (Lady Windermere)
Mycobacterium avium complex (a nontuberculous mycobacterium) causes NODULAR bronchiectasis of the right middle lobe and lingula — the classic 'Lady Windermere syndrome' in older, thin, nonsmoking women who habitually suppress their cough. Diagnose with HRCT (tree-in-bud + cylindrical bronchiectasis) plus repeated sputum AFB cultures; treat with a macrolide-based multidrug regimen for ~12 months after culture conversion.
⚠️ Nodular RML/lingular bronchiectasis in an elderly woman is MAC, not simple post-pneumonic scarring — and the answer is multidrug NTM therapy, not a single macrolide (monotherapy breeds macrolide resistance).
4. MidLung sign
Middle-lobe and lingular involvement is the signature distribution of MAC nodular bronchiectasis, reinforcing the Lady Windermere pattern. The right middle lobe is also anatomically prone to recurrent collapse/infection because of its narrow long bronchus ('right middle lobe syndrome').
⚠️ Isolated middle-lobe/lingular disease in a thin elderly woman should prompt NTM sputum cultures rather than being dismissed as recurrent ordinary pneumonia.
5. ABPA control tower
Allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis is a hypersensitivity reaction to Aspergillus colonizing the airways, occurring in patients with ASTHMA or CF. It produces CENTRAL/perihilar bronchiectasis with mucoid impaction. Diagnostic clues: total IgE >1000 IU/mL, positive Aspergillus-specific IgE and IgG, peripheral eosinophilia, and fleeting infiltrates.
⚠️ PERIPHERAL bronchiectasis argues AGAINST ABPA — ABPA is classically CENTRAL — so choosing ABPA for a peripheral-predominant pattern is wrong; the central location is the discriminator.
6. Aspergillus monster
Aspergillus fumigatus hypersensitivity (Type I and Type III reactions) drives ABPA. Workup: very high total IgE (often >1000 IU/mL), elevated Aspergillus-specific IgE/IgG, and eosinophilia >500/µL. Treatment is systemic corticosteroids to suppress the immune response, with itraconazole/voriconazole as a steroid-sparing antifungal adjunct.
⚠️ ABPA is a HYPERSENSITIVITY reaction, not invasive aspergillosis or an aspergilloma — treat with steroids ± azole antifungal, NOT surgical resection or amphotericin; treating it as invasive infection is the categorization trap.
7. Immunodeficiency cabinets
Humoral (antibody) immunodeficiency — common variable immunodeficiency or other hypogammaglobulinemia — causes recurrent encapsulated-organism sinopulmonary infections that scar airways into bronchiectasis. Screen with quantitative immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM) and vaccine-response titers; treat with immunoglobulin replacement.
⚠️ In recurrent infections plus bronchiectasis, don't stop at repeated antibiotic courses — failing to MEASURE IgG/IgA/IgM and missing CVID (a treatable, IVIG-responsive cause) is the classic omission.
8. Lower lobes sign
LOWER-lobe-predominant bronchiectasis suggests idiopathic, post-infectious (childhood pneumonia/pertussis/measles), recurrent ASPIRATION, or immunodeficiency causes — gravity favors basal infection and aspiration. This is the opposite anchor to the upper-lobe (CF/ABPA/TB) group.
⚠️ Lower-lobe disease should steer AWAY from CF/ABPA and toward post-infectious, aspiration, or immunodeficiency etiologies — anchoring on CF for basal disease is the distribution trap.
9. Wheelchair / chronic patient
Depicts the chronically disabled, recurrently infected patient. A key board pearl: recurrent pneumonia in the SAME lobe should trigger a search for an underlying structural cause — localized bronchiectasis, an endobronchial obstructing lesion (tumor/foreign body), or extrinsic compression.
⚠️ Recurrent pneumonia confined to one lobe is NOT just bad luck — overlooking a fixed obstruction (especially endobronchial tumor in a smoker) and not obtaining bronchoscopy/CT is the workup trap.
10. HIV sign
HIV and other cell-mediated/combined immunodeficiency states predispose to recurrent bacterial and opportunistic infections (including NTM and recurrent pneumonia) that lead to bronchiectasis. Bronchiectasis can also reflect chronic immune dysregulation in poorly controlled HIV.
⚠️ Don't forget to consider HIV/immunosuppression as a driver of bronchiectasis — attributing recurrent infections to 'COPD' alone without checking immune status can miss a treatable underlying cause.
11. Kartagener's sign
Primary ciliary dyskinesia (autosomal recessive dynein-arm defect) impairs mucociliary clearance. Kartagener triad = bronchiectasis + chronic sinusitis + situs inversus (dextrocardia), plus infertility from immotile sperm/dysfunctional fallopian cilia. Confirm with low nasal nitric oxide screening and ciliary ultrastructure on biopsy.
⚠️ Situs inversus + chronic sinopulmonary infection = Kartagener/PCD, NOT cystic fibrosis — confirm with nasal NO and ciliary biopsy; defaulting to CF (or missing the dextrocardia clue on CXR) is the trap.
12. 25-50% idiopathic banner
Even after a complete etiologic workup, roughly 25–50% of bronchiectasis remains IDIOPATHIC. Among identifiable causes, POST-INFECTIOUS is the most common. Across all cases, chronic airway colonization with Pseudomonas aeruginosa (and mucoid strains) marks worse prognosis and more frequent exacerbations.
⚠️ A negative cause workup does NOT mean you missed something — idiopathic bronchiectasis is genuinely common, so endlessly re-testing rather than managing airway clearance/colonization is the trap.
