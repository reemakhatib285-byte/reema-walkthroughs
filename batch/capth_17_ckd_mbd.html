📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Step 1: GFR down / PO4 retained
The cascade begins as GFR falls: the kidney can no longer excrete the daily phosphate load, so phosphate is retained — the first trigger of CKD-MBD. Phosphate retention typically begins around CKD stage 3 (eGFR <60), though serum phosphate may stay normal for a while because FGF23 and PTH ramp up phosphaturia to compensate. Hyperphosphatemia directly lowers calcium (binds it) and stimulates PTH.
⚠️ Reassurance from a normal serum phosphate early in CKD is the trap — phosphate retention starts before the level rises overtly, masked by rising FGF23/PTH, so don't wait for hyperphosphatemia to begin counseling on phosphate control.
2. Step 2: FGF23 / osteocyte alarm
Osteocytes sense the phosphate load and release FGF23 (the earliest hormone to rise in CKD-MBD). FGF23 promotes renal phosphate excretion (phosphaturia) — helpful — but it also SUPPRESSES 1-alpha-hydroxylase, cutting calcitriol production, which is harmful. So the body's first compensatory move simultaneously sets up the calcitriol deficiency that drives secondary hyperparathyroidism.
⚠️ Thinking FGF23 is purely protective is the trap — by suppressing calcitriol it lowers gut calcium absorption and accelerates secondary HPT; FGF23 is the earliest measurable abnormality, rising before phosphate or PTH are overtly off.
3. Step 3: calcitriol down (1α-hydroxylase blocked)
Reduced functional renal mass plus FGF23 suppression cut 1-alpha-hydroxylase activity, so the kidney can't convert 25-OH vitamin D to active 1,25-(OH)2 vitamin D (calcitriol). Low calcitriol means less intestinal calcium absorption and loss of calcitriol's direct suppression of PTH gene transcription — both push PTH up. This step is why CKD patients need calcitriol or analogs (paricalcitol, doxercalciferol), not plain vitamin D.
⚠️ Prescribing cholecalciferol/ergocalciferol to correct the 'vitamin D deficiency' of CKD is the trap — the diseased kidney cannot 1-alpha-hydroxylate it, so you must give ACTIVE vitamin D (calcitriol or an analog) that bypasses the blocked enzyme.
4. Step 4: gut Ca absorption down
Low calcitriol reduces intestinal calcium absorption (calcitriol normally upregulates the gut calcium transporters), producing hypocalcemia. The hypocalcemia, hyperphosphatemia, and loss of calcitriol's direct suppression all converge on the parathyroid to drive PTH up. This is the link between the vitamin D arm and the calcium/PTH arm of the cascade.
⚠️ Treating the low calcium of CKD with calcium-based binders or high-dose calcium is tempting but is the trap — it can push the Ca×PO4 product up and worsen vascular calcification; correct the underlying calcitriol deficiency instead.
5. Step 5: secondary HPT (PTH up)
Three stimuli — hypocalcemia, hyperphosphatemia, and low calcitriol — chronically drive the parathyroids, producing secondary hyperparathyroidism: HIGH PTH with LOW or normal calcium. Over time the glands hyperplasiae. The defining lab signature is high PTH + high PO4 + low/normal Ca + low calcitriol, distinguishing it from the high-Ca hyperparathyroid states.
⚠️ Confusing the three hyperparathyroid states is the high-yield trap: PRIMARY HPT = high Ca + high PTH (low PO4); SECONDARY HPT (CKD) = LOW/normal Ca + high PTH (high PO4); TERTIARY HPT = HIGH Ca + high PTH (autonomous). The calcium direction sorts them.
6. Secondary HPT lab box
Classic secondary HPT panel in CKD: HIGH phosphate, HIGH PTH, LOW/normal calcium, LOW calcitriol, and elevated FGF23. KDIGO targets in CKD aim to lower phosphate toward normal, keep calcium in range, and keep PTH within ~2–9x the upper limit of normal (NOT fully normal) on dialysis.
⚠️ Reading a 'low-normal calcium with markedly high PTH' as primary hyperparathyroidism is the trap — in primary HPT calcium is HIGH; the low/normal calcium with high PTH and high phosphate plus impaired renal function is secondary HPT of CKD.
7. High-turnover bone / osteitis fibrosa
Persistently high PTH drives high-turnover renal osteodystrophy — osteitis fibrosa cystica — with increased osteoclastic resorption, marrow fibrosis, bone pain, and fractures. This is the under-treated end of the spectrum. Histology shows elevated bone turnover; treatment is controlling phosphate, repleting calcitriol, and using calcimimetics to lower PTH.
⚠️ Assuming any CKD bone disease is high-turnover is the trap — over-correcting PTH flips the patient to the OPPOSITE problem, low-turnover adynamic bone, which is now common because of aggressive PTH suppression.
8. Don't over-suppress PTH / adynamic bone
If therapy drives PTH TOO low (over-suppression with calcium, calcitriol, calcimimetics), bone turnover halts — adynamic (low-turnover) bone disease. The 'frozen' bone can't buffer a calcium load, so calcium spills into vessels → hypercalcemia, vascular calcification, and fractures. Risk is higher in elderly and diabetic dialysis patients.
⚠️ Normalizing PTH all the way to the standard reference range in a CKD patient is the trap — some elevation of PTH is needed to maintain bone turnover, so KDIGO targets a PTH 2–9x ULN; over-suppression causes adynamic bone.
9. Tertiary HPT (autonomous, post-transplant)
After years of secondary HPT, the chronically stimulated parathyroid glands become AUTONOMOUS (nodular hyperplasia → tertiary HPT): they secrete PTH independent of calcium, so calcium now rises. Often unmasked after renal transplant when the kidney works again but the glands keep over-secreting. Labs: HIGH PTH and HIGH calcium, frequently refractory to medical therapy.
⚠️ The discriminator from secondary HPT is calcium: tertiary shows HIGH calcium (autonomous secretion), whereas secondary shows low/normal calcium. A CKD/post-transplant patient who becomes HYPERcalcemic with high PTH has progressed to tertiary HPT.
10. Parathyroidectomy (refractory tertiary)
Surgical parathyroidectomy (often subtotal or total with autotransplantation) is indicated for refractory tertiary HPT, or severe secondary HPT that fails medical therapy (uncontrollable hyperphosphatemia/PTH, calciphylaxis, intractable pruritus/bone pain). Watch for hungry bone syndrome — profound post-op hypocalcemia as demineralized bone rapidly reuptakes calcium.
⚠️ Forgetting hungry bone syndrome after parathyroidectomy is the trap — the suddenly PTH-deprived, demineralized skeleton avidly takes up calcium, causing severe prolonged post-op hypocalcemia that needs aggressive calcium and calcitriol.
11. Dietary PO4 restriction
First-line management of CKD-MBD is dietary PHOSPHATE restriction (limit dairy, cola, processed foods/preservatives, which are high in absorbable inorganic phosphate). Controlling phosphate is the foundation because hyperphosphatemia drives the whole cascade (FGF23, low calcitriol, secondary HPT, vascular calcification).
⚠️ Overlooking dietary phosphate while reaching straight for binders/calcitriol is the trap — diet (especially hidden phosphate additives in processed food) is the first and cheapest intervention; binders are added when diet alone is insufficient.
12. Sevelamer (non-Ca binder)
Sevelamer and lanthanum carbonate are NON-calcium phosphate binders taken WITH meals to block intestinal phosphate absorption without adding a calcium load. Preferred when the patient is hypercalcemic, has vascular calcification, or has adynamic bone. Sevelamer also modestly lowers LDL cholesterol.
⚠️ Defaulting to calcium-based binders in a patient with hypercalcemia or vascular calcification is the trap — non-calcium binders (sevelamer, lanthanum) avoid the added calcium load that worsens calcification and adynamic bone.
13. Ca-based binder
Calcium acetate and calcium carbonate are inexpensive phosphate binders that double as a calcium source, useful when the patient is hypocalcemic. But they add a calcium load — limited by hypercalcemia and the risk of vascular calcification, especially with concurrent active vitamin D or in adynamic bone.
⚠️ Using calcium-based binders in a patient who is already hypercalcemic or has low-turnover/adynamic bone is the trap — the calcium load worsens hypercalcemia and vascular calcification; switch to a non-calcium binder in those settings.
14. Cinacalcet / calcimimetic + avoid Ca×PO4 excess
Calcimimetics (cinacalcet, IV etelcalcetide) allosterically sensitize the parathyroid CaSR to calcium, lowering PTH AND calcium — useful when PTH is high but calcium is also high. Active vitamin D analogs (calcitriol, paricalcitol) lower PTH but raise calcium and phosphate. Keep the Ca×PO4 product low (<~55) to limit vascular calcification.
⚠️ Choosing active vitamin D to lower PTH in a patient with high calcium or high Ca×PO4 product is the trap — vitamin D analogs raise both, worsening calcification; cinacalcet lowers PTH while also LOWERING calcium, making it the right tool when calcium is elevated.
