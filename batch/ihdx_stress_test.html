📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Left circumflex blind zone
The LCx territory (lateral/posterior wall) is the classic ECG blind spot — ischemia there can be electrically silent on a standard 12-lead. A normal exercise ECG does not exclude LCx disease; posterior leads or imaging are needed.
⚠️ Calling a normal stress ECG 'no ischemia' — a true posterior/LCx event may show only reciprocal anterior ST depression or nothing at all.
2. Blindfolded ECG tech
Exercise ECG has limited sensitivity (~68%) and specificity (~77%). It detects only marked ischemia. Imaging stress (echo/nuclear) is preferred when baseline ECG is uninterpretable (LBBB, paced, LVH with strain, digoxin, WPW).
⚠️ Ordering exercise ECG in a patient with baseline LBBB or paced rhythm — use vasodilator nuclear or dobutamine echo instead.
3. 75% not 90% pre-test
Stress testing is most useful at INTERMEDIATE pre-test probability. Bayes: a positive test in a low-probability patient is mostly false-positive; in very-high-probability patients (~90%) go straight to angiography — the test rarely changes management.
⚠️ Stress-testing a patient with typical angina and very high pre-test probability instead of proceeding to coronary angiography.
4. ≥1mm flat/downslope ST
A positive exercise ECG = ≥1 mm horizontal or downsloping ST depression 80 ms after the J-point. Upsloping ST depression is NOT diagnostic. ST elevation in non-Q leads suggests transmural ischemia/spasm.
⚠️ Counting upsloping ST depression as a positive test — only flat or downsloping ≥1 mm qualifies.
5. Imaging = most specific
Stress imaging (nuclear MPI or stress echo) adds specificity and localizes ischemia. Reversible perfusion defect = ischemia; fixed defect = scar/infarct. Echo wall-motion abnormality with stress = ischemia.
⚠️ A FIXED defect is scar, not active ischemia — it does not warrant revascularization for symptom relief the way a reversible defect does.
6. Nuclear perfusion — worst sign
Increased lung uptake of thallium (or transient ischemic dilation, large multi-territory reversible defect, drop in EF) signals severe, often left-main/3-vessel disease — high-risk markers that mandate angiography.
⚠️ Treating a large reversible anterior defect or TID as routine — these are high-risk findings requiring prompt invasive evaluation.
7. Systolic BP drop ≥10 — STOP
A FALL in systolic BP ≥10 mmHg during exercise (exertional hypotension) signals severe ischemia/LV dysfunction — an absolute reason to terminate. Normal response is a progressive rise in systolic BP.
⚠️ Continuing the test despite a falling systolic BP — exertional hypotension is a marker of left-main/severe multivessel disease.
8. BP rise = normal
Normal hemodynamic response is a steady rise in systolic BP and heart rate with exercise. Failure to augment HR (chronotropic incompetence) or BP is abnormal and prognostically adverse.
⚠️ Mistaking a blunted/flat HR response on a beta-blocked patient as ischemia — but true chronotropic incompetence off rate-limiting drugs is high-risk.
9. Coronary angiography next
High-risk stress findings (exertional hypotension, early/marked ST depression, large reversible defect, EF drop, ↑lung uptake) → proceed to coronary angiography ± revascularization.
⚠️ Sending a high-risk positive test home on medical therapy alone instead of to the cath lab.
10. Stage III complete
Good exercise capacity (completing later Bruce stages, high METs, Duke Treadmill Score) is strongly protective — exercise tolerance is one of the best prognostic predictors regardless of imaging.
⚠️ Ignoring excellent functional capacity — high METs achieved predicts good outcome even with minor ST changes.
11. Fail Stage II <6 min
Inability to complete early stages / low METs / ischemia at low workload (Duke Treadmill Score ≤ -11 = high risk) predicts severe disease and poor prognosis — refer for angiography.
⚠️ Reassuring a patient who developed angina and ST depression at a low workload — early ischemia is a high-risk finding.
12. ECG ISCHEMIA banner
Absolute termination indications: ST elevation >1 mm in non-Q leads, drop in SBP with signs of ischemia, sustained VT, signs of poor perfusion (cyanosis/pallor), patient's request, or technical failure to monitor.
⚠️ Pushing through sustained VT or ST elevation — these are absolute stop indications, not relative.
