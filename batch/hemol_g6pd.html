📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. G6PD enzyme
G6PD is the rate-limiting enzyme of the hexose-monophosphate (pentose phosphate) shunt, generating NADPH. Its deficiency is the most common human enzymopathy (~400 million people).
⚠️ Confusing it with pyruvate kinase deficiency — PK is a glycolytic (ATP) defect, whereas G6PD is the HMP shunt (NADPH/antioxidant) defect.
2. NADPH / glutathione shield
NADPH keeps glutathione reduced, which neutralizes oxidative radicals and protects hemoglobin and membrane. Without it, oxidant stress denatures hemoglobin into Heinz bodies.
3. X-linked
G6PD deficiency is X-linked recessive, so it predominantly affects males; heterozygous females are usually carriers but can express disease through lyonization.
⚠️ Calling it autosomal — an affected father cannot pass it to sons. (PK deficiency, by contrast, is autosomal recessive.)
4. Oxidative stress
Hemolysis in G6PD is EPISODIC, triggered by oxidative stress; between attacks patients are usually well with a normal hemoglobin.
⚠️ Expecting chronic steady-state anemia — classic G6PD is acute/episodic, not chronic (except rare severe class I variants).
5. Bite cells
Splenic macrophages "bite" out the Heinz-body-laden membrane, producing bite cells (degmacytes) and blister cells on the smear.
⚠️ (with Heinz bodies + a trigger, bite cells point to G6PD)
6. Heinz bodies
Heinz bodies are denatured, precipitated hemoglobin clumped inside the red cell — the fingerprint of oxidative damage.
⚠️ (see crystal-violet trap)
7. Crystal violet stain
Heinz bodies are invisible on routine Wright-Giemsa; they require a SUPRAVITAL stain (crystal violet or new methylene blue).
⚠️ "Heinz bodies seen on the routine smear" — they need a supravital stain to be visualized.
8. Infection (trigger)
Infection is the MOST COMMON overall trigger of G6PD hemolysis.
⚠️ Assuming fava beans or drugs are the most common trigger — infection is.
9. Favism
Fava (broad) beans cause favism — severe acute hemolysis, especially with the Mediterranean variant, and can be life-threatening in children.
⚠️ Expecting favism in the mild African A- variant — severe favism is classically the Mediterranean variant.
10. Oxidant drugs
Oxidant drugs trigger hemolysis: primaquine/tafenoquine, dapsone, sulfonamides (TMP-SMX), nitrofurantoin, methylene blue and rasburicase.
⚠️ (see rasburicase)
11. Rasburicase — tumor lysis
Rasburicase (recombinant uricase for tumor lysis syndrome) is CONTRAINDICATED in G6PD deficiency — it generates hydrogen peroxide causing severe hemolysis AND methemoglobinemia; screen G6PD before giving.
⚠️ Giving rasburicase for tumor lysis without G6PD screening — and note methylene blue, the usual methemoglobinemia antidote, is ALSO contraindicated in G6PD.
12. Safe distractors (acetaminophen, carbamazepine)
These commonly-offered "trigger" distractors are NOT significant oxidant triggers at therapeutic doses — they are safe choices.
⚠️ Picking acetaminophen / carbamazepine / tacrolimus as the G6PD trigger — the answer is the oxidant drug (rasburicase, primaquine, a sulfonamide).
13. False-normal assay — retest 3 months
During an acute attack the most-deficient old cells are already lysed and the marrow releases young reticulocytes (which have higher G6PD), so the assay can read FALSELY NORMAL. Retest 2–3 months after recovery, at steady state.
⚠️ A normal G6PD level during acute hemolysis read as excluding the diagnosis — repeat the assay when the patient is stable.
14. Severity variants
Variants differ: Mediterranean (class II, severe), African A- (class III, milder/self-limited because only older cells are affected). Neonatal jaundice on day 2–3 risks kernicterus.
⚠️ Expecting equal severity — African A- is self-limited/milder; Mediterranean is severe.
