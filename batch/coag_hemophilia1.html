📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Hemarthrosis — hallmark
Hemarthrosis — recurrent spontaneous bleeding into large weight-bearing joints (knees, ankles, elbows) — is the hallmark of severe hemophilia. Repeated bleeds cause synovial iron deposition (hemosiderin), chronic synovitis and cartilage destruction, producing a 'target joint' and crippling hemophilic arthropathy. Acute episodes present with a warm, swollen, painful joint held in flexion.
⚠️ Hemophilia causes DEEP bleeding — hemarthroses, intramuscular hematomas (iliopsoas → femoral nerve palsy, retroperitoneal), and delayed bleeding after trauma/surgery — NOT the immediate mucocutaneous bleeding (epistaxis, petechiae, menorrhagia, gum bleeding) of platelet disorders and von Willebrand disease. Mucocutaneous pattern → think platelet/vWF, not hemophilia.
2. Intracranial bleed danger
Intracranial hemorrhage is the leading cause of bleeding death in hemophilia and can occur spontaneously or after minor head trauma. The rule: give factor replacement IMMEDIATELY to a level of ~80–100% for any suspected serious or CNS bleed — do this BEFORE and without waiting for CT or neuro consultation.
⚠️ Do NOT delay factor replacement to obtain imaging or labs in a hemophiliac with suspected ICH or major bleed — 'treat first, image second' is the tested principle; ordering the CT before giving factor is the wrong answer.
3. Factor VIII = Hemophilia A
Hemophilia A = factor VIII deficiency, the most common severe inherited bleeding disorder, ~1 in 5,000 male births and ~80–85% of hemophilia cases. It is X-linked recessive, so it overwhelmingly affects males. FVIII is part of the intrinsic pathway, so its deficiency prolongs the aPTT.
⚠️ Hemophilia A = factor VIII (the more common 'A'), NOT factor IX; mixing up A=VIII vs B=IX is the most basic and most tested distinction — mnemonic: A comes first alphabetically and is the more common/8.
4. Factor IX = Hemophilia B
Hemophilia B (Christmas disease) = factor IX deficiency, ~1 in 30,000 males, also X-linked recessive. It is clinically INDISTINGUISHABLE from hemophilia A — same hemarthrosis, same deep bleeds, same prolonged aPTT with normal PT — so only a specific factor assay can tell them apart.
⚠️ Hemophilia B = factor IX (Christmas disease), not VIII; you CANNOT distinguish A from B by history, exam or routine coags — the board answer when asked to differentiate is 'specific factor VIII and IX activity assays.' Also note DDAVP does not work in B.
5. X-linked recessive
Both hemophilia A and B are X-linked recessive. Affected fathers cannot pass it to sons (sons get the Y) but all daughters become obligate carriers; carrier mothers pass it to ~50% of sons (affected) and ~50% of daughters (carriers). Rarely, carrier females bleed due to lyonization (skewed X-inactivation).
⚠️ Hemophilia is X-LINKED RECESSIVE, not autosomal — the absence of male-to-male transmission and presence of affected maternal uncles is the pedigree giveaway; females are usually asymptomatic carriers but can be symptomatic with extreme lyonization.
6. Prolonged aPTT, normal PT
The lab signature of hemophilia is an isolated prolonged aPTT with a NORMAL PT, normal platelet count, and normal bleeding time/PFA. A mixing study CORRECTS (1:1 mix with normal plasma normalizes the aPTT), confirming a factor DEFICIENCY rather than an inhibitor. PT stays normal because FVIII/FIX are intrinsic-pathway factors.
⚠️ PT is NORMAL in hemophilia — an abnormal PT points elsewhere (DIC, liver disease, vitamin K deficiency, warfarin); and if the mixing study does NOT correct, suspect an inhibitor (acquired hemophilia or alloantibody), not simple deficiency.
7. Severity by factor level
Severity is graded by residual factor activity: <1% = severe (spontaneous hemarthroses, frequent bleeds), 1–5% = moderate (bleeds with minor trauma), >5–40% = mild (bleeds only with significant trauma or surgery). This is why severe disease presents in infancy and mild disease may go unrecognized for decades.
⚠️ Mild hemophilia (>5% activity) can have an aPTT that looks nearly normal and may first present only at surgery, dental extraction or major trauma — a normal-appearing screening aPTT does not exclude mild hemophilia; a strong bleeding history warrants factor assays.
8. ~30% de novo mutations
About one-third of hemophilia cases (~30%) arise from NEW (de novo) spontaneous mutations with no prior family history. The most common severe FVIII mutation is the intron 22 inversion. Thus a negative family history is common and does not argue against the diagnosis.
⚠️ Absence of a family history does NOT rule out hemophilia — roughly 30% are de novo mutations; do not dismiss a male infant with hemarthrosis/circumcision bleeding because 'no one in the family has it.'
9. vWF protects FVIII
Von Willebrand factor is the plasma carrier that binds and stabilizes FVIII; free (unbound) FVIII has a half-life of only ~12 hours, but bound to vWF it is protected from clearance. Consequently, in von Willebrand disease (especially type 2N and severe type 3), FVIII is also low because its chaperone is missing.
⚠️ Always EXCLUDE von Willebrand disease before labeling an isolated low FVIII as hemophilia A — vWD is far more common, can lower FVIII, and (unlike hemophilia) shows mucocutaneous bleeding and abnormal vWF/ristocetin testing; type 2N vWD in particular masquerades as mild hemophilia A.
10. FVIII dosing rule
FVIII dosing: dose (IU) ≈ weight(kg) × 0.5 × (target% − baseline%), because 1 IU/kg of FVIII raises the plasma level by ~2%. FVIII half-life is ~8–12h, so it is dosed roughly twice daily for ongoing bleeds. Targets: ~30–50% for minor bleeds, ~50–80% for major, ~80–100% for life-threatening/CNS bleeds or major surgery.
⚠️ The multipliers differ between factors — FVIII uses the ×0.5 factor (1 IU/kg → ~2% rise); applying the FIX dosing constant to FVIII (or vice versa) under- or over-doses the patient. Know that FVIII raises level twice as efficiently per IU/kg as FIX.
11. FIX raises ~1% per IU/kg
FIX dosing: dose (IU) ≈ weight(kg) × 1 × (target% − baseline%), because 1 IU/kg of FIX raises the level only ~1% — FIX has a larger volume of distribution (binds collagen/endothelium) so you need roughly DOUBLE the units per kg compared with FVIII. FIX half-life is longer (~18–24h), allowing once-daily dosing.
⚠️ Do NOT use the FVIII multiplier for factor IX — FIX needs about twice the dose per kg for the same percentage rise because of its larger distribution volume; this dosing-constant swap is a favorite calculation trap.
12. Desmopressin (DDAVP) mild A
Desmopressin (DDAVP) triggers release of stored FVIII and vWF from endothelial Weibel-Palade bodies, transiently raising FVIII ~2–4 fold — useful for MILD (and some moderate) hemophilia A and type 1 vWD. It avoids blood-product exposure. Tachyphylaxis develops after repeated doses (stores deplete), and watch for hyponatremia/fluid retention.
⚠️ DDAVP works ONLY in mild/moderate hemophilia A — it does NOTHING in hemophilia B (no FIX to release) and is ineffective in SEVERE hemophilia A (there is essentially no FVIII to release); offering DDAVP for a severe-A or any-B bleed is wrong.
13. Carrier / pregnancy FVIII rises
Pregnancy physiologically raises FVIII and vWF 2–3 fold (estrogen effect), which can normalize the FVIII of a mild-hemophilia-A carrier and even an affected woman during gestation, providing hemostatic cover for delivery. After delivery these levels fall rapidly, creating a postpartum/secondary hemorrhage danger window.
⚠️ Normal FVIII levels during pregnancy do NOT exclude carrier status or mild hemophilia A — measure at baseline (non-pregnant) and remember the postpartum drop predisposes to delayed bleeding; reassure-and-discharge based on a third-trimester FVIII is a trap.
