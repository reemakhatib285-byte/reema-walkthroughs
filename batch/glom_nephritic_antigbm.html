📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Anti-GBM / Goodpasture
Anti-GBM disease is caused by autoantibodies directed at the glomerular basement membrane, producing a rapidly progressive (crescentic) nephritic glomerulonephritis. When the same antibodies also attack the alveolar basement membrane and cause pulmonary hemorrhage, the syndrome is called Goodpasture's. It is one of the three classic causes of RPGN (pulmonary-renal syndrome), alongside ANCA vasculitis and immune-complex GN. Think 'linear ribbon' on IF as the single highest-yield discriminator.
2. Anti-α3 NC1 collagen IV
The antibody (an IgG) targets the noncollagenous (NC1) domain of the α3 chain of type IV collagen — the 'Goodpasture antigen.' This α3 epitope is normally hidden within the collagen network and only certain basement membranes (glomerular and alveolar) express it richly, explaining the kidney-lung specificity. Diagnosis is confirmed serologically by anti-GBM antibody ELISA plus the linear IgG biopsy pattern. Mnemonic: 'GBM = Goodpasture, Basement membrane, Mac (type IV) collagen α3.'
3. Shared lung + kidney antigen
The α3-NC1 collagen IV antigen is shared between alveolar and glomerular basement membranes, so a single autoantibody can strike both organs — the basis of the 'pulmonary-renal syndrome.' This is why a patient can present with both hemoptysis and hematuria/AKI simultaneously. Other classic pulmonary-renal syndromes to keep on the differential are GPA, MPA, and lupus.
4. Hemoptysis / lung
Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage produces hemoptysis, dyspnea, and bilateral infiltrates; only the lung-plus-kidney form is termed Goodpasture's, whereas kidney-only disease is 'anti-GBM disease.' Lung involvement is much more likely in smokers and younger men and can be life-threatening from massive bleeding. DLCO is paradoxically INCREASED in alveolar hemorrhage because intra-alveolar blood binds inhaled CO — a classic board pearl.
⚠️ DLCO is decreased in alveolar hemorrhage (it is actually elevated because alveolar blood takes up the CO)
5. Smoking unmasks epitopes
Inhaled oxidants — cigarette smoke, hydrocarbon solvents, cocaine — injure the alveolar membrane and expose the normally sequestered α3-NC1 epitope, allowing circulating antibody to bind and trigger pulmonary hemorrhage. This is why pulmonary involvement clusters in smokers while non-smokers more often have renal-limited disease. Counseling smoking cessation is part of management.
6. Linear IgG IF
Immunofluorescence shows smooth, continuous LINEAR IgG deposition along the entire GBM — the pathognomonic finding because the antibody binds an evenly distributed intrinsic antigen rather than depositing as discrete complexes. This linear 'ribbon' pattern is the single best discriminator from immune-complex GN. The only other classic cause of linear IgG staining is diabetic nephropathy (nonspecific trapping), but there the clinical picture differs entirely.
⚠️ Granular 'lumpy-bumpy' deposits — that pattern indicates immune-complex GN (PSGN, lupus, endocarditis), not anti-GBM, which is smooth and linear
7. Crescents / RPGN
Severe GBM injury ruptures capillary walls, allowing fibrin and macrophages to spill into Bowman's space and form cellular crescents — the hallmark of crescentic GN, which clinically manifests as RPGN (creatinine doubling over days to weeks). Anti-GBM is the Type I (anti-GBM antibody) pattern of RPGN; Type II is immune-complex and Type III is pauci-immune/ANCA. Crescents demand urgent biopsy and treatment.
8. >50% crescents = worse
Poor renal-prognosis markers are oliguria, creatinine above roughly 5–6 mg/dL (or dialysis-dependence at presentation), and crescents in more than 50% of glomeruli. Patients who are already dialysis-dependent with widespread crescents rarely recover renal function even with aggressive therapy. However, pulmonary hemorrhage is still treated urgently regardless of renal salvageability because it is immediately life-threatening.
9. Normal complement
Serum complement (C3/C4) is NORMAL in anti-GBM disease because the pathology is direct antibody binding to an intrinsic antigen, not deposition of complement-consuming immune complexes. Contrast with the LOW-complement nephritic causes: PSGN, lupus, endocarditis-associated GN, and MPGN. Normal complement + linear IgG + anti-GBM titer nails the diagnosis.
⚠️ Low C3 — complement is normal here; low complement instead points to PSGN, lupus, endocarditis, or MPGN
10. Plasmapheresis
Urgent plasmapheresis (plasma exchange) physically removes circulating anti-GBM antibody and is the cornerstone of induction — typically daily or alternate-day sessions for about 2–3 weeks (roughly 8–14 exchanges) until antibody titers fall and hemorrhage stops. It is the one feature that distinguishes anti-GBM management from most other GN: you must rapidly clear the offending antibody. Start it immediately on strong clinical suspicion, even before biopsy, especially with pulmonary hemorrhage.
11. Immunosuppression
Plasmapheresis is paired with immunosuppression to stop new antibody production: high-dose corticosteroids (e.g., IV methylprednisolone pulse 500–1000 mg/day x3, then oral prednisone ~1 mg/kg/day taper) plus cyclophosphamide (oral ~2 mg/kg/day or IV pulses). Rituximab/MMF are used in selected or relapsing cases. The triad — plasmapheresis + steroids + cyclophosphamide — is the high-yield induction regimen. Unlike many GN, relapse is uncommon once antibodies clear, so prolonged maintenance is usually unnecessary.
12. Bimodal age
Anti-GBM disease has a bimodal age distribution: a young-adult peak (20s–30s, more often men) who present with combined lung-plus-kidney Goodpasture's, and an older peak (60s–70s) who more often have renal-limited, isolated kidney disease. Smoking status largely drives whether the lungs are involved. Recognizing the demographic helps anchor a vignette.
13. Antibody clears before transplant
Before kidney transplantation, confirm that anti-GBM antibody titers have been undetectable for at least ~6–12 months to avoid recurrent disease in the allograft. Transplanting too early, while antibody is still circulating, risks immediate recurrence against the new GBM. This is a favorite exam distinction from other GN where timing of transplant is less antibody-driven.
