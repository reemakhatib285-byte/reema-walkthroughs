📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. ADAMTS13 deficiency
TTP is caused by severely deficient ADAMTS13 activity (<10%). ADAMTS13 is the metalloprotease that normally cleaves ultra-large von Willebrand factor (UL-vWF) multimers as they are released from endothelium. Without it, UL-vWF strings persist, bind and clump platelets in high-shear microvasculature, and form platelet-rich microthrombi — driving consumption and mechanical RBC shearing.
⚠️ A microangiopathy with NORMAL ADAMTS13 activity (>10%) is NOT TTP — think HUS (Shiga-toxin or complement-mediated), DIC, malignant hypertension, or drug-induced TMA instead. The discriminator the boards love: ADAMTS13 <10% defines TTP; preserved ADAMTS13 with a diarrheal prodrome and prominent renal failure in a child points to HUS.
2. Acquired (autoantibody)
Most TTP (especially in adults) is ACQUIRED/immune: an IgG AUTOANTIBODY (inhibitor) against ADAMTS13 neutralizes the enzyme. This is why immunosuppression (steroids, rituximab) is part of treatment in addition to plasma exchange. It peaks in adults, with a female predominance.
⚠️ Assuming all TTP is inherited is the trap — the congenital form (Upshaw-Schulman, ADAMTS13 gene mutation) is rare and presents in neonates/children or in pregnancy. In a previously healthy ADULT presenting acutely, the answer is ACQUIRED autoantibody-mediated TTP, treated with PLEX plus immunosuppression — not gene therapy or plasma infusion alone.
3. Triggers / second hit
Acute TTP often needs a 'second hit' to unmask it: common triggers include HIV (always test), pregnancy/postpartum, certain drugs, infection, and autoimmune disease. The congenital form (Upshaw-Schulman) is similarly precipitated by pregnancy or illness because baseline ADAMTS13 is already low.
⚠️ Missing HIV as a precipitant/association of TTP is a classic omission — every new TTP gets an HIV test. The trap stem describes a young patient with TTP and risk factors but you forget to test HIV; in pregnancy, the mirror trap is reflexively calling microangiopathy 'TTP' when HELLP or preeclampsia may better fit (check LFTs, BP, ADAMTS13).
4. Fever
Fever is the 'F' of the classic pentad (FAT RN = Fever, Anemia [MAHA], Thrombocytopenia, Renal, Neurologic). It is the LEAST consistent feature and is frequently absent. Its presence supports TTP but its absence absolutely does not exclude it.
⚠️ Requiring fever (or any specific pentad member) before diagnosing/treating TTP is the trap. Fever is often missing; demanding it before starting plasma exchange delays life-saving therapy. The threshold to act is MAHA + thrombocytopenia without another explanation — not the complete FAT RN.
5. MAHA anemia
The 'A' = microangiopathic hemolytic anemia: RBCs are physically sheared as they squeeze past platelet-vWF microthrombi, producing SCHISTOCYTES on the smear plus hemolysis labs (high LDH, low haptoglobin, high indirect bilirubin, elevated reticulocytes) and a NEGATIVE Coombs.
⚠️ Calling a Coombs-POSITIVE hemolytic anemia 'TTP' is wrong — TTP hemolysis is mechanical and therefore Coombs/DAT NEGATIVE. A positive Coombs points to autoimmune hemolysis (warm AIHA, or Evans syndrome when paired with ITP), a completely different management pathway (steroids, not PLEX).
6. Thrombocytopenia
The 'T' = consumptive thrombocytopenia: platelets are used up forming the diffuse microthrombi, so counts fall (often profoundly, frequently <30,000). Importantly, this consumption occurs with NORMAL coagulation times — unlike DIC, the clotting cascade is not driving it.
⚠️ Interpreting TTP thrombocytopenia as a clotting-cascade consumptive coagulopathy (DIC) is the trap. In TTP the PT/aPTT/fibrinogen are NORMAL; if those are deranged with low fibrinogen and high D-dimer, the answer is DIC, not TTP. The platelet drop in TTP is from microthrombotic consumption with an intact cascade.
7. Renal + neuro
'R' = renal (usually MILD-to-MODERATE in TTP) and 'N' = neurologic — fluctuating, often dramatic findings: headache, confusion, focal deficits, seizures, or coma from CNS microthrombi. In TTP neuro tends to dominate while renal is comparatively mild.
⚠️ The organ-emphasis discriminator: SEVERE renal failure with only mild/absent neuro signs (especially a child after bloody diarrhea) favors HUS, whereas prominent NEURO findings with milder renal involvement favor TTP. Choosing HUS for a neuro-dominant adult or TTP for a renal-dominant post-diarrheal child is the planted swap.
8. Pentad rare
Fewer than ~25% of TTP patients ever show the FULL pentad. The modern, exam-correct rule is that unexplained MAHA + thrombocytopenia (a dyad) is sufficient to presume TTP and START plasma exchange — you do not wait for the other three elements to appear.
⚠️ Waiting for the complete pentad before initiating therapy is the cardinal management error in TTP. Because untreated TTP mortality approaches ~90% and treated is ~10–20%, the right answer is empiric PLEX on the MAHA + thrombocytopenia dyad. Holding treatment 'until more criteria are met' is wrong and lethal.
9. TTP/HUS vs DIC coags
Key lab fork: in TTP/HUS the PT, aPTT, and fibrinogen are NORMAL (and D-dimer normal/mildly up) because the cascade is not the driver. In DIC the PT and aPTT are PROLONGED, fibrinogen is LOW, and D-dimer is markedly elevated. So normal coags + MAHA + low platelets = TTP/HUS; deranged coags = DIC.
⚠️ The wrong answer treats abnormal coagulation studies as compatible with TTP. Prolonged PT/aPTT, low fibrinogen, and very high D-dimer in a thrombocytopenic patient with schistocytes mean DIC (look for sepsis, malignancy, obstetric catastrophe) — and DIC management (treat the cause, replace factors) differs entirely from PLEX for TTP.
10. Microthrombi
The core lesion is widespread PLATELET-RICH (vWF-rich, fibrin-poor) microthrombi in the small vessels of brain, kidney, heart, and other organs. These both consume platelets (thrombocytopenia) and obstruct/shear flowing RBCs (MAHA), explaining the whole syndrome from one mechanism.
⚠️ Confusing TTP microthrombi with the fibrin-rich, cascade-driven clots of DIC is the trap. TTP clots are platelet/vWF-rich with a NORMAL fibrinogen, so anticoagulation and factor replacement are not the treatment — PLEX is. Treating TTP as if it were DIC (heparin, FFP for factor repletion only) misses the real fix.
11. Schistocytes = MAHA
SCHISTOCYTES (helmet cells, fragmented triangular RBCs) on the peripheral smear are the visual signature of mechanical RBC shearing through microthrombi = microangiopathic hemolytic anemia. Their presence with thrombocytopenia is the trigger to think TTP/HUS/DIC.
⚠️ Schistocytes are NOT a feature of ITP — if a thrombocytopenia stem shows fragmented red cells, you are out of ITP and into a microangiopathy (TTP/HUS/DIC). The trap is to keep an ITP label once schistocytes appear; conversely, their absence with isolated low platelets and large platelets points back toward ITP.
12. Don't wait — start PLEX
Action rule: the moment you have unexplained MAHA + thrombocytopenia, START PLASMA EXCHANGE EMPIRICALLY — do not wait for ADAMTS13 results, neuro signs, or the full pentad. Send ADAMTS13 BEFORE the first exchange (so the result is interpretable) but never let the draw delay therapy. Add steroids; consider caplacizumab/rituximab.
⚠️ Delaying PLEX to wait for the ADAMTS13 activity level to return is the classic fatal mistake — the assay takes days while untreated TTP kills. The correct sequence: draw ADAMTS13 first, then immediately begin empiric plasma exchange. 'Wait for confirmatory testing' is always the wrong TTP answer.
13. Haptoglobin / LDH / bili
Hemolysis lab panel in TTP: haptoglobin LOW (consumed binding free hemoglobin), LDH HIGH (released from lysed RBCs and ischemic tissue — also tracks disease activity/response), and INDIRECT bilirubin HIGH (heme breakdown), with reticulocytosis. LDH is a useful marker to follow treatment response.
⚠️ Don't dismiss TTP because one hemolysis marker looks unimpressive — the pattern (low haptoglobin + high LDH + high indirect bili + schistocytes) is what matters, and a markedly high LDH may reflect both hemolysis and tissue ischemia. Attributing the high indirect bilirubin to liver disease and overlooking hemolysis is the distractor.
14. Coombs negative
The direct Coombs (DAT) is NEGATIVE in TTP because the hemolysis is mechanical (shear), not antibody-mediated. A negative Coombs in a hemolytic, thrombocytopenic patient with schistocytes supports a microangiopathy (TTP/HUS) and excludes autoimmune hemolytic anemia.
⚠️ A POSITIVE Coombs steers you AWAY from TTP toward autoimmune hemolysis — warm AIHA, or Evans syndrome if combined with immune thrombocytopenia (which is treated with steroids/IVIG, not PLEX). The trap is diagnosing TTP despite a positive DAT; in TTP the Coombs must be negative.
