📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. STEP 1: Check TSH
Every thyroid nodule workup begins with serum TSH — it is the single test that branches the entire algorithm. TSH plus a dedicated thyroid ultrasound are the only mandatory first steps. Calcitonin is checked only if medullary carcinoma/MEN2 is suspected; thyroglobulin has no role in initial diagnosis.
⚠️ Jumping straight to FNA or imaging without a TSH — you cannot decide between an uptake scan and US/FNA without it.
2. Do NOT FNA before TSH
TSH first because a LOW TSH redirects you to a radionuclide uptake scan (a hot nodule is essentially never cancer and should not be biopsied), whereas a normal/high TSH sends you to ultrasound and possible FNA. Biopsying first wastes a procedure and can yield indeterminate cytology on what is actually a benign autonomous nodule.
⚠️ FNA before checking TSH — risks an unnecessary biopsy of a hot (benign autonomous) nodule.
3. Low TSH → radionuclide scan
Low/suppressed TSH suggests an autonomously functioning nodule, so the next step is a radioiodine (I-123) or technetium uptake scan to map function. A focal 'hot' area that suppresses the rest of the gland is a toxic adenoma; multiple hot areas = toxic MNG.
4. Hot nodule = never cancer
A hyperfunctioning ('hot') nodule is malignant in well under 1% of cases — effectively never cancer — so it does NOT need FNA. Manage the hyperthyroidism instead (RAI ablation or surgery; thionamides as a bridge). Only cold/hypofunctioning nodules carry meaningful cancer risk and warrant cytologic evaluation.
⚠️ Performing FNA of a hot nodule — unnecessary; treat the hyperthyroidism rather than biopsy.
5. Cold nodule → US ± FNA
A cold (non-functioning) nodule on uptake scan accounts for ~5–15% malignancy risk, so it is characterized by ultrasound and biopsied per sonographic risk + size. Note: most nodules in the population are cold, and most cold nodules are still benign — risk stratification by US (TI-RADS) prevents over-biopsy.
6. Normal/high TSH → US/TI-RADS
A normal or high TSH means the nodule is not autonomous — a uptake scan is pointless (it would not be hot), so go directly to diagnostic ultrasound and apply TI-RADS risk stratification to decide on FNA. A high TSH also raises suspicion for underlying Hashimoto's.
⚠️ Ordering a radionuclide uptake scan when TSH is normal/high — wasteful; the nodule won't be hot, so US is the correct next test.
7. TI-RADS scoring + size cutoffs
ACR TI-RADS assigns points for composition, echogenicity, shape, margin, and echogenic foci to give TR1 (benign) through TR5 (highly suspicious). FNA thresholds: TR1–2 no FNA; TR3 FNA if ≥2.5 cm (follow ≥1.5 cm); TR4 FNA if ≥1.5 cm (follow ≥1 cm); TR5 FNA if ≥1 cm (follow ≥0.5 cm). Higher suspicion → smaller size triggers biopsy.
⚠️ Memorizing one universal size cutoff — the FNA threshold falls as TI-RADS category rises, so size alone is never the rule.
8. FNA = risk + size, not size alone
The FNA decision integrates sonographic suspicion AND size: a small but highly suspicious (TR5) nodule may be biopsied at ≥1 cm, while a large spongiform (TR1–2) nodule may never be. Subcentimeter nodules (<1 cm) are generally not routinely aspirated unless there are clinical red flags (suspicious nodes, extrathyroidal extension).
⚠️ Biopsying every nodule ≥1 cm regardless of features — over-biopsies benign nodules; conversely don't ignore a <1 cm TR5 nodule with suspicious nodes.
9. Spongiform/cystic = benign
A spongiform pattern (aggregation of microcystic spaces in >50% of the nodule) or a purely cystic/anechoic nodule is essentially always benign (TR1) and does not require FNA. These reassuring features are high-yield for distinguishing benign colloid nodules from solid hypoechoic cancers.
10. High-risk US features
The classic malignant sonographic signs (each adds TI-RADS points): marked hypoechogenicity, irregular/lobulated/spiculated margins, microcalcifications (punctate echogenic foci — most specific, suggest psammoma bodies of papillary cancer), taller-than-wide shape on transverse view, extrathyroidal extension, and abnormal/suspicious cervical lymph nodes.
⚠️ Calling coarse/macrocalcifications or comet-tail artifact 'suspicious' — those are lower-risk; microcalcifications are the worrisome finding.
11. Pemberton sign
Pemberton's sign: raising both arms above the head causes facial plethora/congestion, cyanosis, distended neck veins, and stridor/dyspnea — produced by a large substernal goiter obstructing the thoracic inlet ('cork in a bottle'). It signals retrosternal extension and the need for non-contrast CT to map the goiter and assess airway compression.
12. CT contrast contraindicated
Iodinated IV contrast delivers a large iodine load that can trigger Jod-Basedow (iodine-induced hyperthyroidism/storm) in an autonomous nodular goiter AND saturates the gland, blocking any planned radioiodine scan or RAI therapy for weeks to months. Image a substernal goiter with NON-contrast CT.
⚠️ Ordering contrast-enhanced CT in autonomous/nodular goiter — risks Jod-Basedow and delays RAI/uptake imaging by saturating the gland.
13. Risk-factor red flags
Clinical malignancy red flags lowering the threshold to biopsy/refer: childhood head-neck radiation, family history of thyroid cancer or MEN2/MTC, extremes of age (<20 or >65, esp. male), hoarseness/vocal-cord paralysis (recurrent laryngeal nerve invasion), a firm/fixed nodule, fixed lateral neck mass/adenopathy, and rapid growth (rapid growth in an older patient also raises anaplastic carcinoma or lymphoma).
14. PET-avid nodule
An incidentally discovered FOCAL FDG-PET-avid thyroid nodule carries a substantial malignancy risk (~30–35%) and should be evaluated with ultrasound and FNA. By contrast, DIFFUSE FDG uptake throughout the gland usually reflects chronic (Hashimoto's) thyroiditis and is not concerning.
⚠️ Treating diffuse PET uptake the same as focal — diffuse uptake is thyroiditis; only focal uptake demands FNA.
