⚠️ Board traps 15
  1. Uosm >100, low uric acid, euvolemic — WRONG: SIADH causes hypoVOLEMIC hyponatremia. Discriminator — SIADH is EUVOLEMIC; the retained water distributes across total body water without edema or signs of depletion. Hypovolemic hyponatremia (vomiting, diuretics) has low urine Na (<20) when the cause is extrarenal — the opposite of SIADH's high urine Na.
  2. Euvolemic / normal volume — WRONG: SIADH presents with edema and volume overload. Discriminator — edematous hyponatremia is HYPERvolemic (CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome) with low urine Na from neurohormonal avidity; SIADH is euvolemic with HIGH urine Na. Seeing edema should steer you away from SIADH.
  3. Uosm over 100 — WRONG: the urine is maximally dilute (Uosm <100) in SIADH. Discriminator — maximally dilute urine in hypotonic hyponatremia points to primary polydipsia or low solute intake (beer potomania/tea-and-toast), NOT SIADH; SIADH's defining feature is FAILURE to dilute (Uosm >100).
  4. Urine Na over 30 — WRONG: urine sodium is low (<20) in SIADH. Discriminator — low urine Na (<20) indicates a volume-depleted/avid state (true hypovolemia, CHF, cirrhosis); SIADH's euvolemia leaves urine Na HIGH (>30). (Caveat: very low dietary salt intake can lower it.)
  5. Uric acid less than 4 — WRONG: a HIGH serum uric acid supports SIADH. Discriminator — SIADH characteristically gives LOW uric acid from volume-expansion-driven urate clearance; a high uric acid suggests a volume-contracted state instead. Hypouricemia is one of the supporting clues, not hyperuricemia.
  6. Low serum osmolality (hypotonic) — WRONG: SIADH has a high serum osmolality. Discriminator — high measured serum osm with hyponatremia indicates a hypertonic cause (hyperglycemia, mannitol) pulling water out of cells, not SIADH; SIADH is by definition HYPOtonic (low serum osm).
  7. No copeptin diagnostic value — WRONG: a high copeptin (or AVP) level confirms SIADH. Discriminator — AVP/copeptin can be elevated in many hyponatremias (volume depletion, CHF) and is not specific; SIADH is diagnosed by the euvolemic biochemical pattern and exclusion of other causes, not by an AVP level.
  8. CNS causes — WRONG: CNS bleeds cause cerebral salt wasting only and never SIADH. Discriminator — intracranial disease causes BOTH; SIADH is euvolemic/hypervolemic (UNa high, treat with fluid restriction) while cerebral salt wasting is HYPOvolemic (also UNa high, but treat with salt and volume). Volume status, not urine Na, separates them.
  9. Pulmonary causes — WRONG: only malignant lung disease (not infection) causes SIADH. Discriminator — benign pulmonary infections (pneumonia, TB, abscess) are common SIADH triggers, separate from ectopic AVP production by small cell lung CANCER; both lung infection and lung cancer belong on the SIADH causes list.
  10. SCLC / malignancy — WRONG: SQUAMOUS cell lung cancer is the classic SIADH tumor. Discriminator — SMALL cell lung cancer is the paraneoplastic ectopic-ADH producer (and also the classic ectopic-ACTH producer); squamous cell lung cancer is instead the classic cause of paraneoplastic HYPERcalcemia (PTHrP).
  11. Drugs: SSRI / carbamazepine — WRONG: SSRIs and carbamazepine do not cause hyponatremia. Discriminator — both are well-known SIADH drugs (SSRIs notably in older patients); a new hyponatremia after starting an SSRI, carbamazepine, or a thiazide should trigger a med review before invasive workup.
  12. Fluid restriction (first-line) — WRONG: first-line treatment for all SIADH is hypertonic saline. Discriminator — 3% saline is reserved for SEVERE symptomatic (seizure/coma) hyponatremia; routine chronic/asymptomatic SIADH is managed with fluid restriction ± salt/urea, not hypertonic saline.
  13. Vaptans / urea — WRONG: vaptans (tolvaptan/conivaptan) are V1 receptor AGONISTS. Discriminator — vaptans are V2 ANTAGONISTS that block AVP's renal water-reabsorption action to produce a free-water aquaresis; calling them V1 agonists inverts both the receptor and the direction of effect.
  14. 3% hypertonic saline (severe) — WRONG: give hypertonic saline rapidly to fully normalize the sodium. Discriminator — the goal is a small, controlled rise (~4–6 mmol/L) to break severe symptoms, NOT normalization; correcting too fast/too far risks osmotic demyelination. Hypertonic saline is rescue therapy, not a means to a normal Na in one sitting.
  15. Correction rate limit / ODS — WRONG: chronic hyponatremia should be corrected as fast as possible. Discriminator — rapid correction of CHRONIC hyponatremia causes osmotic demyelination; keep the rise <8 mmol/L/24h. Only the initial relief of severe symptoms is brisk (~4–6 mmol/L), after which the daily ceiling governs.
📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Uosm >100, low uric acid, euvolemic
SIADH (syndrome of inappropriate antidiuresis) is EUVOLEMIC hypotonic hyponatremia caused by AVP secretion that persists despite low plasma osmolality. The diagnostic triad: serum osm low (<275), urine inappropriately concentrated (Uosm >100), and urine Na high (>30–40) in a clinically euvolemic patient, supported by low serum uric acid (<4) and normal thyroid/adrenal axes. It is a diagnosis of exclusion.
⚠️ WRONG: SIADH causes hypoVOLEMIC hyponatremia. Discriminator — SIADH is EUVOLEMIC; the retained water distributes across total body water without edema or signs of depletion. Hypovolemic hyponatremia (vomiting, diuretics) has low urine Na (<20) when the cause is extrarenal — the opposite of SIADH's high urine Na.
2. Euvolemic / normal volume
SIADH patients are clinically EUVOLEMIC — no edema, no JVD, no orthostasis, no signs of dehydration. Water retention triggers a transient natriuresis (pressure/volume-mediated) that prevents true volume overload, so the excess water is dilutional rather than third-spaced. Volume status on exam is the key bedside step in classifying hyponatremia.
⚠️ WRONG: SIADH presents with edema and volume overload. Discriminator — edematous hyponatremia is HYPERvolemic (CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome) with low urine Na from neurohormonal avidity; SIADH is euvolemic with HIGH urine Na. Seeing edema should steer you away from SIADH.
3. Uosm over 100
In SIADH urine osmolality is INAPPROPRIATELY high (>100, frequently >300) even though serum is hypotonic — the hallmark of unsuppressed AVP. A normally functioning kidney facing hypotonicity would maximally dilute the urine to <100, so a Uosm above 100 in a hypotonic patient signals continued ADH action.
⚠️ WRONG: the urine is maximally dilute (Uosm <100) in SIADH. Discriminator — maximally dilute urine in hypotonic hyponatremia points to primary polydipsia or low solute intake (beer potomania/tea-and-toast), NOT SIADH; SIADH's defining feature is FAILURE to dilute (Uosm >100).
4. Urine Na over 30
Urine sodium is high (>30–40 mmol/L) in SIADH because the patient is euvolemic — there is no hypovolemic stimulus for the kidney to avidly retain sodium, so it excretes Na normally. This separates SIADH from hypovolemic hyponatremia, where extrarenal losses drive urine Na <20.
⚠️ WRONG: urine sodium is low (<20) in SIADH. Discriminator — low urine Na (<20) indicates a volume-depleted/avid state (true hypovolemia, CHF, cirrhosis); SIADH's euvolemia leaves urine Na HIGH (>30). (Caveat: very low dietary salt intake can lower it.)
5. Uric acid less than 4
Low serum uric acid (<4 mg/dL) supports SIADH: mild volume expansion increases renal urate clearance (and dilution lowers concentration), so hypouricemia plus a high fractional excretion of urate is a useful confirmatory clue. Cerebral salt wasting can also lower uric acid, but it differs by being HYPOvolemic.
⚠️ WRONG: a HIGH serum uric acid supports SIADH. Discriminator — SIADH characteristically gives LOW uric acid from volume-expansion-driven urate clearance; a high uric acid suggests a volume-contracted state instead. Hypouricemia is one of the supporting clues, not hyperuricemia.
6. Low serum osmolality (hypotonic)
SIADH is a HYPOtonic (true) hyponatremia — measured serum osmolality is low (<275 mOsm/kg). Before labeling it SIADH you must exclude pseudohyponatremia (severe hyperlipidemia/hyperproteinemia, which gives a normal measured osm) and hypertonic hyponatremia from hyperglycemia or mannitol (high measured osm). Confirming low measured serum osm is step one.
⚠️ WRONG: SIADH has a high serum osmolality. Discriminator — high measured serum osm with hyponatremia indicates a hypertonic cause (hyperglycemia, mannitol) pulling water out of cells, not SIADH; SIADH is by definition HYPOtonic (low serum osm).
7. No copeptin diagnostic value
Measuring plasma AVP or copeptin does NOT diagnose SIADH — levels overlap with other hyponatremias and add nothing, so SIADH remains a clinical/biochemical diagnosis of exclusion (euvolemia + Uosm >100 + UNa >30 + normal thyroid/adrenal/renal function). This is the opposite of DI, where copeptin is central to typing.
⚠️ WRONG: a high copeptin (or AVP) level confirms SIADH. Discriminator — AVP/copeptin can be elevated in many hyponatremias (volume depletion, CHF) and is not specific; SIADH is diagnosed by the euvolemic biochemical pattern and exclusion of other causes, not by an AVP level.
8. CNS causes
SIADH causes — CNS disease: ischemic/hemorrhagic stroke, subarachnoid hemorrhage, meningitis/encephalitis, head trauma, brain tumor, and Guillain-Barré. These central insults inappropriately stimulate hypothalamic AVP release. SAH/intracranial disease also classically raises the differential of cerebral salt wasting, which must be distinguished by volume status.
⚠️ WRONG: CNS bleeds cause cerebral salt wasting only and never SIADH. Discriminator — intracranial disease causes BOTH; SIADH is euvolemic/hypervolemic (UNa high, treat with fluid restriction) while cerebral salt wasting is HYPOvolemic (also UNa high, but treat with salt and volume). Volume status, not urine Na, separates them.
9. Pulmonary causes
SIADH causes — pulmonary disease: pneumonia (especially Legionella), tuberculosis, lung abscess, and positive-pressure mechanical ventilation. Inflamed/diseased lung tissue and raised intrathoracic pressure stimulate AVP release; hyponatremia in a pneumonia patient often reflects SIADH and tends to resolve as the infection treats.
⚠️ WRONG: only malignant lung disease (not infection) causes SIADH. Discriminator — benign pulmonary infections (pneumonia, TB, abscess) are common SIADH triggers, separate from ectopic AVP production by small cell lung CANCER; both lung infection and lung cancer belong on the SIADH causes list.
10. SCLC / malignancy
SIADH causes — malignancy: SMALL CELL lung carcinoma is the classic ectopic-AVP producer (a paraneoplastic syndrome); also head and neck cancers, and others. Persistent unexplained SIADH in a smoker should prompt evaluation for occult small cell lung cancer.
⚠️ WRONG: SQUAMOUS cell lung cancer is the classic SIADH tumor. Discriminator — SMALL cell lung cancer is the paraneoplastic ectopic-ADH producer (and also the classic ectopic-ACTH producer); squamous cell lung cancer is instead the classic cause of paraneoplastic HYPERcalcemia (PTHrP).
11. Drugs: SSRI / carbamazepine
SIADH causes — drugs: SSRIs (especially in the elderly), carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine, cyclophosphamide (and vincristine), antipsychotics, NSAIDs (potentiate AVP by blocking opposing prostaglandins), thiazides, and MDMA/ecstasy. Drug-induced SIADH usually improves on stopping the offending agent; review the med list in any new hyponatremia.
⚠️ WRONG: SSRIs and carbamazepine do not cause hyponatremia. Discriminator — both are well-known SIADH drugs (SSRIs notably in older patients); a new hyponatremia after starting an SSRI, carbamazepine, or a thiazide should trigger a med review before invasive workup.
12. Fluid restriction (first-line)
First-line treatment of chronic/asymptomatic SIADH is FLUID RESTRICTION (commonly <800–1000 mL/day, and less than urine output). If inadequate, add oral salt tablets and/or oral urea, or a loop diuretic. Treat the underlying cause and stop offending drugs. Restriction is less effective when urine osm is very high (>500) or UNa+UK exceeds serum Na.
⚠️ WRONG: first-line treatment for all SIADH is hypertonic saline. Discriminator — 3% saline is reserved for SEVERE symptomatic (seizure/coma) hyponatremia; routine chronic/asymptomatic SIADH is managed with fluid restriction ± salt/urea, not hypertonic saline.
13. Vaptans / urea
Second-line options promote free-water (aquaretic) excretion: vaptans — tolvaptan (oral) and conivaptan (IV) — are V2-receptor ANTAGONISTS, and oral urea induces an osmotic water diuresis. Tolvaptan requires in-hospital initiation, carries a hepatotoxicity warning, and must be started without concurrent fluid restriction to avoid over-rapid correction. Demeclocycline is older and largely avoided (nephrotoxicity).
⚠️ WRONG: vaptans (tolvaptan/conivaptan) are V1 receptor AGONISTS. Discriminator — vaptans are V2 ANTAGONISTS that block AVP's renal water-reabsorption action to produce a free-water aquaresis; calling them V1 agonists inverts both the receptor and the direction of effect.
14. 3% hypertonic saline (severe)
For SEVERE symptomatic hyponatremia (seizures, obtundation, coma), give 3% hypertonic saline as a bolus (e.g., 100–150 mL, repeatable) targeting a prompt but limited rise of about 4–6 mmol/L to relieve cerebral edema. After the initial controlled bump, switch to careful management while respecting the 24-h correction ceiling.
⚠️ WRONG: give hypertonic saline rapidly to fully normalize the sodium. Discriminator — the goal is a small, controlled rise (~4–6 mmol/L) to break severe symptoms, NOT normalization; correcting too fast/too far risks osmotic demyelination. Hypertonic saline is rescue therapy, not a means to a normal Na in one sitting.
15. Correction rate limit / ODS
Limit sodium correction to <8 mmol/L per 24 h (be even more cautious — <6–8/24h — in chronic hyponatremia or high-risk patients: alcoholism, malnutrition, liver disease, hypokalemia). Over-rapid correction causes OSMOTIC DEMYELINATION SYNDROME (central pontine myelinolysis): delayed dysarthria, dysphagia, quadriparesis, and locked-in syndrome. If correction overshoots, re-lower Na with D5W ± desmopressin.
⚠️ WRONG: chronic hyponatremia should be corrected as fast as possible. Discriminator — rapid correction of CHRONIC hyponatremia causes osmotic demyelination; keep the rise <8 mmol/L/24h. Only the initial relief of severe symptoms is brisk (~4–6 mmol/L), after which the daily ceiling governs.
