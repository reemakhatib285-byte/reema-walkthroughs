📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. V/Q scan — 100% sensitivity rule-out
A normal V/Q scan essentially excludes PE (very high negative predictive value). V/Q is the preferred imaging when CTPA is contraindicated (contrast allergy, renal failure, pregnancy).
⚠️ A NORMAL V/Q rules out PE; a non-diagnostic/intermediate-probability V/Q does not — and it's the common result, demanding further testing.
2. Low specificity — many false PE
V/Q probability categories (high/intermediate/low/normal) suffer poor specificity in the middle bands; a 'high-probability' scan in a high-pretest patient confirms PE, but intermediate scans are frequently non-diagnostic.
⚠️ An intermediate-probability V/Q is NON-DIAGNOSTIC, not reassuring — pair it with pretest probability and proceed to CTPA or ultrasound.
3. Non-diagnostic alone
V/Q must be interpreted with clinical pretest probability; alone, many scans are indeterminate. Abnormal underlying lung (COPD, infiltrates) degrades its value, favoring CTPA when feasible.
⚠️ Don't act on a V/Q in isolation — combine with Wells; baseline lung disease makes V/Q unreliable, so CTPA is preferred there.
4. 40% PE despite low V/Q
High clinical suspicion plus a low-probability V/Q still leaves a meaningful (~40%) residual chance of PE — pretest probability overrides a discordant scan.
⚠️ High pretest probability + low-probability V/Q ≠ excluded — clinical gestalt wins; escalate imaging.
5. CTPA contraindicated → V/Q
Diagnostic sequencing: CTPA is first-line; V/Q is the second-line/alternative when CTPA is contraindicated. The stepwise approach conserves contrast and radiation appropriately.
⚠️ V/Q is the go-to ALTERNATIVE when contrast/renal/pregnancy preclude CTPA — not an inferior routine test for everyone.
6. Trophy shelf — test ranking
Hierarchy of confirmation: CTPA (gold standard) > V/Q (when CTPA contraindicated) > venous ultrasound (confirms DVT, indirectly supports PE).
⚠️ A positive leg ultrasound in a symptomatic patient can confirm VTE and justify treatment WITHOUT chest imaging — useful when CTPA/V/Q are unavailable.
7. McConnell's sign
McConnell's sign on echo = RV free-wall akinesia/hypokinesia with PRESERVED apical motion — relatively specific for acute PE. Echo also shows RV dilation, septal flattening, and elevated PASP.
⚠️ McConnell's sign = free-wall akinesis with SPARED apex (specific for acute PE) — distinguishes PE strain from global RV failure.
8. Echo critical without CTPA
In an unstable, hypotensive patient too sick for CTPA, bedside echo showing acute RV strain/McConnell's supports empiric thrombolysis for presumed massive PE.
⚠️ For a crashing patient, RV strain on bedside echo can justify EMPIRIC thrombolysis — don't delay reperfusion to transport for CTPA.
9. Venous ultrasound exam
Compression ultrasound is the first-line test for suspected DVT — safe, no contrast, bedside. A positive proximal study in a PE-suspect patient confirms VTE and warrants treatment.
⚠️ Whole-leg vs proximal compression matters: a negative single proximal scan with high suspicion warrants a repeat in ~1 week or whole-leg imaging.
10. Loss of compressibility = DVT
The diagnostic ultrasound finding for DVT is a non-compressible vein under probe pressure; absent compressibility = intraluminal thrombus. Color Doppler supports but compression is the key sign.
⚠️ Loss of COMPRESSIBILITY (not just absent Doppler flow) defines DVT on ultrasound — flow artifacts can mislead; compression is the standard.
11. Step 1 → Step 2 sequence
Diagnostic workflow: confirm pretest probability and attempt definitive imaging (CTPA), then escalate to second-line (V/Q) or advanced testing (echo, leg ultrasound) based on contraindications and stability.
⚠️ The pathway is conditional — choose the next test by contraindications and hemodynamics, not a fixed order for every patient.
12. Non-diagnostic V/Q → escalate
When V/Q is indeterminate, escalate to CTPA (if now feasible) or compression ultrasound; a positive leg study closes the loop for VTE treatment.
⚠️ An indeterminate V/Q is an indication to ESCALATE, not to stop — pairing with leg ultrasound often resolves the case.
