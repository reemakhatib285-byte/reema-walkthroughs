📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Armadillo Co. Jail
The armadillo is the classic animal reservoir of Mycobacterium leprae; the bacillus prefers cool body temperatures. Transmission to humans is via prolonged contact and respiratory droplets, not casual contact. M. leprae cannot be cultured on artificial media.
⚠️ M. leprae is the ONLY mycobacterium that does NOT grow on artificial culture media — diagnosis is by skin biopsy/slit-skin smear, not culture; don't expect a positive culture.
2. Armadillo reservoir
Armadillos are a natural zoonotic reservoir of leprosy, especially in the southern US; the organism thrives in their low core temperature. This mirrors the bacterium's preference for cooler human sites (skin, peripheral nerves, nose, earlobes).
⚠️ Cool-loving bacillus → it targets cooler tissues (skin, ulnar/peroneal nerves, nasal mucosa, earlobes), sparing warm core organs — that distribution is the exam clue.
3. Deputy sheriff badge
The acid-fast bacillus is identified with Fite or Ziehl-Neelsen stain on biopsy. M. leprae is an obligate intracellular organism that invades Schwann cells of peripheral nerves.
⚠️ It's acid-fast like M. tuberculosis but is intracellular in SCHWANN CELLS — nerve involvement (not lung) dominates the picture.
4. Lepromatous prisoner (low immunity)
Lepromatous (multibacillary) leprosy reflects a weak Th2-skewed cell-mediated response: numerous bacilli, symmetric widespread skin lesions, leonine facies, saddle-nose, and a low/negative lepromin skin test. Treated with rifampin + dapsone + clofazimine.
⚠️ LEPROMATOUS = poor Th1/CMI, HIGH bacterial load, lepromin-NEGATIVE — opposite of tuberculoid; don't swap their immune profiles.
5. Tuberculoid figure (strong immunity)
Tuberculoid (paucibacillary) leprosy reflects a robust Th1/cell-mediated response: few bacilli, well-demarcated hypopigmented anesthetic plaques, granulomas, and a POSITIVE lepromin test. Treated with rifampin + dapsone.
⚠️ TUBERCULOID = strong Th1/CMI, FEW bacilli, lepromin-POSITIVE, localized lesions — the mirror image of lepromatous.
6. Raised hands — anesthetic skin
Hallmark of leprosy is hypoesthetic/anesthetic skin lesions from peripheral nerve invasion; loss of sensation precedes secondary trauma and deformity.
⚠️ A HYPOPIGMENTED patch with LOSS OF SENSATION = leprosy; a numb skin lesion is the key discriminator from vitiligo or tinea (which are NOT anesthetic).
7. Nerve thickening / claw hand
Schwann-cell invasion causes palpable thickened peripheral nerves (ulnar, common peroneal, great auricular) and motor deformities like claw hand, foot drop, and resorption of digits from repeated unnoticed injury.
⚠️ Palpable, THICKENED peripheral nerves with a numb skin lesion are nearly diagnostic of leprosy — few other diseases enlarge nerves this way.
8. Cowboy on horseback (type 1 reaction)
Leprosy reactions: type 1 (reversal) reaction is a sudden cell-mediated flare causing inflamed skin lesions and acute neuritis — a medical emergency treated with corticosteroids to prevent permanent nerve damage.
⚠️ Acute neuritis/worsening lesions during therapy = TYPE 1 reversal reaction → give STEROIDS, do NOT stop antibiotics.
9. Drawn gun (erythema nodosum leprosum)
Type 2 reaction = erythema nodosum leprosum (ENL), an immune-complex (Th2) reaction with tender skin nodules, fever, and systemic illness, mainly in lepromatous disease; treated with corticosteroids or thalidomide.
⚠️ ENL (type 2) responds to THALIDOMIDE — a classic association — but thalidomide is teratogenic and contraindicated in pregnancy.
10. Window shooter (dapsone)
Dapsone is a backbone of multidrug therapy (inhibits folate synthesis). It can cause hemolysis, especially in G6PD deficiency, and methemoglobinemia.
⚠️ Screen for G6PD deficiency before DAPSONE — it precipitates hemolytic anemia in G6PD-deficient patients.
11. Clofazimine red-brown skin
Clofazimine is added for multibacillary disease and has anti-inflammatory effects; its hallmark side effect is reddish-brown skin discoloration that is reversible.
⚠️ Reddish-brown skin pigmentation in a leprosy patient = CLOFAZIMINE effect, reversible — not disease progression.
12. Rifampin (rifa = red)
Rifampin is the most bactericidal antileprosy drug and the core of all regimens; it turns body fluids red-orange and induces CYP450, lowering levels of many co-administered drugs.
⚠️ Rifampin's red-orange secretions are harmless, but its CYP450 INDUCTION can fail oral contraceptives and lower other drug levels — counsel accordingly.
13. Saddle-nose collapse
Advanced lepromatous leprosy destroys nasal cartilage producing saddle-nose deformity and leonine facies from diffuse dermal infiltration; the nose's cool mucosa is a favored site.
⚠️ Saddle-nose has a differential (congenital syphilis, GPA/Wegener's) — in leprosy it's accompanied by anesthetic skin and nerve thickening, the distinguishing combo.
14. Multidrug therapy duration
WHO multidrug therapy prevents resistance: paucibacillary = rifampin + dapsone (6 months); multibacillary = rifampin + dapsone + clofazimine (12 months). Combination therapy is mandatory.
⚠️ Never treat leprosy with monotherapy — single-drug therapy breeds resistance; multibacillary needs the THREE-drug regimen for 12 months.
