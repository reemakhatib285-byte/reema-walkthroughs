📖 Read-through — all 10 symbols
1. Hyperkalemia NOT precipitant (X)
HYPERkalemia is NOT a precipitant of hepatic encephalopathy — it is HYPOkalemia that precipitates HE (low K+ shifts ammonia into cells/brain and increases renal ammoniagenesis). Don't pick hyperkalemia. The real triggers are listed by the detective below.
⚠️ "Hyperkalemia precipitates hepatic encephalopathy" → wrong; it is HYPOkalemia (plus GI bleed, infection, constipation, dehydration/diuretics, sedatives) that triggers HE.
2. NH3 / ammonia
Gut-derived ammonia accumulates because the failing liver/portosystemic shunting cannot clear it, crossing into the brain (astrocyte glutamine, swelling). Note: a normal ammonia level does NOT exclude HE and a high level alone doesn't confirm it — HE is a clinical diagnosis. Don't trend ammonia to titrate therapy.
3. Asterixis
Asterixis (flapping tremor) is the classic motor sign of HE, a negative myoclonus from impaired posture maintenance. Also seen in CO2 retention and uremia. Graded clinically by West Haven criteria (0-IV).
4. Precipitants: find & fix
Always FIND AND FIX the precipitant: GI bleed, infection (do paracentesis for SBP), constipation, HYPOkalemia, dehydration/over-diuresis, sedatives/opioids, TIPS, dietary protein excess only rarely. Correcting the trigger is as important as lactulose.
5. Lactulose (gold star)
First-line HE therapy is lactulose (a non-absorbable disaccharide) titrated to 2-3 soft stools/day (gold star). It acidifies the colon, trapping NH3 as NH4+ for excretion, and is cathartic. Can be given orally, by NG tube, or as retention enema in obtunded patients.
6. Rifaximin 550
Rifaximin 550 mg twice daily is added to lactulose (especially for secondary prevention/recurrent HE); it is a non-absorbed antibiotic that reduces ammonia-producing gut flora. Combination lactulose + rifaximin lowers recurrence and hospitalization vs lactulose alone.
7. Do NOT restrict protein (X)
Do NOT restrict dietary protein in HE — protein restriction worsens catabolism and sarcopenia. Maintain 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day, favoring vegetable/dairy and branched-chain amino acids. Older teaching of protein restriction is obsolete and a tested trap.
⚠️ "Restrict dietary protein in hepatic encephalopathy" → wrong; maintain 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day protein; restriction causes muscle wasting and is no longer recommended.
8. Rebalanced hemostasis / INR (X)
Cirrhosis is REBALANCED hemostasis: both pro- and anti-coagulant factors fall, so a high INR does NOT reliably predict bleeding. Do NOT transfuse FFP or correct INR based on the number alone in a non-bleeding patient or before low-risk procedures. INR overestimates bleeding risk here.
⚠️ "Transfuse FFP / correct the INR because it is elevated" → wrong; cirrhosis has rebalanced hemostasis and INR doesn't predict bleeding; don't correct INR alone.
9. Platelet goal >50k
For procedures, target platelets >50,000/uL (some endoscopic procedures tolerate lower). Thrombopoietin receptor agonists (avatrombopag, lusutrombopag) can raise platelets pre-procedure and reduce platelet transfusion need. Fibrinogen >120 also matters.
10. 4-factor PCC over FFP
When correction IS needed (active bleeding), prefer 4-factor prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) over FFP — smaller volume avoids volume overload that raises portal pressure and risks rebleeding. Give targeted blood-product support guided by bleeding, not by lab numbers.
