📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. HCV screen — all adults 18–79
USPSTF/CDC recommend ONE-TIME HCV screening for ALL adults aged 18-79, plus periodic screening for ongoing-risk groups (IVDU, HIV, dialysis) and screening in EVERY pregnancy. The screening test is anti-HCV antibody (EIA); positives are reflexed to HCV RNA to confirm active infection.
⚠️ Current guidance is UNIVERSAL screening, not risk-based only — screen even patients who deny risk factors. A stem saying 'no risk factors, so don't test' is the wrong answer.
2. IVDU — leading risk
Injection drug use is the most common route of HCV transmission today. Sharing needles/equipment transmits efficiently because HCV is parenterally spread. Intranasal cocaine use (shared straws) and unregulated tattoos are additional parenteral routes.
⚠️ HCV is primarily PARENTERAL/blood-borne and far less sexually transmitted than HBV. Don't choose 'sexual contact' as the leading HCV route — that's a better fit for HBV.
3. Pre-1992 transfusion
Blood transfusion before donor anti-HCV screening began (mid-1992) is the classic historical HCV exposure — a key reason for the original birth-cohort (1945-1965 'baby boomer') screening recommendation now superseded by universal screening.
⚠️ Post-1992 transfusion HCV risk is negligible due to nucleic-acid blood-supply screening. A current transfusion is not a meaningful HCV risk factor — don't attribute new HCV to a recent transfusion.
4. Transfusion / dialysis patient
Chronic hemodialysis patients and prior blood-product recipients (e.g., clotting-factor concentrates before viral inactivation) carry elevated HCV prevalence and warrant periodic screening.
⚠️ Occupational needlestick HCV seroconversion risk is ~1.8% — higher than HIV (~0.3%) but much lower than HBV (up to ~30% from an HBeAg+ source). Don't rank HCV as the highest needlestick risk; HBV is.
5. Low vertical / sexual transmission
Perinatal HCV transmission is ~5% (rising with high maternal viremia or HIV co-infection), and sexual transmission is uncommon in monogamous heterosexual couples. These contrast with the much higher vertical/sexual efficiency of HBV.
⚠️ Breastfeeding is NOT contraindicated in HCV (unless nipples are cracked/bleeding). Don't tell an HCV+ mother to avoid breastfeeding. Vertical and sexual risk both rise with HIV co-infection.
6. Anti-HCV → confirm with RNA
A positive anti-HCV antibody indicates exposure but NOT necessarily active infection; it must be confirmed with HCV RNA (PCR). Ab+ / RNA+ = current active infection; Ab+ / RNA- = past/cleared or successfully treated infection (antibody persists for life).
⚠️ Ab+ / RNA- = resolved/cured infection, NOT active disease — the antibody stays positive after spontaneous clearance or DAA cure, so a positive antibody alone never proves current infection. Always reflex to RNA.
7. Ab- / RNA+ = acute
In early acute HCV, HCV RNA becomes detectable within 1-2 weeks of exposure, well BEFORE anti-HCV antibody seroconverts (which can take 8-12 weeks). So an antibody-negative, RNA-positive result indicates acute infection in the window.
⚠️ A NEGATIVE HCV antibody does not exclude acute HCV — if exposure was recent (e.g., needlestick, new IVDU), order HCV RNA. Relying on antibody alone misses acute window-period infection.
8. High chronicity rate
Approximately 75-85% of acute HCV infections progress to chronicity — far higher than adult-acquired HBV (~5%). This high chronicity, combined with a usually silent acute course, makes HCV the leading cause of chronic viral hepatitis and cirrhosis in the West.
⚠️ HCV is usually asymptomatic acutely yet MOST cases become chronic — the opposite pattern from adult HBV (often symptomatic acutely but mostly cleared). Don't swap the chronicity rates of HBV and HCV.
9. Often asymptomatic acute
Acute HCV is frequently silent — anicteric and minimally symptomatic — which is precisely why universal screening (rather than waiting for symptoms) is essential to catch it before chronic complications develop.
⚠️ Fulminant acute liver failure from HCV alone is RARE — HCV is a chronic-disease problem. If a stem describes acute liver failure, think acetaminophen, HAV, HBV±HDV, or HEV, not isolated acute HCV.
10. Cirrhosis progression
Chronic HCV drives slowly progressive hepatic fibrosis to cirrhosis over ~20-30 years; roughly 20-30% of chronically infected patients develop cirrhosis. Fibrosis can be staged noninvasively (FibroScan/elastography, FIB-4) to guide surveillance.
⚠️ Cofactors — ALCOHOL, HIV/HBV co-infection, and hepatic steatosis (obesity/diabetes) — markedly ACCELERATE fibrosis. Counseling on alcohol cessation is part of HCV management; don't ignore alcohol as a modifier.
11. HCC risk
HCV-related cirrhosis is a major cause of hepatocellular carcinoma. Patients with cirrhosis need HCC surveillance with abdominal ultrasound (± AFP) every 6 months. Extrahepatic HCV associations to recall: mixed cryoglobulinemia, membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis (MPGN), and porphyria cutanea tarda.
⚠️ HCC surveillance (US ± AFP every 6 months) CONTINUES even AFTER DAA cure if cirrhosis is already established — achieving SVR lowers but does not eliminate HCC risk. Stopping surveillance after cure in a cirrhotic is the trap.
12. Cure with DAAs
Direct-acting antivirals (e.g., sofosbuvir/velpatasvir, glecaprevir/pibrentasvir) cure >95% of HCV across genotypes with 8-12 week oral, well-tolerated courses. Cure = SVR12 (sustained virologic response, undetectable HCV RNA 12 weeks after finishing therapy). Pangenotypic regimens have made routine genotyping often unnecessary.
⚠️ SVR (cure) does NOT reverse established cirrhosis, and a cured patient can be re-infected — antibody stays positive, so re-infection is diagnosed by RNA, not antibody. HCC and varices surveillance must continue in cirrhotics despite cure.
13. Acute liver failure — danger room
Acute liver failure = coagulopathy (INR ≥1.5) PLUS hepatic encephalopathy in a patient without pre-existing cirrhosis, evolving over days-weeks. Management is ICU care plus urgent transplant evaluation; give N-acetylcysteine for acetaminophen (and consider in non-acetaminophen) ALF.
⚠️ ALF is RARE in HCV — when you see fulminant hepatitis, think HAV/HBV/HEV or acetaminophen, not HCV. Also remember encephalopathy is required to diagnose ALF; coagulopathy alone is not enough.
14. HBV screen — all adults ≥18
For contrast: 2023 CDC guidance also recommends screening ALL adults ≥18 at least once for HBV. The proper HBV screen is a TRIPLE panel — HBsAg, anti-HBs, and anti-HBc (total) — to classify infection, immunity, and susceptibility in one shot.
⚠️ Don't conflate the two screens: HCV is screened with anti-HCV antibody (then RNA), whereas HBV needs the triple panel (HBsAg/anti-HBc/anti-HBs), NOT just a single antibody. Screening HBV with anti-HBs alone misses active infection.
