📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Platelets >1,000,000
Essential thrombocythemia (ET) is a clonal myeloproliferative neoplasm of megakaryocytes producing sustained thrombocytosis. The WHO diagnostic threshold is a platelet count ≥450 ×10⁹/L (450K), but counts often run >1,000K. Diagnosis (WHO 2016) requires all 4 major criteria OR first 3 major + 1 minor: (1) platelets ≥450K, (2) marrow with proliferation of enlarged mature hyperlobulated megakaryocytes without significant granulocytic/erythroid increase, (3) not meeting criteria for PV/PMF/CML/MDS, (4) JAK2/CALR/MPL mutation; minor = a clonal marker or no reactive cause. Mnemonic: '450 is the gate, a million is the picture.'
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'platelets >100K is enough to diagnose ET.' The discriminator is the WHO threshold of ≥450K AND demonstration of clonality with exclusion of reactive causes — mild thrombocytosis (450–600K) is far more often reactive (iron deficiency, inflammation, infection, post-splenectomy) than ET.
2. JAK2 ~64% → PV risk
JAK2 V617F is the most common ET driver, found in ~50–65% of cases. JAK2-mutated ET tends to behave more like 'forme fruste PV' — higher hemoglobin, lower platelet counts for a given disease burden, more thrombosis, and a risk of progressing to overt PV. It is enriched in women. Mnemonic: 'JAK2-ET leans toward the red side (PV).'
⚠️ The trap is assuming JAK2 positivity proves ET — WRONG, since JAK2 V617F is shared across PV (~95%) and PMF (~50%). Discriminator: JAK2+ with isolated thrombocytosis, normal/low Hct, and characteristic large mature megakaryocytes on marrow defines ET; the same mutation with high RCM is PV, and with marrow fibrosis is PMF.
3. CALR → PMF risk (men)
CALR (calreticulin) mutations occur in ~20–25% of ET (the second most common driver) and are mutually exclusive with JAK2. CALR-mutated ET is associated with younger men, higher platelet counts, LOWER thrombosis risk, but a greater tendency toward fibrotic (PMF) transformation, especially type 1 CALR. CALR-mutant disease often carries a better overall prognosis than JAK2-mutant. Mnemonic: 'CALR — Counts high, cLots Low, fibrosis risk.'
⚠️ WRONG to think CALR-positive ET is higher-risk for thrombosis because the platelets are so high. Discriminator: despite the highest platelet counts, CALR+ patients have LOWER thrombotic risk than JAK2+ patients; the platelet number alone does not drive thrombosis risk — driver mutation, age, and prior thrombosis do (IPSET-thrombosis score).
4. MPL & exclusion of others
MPL (thrombopoietin receptor) mutations account for ~3–5% of ET. ET is fundamentally a DIAGNOSIS OF EXCLUSION: rule out CML (BCR-ABL1 fusion must be absent), MDS with ring sideroblasts and thrombocytosis (MDS/MPN-RS-T, often SF3B1-mutated), PV, PMF, and reactive thrombocytosis. About 10–15% of ET are 'triple-negative' (no JAK2/CALR/MPL). Mnemonic: 'EXCLUDE before you conclude — BCR-ABL must be negative.'
⚠️ The most important board trap: a high platelet count with basophilia, splenomegaly, and a left-shifted leukocytosis is CML, NOT ET — and the WRONG answer is to start hydroxyurea for 'ET.' Discriminator: ALWAYS test BCR-ABL1; a positive Philadelphia chromosome reclassifies the patient to CML and mandates a tyrosine kinase inhibitor (imatinib).
5. Thrombosis & bleeding
ET produces a paradox of BOTH thrombosis and bleeding. Thrombosis (arterial > venous, including unusual sites like splanchnic/Budd-Chiari) is the leading complication. But at extreme counts (>1,000–1,500K) the huge platelet mass adsorbs and clears high-molecular-weight von Willebrand multimers, causing ACQUIRED von Willebrand disease and mucocutaneous bleeding. Erythromelalgia (burning red painful hands/feet) is the classic microvascular symptom, exquisitely aspirin-responsive. Mnemonic: 'too many platelets clot AND bleed.'
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'ET causes only thrombosis, never bleeding.' Discriminator: at extreme thrombocytosis, screen for acquired vWD (ristocetin cofactor activity) BEFORE giving aspirin — adding aspirin to a patient with acquired vWD can precipitate major bleeding. The bleeding-thrombosis paradox is the high-yield catch.
6. Aspirin
Low-dose aspirin (81 mg) relieves vasomotor symptoms (erythromelalgia, headache, visual disturbance) and reduces thrombosis; it is standard in low-risk patients with vasomotor symptoms and in all higher-risk patients. Mnemonic: 'aspirin melts erythromelalgia.'
⚠️ Giving aspirin reflexively to every ET patient is WRONG when platelets are extreme (>1,000–1,500K) and acquired vWD is present (the red X). Discriminator: check ristocetin cofactor activity first — if acquired vWD is documented, hold aspirin and lower the platelet count with cytoreduction before reintroducing antiplatelet therapy.
7. Hydroxyurea (high-risk)
Cytoreduction is indicated for HIGH-RISK ET (age >60 with JAK2, OR any prior thrombosis). Hydroxyurea is first-line: it lowers platelets, reduces thrombosis, is well tolerated; side effects include myelosuppression, megaloblastic changes, leg ulcers, and skin cancers. Pegylated interferon-alfa is preferred in young patients and pregnancy (non-teratogenic, non-leukemogenic). Risk stratification uses IPSET-thrombosis (age, prior thrombosis, JAK2, CV risk factors). Mnemonic: 'high-risk = Hydroxyurea; young/pregnant = Interferon.'
⚠️ WRONG to cytoreduce a low-risk young ET patient just because the platelet count is alarmingly high. Discriminator: treatment is driven by THROMBOTIC RISK (age >60, prior thrombosis, JAK2 status), NOT by the absolute platelet number — low-risk asymptomatic patients are managed with observation ± aspirin and CV risk-factor control.
8. Anagrelide
Anagrelide is a phosphodiesterase-3 inhibitor that selectively reduces platelet production by impairing megakaryocyte maturation. It is a SECOND-LINE cytoreductive agent. Side effects: headache, palpitations/tachycardia, fluid retention, and high-output cardiac effects. The PT-1 trial showed anagrelide + aspirin was inferior to hydroxyurea + aspirin, with more arterial thrombosis, bleeding, and progression to myelofibrosis. Mnemonic: 'Anagrelide = platelet-selective but second-string.'
⚠️ Choosing anagrelide first-line over hydroxyurea in high-risk ET is the WRONG answer. Discriminator: hydroxyurea + aspirin is first-line; anagrelide is reserved for hydroxyurea intolerance/resistance because PT-1 data show more thrombosis and faster myelofibrotic transformation with anagrelide.
9. Normalizing count ≠ prevent thrombosis
Driving the platelet count fully to the normal range does NOT abolish thrombotic risk in ET — thrombosis is driven by clonal platelet dysfunction, leukocyte activation, JAK2 status, age, and conventional CV risk factors, not by the count alone. The therapeutic target in high-risk patients is roughly <400K, but aspirin and CV risk-factor control remain essential. Mnemonic: 'the number is not the whole risk.'
⚠️ WRONG assumption: 'if I normalize the platelet count, the patient cannot clot.' Discriminator: thrombotic risk persists at normal counts; ongoing aspirin, smoking cessation, BP/lipid/diabetes control, and avoiding estrogen are required — fixating on the platelet number alone misses the dominant risk drivers.
10. Survival ~ general population
ET has the BEST prognosis of the classic MPNs — median survival approaches that of the age-matched general population, especially in patients under 60. Leukemic/myelofibrotic transformation is low (~1–5% to AML, slightly higher to post-ET MF over decades). Mnemonic: 'ET = the most indolent MPN, lives for decades.'
⚠️ WRONG answer: 'ET has the worst MPN survival.' Discriminator: ET is the most indolent classic MPN with near-normal life expectancy; primary myelofibrosis carries the worst prognosis. Confusing ET's benign course with PMF's aggressive one is a common tested error.
11. Marrow: clustered megakaryocytes
ET marrow shows proliferation of LARGE, MATURE, hyperlobulated (staghorn) megakaryocytes in loose clusters, with normal or near-normal erythroid and granulocytic lines and NO significant reticulin fibrosis. Significant reticulin fibrosis or dense/atypical megakaryocyte clusters point to early/pre-fibrotic PMF rather than true ET. Massive splenomegaly is unusual in ET and should prompt reconsideration of another MPN. Mnemonic: 'big mature staghorn megs, no fibrosis = ET.'
⚠️ WRONG to call a thrombocytotic patient with marrow reticulin fibrosis and dense atypical megakaryocyte clustering 'ET.' Discriminator: those features define prefibrotic primary myelofibrosis (pre-PMF), which has worse prognosis and higher transformation risk; the marrow morphology is what separates ET from pre-PMF.
12. Demographics 1-2/100K, female
ET incidence is ~1–2.5 per 100,000/year with a female predominance and a bimodal pattern — a peak around 50–60 years and a smaller peak in young women of childbearing age. Because young women are affected, pregnancy management (aspirin, sometimes interferon, increased miscarriage/thrombosis risk) is a tested scenario. Mnemonic: 'ET = the MPN of younger women.'
⚠️ WRONG to assume a pregnant woman with ET should receive hydroxyurea for cytoreduction. Discriminator: hydroxyurea and anagrelide are teratogenic/contraindicated in pregnancy; the correct agents are low-dose aspirin and, if cytoreduction is needed, interferon-alfa (which is pregnancy-safe).
13. Pseudohyperkalemia artifact
Extreme thrombocytosis causes SPURIOUS (pseudo) hyperkalemia: as the clotted serum sample forms, platelets lyse and release intracellular potassium, falsely elevating the serum K+. The PLASMA potassium (heparinized, unclotted tube) is normal — that is the diagnostic clue. Similar pseudo-elevations can affect serum phosphate. Mnemonic: 'platelets pop in the clot tube and spill K+.'
⚠️ WRONG answer: treat the high potassium as true hyperkalemia with insulin/dextrose, calcium, and kayexalate. Discriminator: an ET patient with high serum K+ but a NORMAL ECG should have a plasma K+ checked — a normal plasma value confirms pseudohyperkalemia, an in-vitro artifact requiring no treatment. Treating it can cause iatrogenic hypokalemia.
14. Iron stores / exclude IDA
Absent marrow iron stores / low ferritin in a thrombocytotic patient demands explanation. Two key scenarios: (1) iron-deficiency itself causes REACTIVE thrombocytosis, which must be excluded before diagnosing ET; (2) coexisting iron deficiency can MASK polycythemia vera — the iron-limited erythropoiesis keeps the Hct deceptively normal while thrombocytosis is the only visible clue. Repleting iron in masked PV can unmask a dangerous Hct rise. Mnemonic: 'low iron — reactive thrombocytosis OR hidden PV.'
⚠️ WRONG to diagnose ET in an iron-deficient patient without addressing the iron status. Discriminator: correct/evaluate iron deficiency first — reactive thrombocytosis resolves with iron repletion (not ET), and in a JAK2+ patient, iron repletion may unmask PV (Hct surges), changing both the diagnosis and the management.
