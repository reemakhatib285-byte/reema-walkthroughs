📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. ASA umpire acetylating COX-1
Aspirin (ASA) irreversibly acetylates serine 530 of COX-1, the enzyme that makes thromboxane A2 (TXA2). This blocks TXA2 synthesis and the platelet's amplification of aggregation. ASA is the prototype antiplatelet.
⚠️ Aspirin acetylation is IRREVERSIBLE and covalent — not competitive. Ibuprofen is reversible and competes for the same channel, so taking ibuprofen before aspirin can block aspirin's antiplatelet effect.
2. COX-1 grilling TXA2
COX-1 converts arachidonic acid to TXA2, a potent pro-aggregatory, vasoconstricting agonist. Aspirin's antiplatelet benefit comes from shutting down platelet COX-1 -> TXA2.
⚠️ TXA2 (platelet, pro-thrombotic) is balanced against endothelial PGI2 (anti-thrombotic). Low-dose aspirin selectively suppresses platelet TXA2 while sparing endothelial PGI2.
3. COX-2 assistant coach (sleeping)
COX-2 is largely inducible by inflammation and is not the main platelet enzyme; platelets rely on COX-1. This is why low-dose aspirin's antiplatelet effect is COX-1 driven.
⚠️ Selective COX-2 inhibitors (coxibs) do NOT give antiplatelet protection and actually raise cardiovascular thrombotic risk by suppressing endothelial PGI2 without blocking platelet TXA2 — a board favorite.
4. No nucleus -> 7-10 day inhibition
Platelets are anucleate and cannot resynthesize COX-1, so a single aspirin dose inhibits that platelet for its ~7-10 day lifespan. Functional recovery needs new platelets (~10-12%/day turnover).
⚠️ This is why aspirin is held ~5-7 days before surgery and why the antiplatelet effect persists long after the drug is cleared. Don't tie aspirin duration to its short plasma half-life.
5. Net effect: low vs high dose
Low-dose ASA (75-100 mg) selectively inhibits platelet TXA2 = antiaggregatory. High dose (~1 g/day) also blocks endothelial prostacyclin (PGI2), shifting toward pro-aggregation and losing benefit.
⚠️ 'More aspirin = more antiplatelet protection' is wrong. High dose blunts endothelial PGI2 (the aspirin/prostacyclin dilemma); the cardioprotective dose is LOW dose.
6. 75-100 mg maintenance dose
75-100 mg daily is the chronic maintenance antiplatelet dose for secondary prevention. It maximizes TXA2 blockade while sparing PGI2 and minimizing GI/bleed risk.
⚠️ Maintenance (75-100 mg daily) is distinct from the loading dose. Confusing maintenance with the 162-325 mg loading dose for ACS is a dosing trap.
7. ≥160-325 mg rapid loading only
A loading dose of ~162-325 mg (chewed, non-enteric) is given once at ACS onset for rapid platelet inhibition, then dropped to low-dose maintenance. Chronic high dose is not beneficial.
⚠️ The high dose is for ACUTE LOADING only, then de-escalate. Continuing 325 mg chronically adds GI/bleeding risk without added benefit and risks the pro-aggregatory PGI2 shift.
8. Road 1 — secondary prevention benefit
In established disease (prior MI, stroke, CAD, PAD), aspirin gives ~25% relative risk reduction in vascular events. Secondary prevention is the clear-benefit indication.
⚠️ Aspirin's strong evidence is for SECONDARY prevention. Don't generalize the 25% RRR to healthy primary-prevention patients, where benefit is marginal.
9. Road 2 — primary prevention closed
For PRIMARY prevention, routine aspirin is no longer recommended because GI and intracranial bleeding risk outweighs benefit; consider only in select high-CV-risk, low-bleed patients.
⚠️ Modern guidelines REMOVED routine aspirin for primary prevention (esp. elderly). The trap is starting aspirin in a healthy patient 'for prevention' — bleeding risk wins.
10. GI Story — dyspepsia to PUD
Aspirin causes dose-dependent GI injury (dyspepsia, erosive gastritis, peptic ulcer) by blocking protective gastric COX-1 prostaglandins — a systemic, not just topical, effect. Manage with PPI and H. pylori eradication.
⚠️ Enteric coating does NOT eliminate GI/ulcer risk because injury is systemic (COX-1 mediated), not just local contact. Switching to enteric-coated to 'prevent ulcers' is the trap.
11. Bleeding Box — 1-3%/year
Major bleeding risk on aspirin is roughly 1-3%/year, rising 2-3x with dual/combination antithrombotic therapy. Low dose is used in all combinations to limit bleeding.
⚠️ Adding a second antiplatelet or an anticoagulant multiplies bleeding risk — keep aspirin at LOW dose in any combo (DAPT or triple therapy).
12. Allergy Balloon — aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease
Aspirin hypersensitivity (~0.3% general; far higher in Samter's triad — asthma, nasal polyps, chronic rhinosinusitis) arises from COX-1 blockade shunting arachidonic acid to excess leukotrienes. Contraindicated in this triad.
⚠️ The reaction is pseudo-allergic (leukotriene shunting), not true IgE allergy, and it cross-reacts with ALL NSAIDs. Giving another NSAID to an aspirin-allergic asthmatic is the trap.
13. Resistance Corner
'Aspirin resistance' (poor compliance, malabsorption, ibuprofen competition at COX-1, COX-2 overexpression) is a research concept; assays are not standardized and not used clinically.
⚠️ Don't order an 'aspirin resistance' test in practice — it is non-standardized/research only. Also: dose ibuprofen >=2h AFTER aspirin to avoid the COX-1 access blockade ('flying tackle').
