📖 Read-through — all 18 symbols
1. Education & self-management — foundation
Patient education and self-management are the foundation of OA care for EVERY patient and the first rung of the ladder. OA is a degenerative (not autoimmune) disease of articular cartilage with secondary subchondral bone change; hallmarks are activity-related pain that improves with rest, morning stiffness LASTING <30 MINUTES, bony enlargement, and crepitus — with NO systemic inflammation (normal ESR/CRP). Education covers pacing, joint protection, and realistic expectations and is combined with exercise and weight loss before drugs.
⚠️ WRONG answer: jumping to a drug (or imaging/referral) first. Discriminator: non-pharmacologic care (education + exercise + weight loss) is first-line for all OA — picking 'start NSAIDs' or 'order MRI' before lifestyle measures is the classic mis-step; X-ray (osteophytes, joint-space narrowing, subchondral sclerosis/cysts) confirms but is not needed to start management.
2. Weight loss
Weight loss is a core, evidence-based intervention, especially for knee (and hip) OA — every pound lost removes roughly 3–4 lb of force across the knee, so even ~5–10% body-weight loss meaningfully reduces pain and improves function. It is the closest thing OA has to a disease-modifying intervention for weight-bearing joints and works best paired with exercise.
⚠️ WRONG answer: dismissing weight loss as 'just general advice' and escalating straight to injections/surgery in an obese knee-OA patient. Discriminator: for overweight patients with knee OA, structured weight loss + exercise is a guideline-strong recommendation that should precede pharmacologic escalation — it modifies the mechanical driver of the disease.
3. Exercise (aerobic + resistance)
Structured exercise — combined aerobic conditioning AND resistance/strengthening (e.g., quadriceps strengthening for knee OA) — improves pain, function, and proprioception and is strongly recommended for all OA patients. Strengthening the periarticular muscles offloads and stabilizes the joint, directly countering the arthrogenic muscle weakness that accompanies OA.
⚠️ WRONG answer: prescribing rest, immobilization, or activity avoidance to 'protect the joint.' Discriminator: exercise HELPS OA and is first-line; rest/immobilization causes deconditioning and worsens outcomes. The wrong answer choice is almost always 'rest the joint' — choose graded exercise/strengthening instead.
4. Low-impact water aerobics / Tai Chi
Aquatic exercise and Tai Chi are favored LOW-IMPACT modalities, particularly for knee and hip OA — water reduces joint loading while allowing strengthening, and Tai Chi has a strong (guideline-recommended) evidence base improving knee-OA pain, balance, and function. Good options for patients who can't tolerate high-impact land exercise.
⚠️ WRONG answer: labeling Tai Chi/aquatic therapy as unproven 'alternative medicine' and excluding them. Discriminator: these are evidence-based, guideline-endorsed for knee OA — unlike glucosamine/chondroitin, PRP, or hyaluronic acid, which are NOT recommended. Don't lump legitimate low-impact exercise in with ineffective supplements.
5. Topical NSAIDs (knee/hand)
Topical NSAIDs (e.g., diclofenac gel) are PREFERRED first-line pharmacotherapy for knee and hand OA — comparable efficacy to oral NSAIDs for localized disease with far lower systemic exposure and fewer GI/CV/renal adverse effects. Especially favored in the elderly and in patients with comorbidities (CKD, prior GI bleed, cardiovascular disease).
⚠️ WRONG answer: starting an ORAL NSAID first for a single painful knee/hand in an elderly or comorbid patient. Discriminator: for localized knee/hand OA, topical NSAIDs are preferred over oral because of the safety margin; reserve systemic NSAIDs for multi-joint/refractory disease where topical isn't practical.
6. Oral NSAIDs (lowest dose, shortest)
Oral NSAIDs are the most effective systemic analgesic for OA — more effective than acetaminophen, which is now considered minimally/ineffective for OA. Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration. Co-prescribe a PPI for GI protection in at-risk patients; avoid/limit in CKD, heart failure, and CV disease (consider a COX-2 selective like celecoxib for lower GI risk, but it still carries CV/renal risk).
⚠️ WRONG answer: choosing acetaminophen as the 'safe first-line analgesic' expecting good relief, or giving an NSAID to a patient with CKD/HF/recent MI. Discriminator: NSAIDs > acetaminophen for efficacy but carry GI/CV/renal risk — match the drug to comorbidities (PPI cover, avoid in CKD/HF). Acetaminophen is weak but is the fallback when NSAIDs are contraindicated.
7. Duloxetine (adjunct)
Duloxetine (an SNRI) is FDA-approved for chronic musculoskeletal pain including OA and is a legitimate analgesic ADJUNCT — most useful for widespread/multi-joint OA pain, central sensitization, or when NSAIDs are insufficient or contraindicated. Typical dosing 30 mg daily up to 60 mg; watch for nausea, somnolence, and serotonin syndrome with other serotonergics.
⚠️ WRONG answer: dismissing duloxetine as 'only an antidepressant, not an analgesic.' Discriminator: it is a recommended OA analgesic adjunct (esp. for diffuse pain or NSAID-intolerant patients) — a valid right answer when NSAIDs are contraindicated, distinct from opioids which are NOT recommended.
8. Intra-articular glucocorticoids
Intra-articular corticosteroid injections provide SHORT-TERM relief (weeks to a few months) for acute painful OA flares, especially of the knee. They are symptomatic only — no disease modification — and frequency is limited (commonly ≤3–4 injections/year per joint) because repeated injections may accelerate cartilage loss. Useful bridge when oral/topical analgesia is inadequate.
⚠️ WRONG answer: presenting IA steroids as disease-modifying or repeating them frequently for chronic baseline pain. Discriminator: they treat flares for weeks–months only and do NOT slow OA progression; over-injecting can harm cartilage. They are a temporizing measure, not a long-term solution.
9. Hyaluronic acid — limited
Intra-articular hyaluronic acid (viscosupplementation) has limited and inconsistent evidence — most trials show benefit no greater than placebo. Major guidelines (ACR) recommend AGAINST its routine use for knee/hip OA. It is not disease-modifying.
⚠️ WRONG answer: selecting viscosupplementation as an effective next step for knee OA. Discriminator: hyaluronic acid is generally NOT recommended (limited/placebo-level benefit) — contrast it with IA steroids, which at least give real short-term flare relief. Both are intra-articular, but only steroids have a clear role.
10. PRP / stem cells — not recommended
Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) and stem-cell/regenerative injections are UNPROVEN for OA — evidence is low quality and inconsistent, products are unstandardized, and guidelines do not recommend them. Often marketed and out-of-pocket; not supported as standard therapy.
⚠️ WRONG answer: choosing PRP or stem-cell injection as evidence-based OA treatment. Discriminator: PRP/stem cells are experimental and not recommended — a distractor designed to look 'cutting-edge.' Stick to proven options (exercise, weight loss, topical/oral NSAIDs, IA steroids for flares, arthroplasty for end-stage).
11. Avoid opioids
Opioids are recommended AGAINST for OA — they provide no meaningful long-term benefit over NSAIDs and carry risks of dependence, tolerance, falls, constipation, and opioid-induced hyperalgesia. Tramadol is only conditionally considered when all else fails and surgery isn't an option, and even that is discouraged.
⚠️ WRONG answer: escalating to a chronic opioid (or tramadol) for persistent OA pain. Discriminator: guidelines recommend against opioids for chronic OA pain — the correct escalation when NSAIDs fail is duloxetine, IA steroids for flares, or arthroplasty for end-stage disease, not opioids.
12. Glucosamine/chondroitin — no benefit
Glucosamine and chondroitin supplements show little to no benefit over placebo in high-quality trials and are NOT recommended by guidelines. Often patient-driven; safe but ineffective and not disease-modifying.
⚠️ WRONG answer: endorsing glucosamine/chondroitin as effective cartilage-protective therapy. Discriminator: these supplements are conditionally recommended AGAINST (placebo-level benefit) — a common 'patient asks about supplements' vignette where the right move is to redirect to proven measures (exercise, weight loss, NSAIDs).
13. Arthroscopy/sham surgery — don't do it
Arthroscopic debridement/lavage for knee OA performs NO BETTER than sham surgery in randomized trials and is not recommended. Likewise, arthroscopic partial meniscectomy for degenerative meniscal tears in the setting of OA offers no benefit over physical therapy. Surgery's role in OA is joint replacement, not 'clean-out' procedures.
⚠️ WRONG answer: offering knee arthroscopy/'debridement' or meniscectomy for OA with a degenerative meniscal tear. Discriminator: arthroscopic clean-out = placebo-equivalent and is the wrong answer; reserve surgery for total joint arthroplasty in end-stage disease. (A locked knee from an acute mechanical/bucket-handle tear is the rare exception that may warrant arthroscopy.)
14. Joint replacement (end-stage)
Total joint arthroplasty is the DEFINITIVE treatment for end-stage OA — indicated for severe, refractory pain and functional limitation despite optimized conservative therapy. It is highly effective for pain relief and function (knee and hip especially) and sits at the TOP rung of the ladder.
⚠️ WRONG answer: jumping to arthroplasty (or arthroscopy) before exhausting non-operative measures. Discriminator: joint replacement is reserved for severe, refractory OA after lifestyle, topical/oral analgesics, and (for flares) IA steroids have failed — it is the last rung, not an early option. Avoid in the patient who hasn't yet tried conservative care.
15. Bracing / cane in opposite hand
Mechanical aids offload the joint: knee braces/unloader braces for varus/valgus malalignment, a thumb CMC (first carpometacarpal) splint/base-of-thumb orthosis for hand OA, and — the classic board pearl — a cane held in the hand OPPOSITE the affected hip or knee. Holding the cane contralaterally shifts the body's center of gravity and reduces load across the painful joint.
⚠️ WRONG answer: telling the patient to hold the cane on the SAME side as the painful hip/knee. Discriminator: the cane goes in the OPPOSITE hand to offload the affected weight-bearing joint — a frequently tested detail. Same-side cane use increases joint load and is the trap.
16. Arthrogenic muscle inhibition (swollen joint → weakness)
Arthrogenic muscle inhibition: a painful, swollen, distended joint capsule reflexively inhibits the surrounding muscles (notably the quadriceps in knee OA), causing weakness and atrophy that further destabilizes the joint and accelerates degeneration — a vicious cycle. This is precisely WHY resistance/strengthening exercise is therapeutic: rebuilding periarticular muscle breaks the inhibition loop and protects the joint.
⚠️ WRONG answer: attributing the muscle weakness/atrophy around an OA knee to a primary neuromuscular or neuropathic disorder and ordering EMG/neuro workup. Discriminator: in OA, periarticular weakness (esp. quadriceps) is reflex arthrogenic inhibition from the affected joint — the treatment is targeted strengthening, not a neurologic evaluation.
17. Drug response is idiosyncratic / placebo effect
OA symptom trials show a large placebo response (often ~50% of the treatment effect), which is a major reason marginal therapies (hyaluronic acid, glucosamine/chondroitin, PRP) appear to 'work' in open-label use but fail versus sham/placebo in blinded RCTs. Analgesic response is also idiosyncratic — a reasonable, guideline-endorsed agent is chosen and adjusted by trial-and-error for the individual.
⚠️ WRONG answer: citing a patient's subjective improvement on glucosamine, viscosupplementation, or PRP as proof of efficacy. Discriminator: the high OA placebo response means uncontrolled improvement is not evidence — guideline recommendations rest on sham/placebo-controlled trials, which is why those interventions remain not recommended despite anecdotal benefit.
18. Footwear / shoe modifications
Appropriate footwear and supportive insoles are part of the non-pharmacologic, biomechanical toolkit for lower-extremity OA — stable, cushioned, well-fitting shoes can reduce knee loading and improve comfort. (Lateral wedge insoles for medial knee OA have weak/limited evidence and are not strongly recommended, but general supportive footwear is reasonable adjunctive advice alongside weight loss, exercise, and bracing.)
⚠️ WRONG answer: relying on specialized 'corrective' orthotics (e.g., lateral wedge insoles) as a primary therapy expecting strong benefit. Discriminator: supportive footwear is a low-risk adjunct, but evidence for specialized wedge insoles is limited — it supplements, never replaces, the core measures of weight loss, exercise, and appropriate analgesia.
