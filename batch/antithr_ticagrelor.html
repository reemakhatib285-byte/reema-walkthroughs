📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Ticagrelor banner
Ticagrelor (brand Brilinta) is a reversible, direct-acting P2Y12 ADP-receptor antagonist. Unlike the thienopyridines it is NOT a prodrug, so it needs no hepatic CYP2C19 activation and gives faster, more consistent platelet inhibition. Dosed 180 mg load then 90 mg BID.
⚠️ Don't call ticagrelor a thienopyridine or prodrug — it is a cyclopentyltriazolopyrimidine that binds reversibly and works directly without CYP2C19 activation.
2. Aspirin dose ceiling
Ticagrelor must be paired with LOW-dose aspirin (<=100 mg/day maintenance). Higher aspirin doses paradoxically reduce ticagrelor's benefit (seen in PLATO subgroup). This is a unique ticagrelor-specific interaction.
⚠️ High-dose aspirin (>100 mg) lowers ticagrelor efficacy — a trap unique to ticagrelor; standard for clopidogrel/prasugrel it does not apply.
3. 180 mg loading egg
Ticagrelor loading dose is 180 mg orally, then 90 mg twice daily maintenance. Rapid onset because no hepatic activation step is required.
⚠️ 180 mg load / 90 mg BID is ticagrelor — do not confuse with prasugrel's 60 mg load / 10 mg once-daily or clopidogrel's 300–600 mg load / 75 mg daily.
4. P2Y12 gate — reversible binding
Ticagrelor binds the P2Y12 receptor REVERSIBLY at an allosteric site, so platelet function recovers as drug clears (offset ~3–5 days). Irreversible binders (clopidogrel, prasugrel) need new platelets to regenerate function (~7–10 days).
⚠️ Reversible offset means ticagrelor washes out faster than prasugrel/clopidogrel — but BID dosing is mandatory because of that same reversibility.
5. 90 mg BID egg
Maintenance ticagrelor is 90 mg twice daily (BID). The twice-daily requirement is a direct consequence of reversible, short-acting binding — missed doses quickly restore platelet activity.
⚠️ Twice-daily = ticagrelor. A once-daily P2Y12 inhibitor is prasugrel (10 mg) or clopidogrel (75 mg); BID is the ticagrelor fingerprint.
6. CYP3A4 liver — interactions
Ticagrelor is metabolized by CYP3A4. Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (ketoconazole, ritonavir, clarithromycin) raise levels and bleeding risk; strong inducers (rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine) lower levels and lose protection.
⚠️ Ticagrelor's key CYP is 3A4 (not 2C19). Clopidogrel/prasugrel depend on CYP2C19/3A4 for ACTIVATION; ticagrelor is already active and 3A4 just clears it.
7. CYP3A4 inhibitors (toxic)
Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors increase ticagrelor exposure → higher bleeding risk. Examples: azole antifungals, protease inhibitors, macrolide antibiotics. Avoid co-administration.
⚠️ A CYP3A4 inhibitor raises ticagrelor (more bleeding); an inducer lowers it (less protection) — opposite directions, commonly swapped on exams.
8. CYP3A4 inducers (no protection)
Strong CYP3A4 inducers (rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine, St John's wort) drop ticagrelor levels to subtherapeutic, risking stent thrombosis. Avoid in patients on ticagrelor.
⚠️ Inducer = loss of antiplatelet protection (thrombosis), not bleeding — easy to reverse the consequence.
9. Dyspnea 15%
Dyspnea occurs in ~15% of ticagrelor patients — a hallmark adverse effect from adenosine accumulation, not bronchospasm, so it does NOT respond to bronchodilators and is usually benign/self-limited.
⚠️ Ticagrelor dyspnea is adenosine-mediated and bronchodilator-UNresponsive — don't treat as asthma/COPD or stop the drug reflexively.
10. Ventricular pauses
Ticagrelor (via adenosine) can cause asymptomatic ventricular pauses/bradyarrhythmia, especially early. Usually benign; caution in sick-sinus or high-grade AV block without a pacemaker.
⚠️ Pauses and dyspnea share one mechanism — adenosine elevation — a ticagrelor-specific cluster absent with clopidogrel/prasugrel.
11. Stop 5 days before surgery
Hold ticagrelor 5 days before major surgery (vs ~7 days for clopidogrel, ~7 days for prasugrel) because reversible binding gives a slightly shorter offset.
⚠️ Ticagrelor 5 days, clopidogrel 5–7 days, prasugrel 7 days pre-op — prasugrel needs the longest hold due to potent irreversible inhibition.
12. Stroke history bonus (prasugrel)
Prasugrel is CONTRAINDICATED in prior stroke or TIA (excess intracranial bleeding in TRITON-TIMI 38), and not recommended in age >=75 or weight <60 kg. Ticagrelor is the preferred potent agent in those patients.
⚠️ Prior stroke/TIA = avoid prasugrel, choose ticagrelor — this is the single most-tested prasugrel contraindication.
13. Prasugrel 60/10 mg
Prasugrel is a thienopyridine prodrug, irreversible P2Y12 blocker, loaded 60 mg then 10 mg once daily. Most potent thienopyridine; reserved for ACS with PCI in patients without stroke history.
⚠️ Prasugrel is given AFTER coronary anatomy is known (at PCI), unlike ticagrelor/clopidogrel which can be pre-loaded upstream.
14. Bentracimab reversal
Bentracimab (PB2452) is an investigational humanized monoclonal antibody Fab fragment that binds and neutralizes ticagrelor, rapidly restoring platelet function. It is reversal specific to ticagrelor — under development, not yet standard of care.
⚠️ Bentracimab reverses ticagrelor only (it binds the free drug). It does NOT reverse clopidogrel or prasugrel, which are irreversible and need platelet transfusion.
15. Platelet transfusion futility
For irreversible thienopyridines (clopidogrel, prasugrel) the only rescue for bleeding is platelet transfusion. For ticagrelor, transfused platelets are quickly inhibited by circulating drug, so transfusion is far less effective — hence bentracimab.
⚠️ Platelet transfusion helps clopidogrel/prasugrel bleeds but is largely futile for ticagrelor — its reversible drug keeps binding new platelets.
