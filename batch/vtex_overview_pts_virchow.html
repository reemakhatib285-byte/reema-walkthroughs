📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Damaged venous valves
Deep vein thrombosis injures valves; incompetent valves cause venous reflux and chronic venous insufficiency. DVT is the proximate event for both PE and post-thrombotic syndrome. Proximal (popliteal/femoral/iliac) DVT carries far higher embolization risk than isolated distal calf DVT.
⚠️ Superficial thrombophlebitis is NOT VTE and rarely embolizes — the board wants DEEP vein involvement before anticoagulating for PE risk.
2. Lower extremity 10x more common
Lower-extremity DVT is ~10x more common than upper-extremity DVT. Upper-extremity DVT is tied to indwelling catheters, pacemakers/ICDs, and effort thrombosis (Paget-Schroetter). Lower limb is the dominant PE source.
⚠️ New upper-extremity DVT with no catheter → think thoracic outlet (Paget-Schroetter) in a young athlete, not simply 'rare site'.
3. Upper extremity — catheters/pacemakers
Catheter-associated and device-lead thrombosis are the leading causes of upper-extremity DVT. Management favors anticoagulation with catheter retention if still needed and functional, rather than automatic removal.
⚠️ Don't reflexively pull a still-needed central line for catheter-associated DVT — anticoagulate and keep the line if functional.
4. Post-thrombotic syndrome (PTS)
PTS follows 20-50% of proximal DVTs: chronic leg pain, edema, hyperpigmentation and venous stasis ulcers. Graduated compression stockings and early ambulation reduce its burden. It reflects valvular damage, not recurrent clot.
⚠️ PTS is a chronic SEQUELA, not acute recurrent DVT — new unilateral swelling years later still warrants Doppler, but PTS itself is not re-treated with full anticoagulation.
5. Medial malleolus ulcer
Venous stasis ulcers classically sit over the medial malleolus (gaiter area) with irregular borders and exudate. Contrast with arterial ulcers (lateral/distal, punched-out, painful) and neuropathic ulcers (plantar pressure points).
⚠️ Medial malleolus + brown skin = VENOUS ulcer (compression). A lateral, punched-out, painful ulcer with absent pulses = ARTERIAL — compression would worsen it.
6. Virchow's triad
Virchow's triad — venous stasis, endothelial injury, and hypercoagulability — underlies all thrombosis. Each clinical risk factor maps to one leg (immobility=stasis, surgery/trauma=injury, malignancy/thrombophilia=hypercoagulability).
⚠️ All three legs need not be present — a single strong factor (e.g., active cancer) suffices to drive VTE risk.
7. Factor V Leiden / Protein C
Factor V Leiden is the most common INHERITED thrombophilia: a mutated factor V resists cleavage by activated protein C (APC resistance). It chiefly raises venous, not arterial, risk. Heterozygotes have modest risk; homozygotes much higher.
⚠️ Factor V Leiden causes APC RESISTANCE — don't confuse with protein C DEFICIENCY. Both are venous, but the mechanism (resistant substrate vs. missing enzyme) is the discriminator.
8. PE chart wall — MI/HF/stroke
Untreated/recurrent PE drives RV pressure overload, raising long-term cardiovascular morbidity. Acute PE classically presents with pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea, and tachycardia; massive PE causes obstructive shock.
⚠️ Sudden dyspnea + hypoxia + clear lungs + tachycardia = PE until proven otherwise — don't anchor on 'atypical MI'.
9. Massive high-risk (5-10%)
Massive (high-risk) PE = sustained hypotension (SBP <90 or drop ≥40 for ≥15 min) or shock. It accounts for ~5-10% of PE and demands immediate reperfusion — systemic thrombolysis (tPA) is first-line if no contraindication.
⚠️ Hypotension defines MASSIVE PE — give thrombolysis. RV strain on echo WITHOUT hypotension is submassive (intermediate), not massive.
10. Submassive intermediate (20-25%)
Submassive (intermediate-risk) PE = normotensive but with RV dysfunction (echo/CT) and/or positive troponin/BNP. Anticoagulate; consider thrombolysis only if the patient deteriorates ('clinical worsening').
⚠️ Don't thrombolyse every submassive PE — reserve lytics for hemodynamic deterioration; routine anticoagulation otherwise.
11. Low-risk (65-75%)
Most PEs (65-75%) are low-risk: normotensive, no RV dysfunction, negative biomarkers. Anticoagulation alone is sufficient; selected low-risk patients (PESI/sPESI low) may be treated as outpatients.
⚠️ Low-risk PE = anticoagulation ONLY — adding thrombolysis adds bleeding risk with no benefit.
12. Septum shifts → ↓LV filling
Acute RV pressure overload dilates the RV and bows the interventricular septum into the LV, reducing LV preload and cardiac output — the mechanism of obstructive shock in massive PE.
⚠️ In PE shock the problem is RV failure/septal shift, not LV failure — fluids must be cautious; aggressive volume worsens RV distension.
13. RCA compression → RV ischemia
RV wall tension plus systemic hypotension reduces right coronary perfusion, causing RV ischemia and troponin leak — a poor-prognosis marker in PE. This is demand ischemia, not coronary thrombosis.
⚠️ Positive troponin in PE is RV strain — don't misroute the patient to cardiac cath for 'NSTEMI'.
14. CTEPH
Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH) develops in ~2-4% after PE from unresolved organized clot, causing progressive dyspnea and pulmonary HTN. Pulmonary thromboendarterectomy is potentially curative; lifelong anticoagulation is required.
⚠️ Persistent dyspnea/PH months after PE → V/Q scan for CTEPH (preferred screen over CTPA), then refer for endarterectomy — not just 'deconditioning'.
15. Circulatory collapse
The terminal pathway of massive PE: RV failure → low CO → obstructive shock and arrest. PEA arrest with risk factors should prompt empiric consideration of thrombolysis.
⚠️ Cardiac arrest with high PE suspicion can justify EMPIRIC thrombolysis even without confirmatory imaging.
