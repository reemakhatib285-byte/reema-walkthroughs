⚠️ Board traps 12
  1. Prerenal column — WRONG answer is treating cardiorenal or hepatorenal 'prerenal' physiology with crystalloid boluses. These patients are total-body overloaded with low EFFECTIVE volume. Discriminator: HF cardiorenal AKI needs decongestion/improved output; HRS needs albumin + vasoconstrictors (terlipressin/midodrine-octreotide) — saline worsens both despite the prerenal indices.
  2. ATN / Intrinsic column — WRONG answer is assuming all ATN starts with a high FENa. Early sepsis-, contrast-, and pigment-associated ATN can show FENa <1% before frank necrosis. Discriminator: the muddy-brown granular casts are more specific for ATN than the FENa — follow the sediment and Cr trend when the index is misleadingly low.
  3. Postrenal column — WRONG answer is ruling out postrenal AKI because the FENa is low or the ultrasound shows no hydronephrosis. Early obstruction gives low FENa, and dehydrated/encased obstruction can lack hydronephrosis. Discriminator: confirm with a post-void residual/bladder scan and, if suspicion is high, CT — relief of obstruction (Foley/stent/nephrostomy) is both diagnostic and therapeutic.
  4. Main problem — WRONG answer is applying the same treatment to all three (e.g., fluids for everything). Discriminator: fluids help prerenal, do little for established ATN (and harm if oliguric/overloaded), and are irrelevant to postrenal, which needs decompression — match the intervention to the underlying problem.
  5. Kidney structure — WRONG answer is assuming relieved obstruction always restores baseline function. Prolonged obstruction (weeks) causes permanent tubulointerstitial damage and incomplete recovery. Discriminator: the longer the kidney has been obstructed, the lower the chance of full recovery — relieve obstruction PROMPTLY and counsel about possible residual CKD.
  6. BUN/Cr — WRONG answer is calling any BUN:Cr >20 prerenal. GI bleeding, high protein/TPN, steroids, and tetracyclines raise BUN extrarenally. Discriminator: corroborate with FENa <1% and bland sediment; a melena/hematemesis history explains a high BUN:Cr without true prerenal physiology.
  7. FeNa — FeNa <1% always = prerenal — this is the WRONG answer. Low-FENa EXCEPTIONS (intrinsic AKI): early sepsis, contrast nephropathy, pigment nephropathy (rhabdo/hemolysis), early/acute GN, and HRS. Discriminator: when low FENa coexists with muddy-brown casts, elevated CK, recent contrast, or RBC casts, the lesion is intrinsic — pair FENa with the sediment.
  8. FeUrea — WRONG answer is using FENa to call ATN in a patient actively on furosemide. Diuretics force natriuresis and push FENa >1% even in true prerenal states. Discriminator: switch to FeUrea — a value <35% indicates prerenal AKI despite a misleadingly high FENa under diuresis.
  9. Urine Na — WRONG answer is interpreting urine Na in isolation right after a diuretic dose. Loop/thiazide diuretics raise urine Na regardless of volume status. Discriminator: time the sample away from diuretics, or rely on FeUrea; and remember a low urine Na is one of the low-FENa exception scenarios (sepsis/contrast/pigment) when intrinsic injury is present.
  10. Urine osm — WRONG answer is expecting high urine osm to persist into established ATN or after diuretics/osmotic agents. Loss of concentrating ability (isosthenuria) is a hallmark of tubular injury. Discriminator: urine osm <350 with muddy-brown casts confirms ATN; a urine osm >500 in oliguric AKI points to a still-functional prerenal nephron.
  11. Urine sediment — WRONG answer is overriding the sediment with a conflicting FENa. The sediment is MORE specific for localizing the lesion than the indices. Discriminator: muddy-brown granular casts diagnose ATN even if FENa is <1% (early sepsis/contrast/pigment exception); hyaline-only/bland sediment supports prerenal even if a diuretic pushed FENa above 1%.
  12. Response to fluids — ATN responds to a fluid bolus — this is the WRONG answer. Once tubular necrosis is established, fluids do not reverse it and excess fluid causes pulmonary edema in an oliguric patient. Discriminator: a creatinine that falls promptly after volume = prerenal; failure to improve (with muddy-brown casts, FENa >2%) = ATN — stop fluids, avoid nephrotoxins, and provide supportive/RRT care as needed.
📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. Prerenal column
The hypoperfusion / 'conserving' pattern. A structurally intact nephron under RAAS + ADH activation avidly reabsorbs Na and water: FENa <1%, urine Na <20, urine osm >500, BUN:Cr >20, bland/hyaline-cast sediment. Causes: volume loss, HF, cirrhosis, sepsis, NSAIDs/ACE-I. Fully reversible if perfusion is restored before ischemic ATN develops.
⚠️ WRONG answer is treating cardiorenal or hepatorenal 'prerenal' physiology with crystalloid boluses. These patients are total-body overloaded with low EFFECTIVE volume. Discriminator: HF cardiorenal AKI needs decongestion/improved output; HRS needs albumin + vasoconstrictors (terlipressin/midodrine-octreotide) — saline worsens both despite the prerenal indices.
2. ATN / Intrinsic column
The tubular-injury / 'wasting' pattern. Damaged tubules cannot reabsorb Na or concentrate urine: FENa >2%, urine Na >40, urine osm <350 (isosthenuric), BUN:Cr 10-15, MUDDY-BROWN granular and RTEC casts. Causes: ischemia, sepsis, nephrotoxins (aminoglycosides, contrast, cisplatin), pigments (rhabdo/hemolysis). Phases: initiation → maintenance (oliguric) → recovery (polyuric).
⚠️ WRONG answer is assuming all ATN starts with a high FENa. Early sepsis-, contrast-, and pigment-associated ATN can show FENa <1% before frank necrosis. Discriminator: the muddy-brown granular casts are more specific for ATN than the FENa — follow the sediment and Cr trend when the index is misleadingly low.
3. Postrenal column
The obstruction pattern. Indices are VARIABLE: early on the kidney behaves prerenal-like (low FENa, concentrated urine); with prolonged obstruction it shifts to an ATN-like wasting pattern (high FENa, isosthenuria). Requires bilateral obstruction (or a solitary functioning kidney). Diagnosed by bladder scan + renal ultrasound; improves only after relieving the obstruction.
⚠️ WRONG answer is ruling out postrenal AKI because the FENa is low or the ultrasound shows no hydronephrosis. Early obstruction gives low FENa, and dehydrated/encased obstruction can lack hydronephrosis. Discriminator: confirm with a post-void residual/bladder scan and, if suspicion is high, CT — relief of obstruction (Foley/stent/nephrostomy) is both diagnostic and therapeutic.
4. Main problem
The core lesion defining each column: prerenal = ↓renal PERFUSION (intact nephron, hypoperfused); ATN/intrinsic = tubular/parenchymal INJURY (necrotic tubular cells); postrenal = mechanical OBSTRUCTION to urine outflow. Identifying which 'problem' is present dictates therapy — restore perfusion, support and remove toxins, or relieve obstruction, respectively.
⚠️ WRONG answer is applying the same treatment to all three (e.g., fluids for everything). Discriminator: fluids help prerenal, do little for established ATN (and harm if oliguric/overloaded), and are irrelevant to postrenal, which needs decompression — match the intervention to the underlying problem.
5. Kidney structure
Prerenal: structurally INTACT (functional, fully reversible). ATN: tubular cells DAMAGED/necrotic (structural injury, recovery over days-weeks). Postrenal: initially intact but becomes damaged if obstruction is PROLONGED (irreversible after weeks of high back-pressure). This explains why prerenal reverses fast while delayed relief of obstruction may not fully recover.
⚠️ WRONG answer is assuming relieved obstruction always restores baseline function. Prolonged obstruction (weeks) causes permanent tubulointerstitial damage and incomplete recovery. Discriminator: the longer the kidney has been obstructed, the lower the chance of full recovery — relieve obstruction PROMPTLY and counsel about possible residual CKD.
6. BUN/Cr
Prerenal >20:1 — low tubular flow drives urea reabsorption (with Na/water) while creatinine is not reabsorbed. ATN 10-15:1 — impaired tubular reabsorption normalizes the ratio. Postrenal variable depending on duration/phase.
⚠️ WRONG answer is calling any BUN:Cr >20 prerenal. GI bleeding, high protein/TPN, steroids, and tetracyclines raise BUN extrarenally. Discriminator: corroborate with FENa <1% and bland sediment; a melena/hematemesis history explains a high BUN:Cr without true prerenal physiology.
7. FeNa
FENa = (urine Na × plasma Cr)/(plasma Na × urine Cr) × 100. Prerenal <1% (Na avidly conserved); ATN >2% (Na wasted by injured tubules); postrenal variable. It is the most-tested discriminator on boards — but is reliable only in oliguric AKI off diuretics.
⚠️ FeNa <1% always = prerenal — this is the WRONG answer. Low-FENa EXCEPTIONS (intrinsic AKI): early sepsis, contrast nephropathy, pigment nephropathy (rhabdo/hemolysis), early/acute GN, and HRS. Discriminator: when low FENa coexists with muddy-brown casts, elevated CK, recent contrast, or RBC casts, the lesion is intrinsic — pair FENa with the sediment.
8. FeUrea
Fractional excretion of urea: prerenal <35%, ATN >50%. Because urea handling is largely independent of the distal Na transport that diuretics block, FeUrea is the preferred index when the patient is on loop/thiazide diuretics (which artificially raise FENa).
⚠️ WRONG answer is using FENa to call ATN in a patient actively on furosemide. Diuretics force natriuresis and push FENa >1% even in true prerenal states. Discriminator: switch to FeUrea — a value <35% indicates prerenal AKI despite a misleadingly high FENa under diuresis.
9. Urine Na
Prerenal urine Na <20 mEq/L (RAAS/ADH-driven sodium conservation); ATN urine Na >40 mEq/L (injured tubules cannot reabsorb Na). It tracks with FENa but is a cruder, single-sample measure subject to the same exceptions and diuretic effects.
⚠️ WRONG answer is interpreting urine Na in isolation right after a diuretic dose. Loop/thiazide diuretics raise urine Na regardless of volume status. Discriminator: time the sample away from diuretics, or rely on FeUrea; and remember a low urine Na is one of the low-FENa exception scenarios (sepsis/contrast/pigment) when intrinsic injury is present.
10. Urine osm
Prerenal urine osm >500 mOsm/kg — the intact, ADH-responsive nephron concentrates urine. ATN urine osm <350 (isosthenuric, fixed near plasma ~300) — injured tubules lose concentrating ability. A high urine osm with oliguria favors a functional (prerenal) kidney.
⚠️ WRONG answer is expecting high urine osm to persist into established ATN or after diuretics/osmotic agents. Loss of concentrating ability (isosthenuria) is a hallmark of tubular injury. Discriminator: urine osm <350 with muddy-brown casts confirms ATN; a urine osm >500 in oliguric AKI points to a still-functional prerenal nephron.
11. Urine sediment
The most specific localizing test. Prerenal: BLAND, occasional hyaline casts. ATN: MUDDY-BROWN granular + renal tubular epithelial cell casts. Postrenal: crystals, hematuria, or bland. (Adjacent intrinsic patterns for contrast: RBC casts/dysmorphic RBCs = GN; WBC casts + eosinophiluria = AIN.)
⚠️ WRONG answer is overriding the sediment with a conflicting FENa. The sediment is MORE specific for localizing the lesion than the indices. Discriminator: muddy-brown granular casts diagnose ATN even if FENa is <1% (early sepsis/contrast/pigment exception); hyaline-only/bland sediment supports prerenal even if a diuretic pushed FENa above 1%.
12. Response to fluids
Prerenal IMPROVES with restored perfusion/volume (creatinine falls within 24-72h). ATN does NOT rapidly improve with fluids — it recovers over days to weeks of supportive care. Postrenal improves only after the obstruction is RELIEVED (Foley/stent/nephrostomy), not with fluids. A fluid challenge is therefore both diagnostic and therapeutic in prerenal AKI.
⚠️ ATN responds to a fluid bolus — this is the WRONG answer. Once tubular necrosis is established, fluids do not reverse it and excess fluid causes pulmonary edema in an oliguric patient. Discriminator: a creatinine that falls promptly after volume = prerenal; failure to improve (with muddy-brown casts, FENa >2%) = ATN — stop fluids, avoid nephrotoxins, and provide supportive/RRT care as needed.
