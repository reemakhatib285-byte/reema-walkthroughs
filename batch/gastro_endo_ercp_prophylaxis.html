📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Side-viewing duodenoscope mechanic
ERCP uses a side-viewing duodenoscope passed to the second part of the duodenum, cannulating the ampulla of Vater to access the biliary/pancreatic ducts and extract stones. It is therapeutic, not just diagnostic. Indicated for choledocholithiasis with cholangitis or biliary obstruction.
2. ERCP indication tag
Strongest indication = obstructive jaundice with ascending cholangitis/biliary sepsis (Charcot triad: fever, RUQ pain, jaundice; Reynolds pentad adds hypotension + confusion) → urgent ERCP for drainage. Also for retained CBD stones and malignant obstruction stenting.
3. MRCP & EUS FIRST (gold star)
GOLD STAR: when a CBD stone is only intermediate/low probability, do non-invasive MRCP or EUS FIRST to confirm before committing to ERCP. ERCP carries real procedural risk (pancreatitis, perforation) and shouldn't be diagnostic-only. Reserve ERCP for when intervention is needed.
⚠️ 'Go straight to ERCP to look for a possible stone' → wrong; if a stone is unlikely, MRCP or EUS first spares unnecessary ERCP-related pancreatitis.
4. Post-ERCP pancreatitis volcano (~5%)
Post-ERCP pancreatitis is the MOST COMMON complication, ~5% overall. Caused by ampullary trauma/contrast injection. Presents with abdominal pain and elevated lipase hours after the procedure. Prevent with aggressive hydration, rectal NSAIDs, and a pancreatic-duct stent in high-risk cases.
5. Young/anicteric/narrow duct = HIGH risk
Highest pancreatitis risk: young, female, anicteric (normal bilirubin), normal/narrow non-dilated duct, and suspected/confirmed sphincter-of-Oddi dysfunction — risk climbs to 25–30%. Difficult cannulation and pancreatic-duct injection add risk. These patients most need prophylaxis.
⚠️ 'A young woman with a normal-caliber duct is low pancreatitis risk' → wrong; that exact profile (young, female, anicteric, narrow duct, SOD) is the HIGHEST risk, 25–30%.
6. Rectal indomethacin + PD stent (prophylaxis)
Post-ERCP pancreatitis prophylaxis = rectal indomethacin (or diclofenac) immediately + prophylactic pancreatic-duct stent in high-risk patients, plus periprocedural IV fluids (lactated Ringer's). This is the highest-yield prevention combo on exams.
7. Ascending cholangitis fire
Ascending cholangitis can follow ERCP, especially if biliary drainage is incomplete — bacteria seed the stagnant obstructed system. This is why antibiotic prophylaxis is given when drainage is expected to be incomplete.
8. Duodenal perforation crack
Duodenal/retroperitoneal perforation is a rare but serious ERCP complication, often related to sphincterotomy or scope trauma. Sphincterotomy itself carries ~1% bleeding/perforation risk.
9. Sphincterotomy ~1%
Sphincterotomy carries about 1% bleeding risk. Bleeding can be immediate or delayed; managed endoscopically. Avoid in coagulopathy/antithrombotics where feasible.
10. GIVE prophylaxis — cefazolin (gold star)
GOLD STAR: GIVE antibiotic prophylaxis (cefazolin/ceftriaxone) when biliary drainage will be incomplete (e.g., hilar stricture, PSC, after liver transplant) and for PEG tube placement. Also for EUS-FNA of cystic lesions. Covers seeding of obstructed/contaminated systems.
11. PEG tube — cefazolin
PEG (percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy) placement → give a single prophylactic dose of cefazolin to prevent peristomal wound infection. One of the few endoscopic procedures with a clear antibiotic indication.
12. SKIP for solid EUS-FNA (red X)
EUS-FNA of a SOLID lesion does NOT need antibiotic prophylaxis. Cystic lesions are the exception (give). 'Skip' and 'always' are both wrong defaults — base it on the indication.
⚠️ 'Give antibiotics before every EUS-FNA' → wrong; solid-lesion FNA needs none — only cystic-lesion FNA gets prophylaxis.
13. Cystic EUS-FNA — GIVE
EUS-FNA of a CYSTIC lesion (e.g., pancreatic cyst) → GIVE prophylaxis (fluoroquinolone/cephalosporin) to prevent cyst infection. Contrast with solid lesions, which need none.
14. Endocarditis / prosthetic joint (red X)
GI endoscopy is NOT an indication for endocarditis prophylaxis (current guidelines dropped GI/GU procedures) and prosthetic joints do NOT require antibiotics for endoscopy. Prophylaxis is for biliary drainage/PEG/cystic-FNA, never these.
⚠️ 'Give antibiotics before colonoscopy/ERCP to prevent endocarditis or protect a prosthetic joint' → wrong; neither needs GI-endoscopy antibiotic prophylaxis.
