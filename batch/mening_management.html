⚠️ Board traps 15
  1. Triage: act within 60 min — WRONG: withholding antibiotics until after CT and LP are completed. Every hour of delay increases death and disability — give empiric therapy immediately after blood cultures; CSF Gram stain/culture yield drops only modestly in the first few hours, so the patient's life takes priority over a perfect culture.
  2. Decision fork: who needs CT first — WRONG: getting a CT on every suspected meningitis patient 'to be safe.' Universal pre-LP CT delays diagnosis and treatment in low-risk patients who can be tapped immediately; reserve CT for those with the specific IDSA risk features, and never let CT delay antibiotics.
  3. Left path: direct LP — WRONG: assuming a normal neuro exam means you can safely delay antibiotics while arranging the LP. Even in the direct-LP path, if there is any hold-up obtaining CSF, give antibiotics immediately after blood cultures — do not let a 'simple' tap delay treatment.
  4. Right path: CT then LP — WRONG ordering: CT → LP → then antibiotics. The correct sequence when CT is indicated is blood cultures + steroids + antibiotics → CT → LP. Reversing it (waiting for imaging before treating) is the classic exam error and worsens outcomes.
  5. Empiric: ceftriaxone + vancomycin + acyclovir — WRONG: ceftriaxone monotherapy. Up to ~25% of pneumococci have reduced cephalosporin susceptibility, so vancomycin is mandatory in empiric coverage until susceptibilities return — dropping vancomycin risks under-treating resistant pneumococcus.
  6. Add ampicillin >50 / immunocompromised — WRONG: relying on ceftriaxone + vancomycin alone in a 65-year-old or immunosuppressed patient. Cephalosporins do not cover Listeria, so omitting ampicillin in a Listeria-risk host leaves a fatal gap — the discriminator is age >50 or impaired cell-mediated immunity.
  7. Penicillin allergy: TMP-SMX — WRONG: substituting another cephalosporin (e.g., cefepime) for ampicillin in a penicillin-allergic, Listeria-risk patient. No cephalosporin covers Listeria — the correct swap is TMP-SMX or meropenem, not another beta-lactam in the cephalosporin class.
  8. Nosocomial / post-neurosurgery — WRONG: using community-acquired ceftriaxone-based therapy after neurosurgery or in a shunt infection. Ceftriaxone does NOT cover Pseudomonas and under-covers staph — the discriminator is the healthcare-associated setting, which mandates antipseudomonal + anti-staph (MRSA) coverage.
  9. Dexamethasone BEFORE antibiotics — WRONG: giving dexamethasone after antibiotics have already been running. Once bacteriolysis and the cytokine cascade are underway, late steroid provides little benefit — but the converse trap is also wrong: do NOT delay antibiotics to obtain steroid. Give them together if steroid isn't immediately ready.
  10. Dexamethasone dose — WRONG: continuing the full 4-day steroid course regardless of the organism. If cultures reveal a non-pneumococcal pathogen in an adult (e.g., meningococcus, Listeria) or a viral cause, the benefit is unproven/absent — stop dexamethasone rather than completing the course reflexively.
  11. Dexamethasone benefit: pneumococcus — WRONG: assuming steroids help all-comers equally. The strongest evidence is pneumococcus (adults) and Hib (kids); for meningococcal, Listeria, gram-negative, or viral meningitis the benefit is not demonstrated, so steroids should be stopped once those are confirmed.
  12. Mechanism: reduces inflammation — WRONG: thinking the steroid is treating the infection. Dexamethasone is purely anti-inflammatory adjunct — it does nothing to the organism, so it must always accompany (never replace) appropriate antibiotics.
  13. Dexa improves vancomycin penetration — WRONG: giving vancomycin intrathecally to overcome poor CSF penetration. The correct fix is to RAISE the IV vancomycin dose and follow troughs (or add rifampin); routine intrathecal vancomycin is not standard for community-acquired meningitis and carries toxicity risk.
  14. Add acyclovir / ganciclovir — WRONG: using acyclovir for CMV encephalitis. Acyclovir has essentially no activity against CMV — the correct agent is ganciclovir (or foscarnet). Conversely, do not reach for ganciclovir for HSV; matching drug to virus is the tested distinction.
  15. Repeat LP / treatment duration — WRONG: applying a single fixed antibiotic course to every organism. Duration is pathogen-specific — Listeria and gram-negative bacilli need ≥3 weeks, far longer than meningococcus (7 days); under-treating Listeria leads to relapse.
📖 Read-through — all 15 symbols
1. Triage: act within 60 min
Sequence: draw blood cultures (2 sets) → give dexamethasone with/just before the first antibiotic → give empiric antibiotics, all within 60 minutes of presentation (door-to-antibiotic time drives mortality). If LP can be done immediately and no CT is indicated, tap first; otherwise antibiotics must NOT wait. Blood cultures are positive in ~50-70% and preserve a microbiologic diagnosis even when CSF is sterilized by antibiotics.
⚠️ WRONG: withholding antibiotics until after CT and LP are completed. Every hour of delay increases death and disability — give empiric therapy immediately after blood cultures; CSF Gram stain/culture yield drops only modestly in the first few hours, so the patient's life takes priority over a perfect culture.
2. Decision fork: who needs CT first
Obtain CT head BEFORE LP only if any IDSA red flag is present: focal neurologic deficit, papilledema, new-onset seizure (within 1 week), moderate-to-severe altered consciousness (GCS drop), immunocompromise (HIV, transplant, immunosuppressants), or history of CNS disease/mass. These features raise concern for a mass lesion and cerebral herniation risk from LP.
⚠️ WRONG: getting a CT on every suspected meningitis patient 'to be safe.' Universal pre-LP CT delays diagnosis and treatment in low-risk patients who can be tapped immediately; reserve CT for those with the specific IDSA risk features, and never let CT delay antibiotics.
3. Left path: direct LP
Immunocompetent patient with normal mentation and no focal deficits, no papilledema, no recent seizure → proceed DIRECTLY to LP without CT, then start dexamethasone + empiric antibiotics. Sending CSF before antibiotics (when feasible without delay) maximizes Gram stain and culture yield.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming a normal neuro exam means you can safely delay antibiotics while arranging the LP. Even in the direct-LP path, if there is any hold-up obtaining CSF, give antibiotics immediately after blood cultures — do not let a 'simple' tap delay treatment.
4. Right path: CT then LP
Concerning features → blood cultures + dexamethasone + empiric antibiotics FIRST, THEN CT to exclude a mass/herniation risk, THEN LP if CT is clear. The antibiotics-before-CT order is the high-yield point: imaging must never push back the first antibiotic dose.
⚠️ WRONG ordering: CT → LP → then antibiotics. The correct sequence when CT is indicated is blood cultures + steroids + antibiotics → CT → LP. Reversing it (waiting for imaging before treating) is the classic exam error and worsens outcomes.
5. Empiric: ceftriaxone + vancomycin + acyclovir
Standard empiric regimen for immunocompetent adults: ceftriaxone 2 g IV q12h (covers S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, H. influenzae) PLUS vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h (covers ceftriaxone-resistant pneumococcus). Add acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h if HSV encephalitis is on the differential (altered mentation, temporal-lobe signs, seizures).
⚠️ WRONG: ceftriaxone monotherapy. Up to ~25% of pneumococci have reduced cephalosporin susceptibility, so vancomycin is mandatory in empiric coverage until susceptibilities return — dropping vancomycin risks under-treating resistant pneumococcus.
6. Add ampicillin >50 / immunocompromised
Add AMPICILLIN 2 g IV q4h for Listeria monocytogenes coverage in age >50, neonates, pregnancy, alcoholism, and immunocompromise (steroids, transplant, HIV, malignancy). Cephalosporins have NO Listeria activity, so ampicillin (or penicillin G) is the dedicated agent for this gram-positive rod.
⚠️ WRONG: relying on ceftriaxone + vancomycin alone in a 65-year-old or immunosuppressed patient. Cephalosporins do not cover Listeria, so omitting ampicillin in a Listeria-risk host leaves a fatal gap — the discriminator is age >50 or impaired cell-mediated immunity.
7. Penicillin allergy: TMP-SMX
For Listeria coverage in a true penicillin-allergic patient, use TMP-SMX (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) IV; meropenem is an alternative that also covers Listeria plus gram-negatives. Note meropenem (a carbapenem) can usually be given to penicillin-allergic patients with low cross-reactivity except in severe/anaphylactic histories.
⚠️ WRONG: substituting another cephalosporin (e.g., cefepime) for ampicillin in a penicillin-allergic, Listeria-risk patient. No cephalosporin covers Listeria — the correct swap is TMP-SMX or meropenem, not another beta-lactam in the cephalosporin class.
8. Nosocomial / post-neurosurgery
Healthcare-associated meningitis (post-neurosurgery, CSF shunt/drain, penetrating head trauma, neutropenic) needs vancomycin (MRSA, coagulase-negative staph) PLUS an antipseudomonal beta-lactam: cefepime, ceftazidime, or meropenem. These hosts harbor staphylococci, Pseudomonas, and other gram-negatives not covered by ceftriaxone.
⚠️ WRONG: using community-acquired ceftriaxone-based therapy after neurosurgery or in a shunt infection. Ceftriaxone does NOT cover Pseudomonas and under-covers staph — the discriminator is the healthcare-associated setting, which mandates antipseudomonal + anti-staph (MRSA) coverage.
9. Dexamethasone BEFORE antibiotics
Give dexamethasone 10 mg IV 15-20 minutes BEFORE, or concurrently with, the first antibiotic dose in suspected bacterial meningitis. Antibiotics lyse bacteria and release cell-wall fragments that trigger a cytokine surge; pre-dosing steroid blunts this inflammatory storm and reduces death and neurologic sequelae (notably in pneumococcal disease).
⚠️ WRONG: giving dexamethasone after antibiotics have already been running. Once bacteriolysis and the cytokine cascade are underway, late steroid provides little benefit — but the converse trap is also wrong: do NOT delay antibiotics to obtain steroid. Give them together if steroid isn't immediately ready.
10. Dexamethasone dose
Dexamethasone dosing: 10 mg IV every 6 hours for 4 days (pediatric: 0.15 mg/kg q6h x4 days). Continue only if CSF/cultures confirm S. pneumoniae (adults) or H. influenzae (children); discontinue if another organism or a non-bacterial cause is identified.
⚠️ WRONG: continuing the full 4-day steroid course regardless of the organism. If cultures reveal a non-pneumococcal pathogen in an adult (e.g., meningococcus, Listeria) or a viral cause, the benefit is unproven/absent — stop dexamethasone rather than completing the course reflexively.
11. Dexamethasone benefit: pneumococcus
Dexamethasone's proven mortality benefit is greatest in PNEUMOCOCCAL meningitis in adults; in children it reduces sensorineural HEARING LOSS in H. influenzae type b meningitis. Benefit is established mainly in high-income settings; data in resource-limited settings and in non-pneumococcal organisms are weak.
⚠️ WRONG: assuming steroids help all-comers equally. The strongest evidence is pneumococcus (adults) and Hib (kids); for meningococcal, Listeria, gram-negative, or viral meningitis the benefit is not demonstrated, so steroids should be stopped once those are confirmed.
12. Mechanism: reduces inflammation
Mechanism: antibiotics rapidly kill bacteria, releasing cell-wall components (pneumococcal peptidoglycan, LPS) that activate macrophages/microglia to release TNF-alpha and IL-1beta. This cytokine surge drives meningeal inflammation, cerebral edema, vasculitis, and raised ICP. Dexamethasone suppresses this host inflammatory response, limiting secondary CNS injury.
⚠️ WRONG: thinking the steroid is treating the infection. Dexamethasone is purely anti-inflammatory adjunct — it does nothing to the organism, so it must always accompany (never replace) appropriate antibiotics.
13. Dexa improves vancomycin penetration
Dexamethasone reduces meningeal inflammation and thereby decreases blood-brain-barrier permeability, lowering vancomycin penetration into CSF. When giving dexamethasone WITH vancomycin for resistant pneumococcus, use higher vancomycin dosing (target trough 15-20, ~45-60 mg/kg/day) and consider adding rifampin if response is poor.
⚠️ WRONG: giving vancomycin intrathecally to overcome poor CSF penetration. The correct fix is to RAISE the IV vancomycin dose and follow troughs (or add rifampin); routine intrathecal vancomycin is not standard for community-acquired meningitis and carries toxicity risk.
14. Add acyclovir / ganciclovir
Add acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h empirically when HSV (or VZV) encephalitis is suspected — fever with altered mentation, focal/temporal-lobe signs, seizures, or CSF lymphocytosis with RBCs. Acyclovir covers HSV-1/2 and VZV; CMV requires ganciclovir/foscarnet instead. Maintain hydration to prevent acyclovir crystalline nephropathy.
⚠️ WRONG: using acyclovir for CMV encephalitis. Acyclovir has essentially no activity against CMV — the correct agent is ganciclovir (or foscarnet). Conversely, do not reach for ganciclovir for HSV; matching drug to virus is the tested distinction.
15. Repeat LP / treatment duration
Added clinical anchor: typical treatment durations are N. meningitidis 7 days, H. influenzae 7 days, S. pneumoniae 10-14 days, Listeria ≥21 days, gram-negative bacilli 21 days. Repeat LP is indicated if there is no clinical improvement at 48h, for resistant pneumococcus on steroids, gram-negative bacillary meningitis, or neonates.
⚠️ WRONG: applying a single fixed antibiotic course to every organism. Duration is pathogen-specific — Listeria and gram-negative bacilli need ≥3 weeks, far longer than meningococcus (7 days); under-treating Listeria leads to relapse.
