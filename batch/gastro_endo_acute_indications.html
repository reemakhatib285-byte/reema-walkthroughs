📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Low-hemoglobin / anemic patient
Iron-deficiency anemia in a man or postmenopausal woman is GI cancer until proven otherwise — it drives the work-up to colonoscopy (and often EGD). Microcytic anemia + low ferritin = occult GI blood loss source must be found.
2. Occult blood positive paper
A positive fecal occult blood / FIT result mandates a full colonoscopy, not a repeat stool test. The positive test is the trigger to scope the entire colon to find the lesion.
3. STOOL TEST repeat (red X)
Repeating a stool test in an anemic or occult-blood-positive patient is wrong — you must go to COLONOSCOPY. The caption notes ~30% have polyps and ~10% have cancer, which is what makes the colonoscopy urgent.
⚠️ 'Repeat the stool test in an anemic patient with positive occult blood' → wrong; an anemic man/postmenopausal woman needs colonoscopy (~30% polyps, ~10% cancer).
4. 30% polyps / 10% cancer urgency
Yield statistics: roughly 30% of these patients have polyps and ~10% have colorectal cancer — the high pretest probability of malignancy is the board's justification for direct colonoscopy over conservative repeat testing.
5. Alarm features banner
Dyspepsia/dysphagia with ALARM features → urgent upper endoscopy: weight loss, GI bleeding/anemia, progressive dysphagia, odynophagia, persistent vomiting, impacted food bolus, age >60 with new symptoms, palpable mass. No 'test-and-treat' for H. pylori until cancer excluded.
6. Band ligation — varices (gold star)
GOLD STAR: bleeding esophageal varices → endoscopic band ligation (EVL) is first-line, plus a vasoactive drug (octreotide/terlipressin) and prophylactic antibiotics (ceftriaxone). EVL beats sclerotherapy. Pre-endoscopy: resuscitate, restrictive transfusion to Hb ~7.
7. TIPS / Blakemore / glue backups
Refractory/uncontrolled variceal bleeding → TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt) as rescue; balloon tamponade (Sengstaken-Blakemore) as a temporizing bridge. GASTRIC varices are treated with cyanoacrylate glue injection rather than banding.
8. Peptic ulcer stigmata ladder
Forrest rebleeding ladder (high→low risk): active arterial spurt > non-bleeding visible vessel > adherent clot > flat pigmented spot > clean base. High-risk stigmata (spurt, visible vessel, ± clot) get endoscopic therapy; clean base / flat spot do not and can feed early.
9. IV PPI
High-risk bleeding peptic ulcers → IV PPI (e.g., pantoprazole bolus then infusion) raises gastric pH to stabilize clot. Combined with endoscopic hemostasis. Test and treat H. pylori; stop NSAIDs.
10. Epinephrine alone insufficient (red X)
Epinephrine injection achieves temporary tamponade/vasoconstriction but has high rebleed rates ALONE — it must be combined with a second modality: thermal coagulation, hemoclips, or sclerosant. Dual therapy is the standard for high-risk ulcers.
⚠️ 'Treat a spurting ulcer with epinephrine injection alone' → wrong; epinephrine monotherapy rebleeds — combine with clips or thermal/second method.
11. Ogilvie pseudo-obstruction
Ogilvie = acute colonic pseudo-obstruction: massive colonic dilation without mechanical cause, in sick/post-op/elderly patients. Risk of ischemia/perforation rises sharply when cecal diameter >12 cm. First exclude mechanical obstruction (CT) and toxic megacolon.
12. Ruler 12 cm cecum
Cecal diameter >12 cm is the threshold of concern for perforation in Ogilvie syndrome and prompts active intervention (neostigmine, decompression) rather than continued watchful waiting.
13. Neostigmine + fix electrolytes
Ogilvie management: correct electrolytes (K, Mg, Ca), stop opioids/anticholinergics, NG/rectal tube. IV neostigmine is the pharmacologic treatment (monitor for bradycardia, have atropine ready). Colonoscopic decompression if neostigmine fails.
14. Surgery first (red X)
Surgery is NOT first-line for Ogilvie — it's reserved for ischemia, perforation, or failure of medical therapy plus decompression. The board answer is neostigmine + supportive measures first.
⚠️ 'Take the Ogilvie patient to surgery first' → wrong; first-line is neostigmine + electrolyte correction + decompression — surgery only for perforation/ischemia.
