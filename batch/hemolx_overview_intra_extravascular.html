📖 Read-through — all 16 symbols
1. Intake wall 2x2 grid
Classify hemolysis on two axes: inherited vs acquired, and intracorpuscular (defect inside RBC) vs extracorpuscular (defect outside). Inherited+intracorpuscular = membrane/enzyme/Hb defects; acquired+extracorpuscular = immune, MAHA, infection. This 2x2 organizes the whole differential.
⚠️ Mixing axes — e.g. calling PNH inherited; PNH is an ACQUIRED intracorpuscular defect (somatic PIGA mutation).
2. PNH = acquired intracorpuscular
PNH is the exception: an ACQUIRED (somatic PIGA mutation) yet INTRACORPUSCULAR defect, lacking GPI-anchored CD55/CD59 so complement lyses RBCs. It is the one acquired cause that is intrinsic to the cell. Causes intravascular hemolysis and thrombosis.
⚠️ Classifying PNH as inherited because the defect is intrinsic; it is acquired (clonal somatic mutation).
3. Hereditary spherocytosis
Inherited intracorpuscular causes: membrane (hereditary spherocytosis — ankyrin/spectrin, EMA-binding test, splenectomy curative), enzyme (G6PD, PK), and hemoglobinopathy (sickle, thalassemia). These RBCs are destroyed extravascularly in the spleen.
⚠️ Ordering osmotic fragility as definitive for HS; EMA binding flow cytometry is now the preferred confirmatory test.
4. Hypersplenism / extravascular
Extravascular hemolysis: splenic macrophages remove antibody- or defect-coated RBCs. Markers — high UNCONJUGATED bilirubin, high urobilinogen, splenomegaly, spherocytes, pigment gallstones. Haptoglobin only mildly low. Warm AIHA and HS are classic.
⚠️ Expecting hemoglobinuria in extravascular hemolysis; free Hb stays low, so urine hemoglobin is an INTRAvascular marker.
5. Unconjugated bilirubin
Hemolysis releases heme, raising INDIRECT (unconjugated) bilirubin and urobilinogen — jaundice and pigment (calcium bilirubinate) gallstones. LDH rises and haptoglobin falls. These confirm hemolysis regardless of site.
⚠️ Seeing conjugated hyperbilirubinemia and calling it hemolysis; hemolytic jaundice is unconjugated/indirect.
6. Intravascular hemolysis
Intravascular hemolysis lyses RBCs in the circulation: very low/absent haptoglobin, high LDH, hemoglobinemia, hemoglobinuria, hemosiderinuria. Causes — PNH, MAHA (TTP/HUS/DIC), transfusion reactions, mechanical valves, severe G6PD crisis.
⚠️ Expecting prominent splenomegaly in intravascular hemolysis; destruction is in vessels, spleen is not the main site.
7. Haptoglobin
Free plasma Hb binds haptoglobin, which is then cleared — so haptoglobin DROPS, most markedly in intravascular hemolysis. A low haptoglobin with high LDH and reticulocytosis is the sensitive signature of hemolysis.
⚠️ Using a normal haptoglobin to exclude hemolysis; it is an acute-phase reactant and can be falsely normal with inflammation.
8. Hemoglobinuria
When haptoglobin is saturated, free Hb is filtered: hemoglobinuria (early) and hemosiderinuria (days later, from tubular cell turnover). These are specific for INTRAvascular hemolysis. Dipstick positive for blood but no RBCs on micro.
⚠️ Calling hematuria when dipstick is blood-positive but no RBCs are seen; that pattern is hemoglobinuria/myoglobinuria.
9. Erythroid hyperplasia
Marrow compensates with erythroid hyperplasia driven by EPO, raising the reticulocyte count and reticulocyte production index. A retic count that fails to rise signals an aplastic crisis or coexisting marrow problem.
⚠️ Expecting a low retic count in hemolysis; reticulocytosis is the hallmark unless marrow fails (aplastic crisis).
10. Reticulocyte counter
Reticulocytosis confirms an appropriate marrow response to hemolysis. Use the reticulocyte production index (corrected for anemia/shift) to judge adequacy. Hemolytic anemia is a hyperproliferative (high-retic) anemia.
⚠️ Confusing high retics from hemolysis with high retics from acute blood loss; both raise retics — use clinical context and hemolysis labs.
11. Compensated vs decompensated
Compensated hemolysis: marrow production matches destruction, hemoglobin normal despite high turnover (high LDH/retics, low haptoglobin, no anemia). Decompensated: destruction outpaces production and anemia appears. Severity depends on this balance.
⚠️ Excluding hemolysis because hemoglobin is normal; compensated hemolysis has normal Hb with abnormal hemolysis labs.
12. Aplastic crisis
Parvovirus B19 infects erythroid progenitors and halts production for ~7-10 days. In chronic hemolysis (HS, sickle) this causes a sudden APLASTIC CRISIS — abrupt Hb drop with a LOW reticulocyte count. Supportive transfusion; self-limited.
⚠️ Seeing a low retic in a known hemolytic patient and missing parvovirus B19 aplastic crisis.
13. Iron consequences
INTRAvascular hemolysis loses iron in the urine (hemosiderinuria) and can cause iron DEFICIENCY (e.g. chronic PNH). EXTRAvascular hemolysis recycles iron internally and, with transfusions, leads to iron OVERLOAD. Site predicts iron balance.
⚠️ Assuming all chronic hemolysis causes iron overload; chronic intravascular hemolysis can deplete iron via urinary loss.
14. Schistocytes / MAHA
Mechanical/microangiopathic hemolysis shears RBCs into schistocytes — seen in thrombotic microangiopathies, DIC, malignant hypertension, prosthetic valves. Schistocytes plus low platelets demand urgent workup. This is intravascular and often life-threatening.
⚠️ Dismissing a few schistocytes; with thrombocytopenia they signal microangiopathy needing emergent evaluation.
15. WBC / platelet bystanders
Pure hemolysis spares WBCs and platelets. If anemia is accompanied by cytopenias (low WBC/platelets), think marrow infiltration, PNH, TMA, or hypersplenism rather than isolated hemolysis. Pattern of cell lines narrows the cause.
⚠️ Calling isolated hemolysis when pancytopenia is present; multilineage cytopenia points to PNH, TMA, or marrow disease.
16. Direct antiglobulin (Coombs)
The direct antiglobulin (Coombs) test separates immune (DAT+) from non-immune (DAT-) acquired hemolysis. DAT+ = warm/cold AIHA, drug-induced; DAT- with schistocytes = MAHA. It is the first branch point in acquired hemolysis.
⚠️ Skipping the DAT in acquired hemolysis; it is the single test that splits immune from microangiopathic causes.
