📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Emergency = end-organ damage
Hypertensive emergency = severely elevated BP (usually >180/120) WITH acute target-organ damage. It requires admission (usually ICU), IV titratable therapy, and arterial monitoring. The defining feature is the organ injury, not the BP number — a patient at 250/140 with no acute damage is an urgency, while one at 185/115 with papilledema or encephalopathy is an emergency. Goal: controlled, gradual reduction, not normalization.
⚠️ Treating a BP of 220/120 with IV antihypertensives because the number is alarming, even though the patient is asymptomatic with normal labs and exam — WRONG. The discriminator: it is the presence of acute end-organ damage, not the absolute number, that defines an emergency and justifies IV therapy; without damage this is urgency, managed with oral agents.
2. End-organ damage gallery
Acute target-organ damage of hypertensive emergency: brain (hypertensive encephalopathy, ischemic/hemorrhagic stroke), eye (papilledema, retinal hemorrhages — grade III/IV retinopathy), kidney (acute kidney injury, hematuria), heart (ACS, acute pulmonary edema/HF), aorta (acute dissection), and pregnancy (eclampsia/severe preeclampsia). Each scenario dictates a specific drug and target.
⚠️ Calling chronically stable findings like long-standing LVH or baseline proteinuria 'acute end-organ damage' — WRONG. The discriminator: emergency requires NEW, ACUTE organ injury (rising creatinine, papilledema, encephalopathy, ACS, dissection, eclampsia), not chronic sequelae of hypertension.
3. Urgency = no organ damage
Hypertensive urgency = severely elevated BP (often >180/120) with NO acute end-organ damage. Manage by restarting/optimizing ORAL agents and reducing BP gradually over 24-48 hours in the outpatient setting, with close follow-up. Rapid IV lowering is harmful here. Common cause is medication nonadherence.
⚠️ Admitting a hypertensive-urgency patient to the ICU for rapid IV BP reduction to normal — WRONG and dangerous (causes hypoperfusion). The discriminator: with no acute organ damage, use oral agents and gradual outpatient lowering; reserve IV therapy and rapid reduction for true emergencies.
4. Small kidneys / RBBB not emergencies
Distractor findings that are CHRONIC, not acute end-organ damage: bilaterally small kidneys (chronic CKD/long-standing HTN, not acute injury) and an isolated right bundle branch block (a baseline conduction pattern, not acute ischemia). Neither converts an urgency into an emergency.
⚠️ Diagnosing hypertensive emergency because imaging shows small kidneys or the ECG shows RBBB — WRONG. The discriminator: these are chronic/baseline findings; an emergency needs evidence of ACUTE injury (rising creatinine, ST changes/ACS, encephalopathy), so an asymptomatic patient with these findings is an urgency.
5. First hour: reduce <=25%
In MOST hypertensive emergencies, lower the mean arterial pressure (MAP) by no more than ~10-25% during the FIRST hour using IV titratable agents. This protects autoregulation in brain, heart, and kidney. Exceptions where you go faster/lower: aortic dissection (SBP <120 rapidly) and severe preeclampsia/eclampsia. Exception where you go slower/higher: acute ischemic stroke.
⚠️ Rapidly normalizing BP to 120/80 in the first hour of a hypertensive emergency — WRONG. The discriminator: dropping BP below the autoregulatory range causes watershed ischemia (stroke, MI, AKI); limit the first-hour reduction to ~25% of MAP except in aortic dissection and eclampsia.
6. Next 2-6 hours cautious
After the initial ~25% first-hour reduction, lower BP to approximately 160/100-110 over the next 2-6 hours, then cautiously toward normal over the following 24-48 hours if the patient is stable. This staged approach prevents hypoperfusion while still reversing the acute damage.
⚠️ Achieving full normalization within the first 6 hours of an emergency — WRONG. The discriminator: the protocol is deliberately staged (25% in hour 1, ~160/100 by 2-6h, normal over 24-48h); compressing the timeline risks ischemic organ injury.
7. Aortic dissection station
Acute aortic dissection is the most aggressive target: rapidly lower SBP to <120 mmHg AND heart rate to <60 bpm within ~20 minutes to reduce aortic wall shear stress (dP/dt). Give a BETA-BLOCKER FIRST (IV esmolol or labetalol) to control HR, THEN add a vasodilator (nitroprusside or nicardipine). Pain control with opioids also helps.
⚠️ Starting nitroprusside (a vasodilator) before beta-blockade in aortic dissection — WRONG. The discriminator: a vasodilator alone triggers reflex tachycardia that INCREASES shear stress and propagates the dissection; control heart rate with a beta-blocker first. Also, the ~25% rule does NOT apply here — dissection needs aggressive SBP <120.
8. IV drug dispensers
Titratable IV agents for emergencies, matched to scenario: nicardipine/clevidipine (most general use, neuro-friendly), labetalol/esmolol (pregnancy, dissection, most settings), nitroprusside (potent, short-acting; cyanide risk), nitroglycerin (ACS/flash pulmonary edema), hydralazine (pregnancy), and phentolamine (catecholamine excess — pheochromocytoma, cocaine/MAOI crisis).
⚠️ Using a single default agent for every emergency regardless of the organ involved — WRONG. The discriminator: the agent is scenario-specific — labetalol for eclampsia, beta-blocker-first then vasodilator for dissection, nitroglycerin for ACS/pulmonary edema, phentolamine for pheochromocytoma/cocaine.
9. Nicardipine = most general use
Nicardipine is a titratable IV dihydropyridine CCB and a versatile first choice for most hypertensive emergencies, including hypertensive encephalopathy and stroke-related HTN (it does not raise intracranial pressure or worsen cerebral autoregulation the way nitroprusside can). Clevidipine is an ultra-short-acting alternative.
⚠️ Defaulting to nitroprusside for a neuro emergency when nicardipine is available — WRONG. The discriminator: nitroprusside can raise intracranial pressure and risks cyanide toxicity; nicardipine/clevidipine are preferred, controllable choices for most emergencies including neurologic ones.
10. Labetalol = pregnancy
Labetalol is a combined alpha-1 and beta blocker — preferred in pregnancy/eclampsia (alongside IV hydralazine and oral nifedipine) and safe across most emergencies, including aortic dissection (HR + BP control in one agent). Its mixed blockade avoids reflex tachycardia. Avoid in decompensated HFrEF, bradycardia, severe asthma, and cocaine toxicity (relative — unopposed alpha concern).
⚠️ Choosing an ACEi, ARB, or nitroprusside for a hypertensive emergency in a pregnant patient — WRONG. The discriminator: ACEi/ARBs are teratogenic and nitroprusside risks fetal cyanide accumulation; labetalol (or hydralazine/nifedipine) is the safe choice for hypertensive emergency in pregnancy.
11. Nitroprusside = cyanide risk
Sodium nitroprusside is a potent arterial AND venous vasodilator with rapid on/off control, but its metabolism releases cyanide and thiocyanate — risking toxicity with high doses, prolonged use (>24-48h), or renal/hepatic impairment. It can also raise intracranial pressure and cause coronary steal. Watch for unexplained metabolic acidosis and altered mental status (cyanide).
⚠️ Continuing a nitroprusside infusion for days in a patient with renal failure and attributing new confusion/lactic acidosis to the underlying illness — WRONG. The discriminator: suspect cyanide/thiocyanate toxicity; limit duration/dose, avoid in renal failure, and choose nicardipine or labetalol when prolonged therapy is anticipated.
12. Rapid normalization harms
Over-rapid BP lowering drops perfusion below the shifted autoregulatory threshold in chronically hypertensive patients, causing watershed ischemia: ischemic stroke, AKI, and MI. This is the core rationale for the staged 25%-first-hour protocol. The goal is controlled reduction to a safe range, never abrupt normalization.
⚠️ Believing that the faster BP is brought to normal, the safer the patient — WRONG, it is the opposite. The discriminator: rapid normalization causes ischemic organ injury; chronically hypertensive brains/kidneys autoregulate at higher pressures, so reduce gradually.
13. Stroke exception (CVA)
Acute ISCHEMIC stroke is the permissive-hypertension exception: do NOT lower BP unless it exceeds 220/120 (then reduce ~15% cautiously), OR the patient is a thrombolysis/thrombectomy candidate, in which case lower to <185/110 before tPA and keep <180/105 for 24h afterward. Higher BP maintains perfusion of the ischemic penumbra. (Acute hemorrhagic stroke has its own, lower targets, e.g. SBP ~140.)
⚠️ Aggressively normalizing BP of 200/110 in a patient with an acute ischemic stroke who is not getting tPA — WRONG. The discriminator: permissive hypertension preserves penumbral perfusion; only treat if >220/120 (no tPA) or >185/110 (before tPA). Do not apply the 25% emergency rule to acute ischemic stroke.
