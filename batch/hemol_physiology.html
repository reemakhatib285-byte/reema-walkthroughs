⚠️ Board traps 14
  1. No mitochondria — RBCs make ATP via oxidative phosphorylation. WRONG — without mitochondria, mature RBCs depend entirely on anaerobic glycolysis (2 ATP/glucose). Discriminator: this is why pyruvate kinase deficiency (a glycolytic, ATP-generating step) causes chronic hemolysis with paradoxically improved exercise tolerance from elevated 2,3-BPG, not an oxidative-phosphorylation problem.
  2. No ribosomes — WRONG: 'Because G6PD-deficient cells cannot replace the enzyme, all RBCs of all ages are equally affected during an oxidant crisis.' Discriminator: lacking ribosomes, G6PD activity falls with cell age, so the OLDEST cells lyse first and young reticulocytes (enzyme-replete) are spared — explaining the self-limited hemolysis and falsely normal assay during a crisis.
  3. Bone marrow overtime — WRONG: 'A normal absolute reticulocyte count in an anemic patient still allows a diagnosis of pure hemolysis.' Discriminator: true hemolysis demands a brisk reticulocytosis (corrected retic >2-3% / reticulocyte production index >2); a normal/low retic count points to a hypoproliferative process or a concurrent marrow insult (e.g., parvovirus B19 aplastic crisis, folate deficiency).
  4. Reticulocytes: the clue — Hemolysis lowers reticulocytes. WRONG — hemolysis RAISES reticulocytes as the marrow compensates; a LOW retic during hemolysis signals a superimposed problem. Discriminator: the classic exam scenario is a chronic-hemolysis patient whose retic count suddenly DROPS — that is parvovirus B19 aplastic crisis, not the hemolysis itself.
  5. Compensated vs destruction — WRONG: 'A normal hemoglobin excludes significant hemolysis.' Discriminator: compensated hemolysis maintains a normal Hb while LDH and indirect bilirubin are high, haptoglobin is low, and reticulocytes are elevated — the hemolysis is real and can decompensate abruptly under stress.
  6. Aplastic crisis (B19) — B19 causes hemolysis directly. WRONG — parvovirus B19 does NOT lyse mature RBCs; it shuts down erythroid PRODUCTION, causing an aplastic crisis (Hb falls because nothing replaces the normally-short-lived cells). Discriminator: the giveaway is a sudden Hb drop with a LOW reticulocyte count in a chronic hemolytic patient — production failure, not increased destruction.
  7. Folate mandatory — WRONG: 'Megaloblastic changes on the smear of a chronic hemolytic patient indicate the original diagnosis was wrong.' Discriminator: chronic hemolysis depletes folate, producing a SUPERIMPOSED megaloblastic anemia with macro-ovalocytes and hypersegmented neutrophils and a dropping retic — give folate, not a new diagnosis.
  8. Pigmented gallstones — WRONG: 'A young patient with cholelithiasis and no metabolic risk factors most likely has cholesterol stones.' Discriminator: in a young, otherwise-healthy patient, pigment (calcium bilirubinate) stones from chronic hemolysis are the classic answer — look for jaundice, splenomegaly, reticulocytosis, and a hemolytic anemia underlying the gallstones.
  9. Hypersplenism → cytopenias — WRONG: 'Pancytopenia plus splenomegaly in a chronic hemolytic patient indicates bone marrow failure.' Discriminator: hypersplenism causes pancytopenia by peripheral sequestration with a HYPERcellular (compensating) marrow — the cytopenias correct after splenectomy, unlike true marrow aplasia where the marrow is hypocellular.
  10. Decompensation alarm — WRONG: 'A sudden worsening of anemia in stable chronic hemolysis is always due to accelerated hemolysis.' Discriminator: most acute decompensations are from a MARROW stressor (B19 aplastic crisis, folate deficiency, renal EPO loss, pregnancy) — check the reticulocyte count: it falls with marrow causes but rises if hemolysis truly accelerated.
  11. Intravascular → iron loss — WRONG: 'Hemoglobinuria and hemosiderinuria in a hemolytic patient indicate iron overload and warrant chelation.' Discriminator: INTRAVASCULAR hemolysis LOSES iron in the urine and can cause iron DEFICIENCY (as in PNH) — chelation would be harmful; it is EXTRAVASCULAR hemolysis (iron recycled internally) that risks overload.
  12. Extravascular → iron overload — WRONG: 'A transfusion-dependent thalassemia patient with a high ferritin has iron overload solely from the transfused blood.' Discriminator: ineffective erythropoiesis suppresses hepcidin, driving EXCESS gut iron absorption ON TOP of transfusional iron — both contribute, which is why chelation is needed even in less heavily transfused patients.
  13. Cr-51 survival gold standard — WRONG: 'The 51Cr RBC survival study is the first-line test to confirm hemolysis in a new patient.' Discriminator: although it is the gold standard for measuring RBC lifespan, it is rarely used in practice — initial workup relies on reticulocyte count, LDH, indirect bilirubin, haptoglobin, smear, and direct Coombs.
  14. Haptoglobin consumed — WRONG: 'A normal haptoglobin level excludes hemolysis.' Discriminator: haptoglobin is an acute-phase reactant, so concurrent inflammation or infection can keep it in the normal range despite active hemolysis — interpret it alongside LDH, indirect bilirubin, and reticulocyte count rather than in isolation.
📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. No mitochondria
Mature RBCs extrude their mitochondria during maturation, so they CANNOT perform oxidative phosphorylation or the Krebs cycle — all ATP comes from anaerobic glycolysis (Embden-Meyerhof pathway), netting only 2 ATP per glucose. This ATP runs the Na+/K+ and Ca2+ pumps that maintain biconcave shape and ion gradients; when ATP fails (e.g., pyruvate kinase deficiency, the most common glycolytic enzymopathy), cells dehydrate, become rigid echinocytes, and are removed extravascularly.
⚠️ RBCs make ATP via oxidative phosphorylation. WRONG — without mitochondria, mature RBCs depend entirely on anaerobic glycolysis (2 ATP/glucose). Discriminator: this is why pyruvate kinase deficiency (a glycolytic, ATP-generating step) causes chronic hemolysis with paradoxically improved exercise tolerance from elevated 2,3-BPG, not an oxidative-phosphorylation problem.
2. No ribosomes
Mature RBCs also lack ribosomes and a nucleus, so they cannot synthesize new protein — damaged enzymes (e.g., G6PD) cannot be replaced and decline as the cell ages. This is why G6PD deficiency hits the OLDEST RBCs first and why a level checked during acute hemolysis (when only young, enzyme-replete reticulocytes survive) is falsely normal. Reticulocytes still retain residual ribosomal RNA, which is what supravital stains (new methylene blue) detect.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Because G6PD-deficient cells cannot replace the enzyme, all RBCs of all ages are equally affected during an oxidant crisis.' Discriminator: lacking ribosomes, G6PD activity falls with cell age, so the OLDEST cells lyse first and young reticulocytes (enzyme-replete) are spared — explaining the self-limited hemolysis and falsely normal assay during a crisis.
3. Bone marrow overtime
In hemolysis the marrow ramps erythropoiesis up to 6-8 times baseline under EPO drive, the hallmark of a destructive (rather than productive) anemia. This compensatory erythroid hyperplasia can expand marrow space (frontal bossing, 'hair-on-end' skull, hepatosplenomegaly in chronic congenital hemolysis like thalassemia/HS) and raises folate demand. Labs of hemolysis: high LDH, high indirect bilirubin, low haptoglobin, high reticulocytes.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A normal absolute reticulocyte count in an anemic patient still allows a diagnosis of pure hemolysis.' Discriminator: true hemolysis demands a brisk reticulocytosis (corrected retic >2-3% / reticulocyte production index >2); a normal/low retic count points to a hypoproliferative process or a concurrent marrow insult (e.g., parvovirus B19 aplastic crisis, folate deficiency).
4. Reticulocytes: the clue
A high reticulocyte count is the single best clue that anemia is due to peripheral destruction/loss with an intact, responding marrow. Always CORRECT the retic for the degree of anemia (reticulocyte production index): RPI >2-3 = appropriate marrow response (hemolysis/blood loss); RPI <2 = inadequate response (marrow failure, nutrient deficiency). Polychromasia on smear is the morphologic counterpart of reticulocytosis.
⚠️ Hemolysis lowers reticulocytes. WRONG — hemolysis RAISES reticulocytes as the marrow compensates; a LOW retic during hemolysis signals a superimposed problem. Discriminator: the classic exam scenario is a chronic-hemolysis patient whose retic count suddenly DROPS — that is parvovirus B19 aplastic crisis, not the hemolysis itself.
5. Compensated vs destruction
In COMPENSATED hemolysis the marrow's increased output matches the rate of RBC destruction, so hemoglobin stays near-normal despite ongoing hemolysis — the patient has reticulocytosis, high LDH/indirect bilirubin, and low haptoglobin but little or no anemia. Decompensation (overt anemia) occurs when destruction outpaces a marrow stressed by an added insult (infection, folate deficiency, pregnancy, aplastic crisis).
⚠️ WRONG: 'A normal hemoglobin excludes significant hemolysis.' Discriminator: compensated hemolysis maintains a normal Hb while LDH and indirect bilirubin are high, haptoglobin is low, and reticulocytes are elevated — the hemolysis is real and can decompensate abruptly under stress.
6. Aplastic crisis (B19)
Parvovirus B19 infects and lyses erythroid progenitors (it uses the P antigen/globoside as its receptor), transiently halting red cell production for ~7-10 days. In someone with shortened RBC survival (HS, sickle cell, thalassemia), this brief shutdown causes a precipitous Hb drop with a near-zero reticulocyte count = aplastic crisis. Treatment is transfusion (and IVIG if immunocompromised with chronic B19); it is self-limited in the immunocompetent.
⚠️ B19 causes hemolysis directly. WRONG — parvovirus B19 does NOT lyse mature RBCs; it shuts down erythroid PRODUCTION, causing an aplastic crisis (Hb falls because nothing replaces the normally-short-lived cells). Discriminator: the giveaway is a sudden Hb drop with a LOW reticulocyte count in a chronic hemolytic patient — production failure, not increased destruction.
7. Folate mandatory
Chronic high-turnover erythropoiesis consumes folate, so daily folic acid supplementation (typically 1 mg/day, or 5 mg in pregnancy) is mandatory in chronic hemolytic anemias to prevent a superimposed megaloblastic crisis. A folate-deficient hemolytic patient can paradoxically develop a FALLING reticulocyte count (the marrow runs out of substrate) — a common decompensation trigger distinct from B19.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Megaloblastic changes on the smear of a chronic hemolytic patient indicate the original diagnosis was wrong.' Discriminator: chronic hemolysis depletes folate, producing a SUPERIMPOSED megaloblastic anemia with macro-ovalocytes and hypersegmented neutrophils and a dropping retic — give folate, not a new diagnosis.
8. Pigmented gallstones
Chronic hemolysis floods the bile with unconjugated bilirubin, which precipitates as calcium bilirubinate to form BLACK PIGMENT gallstones — a frequent complication of HS, sickle cell disease, and thalassemia, sometimes presenting in adolescence with cholecystitis. Contrast: cholesterol stones (obesity, female, estrogen) and BROWN pigment stones (biliary infection/stasis). Cholecystectomy may be combined with splenectomy in HS.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A young patient with cholelithiasis and no metabolic risk factors most likely has cholesterol stones.' Discriminator: in a young, otherwise-healthy patient, pigment (calcium bilirubinate) stones from chronic hemolysis are the classic answer — look for jaundice, splenomegaly, reticulocytosis, and a hemolytic anemia underlying the gallstones.
9. Hypersplenism → cytopenias
Persistent extravascular hemolysis enlarges the spleen, and an enlarged spleen sequesters and destroys ALL three lineages → hypersplenism with pancytopenia (anemia + leukopenia + thrombocytopenia) plus a still-cellular/hyperplastic marrow. This is mechanical pooling, not marrow failure, and it can be reversed by splenectomy. Distinguish from bone marrow failure, where the marrow itself is hypocellular.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Pancytopenia plus splenomegaly in a chronic hemolytic patient indicates bone marrow failure.' Discriminator: hypersplenism causes pancytopenia by peripheral sequestration with a HYPERcellular (compensating) marrow — the cytopenias correct after splenectomy, unlike true marrow aplasia where the marrow is hypocellular.
10. Decompensation alarm
Compensated chronic hemolysis decompensates into overt anemia when the marrow is stressed by: pregnancy (increased demand + dilution), folate deficiency (substrate exhaustion), renal failure (reduced EPO), or infection (especially parvovirus B19, which causes aplastic crisis). Recognizing the specific trigger directs management — folate, EPO, transfusion, or treating the infection.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A sudden worsening of anemia in stable chronic hemolysis is always due to accelerated hemolysis.' Discriminator: most acute decompensations are from a MARROW stressor (B19 aplastic crisis, folate deficiency, renal EPO loss, pregnancy) — check the reticulocyte count: it falls with marrow causes but rises if hemolysis truly accelerated.
11. Intravascular → iron loss
In INTRAVASCULAR hemolysis, hemoglobin is released directly into plasma: haptoglobin is consumed (low/absent), free Hb is filtered, and tubular cells take it up — sloughed tubular cells laden with hemosiderin appear in urine days later (hemosiderinuria, Prussian-blue positive). Chronic urinary iron loss can paradoxically cause iron-deficiency anemia. Classics: PNH, mechanical valve hemolysis, severe G6PD crisis, microangiopathic anemias.
⚠️ WRONG: 'Hemoglobinuria and hemosiderinuria in a hemolytic patient indicate iron overload and warrant chelation.' Discriminator: INTRAVASCULAR hemolysis LOSES iron in the urine and can cause iron DEFICIENCY (as in PNH) — chelation would be harmful; it is EXTRAVASCULAR hemolysis (iron recycled internally) that risks overload.
12. Extravascular → iron overload
In EXTRAVASCULAR hemolysis, splenic/hepatic macrophages phagocytose RBCs and RECYCLE the iron internally, so iron is retained; combined with transfusions and increased gut absorption (ineffective erythropoiesis suppresses hepcidin), chronic cases (thalassemia, repeated transfusions) develop iron OVERLOAD → cirrhosis, restrictive/dilated cardiomyopathy, endocrinopathies. Manage with iron chelation (deferasirox/deferoxamine) guided by ferritin and liver/cardiac MRI.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A transfusion-dependent thalassemia patient with a high ferritin has iron overload solely from the transfused blood.' Discriminator: ineffective erythropoiesis suppresses hepcidin, driving EXCESS gut iron absorption ON TOP of transfusional iron — both contribute, which is why chelation is needed even in less heavily transfused patients.
13. Cr-51 survival gold standard
51Cr-labeled autologous RBC survival study is the historical GOLD STANDARD for quantifying hemolysis and localizing the site of destruction (splenic vs hepatic sequestration via surface counting, which can predict splenectomy benefit). It is rarely used now because it is slow, requires radioisotope, and routine markers (retic, LDH, haptoglobin, indirect bilirubin) plus targeted assays usually suffice.
⚠️ WRONG: 'The 51Cr RBC survival study is the first-line test to confirm hemolysis in a new patient.' Discriminator: although it is the gold standard for measuring RBC lifespan, it is rarely used in practice — initial workup relies on reticulocyte count, LDH, indirect bilirubin, haptoglobin, smear, and direct Coombs.
14. Haptoglobin consumed
Haptoglobin is an acute-phase plasma protein that binds free hemoglobin; the complex is cleared by macrophages, so haptoglobin is CONSUMED and falls (often undetectable) in hemolysis — most dramatically in INTRAVASCULAR hemolysis. A low haptoglobin with high LDH is highly specific for hemolysis (LR ~100 when combined). Caveat: haptoglobin is an acute-phase reactant, so inflammation/infection can spuriously normalize it, and liver disease lowers it.
⚠️ WRONG: 'A normal haptoglobin level excludes hemolysis.' Discriminator: haptoglobin is an acute-phase reactant, so concurrent inflammation or infection can keep it in the normal range despite active hemolysis — interpret it alongside LDH, indirect bilirubin, and reticulocyte count rather than in isolation.
