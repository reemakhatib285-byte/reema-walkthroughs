📖 Read-through — all 14 symbols
1. Electrolyte chaos banner
As GFR falls below ~30 mL/min (CKD G4–5), the kidney loses its ability to fine-tune water, sodium, potassium, hydrogen ion and ammonium handling, so derangements cluster together. The three boards-classic abnormalities of advanced CKD are hyperkalemia, high-anion-gap metabolic acidosis, and hyperphosphatemia. These tend to appear together late and are the targets of medical management and, ultimately, dialysis.
⚠️ WRONG to expect symmetric loss of all functions early — CKD preserves K+, acid and phosphate homeostasis until G4–5 via compensatory mechanisms (aldosterone, ammoniagenesis, FGF23). A patient with G2–G3a and a normal K+/HCO3/phosphate is the rule, not a reason to doubt the CKD diagnosis.
2. Flooded village / HTN + edema
The failing kidney cannot excrete a sodium load, so Na+ and water are retained in iso-osmotic proportion (isotonic expansion of the extracellular fluid), producing volume overload, hypertension, and peripheral/pulmonary edema. Serum sodium concentration is typically normal because water follows salt. Management is dietary sodium restriction (<2 g/day) plus diuretics; volume overload, not renin, is the dominant driver of CKD hypertension.
⚠️ WRONG to call this hyponatremia or expect a low serum Na+ — isotonic expansion keeps [Na+] normal even as total-body sodium and water rise. True hyponatremia in CKD instead reflects impaired free-water excretion (excess water relative to salt), a different problem.
3. Furosemide strongman
Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide) block the Na-K-2Cl cotransporter in the thick ascending limb and remain the workhorse for volume control in CKD. As GFR falls, less drug is delivered into the tubular lumen and organic-anion competition rises, so higher doses are needed (diuretic resistance) — e.g., IV furosemide 80–200 mg or torsemide, which has better oral bioavailability. Torsemide/bumetanide are favored over furosemide when gut absorption is erratic.
⚠️ WRONG to abandon loops or switch to a thiazide alone at low GFR. Thiazides lose natriuretic efficacy below eGFR ~30, whereas loops still work and simply need uptitration; the discriminator is that loops act in the thick ascending limb where filtered Na+ load is high.
4. Metolazone / thiazide add-on
Metolazone is a thiazide-like agent that blocks the distal convoluted tubule Na-Cl cotransporter; added to a loop it produces sequential nephron blockade, defeating the post-loop distal sodium reabsorption that drives refractory edema. Unlike pure thiazides, metolazone retains efficacy at low GFR, making it the go-to add-on even in CKD G4. Dose ~2.5–10 mg given ~30 min before the loop; watch for profound diuresis, hypokalemia and volume depletion.
⚠️ WRONG to add metolazone and not monitor — sequential blockade can cause brisk hypokalemia, hyponatremia and prerenal AKI. The combination is potent, so it requires close electrolyte and volume surveillance rather than being a benign add-on.
5. Volume depletion / prerenal AKI
Over-diuresis, vomiting/diarrhea, or poor intake during illness causes volume depletion and prerenal acute kidney injury superimposed on CKD (acute-on-chronic), with a rising creatinine, BUN:Cr >20, and bland urine. 'Sick-day rules' instruct patients to temporarily hold diuretics, ACEi/ARB, SGLT2 inhibitors and metformin when acutely ill to protect the kidney. Volume status, not a single creatinine, guides resuscitation.
⚠️ WRONG to keep diuretics/RAAS blockers running through an acute diarrheal illness — this converts a recoverable prerenal insult into ischemic ATN. The discriminator from intrinsic AKI is the bland sediment and rapid creatinine recovery once volume is restored.
6. K stays normal until tipped
Serum potassium is defended until advanced CKD (G4–G5) because remaining nephrons increase distal K+ secretion and the colon ramps up K+ excretion. Hyperkalemia then becomes the lethal danger, with ECG changes progressing from peaked T waves to widened QRS to a sine wave and arrest. Acute hyperK is treated with IV calcium gluconate (membrane stabilization), then insulin+glucose and albuterol (intracellular shift), then loop diuretics/binders/dialysis (removal).
⚠️ WRONG to attribute a high K+ in early CKD to the renal disease itself — significant hyperkalemia in G1–G3 should prompt a search for a trigger (RAAS blocker, high-K diet, NSAID, acidosis, tissue breakdown, type 4 RTA/hypoaldosteronism), not be dismissed as expected for CKD.
7. Aldo + GI compensate
Two adaptations keep K+ normal until late CKD: aldosterone drives principal-cell K+ secretion in surviving nephrons, and the colon upregulates K+ excretion (GI compensation can clear up to ~30–50% of a K+ load in advanced disease). When these are overwhelmed or blunted (e.g., by ACEi/ARB/MRA, low aldosterone in diabetic type 4 RTA), hyperkalemia emerges.
⚠️ WRONG to assume the kidney is the only K+ excretion route — in advanced CKD the colon becomes a major escape valve, which is why constipation or stopping a potassium binder can unmask hyperkalemia.
8. Hyperkalemia triggers (RAAS, acidosis)
Common triggers that tip K+ up in CKD: high-potassium diet, metabolic acidosis (drives K+ out of cells in exchange for H+), tissue breakdown/hemolysis, and RAAS inhibitors (ACEi, ARB, MRA, finerenone). Treating the modifiable drivers — correcting acidosis with bicarbonate, restricting dietary K+, adding a K+ binder — often allows the nephroprotective RAAS blocker to be continued rather than stopped.
⚠️ WRONG to reflexively discontinue the ACEi/ARB for mild–moderate hyperkalemia. The board-preferred move is to treat the potassium (dietary K+ restriction, sodium bicarbonate for acidosis, patiromer/SZC) so the patient keeps the proven renal/cardiac protection; stopping RAAS blockade sacrifices long-term benefit for a manageable lab.
9. K binders (patiromer / SZC)
Potassium binders exchange K+ in the gut for excretion. Newer agents — patiromer (binds K+ for Ca2+ in the colon) and sodium zirconium cyclosilicate/SZC (a selective inorganic lattice, fast-acting) — are well tolerated for chronic outpatient hyperK and let patients stay on RAAS therapy. Older sodium polystyrene sulfonate (SPS/Kayexalate) is slower and carries a rare risk of intestinal necrosis, especially with sorbitol or postoperatively.
⚠️ WRONG to choose SPS as a fast emergency treatment for severe hyperkalemia — it is slow and risks colonic necrosis. Acute, ECG-positive hyperK is managed with calcium → insulin/glucose + albuterol → definitive removal (diuretics/dialysis); binders are for chronic control.
10. Avoid salt substitutes
CKD patients are counseled to restrict dietary potassium (high-K culprits: bananas, oranges/orange juice, tomatoes, potatoes, salt substitutes). 'Low-sodium' or 'lite' salt substitutes are largely potassium chloride, so they trade a sodium benefit for a dangerous potassium load. This counseling matters most in G4–5 and in patients on RAAS blockers or with type 4 RTA.
⚠️ WRONG to recommend a 'salt substitute' to a CKD patient as a heart-healthy swap — these products are potassium chloride and can precipitate life-threatening hyperkalemia. The discriminator is reading the label: KCl, not NaCl.
11. Baking soda / sodium bicarbonate
Oral sodium bicarbonate is given to keep serum HCO3 in the ~22–26 mEq/L range. Correcting metabolic acidosis reduces muscle protein catabolism, improves bone health, lowers potassium, and may slow CKD progression. Typical dosing is sodium bicarbonate 650 mg tablets titrated to the bicarbonate level; veverimer is an investigational non-absorbed alternative.
⚠️ WRONG to ignore the obligatory sodium load of NaHCO3 in a volume-overloaded or hypertensive patient — each gram delivers sodium that can worsen edema and BP. Balance acidosis correction against volume; this is the discriminator versus simply maximizing bicarbonate.
12. Early / late metabolic acidosis
CKD acidosis evolves in two stages. Early (G3) it is a normal-anion-gap (hyperchloremic) metabolic acidosis from impaired ammoniagenesis and reduced acid excretion. Late (G4–5) it becomes a high-anion-gap metabolic acidosis as phosphate, sulfate and other organic acids are retained behind the falling GFR. The anion gap pattern thus tracks CKD severity.
⚠️ WRONG to expect a high anion gap in early CKD — the G3 pattern is a NAGMA (hyperchloremic). Seeing a wide gap signals advanced disease (G4–5) or a separate cause (lactate, ketones, toxins); using the gap to stage acidosis is the key discriminator.
13. Retained NH4 / ammonium
Net acid excretion depends largely on urinary ammonium (NH4+); in CKD, fewer functioning nephrons make less ammonia, so the kidney cannot buffer and excrete the daily acid load. Because titratable acid and NH4+ excretion fall, urine pH is often inappropriately near-normal (~5.5) despite systemic acidosis — the defect is quantity of buffer, not the ability to acidify.
⚠️ WRONG to interpret a non-maximally-acidic urine pH in CKD acidosis as a distal (type 1) RTA. In CKD the urine can still acidify; the problem is reduced ammoniagenesis (low urinary ammonium / low urine anion gap interpretation), not a distal H+ secretion defect.
14. Bone buffering / demineralization skeleton
Chronic acid retention is buffered in part by bone, which releases carbonate and phosphate at the cost of demineralization, worsening CKD–mineral and bone disorder (CKD-MBD) and renal osteodystrophy. This bone buffering is another reason to correct acidosis with bicarbonate. CKD-MBD also involves hyperphosphatemia, low calcitriol, secondary hyperparathyroidism and high FGF23.
⚠️ WRONG to treat CKD bone disease as ordinary osteoporosis with bisphosphonates first — the underlying drivers are acidosis, phosphate retention, low active vitamin D and secondary hyperPTH. The fix is treating CKD-MBD (phosphate binders, calcitriol/calcimimetics, bicarbonate), not reflexive antiresorptive therapy.
