📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Target SpO2 88–92%
The oxygen saturation TARGET in COPD is 88-92% (PaO2 ~60-70 mmHg) — deliberately below normal. Titrate flow to stay within this window, typically via nasal cannula or controlled-FiO2 (Venturi) mask. This balances adequate tissue oxygenation against the risk of CO2 retention.
⚠️ Aiming for 94-100% ‘to be safe’ is WRONG — over-oxygenation worsens V/Q matching (loss of hypoxic vasoconstriction), blunts respiratory drive, and via the Haldane effect raises PaCO2, risking hypercapnic encephalopathy.
2. Too high → V/Q worsens (95%)
Excess O2 RELEASES compensatory hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction, redistributing blood to poorly ventilated alveoli and increasing dead-space ventilation → worsened V/Q mismatch and rising PaCO2. It also reduces hypoxic respiratory drive. These mechanisms, plus the Haldane effect, explain O2-induced hypercapnia.
⚠️ More oxygen is NOT always better in COPD — over-shooting SpO2 to 95-100% can precipitate hypercapnic respiratory failure.
3. Too low → tissue death (85%)
Under-oxygenating below the 88% floor causes tissue hypoxia, lactic acidosis, arrhythmia, and end-organ injury. The therapeutic window is bounded on BOTH sides — give enough O2 to clear hypoxemia, but no more than needed.
⚠️ Permissive hypoxia is wrong — don’t let a COPD patient ride at 85% out of CO2-retention fear; treat the hypoxemia and titrate to 88-92%.
4. ‘Withhold O2 / fear CO2’ = MYTH
Never WITHHOLD needed oxygen from a hypoxic COPD patient out of fear of CO2 retention — hypoxia kills faster than hypercapnia. The correct approach is to GIVE oxygen and TITRATE to 88-92%, monitoring with serial ABGs for rising CO2.
⚠️ Withholding oxygen from a hypoxic COPD patient is the wrong, dangerous answer; the right move is controlled, titrated O2 (88-92%).
5. Haldane effect (CO2 gargoyle)
The Haldane effect: oxygenated hemoglobin has LOWER affinity for CO2, so giving excess O2 displaces CO2 off hemoglobin into plasma → rising PaCO2. This acts together with loss of hypoxic vasoconstriction (worse V/Q) and reduced respiratory drive to produce O2-induced hypercapnia.
⚠️ O2-induced hypercapnia is NOT simply ‘knocking out respiratory drive’ — the Haldane effect and V/Q redistribution are the dominant mechanisms (the loss of drive is the smaller contributor).
6. NIPPV ventilator
Non-invasive positive pressure ventilation (BiPAP) is FIRST-LINE for a COPD exacerbation with respiratory acidosis (pH <7.35 with PaCO2 >45). It unloads respiratory muscles, improves alveolar ventilation, and reduces intubation, length of stay, and mortality.
⚠️ Jumping straight to intubation is wrong when NIPPV is appropriate — NIPPV is the first ventilatory step in hypercapnic COPD exacerbation unless contraindicated.
7. NIPPV contraindications (4 red doors)
NIPPV fails or is contraindicated with: hemodynamic instability/shock, impaired mental status/obtundation, inability to cooperate or protect the airway, copious secretions, facial trauma/surgery, or respiratory arrest. In these situations proceed to INVASIVE mechanical ventilation.
⚠️ Forcing NIPPV on an obtunded, vomiting, or hemodynamically unstable patient is the wrong choice — these patients need intubation, not BiPAP.
8. Systemic steroids
Systemic corticosteroids (prednisone ~40 mg/day for 5 days) shorten recovery, improve FEV1 and oxygenation, and reduce treatment failure/relapse in COPD exacerbations. Oral is as effective as IV in most patients. A short fixed course needs NO taper.
⚠️ A long steroid taper is the wrong answer for a short (~5-day) exacerbation course; extending steroids only adds hyperglycemia and other side effects without benefit.
9. Bronchodilators
Short-acting bronchodilators are the backbone of acute symptom control: SABA (albuterol) +/- SAMA (ipratropium), delivered by MDI-with-spacer or nebulizer. They provide rapid relief of bronchospasm during an exacerbation.
⚠️ Don't rely on long-acting maintenance inhalers for acute relief — SABA +/- SAMA is the rescue regimen in an exacerbation.
10. WANTED: exacerbation predictors
The single STRONGEST predictor of a future exacerbation is a PRIOR exacerbation ('frequent exacerbator' phenotype). Most exacerbations are infectious (>50%), often from acquisition of a new bacterial strain. Always consider PULMONARY EMBOLISM as a 'don't-miss' mimic of an unexplained exacerbation.
⚠️ FEV1 alone is NOT the strongest predictor of exacerbations — prior exacerbation history is. And an 'exacerbation' that doesn't fit (no infection, unilateral pleuritic pain) should prompt evaluation for PE.
11. Long-term O2 criteria (SpO2 ≤88%)
Long-term oxygen therapy (>=15 h/day) gives a MORTALITY benefit only when resting hypoxemia criteria are met: PaO2 <=55 mmHg or SpO2 <=88%, OR PaO2 56-59 / SpO2 89% WITH cor pulmonale, right heart failure, or erythrocytosis (Hct >55%). Reassess after the patient is clinically stable, not during an acute exacerbation.
⚠️ Exercise-induced or isolated nocturnal desaturation alone does NOT qualify for mortality-benefit LTOT — only resting hypoxemia meeting the thresholds does.
12. Oxygen — last resort, 17–49% mortality
Severe exacerbations requiring maximal support (ICU, invasive ventilation) carry high in-hospital mortality (~17-49% depending on severity and need for mechanical ventilation). This underscores escalating care appropriately and addressing goals of care in advanced disease.
13. Outside / ambient air
Patients who fail NIPPV or meet contraindications move 'outside' the non-invasive pathway to invasive mechanical ventilation. Decisions to escalate should weigh reversibility of the exacerbation against the patient's baseline function and advance directives.
