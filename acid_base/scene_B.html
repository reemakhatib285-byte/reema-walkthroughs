📖 Read-through — all 12 symbols
1. OSM gap rises FIRST, AG rises LATE
The intact parent alcohol is a small osmotically active molecule, so it raises the osmolar gap immediately on ingestion. Only after alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) and aldehyde dehydrogenase metabolize it to organic acids does the high anion-gap metabolic acidosis develop, hours later. The classic teaching is a high osm gap EARLY and a high AG LATE; the two findings cross over as the toxin is converted. Mnemonic: ‘parent = osmoles, metabolite = acid.’
⚠️ A student sees a normal anion gap shortly after ingestion and rules out toxic alcohol — WRONG. Early on the acid hasn't formed yet; the discriminator is the osmolar gap, which is already elevated. Conversely, a late presenter may have a high AG with a near-normal osm gap.
2. Osmolar gap = EARLY clue
Osmolar gap = measured serum osmolality − calculated osmolality, where calculated osm = 2[Na] + glucose/18 + BUN/2.8 (+ ethanol/4.6 if present). A gap >10 mOsm/kg is abnormal and suggests an unmeasured osmole such as a toxic alcohol. It rises early while the unmetabolized alcohol circulates and is the most sensitive early screen.
⚠️ A normal osm gap does NOT exclude toxic alcohol if presentation is late — by then the alcohol has been converted to acid and the gap can normalize. Also remember to subtract ethanol if the patient co-ingested it, or you'll falsely attribute the gap to a toxic alcohol.
3. Anion gap = LATE clue
Anion gap = Na − (Cl + HCO3); normal ≈ 8–12. As methanol and ethylene glycol are converted to formic acid and glycolic/oxalic acid respectively, unmeasured anions accumulate and the AG climbs while bicarbonate falls. The osm gap can normalize at the same time, so the two markers move in opposite directions over the time course.
⚠️ Late presenters may have a high AG with a near-normal osm gap, fooling a student into chasing other causes of HAGMA (think MUDPILES). Isopropyl alcohol is the exception — it never produces a high AG because acetone is a ketone, not an acid.
4. Ethylene glycol — fomepizole first-line
Fomepizole competitively inhibits alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH), the first and rate-limiting enzyme, halting conversion of ethylene glycol to its toxic acid metabolites. Dosing: 15 mg/kg IV loading, then 10 mg/kg q12h. It is first-line for both ethylene glycol and methanol and is given empirically when toxic alcohol is suspected, before levels return. Add hemodialysis for severe acidosis, high levels, or renal failure.
⚠️ Choosing ethanol infusion as the antidote is a defensible but inferior answer — fomepizole is preferred because it needs no level monitoring, causes no CNS depression or hypoglycemia, and has predictable kinetics. Ethanol is only used when fomepizole is unavailable.
5. Ethylene glycol — oxalate crystals + AKI
Ethylene glycol (antifreeze) → glycoaldehyde → glycolic acid → oxalic acid, which chelates calcium to form calcium-oxalate monohydrate crystals (envelope- or needle-shaped) that deposit in renal tubules, causing acute kidney injury and hypocalcemia. Produces a high AG + high osm gap. Urine may fluoresce under Wood's lamp if the antifreeze contained fluorescein.
⚠️ Calcium-oxalate crystalluria + AKI points to ethylene glycol, NOT methanol — a classic swap on exams. Methanol spares the kidney and hits the optic nerve instead. Don't confuse oxalate crystals here with the oxalate stones of enteric hyperoxaluria.
6. Methanol — blindness
Methanol (windshield wiper fluid, moonshine, solvents) → formaldehyde → formic acid, which inhibits cytochrome oxidase and is directly toxic to the retina and optic nerve, causing a classic ‘snowfield’ blurry vision progressing to blindness. Funduscopy shows optic disc hyperemia/edema. Produces high AG + high osm gap. Treat with fomepizole + hemodialysis + folinic/folic acid (cofactor that speeds formate metabolism to CO2 and water).
⚠️ Visual symptoms (blurry vision, blindness) point to methanol, NOT ethylene glycol, which targets the kidney with oxalate crystals. Forgetting folate/folinic acid is a common omission — it is methanol-specific adjunct therapy.
7. Antidote: block ADH + dialysis
Core principle for methanol and ethylene glycol: block alcohol dehydrogenase with fomepizole (or ethanol) to stop formation of toxic acid metabolites, then add hemodialysis to remove both the parent alcohol and accumulated acid and to correct severe acidosis. Dialysis criteria: pH <7.25–7.30, end-organ damage (visual or renal), or very high serum levels (>50 mg/dL).
⚠️ Relying on fomepizole alone in a patient with severe acidosis, renal failure, or visual loss is the wrong answer — by that point you also need dialysis to clear toxin already present. Fomepizole only prevents NEW acid formation; it doesn't remove what's there.
8. Isopropyl alcohol — ketosis, NO acidosis
Isopropyl alcohol (rubbing alcohol, hand sanitizer) → acetone via ADH. Acetone is a ketone, NOT a carboxylic acid, so it produces ketosis with a HIGH osm gap but a NORMAL anion gap and NORMAL pH. Causes profound CNS depression and a fruity breath. Key combo: ‘osmolar gap up, ketones up, acidosis absent.’
⚠️ A student sees ketones and diagnoses DKA — WRONG. Isopropyl gives ketosis WITHOUT a high anion gap and WITHOUT acidosis, and glucose is typically normal/low rather than high. The discriminator is the normal AG and normal pH despite ketonemia.
9. Isopropyl — fomepizole NOT needed
Because acetone is non-acidic and non-organ-toxic, isopropyl poisoning is managed supportively (airway, fluids, observation); hemodialysis is reserved for severe hypotension or very high levels. Fomepizole is NOT indicated — there is no toxic acid pathway to block, and blocking ADH would only prolong the parent alcohol's CNS effect.
⚠️ Giving fomepizole for isopropyl ingestion is a distractor answer — there is no acid metabolite to prevent. The right move is supportive care ± dialysis if severe.
10. Propylene glycol — lactic acidosis
Propylene glycol is the IV solvent vehicle in continuous infusions of lorazepam, diazepam, phenobarbital, and IV trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole. It is metabolized to lactate, producing a high AG metabolic acidosis plus an elevated osm gap, and can cause AKI. Suspect it in any ICU patient on prolonged high-dose IV lorazepam with an unexplained rising lactate and osm gap.
⚠️ The lactic acidosis is mistaken for sepsis or shock — WRONG; the discriminator is the elevated OSMOLAR gap accompanying the lactate plus the offending infusion. Treatment is to stop the infusion (switch to an oral or non-propylene-glycol agent), not to escalate sepsis therapy.
11. 5-oxoproline (pyroglutamic acid)
Chronic therapeutic acetaminophen use — classically in malnourished women, sepsis, pregnancy, or vegetarians — depletes glutathione and disrupts the γ-glutamyl cycle, causing accumulation of 5-oxoproline (pyroglutamic acid). This yields a high anion-gap metabolic acidosis with a NORMAL osmolar gap. Diagnosis is confirmed by elevated urine organic acids (pyroglutamic acid).
⚠️ An unexplained HAGMA with a normal osm gap and no toxic alcohol is wrongly chalked up to lactic acidosis or ketoacidosis — think 5-oxoproline from chronic acetaminophen in a malnourished patient. The acetaminophen level is often therapeutic, so a normal level does NOT exclude it.
12. 5-oxoproline — stop APAP, give NAC
Management of 5-oxoproline acidosis: stop acetaminophen (the trigger) and give N-acetylcysteine to replenish glutathione and restore normal function of the γ-glutamyl cycle. Supportive care and treating malnutrition help; the acidosis resolves once APAP is withheld.
⚠️ Continuing acetaminophen for ongoing pain control is the wrong move — it is the cause of the acidosis. The correct discriminator action is to STOP the drug and start NAC, the same antidote used for acute APAP hepatotoxicity but here for a different mechanism.
