📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. Urine chloride: <20 vs >20
Urine chloride classifies metabolic alkalosis: < 20 = saline (chloride)-responsive; > 20 = saline-resistant.
⚠️ Use urine CHLORIDE, not urine sodium — vomiting can keep urine Na high while Cl is low.
2. Vomiting / NG suction loses HCl
Loss of gastric H+ and Cl− generates the alkalosis; volume/Cl loss then maintains it. Classic saline-responsive cause (urine Cl < 20).
⚠️ Expect paradoxical aciduria — urine is acidic despite alkalemia due to avid Na/H+ handling.
3. Saline-responsive → NaCl + KCl
Chloride-responsive alkalosis (urine Cl < 20) corrects with normal saline plus potassium chloride.
⚠️ Giving saline to a saline-RESISTANT (urine Cl > 20) alkalosis won't fix it.
4. Contraction: Cl + K + volume depletion
Volume contraction with chloride and potassium depletion sustains the alkalosis (the ‘contraction’ corridor).
⚠️ You must replace Cl− AND K+ — correcting volume alone may not fix it.
5. Distal Na delivery → K+/H+ wasting
Increased distal sodium delivery + aldosterone drives K+ and H+ secretion → worsening hypokalemia and alkalosis.
⚠️ Hypokalemia both causes and worsens metabolic alkalosis — fix the K+.
6. Exogenous alkali / milk-alkali
Ingested alkali (bicarbonate, milk-alkali syndrome, massive citrate/transfusion) can generate alkalosis, especially with renal impairment.
⚠️ Milk-alkali = hypercalcemia + metabolic alkalosis + AKI from calcium/alkali intake.
7. Don't over-correct (caution)
Watch for mixed pictures and overshoot; post-DKA or post-hypercapnia states can leave a metabolic alkalosis.
⚠️ A ‘normal’ pH can still hide a mixed disorder — check the gaps and compensation.
8. Saline-resistant: mineralocorticoid excess
Excess mineralocorticoid (primary hyperaldosteronism, Cushing, Liddle) drives Na retention and K+/H+ loss → saline-resistant alkalosis (urine Cl > 20).
⚠️ These won't respond to saline — treat the underlying drive.
9. Hyperaldo → HTN + low K+
Primary hyperaldosteronism: hypertension + hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis, urine Cl > 20, low renin.
⚠️ Hypertensive + hypokalemic alkalosis = think primary hyperaldosteronism.
10. Spironolactone for aldo excess
Aldosterone-receptor blockers (spironolactone/eplerenone) treat mineralocorticoid-excess alkalosis.
⚠️ Works for aldosterone-driven causes — but NOT for Liddle (receptor-independent).
11. Liddle → amiloride (not spironolactone)
Liddle syndrome = constitutively active ENaC with LOW aldosterone/renin → use ENaC blockers (amiloride/triamterene).
⚠️ Spironolactone fails in Liddle because the channel is active independent of aldosterone.
12. Acetazolamide for volume-overloaded alkalosis
When you can't give saline (e.g. CHF / volume overload), acetazolamide induces bicarbonaturia to correct the alkalosis.
⚠️ Useful exactly when saline is contraindicated by volume status.
13. HCl infusion / dialysis = last resort
Severe symptomatic alkalemia (pH > 7.55) may need HCl infusion or dialysis.
⚠️ Reserved for severe, refractory cases — not first-line.
