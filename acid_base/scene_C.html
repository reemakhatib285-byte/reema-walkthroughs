📖 Read-through — all 13 symbols
1. DKA vs AKA vs MALA table
DKA: glucose HIGH. AKA: glucose LOW/normal. MALA: glucose variable. All three give a high anion-gap metabolic acidosis.
⚠️ Don't anchor on glucose — AKA and MALA can have normal glucose yet a severe high-AG acidosis.
2. DKA — lipolysis → ketoacids
Insulin deficiency drives lipolysis → free fatty acids → β-hydroxybutyrate + acetoacetate → high-AG ketoacidosis with hyperglycemia.
⚠️ The dominant ketone is β-OHB — not acetoacetate.
3. DKA — isotonic saline FIRST
Start fluid resuscitation with isotonic saline before/with insulin — these patients are profoundly volume-depleted.
⚠️ Insulin before fluids is the wrong order.
4. DKA — insulin 0.1 U/kg/h
Continuous IV insulin at ~0.1 U/kg/h shuts off ketogenesis and lowers glucose.
⚠️ Add dextrose once glucose ~200 to keep the insulin running and clear ketones — don't just stop insulin.
5. DKA — replace K+ before insulin
Insulin drives K+ into cells. Check K+ first; if K+ < 3.3, replace before starting insulin.
⚠️ Giving insulin to a hypokalemic patient can cause fatal arrhythmia — K+ comes first.
6. DKA — bicarb only if pH < 7.0
Bicarbonate is reserved for severe acidemia (pH < 6.9–7.0); fluids + insulin correct most cases.
⚠️ Routine bicarbonate in DKA is wrong and may worsen hypokalemia.
7. Euglycemic DKA (SGLT2 inhibitors)
SGLT2 inhibitors can cause DKA with near-normal glucose.
⚠️ Normal glucose does NOT rule out DKA in a patient on an SGLT2 inhibitor — check ketones/pH.
8. Always measure SERUM β-OHB
Urine/nitroprusside ketone tests detect acetoacetate but miss β-hydroxybutyrate — measure serum β-OHB.
⚠️ A ‘negative’ urine ketone can be falsely reassuring early — β-OHB predominates.
9. AKA — β-OHB dominant, glucose low/normal
Alcoholic ketoacidosis: chronic alcohol + starvation/vomiting → β-OHB-predominant high-AG acidosis; glucose low or normal.
⚠️ Low/normal glucose with high-AG acidosis in an alcoholic is AKA, not DKA.
10. AKA — D5NS + thiamine
Treat with dextrose-containing saline (D5NS) to switch off ketogenesis, plus thiamine.
⚠️ Give thiamine BEFORE glucose in an alcoholic to avoid precipitating Wernicke encephalopathy.
11. MALA — risk factors
Metformin-associated lactic acidosis is precipitated by renal impairment, liver failure, hypoxia, sepsis or heart failure (metformin accumulates).
⚠️ Metformin should be held in these settings and around contrast/illness to prevent MALA.
12. MALA — high AG, NORMAL osm gap
MALA is a high anion-gap lactic acidosis with a normal osmolar gap.
⚠️ A normal osm gap helps separate MALA/lactic acidosis from toxic-alcohol causes.
13. MALA — stop metformin + dialysis
Stop metformin, give supportive care, and use hemodialysis for severe acidosis / renal failure (also clears metformin).
⚠️ Fomepizole does nothing for MALA — it's not a toxic-alcohol problem.
